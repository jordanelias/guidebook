## Bias pattern (READ THIS BEFORE ANY RESEARCH)

The single most important documented finding from this session:

> **Conflating "evidence on the topic" with "evidence supporting the specific claim"**

When you find a real, verifiable citation that addresses your topic, the verification feels complete. It is not. The citation may speak to the topic without supporting your specific claim.

**Examples:**
- IEC 60118-4 is real, verified, current. It specifies hearing loop *performance*. It does NOT specify *where* loops should be installed (counters vs perimeter vs portable). The "install at counter" claim is design-derived, not evidence-derived.
- Erber 1974 is real, peer-reviewed. It demonstrates *shadow effects* on lipreading (3-12% reduction). It does NOT specify *quantitative lux thresholds* for shadow-free face illumination.
- Griggs 2019 (PMID 31414956) is real, verified. It studies *evaporative heat loss* in SCI users at rest in heat. It does NOT specify *built-environment temperature thresholds* for buildings.
- Bluetooth SIG Auracast specification is real. ABI Research forecasts 2.5M deployments by 2030. This shows *technology viability*, not that *specifying readiness now in 2026* outperforms *retrofit when needed in 2030*.

**Detection question to answer in writing before any closure:**

> Does the cited evidence specifically validate THE SPECIFIC CLAIM, or does it just speak to the topic?

If the answer is "speaks to the topic," the gap stays OPEN, or closes with low confidence and explicit acknowledgment of the gap between cited evidence and specific claim.
