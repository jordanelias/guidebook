Corridor clear width

How wide a corridor must be depends on which function the route has to serve. This page helps you decide, and it is honest about how firm each number is.

Every number here is provisional. It was re-derived by hand from source and is not yet finalised by the project's review system. Do not cite it as a settled requirement. The full record is in § 10 · Provenance.
Why provisional — project status

The underlying evidence corpus is in a deliberate pre-rehabilitation hold. The system that will render finalised, adjudicated values is not yet built. This page is a hand-authored re-derivation from the project spine (the value-genealogy worked example and the tier system). § 1

The decision

A corridor is a stack of requirements. Pick the widest function your route must serve.

Which function → which width
If the route must support…Design toWhat kind of number this is
One powered wheelchair, one direction ≥ 1200 mm Code minimum. A floor, not a target.
Two mobility devices passing ≥ 1800 mm Code convergence. A floor. No traceable measurement behind it.
The swept path of a scooter, bariatric, or children's powered chair 1900–2400 mm Measured swept envelope, by device class. See § 4.
Signed conversation in motion — any Deaf or signing use is in scope ≥ 2440 mm Required on that route. Anchored by Deaf-community evidence. See § 4 and § 6.

The one idea that decides this page

Best practice is the number with a traceable measurement root, not the number the most codes share. Many national codes agree on 1200 mm and 1800 mm. That agreement is real, but no measurement study sits behind it. The one width with independent measurement behind it — the space two people need to sign while walking — is larger. So the codes are the floor, and the evidence is above them.

One caveat travels with the ≥ 2440 mm figure everywhere on this page. It comes from one Deaf institution and one signed language (ASL). The need for generous signing space is well established. The exact millimetre is not yet confirmed across other signed languages, and a wheelchair swept-path measurement that lands nearby has not yet been retrieved and verified.

§ 2

Summary

The same corridor, read four ways.

◇ plain-language summary

At 915 mm, one manual wheelchair fits. That is the United States floor, and it was set for manual chairs.

At about 1200 mm, one powered wheelchair fits with a little clearance. That is the UK and ISO minimum.

At about 1800 mm, two devices can pass each other. Nearly every code agrees on this. No one can point to the study behind it.

At 2440 mm, two people can walk side by side and hold a signed conversation, and a powered chair has room for the path it actually sweeps when it turns. This is the only one of the four widths with independent measurement behind it.

So on a route where signed communication is in scope, the Guidebook value is ≥ 2440 mm. On other routes, size to the widest function present — usually two-device passing or the swept path of the chairs in use.

Jump to the targets (§ 4), how it fails (§ 7), or the standards table (§ 8).

§ 3

Diagram & detail

Four functions nested in one corridor. Only the outer band has a measurement root.

  • How to measure Against the device's swept path, not an idealised turning circle.
  • Clear datum Floor to 2050 mm above finished floor level.
  • Projections Any continuous projection over 25 mm below the datum is deducted. A handrail takes about 50 mm.
  • Above 2050 mm Signage, luminaires and services may project without counting.
  • Signing junctions Glaze corners so an approaching person is seen in advance. Light diffusely, at least 300 lux at face height, with no backlighting.

Reading the evidence marks

The ● full, ◐ partial and ○ weak marks say what kind of source a value rests on. They do not say how precise or how verified the number is. That is a separate note, marked confidence. A value can rest on strong-kind evidence and still have an unsettled exact figure.

◆ value

◆ evidence · ● Co-1

This value is anchored by Deaf-community knowledge (DeafSpace, Gallaudet).

Under the tier system, lived and cultural evidence ranks alongside primary research. It is not below it. The number stands on the community's own authority.

A wheelchair swept-path study (Steinfeld 2006) measures a similar width from a different direction. It is recorded as corroboration only, and it is not yet retrieved or verified.

▲ confidence

The need for generous signing space is community-authoritative. Confidence in that is high.

The exact 2440 mm figure is ASL-derived at one institution. It has not been replicated across other signed languages. It may be conservative for BSL or DGS, and the source itself warns it may be too small for large ASL groups. Treat it as a floor, not a ceiling.

○ floor · convergence Two devices passingregulatory floor ≥ 1800 mm ›
○ evidence · ○ weak

BS 8300-2, DIN 18040-1, AS 1428.1 and ISO 21542 all state 1800 mm.

That is agreement across codes. It is not measurement. No study behind the figure exists in the corpus.

Adopt 1800 mm as a floor only where 2440 mm cannot be met. Never relabel it "preferred."

Dutch case evidence (VNG 2016) confirms 1200 mm is too tight to pass or turn, that 1500 mm allows a pedestrian and a wheelchair to pass, and that 1800 mm allows two wheelchair or scooter users to pass.

▣ the class-transfer trap

A width measured for one device class is not evidence about another.

Portugal's DL 163/2006 and Korea's accessibility law both state their 1500 mm figures are manual-wheelchair-based. Singapore's 2025 code revision explicitly recognises that older codes were too small for contemporary devices.

The base anthropometry is dated (a 2010 base, now old against heavier chairs and scooters). The US 1524 mm figure is cited here as evidence of regulatory lag, not as a design value.

retrofit When you cannot reach the targetthe fallback ladder document it ›
◆ step down in order

1. Target the governing function's width (2440 mm where signing is in scope).

2. If a fixed constraint prevents it, step to the 1800 mm passing floor.

3. If even that is impossible, step to the device-class floor (1500 mm manual).

4. Below that, record it as a documented design compromise with a named decision-maker. Do not present it as compliance.

On a route where signing use is in scope, dropping below 2440 mm removes signing space. Treat that as a loss to justify, not a default to reach for.

Specification language

CORRIDOR CLEAR WIDTH — PRIMARY ROUTES  [PROVISIONAL — not for compliance]

Performance requirement:
  Primary circulation routes shall provide a clear width sufficient
  for the functions the route serves. These functions are:
    - two people passing while signing side by side, where Deaf or
      signing use is within the project brief, and
    - the swept path of the specified powered-mobility fleet.
  On signing routes this shall be not less than 2440 mm clear.
  On secondary routes it shall be not less than 1830 mm.

Compliance baseline:
  The applicable statutory code minimum (see the standards table)
  remains the compliance baseline. The values above are enhanced
  targets, not code citations.

Measurement:
  Clear width is measured against the device swept envelope, from
  finished floor to 2050 mm. Any continuous projection over 25 mm
  below that height is deducted. Recess fire extinguishers,
  sanitiser stations, call points and loose furniture.

Note: Do not cite an unverified source in a binding document.
Bind the function and the outcome, not a contested reference.
§ 5

Considerations & questions

What to settle before you fix the number.

  • Function scope Which functions must this route serve? See the decision table in § 1. The answer sets the width.
  • Signing use Is any Deaf or signing use in scope? If it is plausible, the route is a 2440 mm route. The building cannot infer this. The brief must state it.
  • Silent deletion If the population scope is "mobility access" only, does stopping at 1800 mm quietly remove signing space? Confirm Deaf use is named. The project's own data dropped it (see § 6).
  • Device classes Manual, powered, scooter, bariatric, children's powered, or attendant-propelled? The ladder in § 4 differs by class. Scooter and attendant-propelled envelopes are not modelled. Treat them as open risk.
  • New-build or retrofit Is the target achievable? If a fixed constraint forces you below it, follow the fallback ladder in § 4 and document the compromise.
  • Mixed populations Do you also serve people who need enclosure, such as some neurodivergent or dementia users? Open signing sightlines can conflict with enclosure. Resolve this by zoning distinct areas, not by averaging to a middle width. See § 7.
  • Measurement basis Are you measuring against the swept path or an idealised turning circle? Use the swept path.
  • Fixtures and seating Where will fixtures and rest seating sit? Recess them so they cannot creep into the clear width after handover.
§ 6

Populations affected

Most groups' corridor need is not a width at all. It is a different thing that happens to share the same hallway.

Ask many groups "what is your corridor requirement" and they answer with a different variable. Flattening them all into one contested millimetre is the mistake this section exists to prevent. The column on the right names what each group is actually trying to do.

DEAF · sets the width Deaf and signing peoplea language needs room to be used ≥ 2440 mm ›
◆ what the width is for

Signed conversation happens in the space between and around two people's hands, faces and upper bodies. To keep talking while walking, two signers travel side by side and need room to see each other and to watch what is ahead.

This is a communicative and cultural need, not a medical accommodation. Sign language is a language. It needs room, the way a conversation needs air.

Below the signing width, friends are forced into single file. A conversation is cut off mid-sentence. A group cannot gather. In a building meant to include Deaf people, the corridor is where their language is quietly switched off.

◆ this is a requirement, not a bonus

On a route where signing use is in scope, 2440 mm is the requirement. It is not a stretch goal to trade away under budget pressure.

The honest caveat points the other way from the usual one: the figure may be too small for large groups, not too large.

MOB / SCI · sets the width Wheeled-mobility userspassing, and the swept turn 1800–2400 mm ›
◆ what the width is for

Two devices passing (the 1800 mm floor) and the real path a powered chair sweeps when it turns (up to about 2400 mm for the whole sample).

This is also a bodily matter, not only a spatial one. Below the swept envelope, a wheelchair user is forced into a hard braking turn. The load on one arm in that turn is many times a straight push. It is repeated hundreds of times a day, over a whole life of using the chair. Upper-limb injury is common among manual users. A tight corridor is a repeated strain, not an inconvenience.

▣ capacity

In activity terms, this is moving around using equipment, and changing body position. The turn is not one event. A gentle 90° corner is not a 180° turn in an enclosed bay.

DEM · not a width People living with dementiathe variable is route shape topology ›
◆ the real variable

For dementia, the corridor variable is the shape of the route, not its width. The evidence points to simple loops and one visible choice at a time.

An older idea that "narrow corridors calm" has been retired. It never had a sound source. Dementia inherits a width only incidentally.

NDV · not a width Neurodivergent peoplethe variable is sensory load independent ›
◆ the real variable

Width and sensory load are independent. No study shows wide corridors harm. The real variables are sound, transition zones and visual clutter.

An old "wide corridors overwhelm" conflict was retired for that reason. The genuine tension is with open sightlines, not with width. See § 7.

OFS · PAIN · not a width Fatigue and chronic-pain conditionsthe variable is rest interval interval ›
◆ the real variable

The variable here is how far apart rest points sit, not how wide the corridor is.

It touches width in one way only, the alcove rule: recess rest seating at least 200 mm off the clear width, so a place to rest never eats the passing space.

VIS · inherits width Blind and low-vision peopleguide-pair travel guide-pair ›
◆ the real variable

A sighted guide and traveller walk shoulder to shoulder, so this group inherits a two-person width.

Its own primary need is about detection and edges, not cross-section.

The project's own data dropped the group that anchors this item. As of this session, E-08's population links in the live database are dementia, mobility, spinal-cord injury and vision. Deaf is absent, even though Deaf-community evidence supplies the strongest width on the page. Restoring that link is on the correction list in § 10.
§ 7

Failure cases

How this provision goes wrong in real buildings.

✗ single-file pinch"One chair fits" mistaken for "the corridor works"at 1200›
▲ what happens

A corridor at the 1200 mm minimum takes one powered chair and nothing else.

Two devices cannot pass. A signed conversation collapses into single file. Meeting the minimum is not meeting the need.

✗ signing space deletedA mobility-only spec quietly removes the Deaf routesilent loss›
▲ what happens

A team designs for "mobility access", stops at the 1800 mm passing floor, and moves on.

No one decided to remove signing space. It was simply never named, so it was never drawn. The corridor is built, and Deaf users find they cannot hold a conversation in it. Because the data also dropped the Deaf link (§ 6), nothing in the system flags the loss either.

The guard is simple: the 2440 mm signing width already contains the 1800 mm passing width. Build the larger, and both are served.

✗ injury doseA width below the swept path forces the hard turnrepeated strain›
▲ what happens

Below the swept envelope, a wheelchair user must brake and pivot hard to turn.

That turn loads one arm many times over a straight push. Repeated all day, for years, it is a documented source of upper-limb injury. The corridor width is, in effect, a dose.

✗ sightlines vs enclosureOpen signing design clashes with enclosure needszone it›
▲ what happens

The open sightlines that serve Deaf users can be the opposite of what some neurodivergent or dementia users need, which is enclosure and clear bounds.

You cannot resolve this by picking a width in the middle. Resolve it by giving each need its own zone: open communication areas, and separate calm, bounded retreats.

✗ creepFixtures and doors eat the clear width after handover−width›
▲ what happens

A fire extinguisher, a sanitiser stand or a planter is added after the building opens, and projects into the pinch point.

Or a door opening is narrower than the corridor, and re-creates the very pinch the width was meant to remove. Recess fixtures, hatch the clear zone on the drawings, and walk the route after occupancy.

✗ convergence sold as evidence"1800 is best practice because every code agrees"method error›
▲ what happens

Agreement among codes gets read as proof. It is not. It is the floor, with no measurement behind it.

This is the exact error an earlier version of this very page committed, by calling the 1200 and 1800 figures the Guidebook value.

§ 9

Relevant products

Components the width implies. These are classes, not brands.

Measurable quick reference

  • Continuous handrail: deduct about 50 mm from the clear width.
  • Recessed fixtures (extinguisher, sanitiser, call point): 0 mm projection into the clear zone.
  • Rest-seating alcove: recessed at least 200 mm off the clear width.
  • Door clear opening: at least 900 mm; not narrower than the corridor.
  • Communication-zone lighting: at least 300 lux at face height, diffuse, no backlighting.
handrailsContinuous handrailsprojection deducts from clear width−50 mm›
◆ effect

A continuous rail counts against the clear width, because a wheelchair cannot pass through it. Stainless is about 50 mm. Brass or timber project more.

A wall-recessed grab rail does not count. Size the wall-to-wall so the clear dimension survives the deduction.

glazingGlazed junction cornerssee an approaching person in advanceat junctions›
◆ effect

Glaze corners rather than leaving them solid. A signer walking and watching their companion's hands needs advance sight of anyone approaching the junction. This is the sightline half of the signing provision.

finishes + lightMatte finishes and diffuse lightso hands and faces stay legible≥ 300 lux›
◆ effect

Use matte, medium-contrast wall finishes so that hands, faces and facial grammar read clearly against them. Aim for a mid-tone that holds contrast against a range of skin tones.

Light the communication zone diffusely, at least 300 lux at face height, with no backlighting and no glare. As a testable target, choose finishes in a mid light-reflectance band rather than specifying a colour by name.

recessed fixturesWall-recessed fixturesso they cannot creep into the clear width0 mm›
◆ effect

Recess fire extinguisher cabinets, sanitiser stands, call points and low signage below the 2050 mm datum, so they do not eat the clear width after handover. Mark the clear zone in hatching at the fixtures review.

seating + doorsAlcove seating and wide doorskeep the passing width intact≥ 200 mm recess›
◆ effect

Recess rest seating at least 200 mm off the clear width. Keep door openings at least 900 mm, and no narrower than the corridor.

In signing zones, use movable furniture without fixed armrests, so people can form a conversation circle.

The corpus holds no product catalogue. These are component classes implied by the requirements above, not brand recommendations.

Provenance, integrity & evidence

What this page is, what it is built from, and what it fixes.

Sources you can cite

Deaf-community · anchorDeafSpace and Deaf-authored sourcesthe ≥ 2440 mm anchorCo-1›
◆ citations

DeafSpace Design Guidelines, Gallaudet University (2010). This is the primary source for the signing-space dimensions. It is community-informed, led by an architect at a Deaf university. It is an institutional guideline, not a disabled-persons'-organisation output.

Vaughn, DeafScape (2018), extends DeafSpace principles.

Cloete & Rout (2025), a scoping review, confirms the principles apply across cultures. It does not re-measure the 2440 mm figure.

Deaf-authored scholarship — Kusters (2015) and Bauman & Murray, Deaf Gain (2014) — grounds the cultural and linguistic framing and should be read alongside the guideline.

No individual Deaf lived-experience account is yet recorded for this item. A spec anchored by Deaf knowledge with no individual Deaf voice on file is an obligation, not a footnote. Recording that testimony is on the queue below.

swept path · unverifiedWheeled-mobility measurementcorroborates 2440, not yet retrievedpending›
◐ status

Steinfeld and colleagues (2006, 2010) measured swept-path envelopes. The whole-sample figure reaches about 2400 mm in an enclosed bay.

The 2006 measurement is not yet retrieved and verified in the corpus. Until it is, it corroborates the signing width but does not co-anchor it.

The injury-dose figures in § 6 and § 7, and the turn statistics, are relayed from the project's methodology notes. They are not yet independently registered in our reference list. Treat them as directional.

codes · floorStatutory codesthe § 8 tablefloor›
◆ role
