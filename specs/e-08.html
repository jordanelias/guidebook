E-08 Cat. E · Circulation & Access parameter · clear_width ◐ provisional

Corridor clear width

How wide a corridor must be depends on which function the route serves, including the path a wheelchair sweeps when it turns. This page helps you decide, and every value carries its sources.

Every number here is provisional. It was re-derived by hand from source and is not yet finalised by the project's review system. Do not cite it as a settled requirement. The full record is in § 10 · Provenance.
Why provisional — project status

The underlying evidence corpus is in a deliberate pre-rehabilitation hold. The system that will render finalised, adjudicated values is not yet built. This page is a hand-authored re-derivation from the project spine. It replaces an earlier version of this file that carried a fabricated citation. See § 10.

§ 1

The decision

A corridor is a stack of requirements. Pick the widest function your route must serve.

Which function → which width
If the route must support…Design toWhat kind of number this isSources
One powered wheelchair, one direction ≥ 1200 mm Code minimum. A floor, not a target.
Two mobility devices passing without turning ≥ 1800 mm Code convergence. A floor. No measurement behind it.
A device turning or maneuvering — junctions, room entries, passing with rotation to ≈ 2400 mm The swept path governs here, not a straight-line width. Measured envelope.
Signed conversation in motion — any Deaf or signing use is in scope ≥ 2440 mm Required on that route. Anchored by Deaf-community evidence.

Two of these four push the corridor to about the same place. Wherever a device must turn, the swept envelope reaches roughly 2400 mm, and a corridor wide enough to pass without turning removes the hardest maneuver entirely. Where signing is in scope, the signing pair needs about 2440 mm. So the mobility case and the Deaf case converge on best practice from two independent directions.

The one idea that decides this page

Best practice is the number with a traceable measurement root, not the number the most codes share. Many national codes agree on 1200 mm and 1800 mm. That agreement is real, but no measurement study sits behind it. The widths with measurement behind them — the swept path of a turning chair, and the space two people need to sign while walking — are larger. So the codes are the floor, and the evidence sits above them.

One caveat travels with the ≥ 2440 mm figure everywhere on this page. It comes from one Deaf institution and one signed language (ASL). The need for generous signing space is well established. The exact millimetre is not yet confirmed across other signed languages, and the wheelchair swept-path measurement that lands nearby has not yet been retrieved into the project's reference table.

§ 2

Summary

The same corridor, read four ways.

◇ plain-language summary

At 915 mm, one manual wheelchair fits. That is the United States floor, and it was set for manual chairs.

At about 1200 mm, one powered wheelchair fits with a little clearance. That is the UK and ISO minimum.

At about 1800 mm, two devices can pass each other. Nearly every code agrees on this. No one can point to the study behind it.

At about 2400 mm, a powered chair has room for the path it actually sweeps when it turns, so it never has to force a tight, high-strain spin.

At 2440 mm, two people can also walk side by side and hold a signed conversation. The mobility width and the signing width land in the same place.

So on a route where signing is in scope, the Guidebook value is ≥ 2440 mm. Elsewhere, size to the widest function present — usually two-device passing, or the swept path wherever the route turns.

Jump to the targets (§ 4), how it fails (§ 7), or the standards table (§ 8).

Diagram & detail

Four functions nested in one corridor. Two of them reach ~2400 mm.

  • How to measure Against the device's swept path, not an idealised turning circle.
  • Clear datum Floor to 2050 mm above finished floor level.
  • Projections Any continuous projection over 25 mm below the datum is deducted. A handrail takes about 50 mm.
  • The turn A width below the swept envelope forces a tight spin turn, at a high braking-force cost to the arm.
  • Signing junctions Glaze corners so an approaching person is seen in advance. Light diffusely, at least 300 lux at face height, no backlighting.
§ 4

Best-practice targets

Each value, with the kind of evidence behind it kept separate from how firm the number is.

Two roads to about 2400 mm

The best-practice width is not carried by one source. Two independent needs arrive at nearly the same figure. The swept path of a turning powered chair reaches about 2400 mm, and a corridor that wide lets people pass without the injury-dose spin turn. The signing pair needs about 2440 mm to keep a conversation going while walking. One is a mobility measurement, the other is Deaf-community knowledge. They agree.

Reading the evidence marks

The ● full, ◐ partial and ○ weak marks say what kind of source a value rests on. They do not say how precise or how verified the number is. That is a separate note, marked confidence.

● signing routes Signed conversation in motiontwo people signing, side by side ≥ 2440 mm ›
◆ value

Primary routes: ≥ 2440 mm. Secondary routes: ≥ 1830 mm. Exterior routes with signing groups: ≥ 3050 mm.

Distance between people signing: 900–3050 mm, by group size. Sightlines unobstructed for at least 3 m.

◆ evidence · ● Co-1

Anchored by Deaf-community knowledge (DeafSpace, Gallaudet). Under the tier system, lived and cultural evidence ranks alongside primary research, not below it. The number stands on the community's own authority.

▲ confidence

The need for generous signing space is community-authoritative. Confidence in that is high.

The exact 2440 mm figure is ASL-derived at one institution. It is not yet replicated across other signed languages. The source warns it may be too small for large ASL groups. Treat it as a floor, not a ceiling.

◐ turning & maneuvering The swept path of a turning chairthis is where width is really tested to ≈ 2400 mm ›
◆ why turning sets the width

A wheelchair does not pivot on a spot. When it turns to enter a room, round a junction, or pass another device, it sweeps an asymmetric path wider than its own footprint. Footrests and feet swing through a separate forward arc.

Measured whole-sample swept envelopes reach about 2400 mm in an enclosed bay. The project's own derivation sets powered-chair turning space at not less than 1800 by 1800 mm. A straight passing width alone does not cover the turn.

▲ the cost of getting it wrong

A width below the swept envelope forces a tight spin turn. In that turn the braking force on the inner hand is many times a straight push, and a wheelchair user makes hundreds of turns a day.

Over years this is a documented source of shoulder, elbow and wrist injury. A corridor wide enough to turn, or wide enough to avoid turning, lowers that dose.

○ floor · convergence Two devices passing straightregulatory floor ≥ 1800 mm ›
○ evidence · ○ weak

BS 8300-2, DIN 18040-1, AS 1428.1 and ISO 21542 all state 1800 mm. That is agreement across codes. It is not measurement.

Adopt 1800 mm as a floor only where the governing width cannot be met. Never relabel it "preferred."

Dutch case evidence confirms 1200 mm is too tight to pass or turn, 1500 mm lets a pedestrian and a wheelchair pass, and 1800 mm lets two wheelchair or scooter users pass.

retrofit When you cannot reach the targetthe fallback ladder document it ›
◆ step down in order

1. Target the governing function's width. That is 2440 mm where signing is in scope, or the swept-path width where the route turns.

2. If a fixed constraint prevents it, step to the 1800 mm straight-passing floor.

3. If even that is impossible, step to the device-class floor, 1500 mm for manual.

4. Below that, record a documented design compromise with a named decision-maker. Do not present it as compliance.

On a signing route, dropping below 2440 mm removes signing space. Treat that as a loss to justify.

Performance requirement:
  Primary circulation routes shall provide a clear width sufficient
  for the functions the route serves. These functions are:
    - two people passing while signing side by side, where Deaf or
      signing use is within the project brief,
    - the swept path of the specified powered-mobility fleet where
      the route turns, meets a junction, or admits a room, and
    - two mobility devices passing in a straight run.

  On secondary routes it shall be not less than 1830 mm.

  The applicable statutory code minimum (see the standards table)
  remains the compliance baseline. The values above are enhanced
  targets, not code citations.

  Clear width is measured against the device swept envelope, from

§ 5

Considerations & questions

What to settle before you fix the number.

  • Function scope Which functions must this route serve? See the decision table in § 1. The answer sets the width.
  • Turning points Where does the route turn, meet a junction, or admit a room? Those points are governed by the swept path, not the straight-passing width. Size them to the envelope.
  • Signing use Is any Deaf or signing use in scope? If it is plausible, this is a 2440 mm route. The building cannot infer it. The brief must state it.
  • Silent deletion If the scope is "mobility access" only, does stopping at 1800 mm quietly remove signing space? The project's own data dropped the Deaf link (see § 6).
  • Device classes Manual, powered, scooter, bariatric, children's powered, or attendant-propelled? The ladder in § 4 differs by class. Scooter and attendant-propelled envelopes are not modelled.
  • New-build or retrofit Is the target achievable? If a fixed constraint forces you below it, follow the fallback ladder in § 4 and document the compromise.
  • Mixed populations Do you also serve people who need enclosure, such as some neurodivergent or dementia users? Open signing sightlines can conflict with enclosure. Zone the two needs. Do not average them. See § 7.
  • Measurement basis Are you measuring against the swept path or an idealised turning circle? Use the swept path.
§ 6

Populations affected

Most groups' corridor need is not a width at all. It is a different thing sharing the same hallway.

Ask many groups "what is your corridor requirement" and they answer with a different variable. Flattening them all into one contested millimetre is the mistake this section prevents. The right-hand tag names what each group is actually trying to do.

DEAF · sets the widthDeaf and signing peoplea language needs room to be used≥ 2440 mm›
◆ what the width is for

Signed conversation lives in the space between and around two people's hands, faces and upper bodies. To keep talking while walking, two signers travel side by side, and need to see each other and what is ahead.

This is a communicative and cultural need, not a medical accommodation. Sign language is a language. It needs room, the way a conversation needs air.

Below the signing width, friends are forced into single file. A conversation is cut off mid-sentence. A group cannot gather. In a building meant to include Deaf people, the corridor is where their language is quietly switched off.

◆ a requirement, not a bonus

On a route where signing is in scope, 2440 mm is the requirement. It is not a stretch goal to trade away.

The honest caveat points toward "may be too small for large groups," not "maybe more than needed."

MOB / SCI · sets the widthWheeled-mobility userspassing, and the swept turn1800–2400 mm›
◆ what the width is for

Two devices passing sets the 1800 mm floor. But the harder test is the turn. When a powered chair turns into a room or rounds a junction, it sweeps a path up to about 2400 mm for the whole sample. Wherever the route turns, that envelope, not the straight width, governs.

▲ a bodily cost, not only a spatial one

Below the swept envelope, a wheelchair user is forced into a hard braking turn. The load on one arm in that turn is many times a straight push. It is repeated hundreds of times a day, over a whole life of using the chair.

Upper-limb injury is common among manual users. A tight corridor is a repeated strain, not an inconvenience. A width that lets the chair turn freely, or pass without turning, lowers the dose.

DEM · not a widthPeople living with dementiathe variable is route shapetopology›
◆ the real variable

For dementia, the corridor variable is the shape of the route, not its width. The evidence points to simple loops and one visible choice at a time.

An older idea that "narrow corridors calm" has been retired. It never had a sound source. Dementia inherits a width only incidentally.

NDV · not a widthNeurodivergent peoplethe variable is sensory loadindependent›
◆ the real variable

Width and sensory load are independent. No study shows wide corridors harm. The real variables are sound, transition zones and visual clutter.

An old "wide corridors overwhelm" conflict was retired for that reason. The genuine tension is with open sightlines, not with width. See § 7.

OFS · PAIN · not a widthFatigue and chronic-pain conditionsthe variable is rest intervalinterval›
◆ the real variable

The variable here is how far apart rest points sit, not how wide the corridor is.

It touches width in one way only, the alcove rule: recess rest seating at least 200 mm off the clear width, so a place to rest never eats the passing space.

VIS · inherits widthBlind and low-vision peopleguide-pair travelguide-pair›
◆ the real variable

A sighted guide and traveller walk shoulder to shoulder, so this group inherits a two-person width.

Its own primary need is about detection and edges, not cross-section.

The project's own data dropped the group that anchors this item. As of this session, E-08's population links in the live database are dementia, mobility, spinal-cord injury and vision. Deaf is absent, even though Deaf-community evidence supplies the strongest width on the page. Restoring that link is on the correction list in § 10.
§ 7

Failure cases

How this provision goes wrong in real buildings.

✗ single-file pinch"One chair fits" mistaken for "the corridor works"at 1200›
▲ what happens

A corridor at the 1200 mm minimum takes one powered chair and nothing else.

Two devices cannot pass. A signed conversation collapses into single file. Meeting the minimum is not meeting the need.

✗ signing space deletedA mobility-only spec quietly removes the Deaf routesilent loss›
▲ what happens

A team designs for "mobility access", stops at the 1800 mm passing floor, and moves on.

No one decided to remove signing space. It was simply never named, so it was never drawn. The corridor is built, and Deaf users find they cannot hold a conversation in it. Because the data also dropped the Deaf link (§ 6), nothing in the system flags the loss either.

The guard is simple. The 2440 mm signing width already contains the 1800 mm passing width. Build the larger, and both are served.

✗ injury doseA width below the swept path forces the hard turnrepeated strain›
▲ what happens

Below the swept envelope, a wheelchair user must brake and pivot hard to turn.

That turn loads one arm many times over a straight push. Repeated all day, for years, it is a documented source of upper-limb injury. The corridor width is, in effect, a dose. A route sized to the swept path, or wide enough to avoid the turn, reduces it.

✗ sightlines vs enclosureOpen signing design clashes with enclosure needszone it›
▲ what happens

The open sightlines that serve Deaf users can be the opposite of what some neurodivergent or dementia users need, which is enclosure and clear bounds.

You cannot resolve this by picking a width in the middle. Resolve it by giving each need its own zone: open communication areas, and separate calm, bounded retreats.

✗ creepFixtures and doors eat the clear width after handover−width›
▲ what happens

A fire extinguisher, a sanitiser stand or a planter is added after the building opens, and projects into the pinch point.

Or a door opening is narrower than the corridor, and re-creates the very pinch the width was meant to remove. Recess fixtures, hatch the clear zone on the drawings, and walk the route after occupancy.

✗ convergence sold as evidence"1800 is best practice because every code agrees"method error›
▲ what happens

Agreement among codes gets read as proof. It is not. It is the floor, with no measurement behind it.

This is the exact error an earlier version of this very page committed, by calling the 1200 and 1800 figures the Guidebook value.

§ 9

Relevant products

Components the width implies. These are classes, not brands.

Measurable quick reference

  • Continuous handrail: deduct about 50 mm from the clear width.
  • Recessed fixtures (extinguisher, sanitiser, call point): 0 mm projection into the clear zone.
  • Rest-seating alcove: recessed at least 200 mm off the clear width.
  • Door clear opening: at least 900 mm, and not narrower than the corridor.
  • Communication-zone lighting: at least 300 lux at face height, diffuse, no backlighting.
handrailsContinuous handrailsprojection deducts from clear width−50 mm›
◆ effect

A continuous rail counts against the clear width, because a wheelchair cannot pass through it. Stainless is about 50 mm. Brass or timber project more.

A wall-recessed grab rail does not count. Size the wall-to-wall so the clear dimension survives the deduction.

glazingGlazed junction cornerssee an approaching person in advanceat junctions›
◆ effect

Glaze corners rather than leaving them solid. A signer walking and watching a companion's hands needs advance sight of anyone approaching the junction. This is the sightline half of the signing provision.

finishes + lightMatte finishes and diffuse lightso hands and faces stay legible≥ 300 lux›
◆ effect

Use matte, medium-contrast wall finishes so hands, faces and facial grammar read clearly against them. Choose a mid-tone that holds contrast against a range of skin tones.

Light the communication zone diffusely, at least 300 lux at face height, with no backlighting and no glare. As a testable target, specify finishes in a mid light-reflectance band rather than naming a colour.

recessed fixturesWall-recessed fixturesso they cannot creep into the clear width0 mm›
◆ effect

Recess fire extinguisher cabinets, sanitiser stands, call points and low signage below the 2050 mm datum, so they do not eat the clear width after handover. Mark the clear zone in hatching at the fixtures review.

seating + doorsAlcove seating and wide doorskeep the passing width intact≥ 200 mm recess›
◆ effect

Recess rest seating at least 200 mm off the clear width. Keep door openings at least 900 mm, and no narrower than the corridor.

In signing zones, use movable furniture without fixed armrests, so people can form a conversation circle.

The corpus holds no product catalogue. These are component classes implied by the requirements above, not brand recommendations.

Provenance, integrity & evidence

What this page is, what it is built from, and what it fixes.

This page is a hand-authored re-derivation from the project spine. It is not produced by an adjudication engine, because that engine is not yet built. Sources are named by author, year and title, which are stable. Internal reference numbers are avoided in the text, because they are known to collide across the project's files. Every claim above links to a source list here or to a primary reference.

Sources you can cite

Deaf-community · anchorDeafSpace and Deaf-authored sourcesthe ≥ 2440 mm anchorCo-1›
◆ citations

DeafSpace Design Guidelines, Gallaudet University (2010), is the primary source for the signing dimensions. It is community-informed, led by an architect at a Deaf university. It is an institutional guideline, not a disabled-persons'-organisation output.

Vaughn, DeafScape (2018), extends the principles. Cloete & Rout (2025), a scoping review, confirms they apply across cultures but does not re-measure 2440 mm. Deaf-authored scholarship — Kusters (2015) and Bauman & Murray, Deaf Gain (2014) — grounds the cultural framing.

No individual Deaf lived-experience account is yet recorded for this item. A spec anchored by Deaf knowledge with no individual Deaf voice on file is an obligation, not a footnote.

swept path + turningMobility measurementthe second driver of ~2400 mmTier 1–3›
◆ citations

Chaikhot et al. (2023) is a Tier 1 biomechanical study of the corridor-entry turn. It is a real, verifiable paper with a DOI and PubMed ID, though it is not yet entered in the project's reference table.

Steinfeld and colleagues (2006, 2010) measured swept-path envelopes reaching about 2400 mm. The 2006 measurement is not yet retrieved and verified into the corpus. Sonenblum et al. (2012) and Boninger et al. supply the turn-frequency and injury figures.

Where a source is not yet in the project reference table, its popup says so. Treat those figures as directional until they are entered.

codes · floorStatutory codesthe § 8 tablefloor›
◆ role

ADA, BS 8300-2, DIN 18040-1, AS 1428.1, TEK17, CSA B651 and ISO 21542 supply the legal floor in each place. The § 8 table is taken cell for cell from the project jurisdiction data.

Their agreement is useful for tracking the law. It is not evidence of best practice.

How to read it

Best practice is the number with a measurement root, not the number the most codes share. Two roots reach ~2400 mm: the swept turn, and the signing pair. Every value carries its sources.

Corrections in this version

A fabricated citation and three unrecorded accounts were removed. The Deaf anchor was restored to the page. All values are provisional.
