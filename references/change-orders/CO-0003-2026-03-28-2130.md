## Rationale

The guidebook currently uses "cross-population" to describe two distinct phenomena:

1. **Intra-individual co-occurrence:** One person has multiple co-occurring disabilities (e.g., a person with dementia who also has mobility impairment, chronic pain, and visual decline). This is the norm, not the exception. The modal resident of a dementia care setting has 4–6 concurrent conditions.

2. **Inter-group co-occupation:** Multiple people with different primary disability profiles occupy the same building or space (e.g., a public library serving DEAF visitors, NDV/AUT visitors, and VIS visitors who are different individuals).

"Cross-population" conflates both cases, implies populations are monolithic groups rather than collections of individuals with variable profiles, and frames disability accommodation as inter-group negotiation rather than individual person-environment fit. This conflicts with:

- The CRPD (individual rights framework)
- The three-tier hierarchy (Tier 2 = individual co-design)
- Lawton's competence-press model (individual competence varies)
- The social model (environment disables individuals, not groups)
- OT clinical practice (OT assesses individuals, not populations)

## Terminology Changes

### Retired terms

| Old term | Problem | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| "cross-population" (generic) | Ambiguous — conflates intra-individual and inter-group | Use specific term below |
| "cross-population conflict" | Implies two groups in opposition | "co-occurring disability conflict" (intra-individual) or "multi-population conflict" (inter-group) |
| "cross-population resolution" | Same | "co-occurrence resolution" or "multi-population resolution" |
| "designing for multiple disability categories" | Frames as categories, not individuals | "designing for co-occurring disabilities" |

### New terms

| Term | Definition | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Co-occurring disability** | Two or more disabilities present in the same individual | Discussing provisions for individuals who have multiple conditions simultaneously — the primary case |
| **Multi-population** | Two or more disability populations with different primary profiles occupying the same building or space | Discussing building-level design where different users have different primary conditions |
| **Intra-individual conflict** | A specification conflict where one person's co-occurring conditions produce competing environmental needs | E.g., a person with MS (needs cool) and fibromyalgia (needs warm) |
| **Inter-group conflict** | A specification conflict where different occupants of the same space have competing environmental needs | E.g., DEAF users need visual alerting; NDV/AUT users are photosensitive |

### Usage rule

Default to **co-occurring disability** unless the context is explicitly about different individuals in the same space. Most "conflicts" in the guidebook are actually intra-individual co-occurrence conflicts, not inter-group conflicts.

### Part 2 — Methodology note addition

Add to Part 2 introduction:

> Disability categories in this guidebook are analytical tools for organising evidence and specification. They do not describe real individuals. Most people who use accessible environments present with co-occurring disabilities — multiple conditions simultaneously. A person accessing a dementia care setting typically has mobility impairment, chronic pain, visual decline, and cognitive change concurrently. The category codes (MOB, VIS, DEM, etc.) identify the evidence tradition from which a specification derives; they do not imply that a building serves discrete, mutually exclusive groups. See Part 3 for co-occurrence design and Part 7 for conflict resolution.

### Part 1 — Framework addition

Add to §1.4.3 (The Three-Tier Hierarchy) or §1.4.4:

> The three-tier hierarchy addresses co-occurring disability directly. Tier 0 universal provisions reduce environmental press for all occupants regardless of disability profile. Tier 1 population-informed defaults calibrate provisions for identified populations — but real individuals rarely map to a single population code. Tier 2 co-design resolves the individual's specific co-occurring needs within Tier 1 ranges through OT assessment. The hierarchy is not a disability accommodation ladder; it is a person-environment fit optimisation framework operating on the Lawton docility-proactivity continuum (CON-0028).

## Population Coding Doctrine Amendment

Add to Population Codes documentation:

> **Co-occurrence notation:** Where a provision addresses co-occurring disabilities in a single individual, list all applicable codes separated by `+` (e.g., `DEM+MOB+PAIN`). Where a provision addresses multi-population building-level design, list codes separated by `/` as currently (e.g., `DEAF / NDV/AUT`). The `+` notation indicates the codes describe one person; the `/` notation indicates they describe different people in the same space.
