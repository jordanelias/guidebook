## FDR-DEM-01 — d460 + dementia → wayfinding (spatial decision reduction, landmark, visual access)

**Sources:** PMC8725382 (Tier 2 SR); PMC11931140 (case studies, Tier 2); PMC8545728 (review, Tier 2); Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Decision points on route — minimised | Maximum 1–2 binary decisions between any origin and destination; eliminate corridor crossroads where possible | Dementia: decision-making capacity progressively impairs; each intersection requiring route choice is a failure point — reduce to minimum | PMC8725382 (wayfinding SR); PMC8545728 | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — decision point minimisation as spatial layout specification absent from BPC |
| Destination visibility — direct sightlines | Key destination (bathroom, bedroom, kitchen) visible from point of daily use origin where feasible; glazed panels in key room doors | Dementia wayfinding case studies: direct visibility to destination reliably supports navigation where spatial memory fails | PMC11931140 (wayfinding case studies); PMC8725382 | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — destination visibility through glazed door or sightline as design specification absent from BPC |
| Corridor crossroads — high-visibility anchor | Architectural articulation (change in ceiling height, lighting, colour, material) at each corridor intersection to serve as a memorable decision-point anchor | Dementia: wayfinding relies on recognisable landmarks at decision points; featureless intersections are disorienting | PMC8725382; PMC8545728 | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — intersection landmark specification absent from BPC |
| Room entry door — high contrast and colour differentiation | Entry doors to key rooms (bathroom, bedroom) use contrasting colour to surrounding wall and to each other; distinctively coloured, not neutral | Dementia: room identification requires visual distinctiveness; neutral or matching doors are indistinguishable | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD); PMC6867684 | Co-2/Tier 2 | REFINES existing BPC colour contrast guidance; adds room-to-room differentiation principle |
| Non-destination doors — reduced salience | Storage/utility doors visually recessed or minimised; consistent with wall surface in colour/texture | Dementia with wandering: salient doors are triggers for exit-seeking; reducing visual salience of non-relevant doors reduces confusion and inappropriate door opening | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD); PMC8545728 | Co-2/Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — non-destination door salience reduction as design specification absent from BPC |
| Signage mounting height | Lower edge ≤1220mm (4ft) AFF | Dementia: habitual visual scan direction is downward; signs at standard mounting height (above eye level) are frequently missed | Benbow 2013 cited in GVSU wayfinding review; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — lower-edge mounting height for dementia signage absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (Environmental Docility Hypothesis):** People with dementia become progressively more dependent on environmental cues as cognitive reserve depletes. The built environment must substitute for failing cognitive processes — the architecture must make the right decision obvious without requiring deliberate reasoning. Decision-point minimisation, destination visibility, and landmark articulation are all applications of this principle to spatial design.

## FDR-DEM-02 — d570 + dementia → bathroom safety (fixture recognition, contrast)

**Sources:** Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings; PMC6867684 (hospital dementia design review, Tier 2); Dementia Australia Built Environment Guidelines (Tier 2); OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| WC — high contrast to floor and wall | WC suite in colour contrasting to floor (≥30 LRV) and to wall (≥30 LRV); avoid white-on-white | Dementia: reduced ability to interpret spatial information — WC must be visually self-announcing against background; white WC on white wall/floor is indistinguishable | PMC6867684; Universal Design Guidelines | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — WC contrast to floor and wall as functional specification absent from BPC |
| Basin — contrasting to surround | Basin colour contrasting to wall/vanity; tap in contrasting colour to basin | Dementia: fixture identification requires clear visual boundary; matching colours create spatial confusion | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — basin contrast specification absent from BPC |
| Bathroom door — distinctive to room identification | Bathroom door colour/material distinctly different from adjacent bedroom doors; consistent across building | Dementia: bathroom access at night requires reliable room identification without reading signs | Universal Design Guidelines; PMC6867684 | Co-2/Tier 2 | REFINES existing bedroom door personalisation finding (FDR-BAB-07); extends to bathroom |
| Mirror placement — disorientation risk | Avoid mirrors that reflect toilet, bed, or doorway directly; or specify with control (cabinet door) | Dementia with agitation: mirror reflections of own movement, toilet, or entry are frequent disorientation triggers — can cause distress and refusal of personal care | OT clinical reasoning; dementia design literature | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — mirror disorientation risk as spatial specification absent from BPC |
| Glazed visual access to bathroom | View into bathroom from bedroom (glazed panel in wall or half-door) visible from bed without getting up | Dementia + nocturia: visual access to bathroom from bed at night allows the person to confirm bathroom location before attempting nocturnal navigation | Universal Design Guidelines; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — bedroom-to-bathroom visual connection as nocturnal navigation specification absent from BPC |
