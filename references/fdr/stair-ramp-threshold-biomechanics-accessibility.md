## FDR-STAIR-01 — d450 + hemiplegia → stair (step height, handrail laterality, sequence)

**Primary source:** Merck Manual Professional Edition — Stroke Rehabilitation (evidence-based clinical reference). PMC7171891 — hemiplegia stair height comparison RCT (Tier 2). PMC4681935 — step climbing vs stair training RCT (Tier 2).

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stair sequencing — hemiplegia rule | Ascent: unaffected (stronger) leg leads; descent: affected (weaker) leg leads — mnemonic "good leads up, bad leads down" | Hemiplegia: unaffected leg drives concentric contraction (ascent) while affected leg follows; descent reverses — affected leg leads eccentric loading where control is reduced | Merck Manual Stroke Rehabilitation; clinical consensus | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — laterality-specific stair sequencing rule as design implication absent from BPC (implies handrail placement) |
| Handrail side — hemiplegia | Handrail on the unaffected (stronger) side is preferred — patient grasps rail with functional hand | Hemiplegia: patient grips handrail with unaffected hand; descending with handrail on affected side requires reaching across body or gripping with impaired hand | Merck Manual Stroke Rehabilitation | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — laterality-specific handrail placement for hemiplegia absent from BPC; implies bilateral handrail provision at all residential stairs |
| Handrail bilateral provision — hemiplegia | Bilateral handrail mandatory at all residential stairs used by hemiplegic users — single-side rail serves only 50% of users depending on affected side | Hemiplegia: affected side is either left or right — a single handrail on one side excludes 50% of users with hemiplegia from safe use | OT clinical reasoning; Merck Manual | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — bilateral handrail as hemiplegic-specific requirement provides independent clinical reasoning beyond standard dual-rail spec |
| Step height — hemiplegia rehabilitation | 10cm step height produces greater balance improvement than 15cm for chronic hemiparesis; 15cm produces greater tibialis anterior activation | Hemiplegia: step height affects which muscles are targeted; 10cm is closer to accessible spec (150–170mm standard); confirms that steps at lower end of standard height range are more accessible for hemiplegic users | PMC7171891 (RCT, Tier 2) | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — step height impact on hemiplegic gait biomechanics absent from BPC; clinical reasoning for lower-end height specification |
| Looking up during stair climb — contraindicated | Avoid designs requiring users to look upward to navigate stair ahead; ceiling-reflected light sources above stair are problematic | Hemiplegia: looking up the staircase can cause vertigo; designs that require upward gaze (e.g., open-tread stairs, high ceiling with no visual reference) worsen vestibular challenge | Merck Manual Stroke Rehabilitation | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — upward gaze avoidance as stair lighting/geometry specification absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (bilateral handrail):** The standard built environment argument for bilateral handrails is symmetry and convenience. The hemiplegia-specific argument is different and stronger: a single handrail on the wrong side for a particular user's affected side renders that stair effectively non-negotiable without assistance. Since affected side is individually variable, the only architectural solution that serves all hemiplegic users is bilateral provision. This provides an independent functional-deficit justification for bilateral handrails beyond the general accessibility argument.

## FDR-STAIR-02 — d450 + lower limb spasticity → ramp (gradient, surface, threshold)

**Sources:** FDR-MOB-03 (cross-reference); SCIRE Spasticity review; PVA SCI CPG; OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ramp gradient — spasticity | 1:20 (5%) maximum; 1:30 or less preferred | Spastic lower limb: sustained incline requires continuous eccentric/isometric contraction against spastic tone — sustained contraction at incline is a spasm trigger; lower gradient reduces continuous demand | OT clinical reasoning; SCIRE spasticity | Co-2 | REFINES — ramp gradient in BPC; adds spasticity-specific mechanism for preferring lower gradient than standard |

| Ramp edge — no abrupt drop | Ramp edge must be flush with adjacent surface; ≤6mm abrupt change | Spasticity: foot contact with drop edge at ramp perimeter triggers extensor spasm — stumble or loss of balance results | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — ramp edge flush requirement with spasticity-specific trigger mechanism absent from BPC |

## FDR-STAIR-03 — d450 + festinating gait (PD) → stair (visual cueing, step geometry)

**Sources:** Parkinson's UK / COT OT Best Practice Guidelines (Aragon & Kings, 2010) [cross-reference FDR-MOB-01]; PMC7683074 (Ramos SR); Physio-pedia PD gait

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stair step edge — natural visual cue for PD | Stair nosing lines function as natural, effective visual gait cues for PD users — stair negotiation is rarely a serious FOG problem unlike level corridors | PD festination/FOG: the horizontal line of each step nosing provides the same visual cueing function that artificially placed floor strips provide on level ground; stairs are therefore often safer than expected for PD | Parkinson's UK / COT OT Best Practice Guidelines (2010) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — stairs as naturally cue-rich environment for PD (safer than level corridors) absent from BPC; inverts expectation |

## FDR-STAIR-04 — d450 + OA / lower limb pain → ramp/stair (load minimisation)

**Sources:** ScienceDirect OA rehabilitation review; lower limb OA biomechanics literature; OT joint protection principles

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stair preference — ramp vs stair for OA | Ramp (1:20 gradient) is biomechanically preferable to stairs for OA users — stairs require peak knee flexion moments that are the primary pain trigger in knee OA | Knee OA: stair descent produces the highest patellofemoral and tibiofemoral joint loads of any daily activity; ramp distributes load more evenly | ScienceDirect OA rehabilitation review; biomechanics literature | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — ramp preference over stairs for OA on joint-load grounds absent from BPC |
| Handrail load offloading — bilateral | Bilateral handrails enable load offloading through upper limb during stair descent; reduces knee joint load | OA: "use two hands for lifting/carrying" OT joint protection principle extends to stair handrail use — bilateral handrails allow bilateral upper limb load offloading, not just balance | OT joint protection principles; OA biomechanics | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — bilateral handrail as load-offloading device for OA (not just safety) absent from BPC |
| Step height — OA | Lower step height (≤150mm preferred; ≤175mm standard) reduces peak knee flexion angle and joint load | OA: each step requires knee flexion to clear the rise; lower rise reduces peak flexion and therefore peak joint load — minor design change with significant functional impact | OA biomechanics literature; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — step height as OA joint-load parameter absent from BPC; provides clinical reasoning for lower-end height specification |
