## FDR-MH-01 — d240 + PTSD / anxiety / trauma history → spatial safety (sightlines, escape, control)

**Sources:** PMC9658651 (trauma-informed design scoping review, Tier 2); Perkins&Will TID; LHB TID guide; MNphy.com TID; Tandf.com TID shelter homes (Tier 2)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clear sightlines — entrance visible | Entry points visible from primary occupation zones; occupant can see who approaches before they arrive | PTSD/trauma: hypervigilance requires continuous monitoring of approach paths — inability to see entrances creates sustained threat arousal | PMC9658651; LHB TID | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — entrance sightline as trauma-informed spatial specification absent from BPC |
| Escape pathway legibility | Exits clearly visible and unobstructed from all points in primary spaces; no dead-end corridors without secondary egress visual | PTSD/anxiety: sense of entrapment activates threat response; clear visibility of exits provides psychological safety ("I can leave if I need to") | PMC9658651; Perkins&Will; Tandf.com | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — exit visibility as psychological safety specification absent from BPC |
| Areas of refuge — within large spaces | Nooks, alcoves, or backed seating areas within or adjacent to shared spaces; ≥1200mm deep × ≥900mm wide for seated refuge | Trauma: fishbowl exposure in large open spaces creates vulnerability; refuge points allow passive observation without exposure — safety through peripheral position | Perkins&Will TID; PMC9658651 | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — refuge nook dimensions and placement as trauma-informed specification absent from BPC |
| Seating orientation — wall-backed | Seating in shared areas oriented facing outward (back to wall, view of entrance) as default arrangement | Trauma: occupants with trauma history instinctively seek wall-backed positions — design that provides this without requiring furniture rearrangement reduces ongoing hypervigilance | MNphy.com TID; LHB | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — wall-backed seating orientation as trauma-informed default absent from BPC |
| Personal control — lighting and sound | Individual-controllable lighting (dimmer) and sound (white noise provision) in private spaces | PTSD/anxiety: sensory environment control directly reduces threat appraisal; inability to control sensory input maintains arousal state | Perkins&Will; Steelcase behavioral health design | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — personal environmental control as trauma-informed design specification absent from BPC |
| Separation from others in distress | Layout provides acoustic and visual separation between individuals experiencing acute distress and general occupants | Trauma: witnessing others in acute distress is a secondary traumatisation trigger; spatial separation protects other occupants | Perkins&Will TID principles | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — separation from distress as spatial provision absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (Environmental Docility applied to trauma):** PMC9658651 identifies safety as the most essential aspect of environmental design for trauma-affected populations. The neuroscientific basis: trauma creates conditioned threat responses to environmental stimuli; spaces that replicate the conditions of traumatic events (entrapment, exposure, lack of control) activate the same neural threat pathways regardless of actual danger. Architectural design must therefore eliminate *perceived* threats, not only physical ones. This inverts standard design priorities — aesthetics and efficiency are secondary to the elimination of threat triggers.

## FDR-MH-02 — d240 + anxiety / MH conditions → natural elements, calm materials

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Natural light — corridors and transition spaces | Daylight access to corridors, stairwells, and transition spaces; avoid windowless internal corridors | Mental health: windowless corridors are documented stress triggers; natural light confirms spatial orientation and time of day, reducing disorientation | PMC9658651; LHB TID | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — corridor daylighting as MH-specific specification absent from BPC |
| Biophilic elements — accessible greenspace | Views to greenspace or external nature; accessible outdoor space within sightline of main building | Mental health: evidence base for nature-contact on psychological wellbeing is robust; access to greenspace reduces stress, depression, and anxiety markers | PMC9658651; Perkins&Will | Tier 2/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — accessible greenspace proximity as MH-specific spatial specification absent from BPC |
