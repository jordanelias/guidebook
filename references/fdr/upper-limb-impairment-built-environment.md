## FDR-UPL-01 — d445 + bilateral UPL weakness → reach and controls in living space

**Sources:** PVA SCI Upper Limb Preservation CPG (Physiopedia); UNSW HMC OT Guidelines; France+Associates case study; Rehab Management OT case (Petito); OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Overhead reach prohibition | Avoid arm above shoulder height for bilateral UPL weakness users; overhead storage and controls contraindicated | Upper limb preservation: overhead reach increases subacromial load and rotator cuff injury risk; for bilateral UPL weakness this is cumulative across every daily reach | PVA SCI CPG (Physiopedia) | Tier 4 CPG | REFINES — overhead reach in BPC as general reach range; adds clinical rationale specific to bilateral UPL and injury prevention |
| All-reach zone height | 380mm–shoulder height of seated user; NO overhead reach zone | Bilateral UPL: every control, switch, outlet, storage item outside this zone requires compensatory technique or carer assistance | PVA SCI CPG; OT clinical reasoning | Tier 4 CPG/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — bilateral UPL all-reach zone as combined lower-and-upper bound absent from BPC |
| Counter/worktop height | Lower counters or height-adjustable; 760–900mm; individualized to seated reach | Bilateral UPL weakness + seated position: standard 900mm worktop is exclusionary; reach AND force both compromised | France+Associates case; Rehab Management OT (Petito) | Case/Co-2 | REFINES — counter height in BPC; adds bilateral UPL reach+force combination as distinct constraint |
| Horizontal reach depth — bilateral UPL | ≤380mm depth without forward lean; pull-out drawer/shelf preferred for depth >380mm | Bilateral UPL: forward lean to reach deep shelves puts both arms in extended/loaded position simultaneously — fall risk and shoulder injury risk combined | AOTA Home Modification Practice Guidelines (2023) | Co-2 | REFINES — shelf depth in bedroom FDR (FDR-BAB-05); confirmed cross-room |
| Control mounting — bilateral UPL | All controls within seated reach zone (380mm–shoulder height); centrally placed between bilateral reach arcs | Bilateral UPL: controls placed at edge of reach zone require maximal extension of both arms simultaneously — bilateral shoulder load at maximum extension | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — bilateral reach arc as control placement constraint absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (bilateral reach arc):** For unilateral UPL, the constraint is which side. For bilateral UPL, the constraint is symmetric loading at extension — both arms are simultaneously at maximum reach, creating peak bilateral shoulder load. The OT solution is to centralise controls and reduce reach distance, not just to ensure the control is within the reach envelope. This inverts the usual reach-range design logic: it is not just about whether the user can reach it, but whether reaching it at the available distance creates injury risk.

## FDR-UPL-02 — d440 + upper limb spasticity → hardware operation

**Sources:** Hand Surgery Resource (spasticity patterns); Upper Limb Orthotics AAPMR; Physiopedia spasticity; OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spasticity pattern — architectural implication | Upper limb spasticity produces characteristic flexion-pronation posture: shoulder adduction/IR, elbow flexion, wrist flexion/ulnar deviation, clenched fist | OT must specify hardware compatible with this posture — lever handles that require supination (palm-up) are inaccessible to flexor-spastic users | Hand Surgery Resource; Physiopedia | Tier 4/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — spasticity-specific posture as hardware compatibility constraint absent from BPC |
| Lever orientation — spastic UL | Downward-press lever (palm-down operation) preferred; avoids supination demand | Flexor-spastic upper limb: pronated forearm is the resting posture; palm-down lever press does not require deviation from resting posture | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — lever orientation for spastic UL absent from BPC |
| Push-plate / touch-sensitive controls | Push-plate operable with dorsum of hand or forearm; touch-sensitive controls operable with any contact point | Severe spasticity: grip is not possible; push-plate can be activated with dorsum (back) of hand even when grip is fully absent | AAPMR Upper Limb Orthotics; ATWiki | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — dorsum activation and touch-sensitive as spasticity-specific control type absent from BPC |
| Velocity-sensitive hardware — contraindicated | Avoid spring-loaded levers that require rapid depression for latch engagement | Spasticity is velocity-dependent (increased by rapid stretch); rapid lever depression may trigger extensor spasm in affected limb, causing loss of contact with lever mid-operation | Physiopedia (spasticity mechanism); OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — velocity-sensitivity contraindication for spring-loaded hardware absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (velocity-dependent spasticity):** The key OT insight is that hardware that requires *rapid* manual action triggers spasticity through the stretch reflex (velocity-dependent increase in tone). A spring-loaded lever that snaps back quickly when released may cause an extensor spasm in the affected limb at the moment of operation. The architectural implication is that hardware with controlled, slow motion characteristics (magnetic latches, slow-return mechanisms) is not just "easier" — it actively reduces the neurological trigger for spasm.

## FDR-UPL-03 — d420 + SCI tetraplegia → door hardware and threshold approach

**Sources:** Physiopedia SCI upper limb positioning; AAPMR upper limb orthotics; PVA SCI CPG; OT clinical reasoning; ATWiki

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Manual door hardware — tetraplegia | Lever/standard hardware inaccessible for C4–C5 complete tetraplegia (no functional grip); automatic powered operator mandatory | Tetraplegia C4–C5: no functional hand grip; tenodesis grasp only at C6; door hardware requiring grip/lever is inaccessible | AAPMR upper limb orthotics; PVA SCI CPG | Tier 4 CPG/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — tetraplegia-specific hardware inaccessibility threshold absent from BPC |
| Automatic door actuator — tetraplegia interface | Touch-sensitive, wave-to-open, or remote-controlled actuator; push-plate as minimum; no grip required | C4–C5: forearm or wrist contact with push-plate possible using BFO/wrist orthosis; wave-to-open for higher lesion levels | AAPMR upper limb orthotics; ATWiki | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — lesion-level-specific actuator interface selection for tetraplegia absent from BPC |
| Tenodesis grasp — C6 lever compatibility | C6 complete: tenodesis grasp allows hook/loop hardware; lever requires wrist extension to engage — possible but limited; low-force lever (<15 N) within tenodesis capacity | C6 complete: wrist extensors preserved; tenodesis provides passive grip when wrist extended; hook-style hardware compatible; standard lever may be at or beyond capacity | Physiopedia SCI UL positioning; PVA SCI CPG | Tier 4 CPG/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — tenodesis grasp compatibility with specific hardware types absent from BPC |
| Approach clearance — power WC + BFO | ≥1800×1800mm turning space; door actuator placed ≥500mm from door hinge side | Power WC with balanced forearm orthosis (BFO) has extended lateral footprint; actuator must be reachable from WC without requiring approach angle that obstructs path | OT clinical reasoning; BS 8300 power WC spec | Co-2/Tier 5 | REFINES — power WC turning in BPC; adds BFO lateral footprint and actuator reach as compounding constraint |

**Clinical reasoning (tenodesis grasp):** The tenodesis grasp (passive finger flexion when wrist extends) is the functional hand mechanism for C6 complete SCI. OTs design task modification around this mechanism: hook-style hardware, loop pulls, and low-force levers are compatible; round knobs and high-force latches are not. The architectural implication is that hardware selection must be categorised by the *grasp type it requires*, not just by force — tenodesis-compatible hardware is a distinct specification category that does not appear in any current building standard.
