## FDR-TDH-01 — d440 + reduced grip force → door hardware (operating force, lever geometry)

**Sources:** ADA §309.4; ICC A117.1-2017 §404.2.7; US Access Board ADA Guide; BS 8300 (UK)
(Note: overlaps with FDR-RRC-01 — cross-reference; door-specific parameters recorded here)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Door hardware operating force | ≤22.2 N (5 lbf) for operable parts; ≤66.7 N (15 lbf) for push/pull hardware | Grip deficit — force above threshold exceeds functional grip capacity of significant proportion of disabled users | ADA §309.4; ICC A117.1-2017 | Tier 5–6 | CONFIRMS existing BPC |
| Rotational hardware torque limit | ≤28 inch-pounds (≤3.16 N·m) | Lever handle operation — wrist rotation under load exceeds capacity for RA, OA, and grip-deficit users | ICC A117.1-2017 §404.2.7 | Tier 5 | REFINES — torque-specific limit adds precision to force-only BPC entry |
| Lever handle — grip geometry | Closed-fist operable; no tight pinch or wrist twist; 1½ inch (38mm) knuckle clearance behind lever | Grip deficit; lever must be operable with palm, wrist, or elbow | ADA §404; US Access Board Guide | Tier 5–6 | CONFIRMS lever requirement; REFINES with knuckle clearance dimension |
| Latch spring weight | Light-action or magnetic latch preferred; spring latches increase combined operating force | Grip deficit: spring latch force adds to closer force — combined load may exceed grip capacity even where each component is individually within spec | OT clinical reasoning; RCOT HAwD | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — spring latch as additive force component absent from BPC |
| Door closer sweep speed | ≥5 seconds from 90° to 12° (ADA); ≥3 seconds closing pressure minimum | Grip deficit + mobility: insufficient time to pass through before closure adds resistance mid-transit; also a fall risk for ambulant users | ADA National Network; RCOT | Tier 5/Co-2 | CONFIRMS |

**Clinical reasoning (latch spring additive load):** OT home assessment practice consistently identifies spring latch resistance as a hidden additive barrier. A door that meets the 22.2 N handle force standard but has a stiff spring latch may require 35–40 N combined to open, exceeding functional grip capacity for RA/OA users. This is why OT prescription includes both hardware type AND latch specification — they are not independent variables.

## FDR-TDH-02 — d440 + tremor → door hardware (latch operation, timing)

**Sources:** OT clinical reasoning (essential tremor practice); RCOT HAwD 2019; OT.com ET course

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Latch operation — tremor | Lever with return spring that resets without sustained hold; magnetic latch preferred | Tremor: sustained grip on lever during latch engagement is unreliable — lever returns while user still engaged, releasing prematurely | OT clinical reasoning (ET practice) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — tremor-specific latch timing requirement absent from BPC |
| Door closer — hold-open duration | Minimum 5 seconds; auto-hold preferred for severe tremor | Tremor: passage through doorway requires more time; insufficient hold time creates collision risk | RCOT HAwD 2019 | Co-2 | REFINES — 5 second closer in BPC; adds tremor-specific rationale |
| Keypad/electronic entry | Large buttons ≥25mm; tactile differentiation; no simultaneous press requirement | Tremor: small keypads require finger precision that is impossible under action tremor; simultaneous key combinations are inaccessible | OT clinical reasoning; ET OT.com course | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — keypad button size as tremor-mitigation entry parameter absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning:** The key OT insight for tremor + door hardware is that the *sequence* matters — it is not just about force. Tremor users typically stabilise the proximal limb first, then engage the distal. A lever that resets before the user has completed this sequence forces repeated attempts, increasing fatigue and frustration. The architectural solution is a lever that holds position during engagement (magnetic latch, not spring latch) and a closer that holds open long enough for passage without rushing.

## FDR-TDH-03 — d445 + unilateral UPL → door hardware (one-hand operation)

**Sources:** ADA §309; France+Associates case study (Alice); ATWiki adaptive door hardware; OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lever handle — one-hand operable by design | Lever operable with single hand without simultaneous actions; all latching mechanisms disengaged with same single-hand motion | Unilateral UPL: second hand unavailable for simultaneous hold-and-operate | ADA §309; OT clinical reasoning | Tier 5–6/Co-2 | CONFIRMS; adds unilateral UPL mechanism |
| Door weight — one-hand pull/push threshold | ≤22.2 N preferred; heavier doors require powered operator for unilateral UPL users | Unilateral UPL: one hand must simultaneously depress lever, pull/push door, and clear the opening — combined force demand exceeds one-hand capacity above 22.2 N | OT clinical reasoning; RCOT | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — combined single-hand force task analysis for unilateral UPL absent from BPC |
| Swing-clear hinge / offset hinge | Provides additional 50mm clear width when door is open | Unilateral UPL + prosthesis: additional body width; also relevant for ambulant users with crutch on affected side who cannot tuck to clear narrow opening | ATWiki adaptive door hardware | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — swing-clear hinge as unilateral UPL provision absent from BPC |
| Automatic door operator — preferred for severe UPL | Push-plate actuator ≥100mm diameter; mounted 800–1100mm AFF, ≥500mm from door edge | Severe unilateral UPL or bilateral UPL: lever operation impossible; powered operator eliminates all manual force requirement | ADA/ADAEZ standards; ATWiki | Tier 5/Co-2 | REFINES — automatic door in BPC; adds push-plate size, mounting height, and setback dimensions |

**Clinical reasoning (combined force analysis):** OT assessment for unilateral UPL applies a combined force task analysis that standards do not: the user must *simultaneously* depress the lever, pull/push the door weight, maintain balance, and clear the opening. Each force component may be within standard limits, but the *simultaneous* demand of all components with one hand routinely exceeds functional capacity. This is why OTs prescribe automatic operators for severe unilateral UPL even where door weight meets standard — it is the simultaneity problem, not the individual force that is the barrier.

## FDR-TDH-04 — d455 + reduced lower limb strength → threshold negotiation

**Sources:** Iwarsson & Ståhl 2003 (conceptual framework, Disabil Rehabil); OT clinical reasoning; PVA SCI CPG

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Threshold height — ambulant lower limb weakness | Zero threshold preferred; ≤6mm absolute maximum | Reduced lower limb strength: foot clearance during swing phase is reduced; even low thresholds cause trip/stumble when clearance margin is small | Iwarsson & Ståhl 2003 (accessibility/usability framework); OT clinical reasoning | Tier 5/Co-2 | CONFIRMS zero-threshold from BPC; adds ambulant lower-limb-weakness mechanism (distinct from WC clearance rationale) |
| Threshold bevelling — mandatory when present | 1:2 bevel on each side of any threshold above 6mm | Lower limb weakness: a square-edge threshold creates a trip edge that a bevelled threshold converts to a ramp — reduced risk of catch and stumble | ADA threshold specifications; OT clinical reasoning | Tier 5–6 | CONFIRMS |
| Threshold surface discontinuity — tactile warning | Colour/tone contrast at threshold level change ≥30 LRV difference | Lower limb weakness + visual impairment: users who cannot see the threshold cannot pre-position foot for clearance | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — dual sensory cue for threshold position absent from BPC as functional specification |

**Clinical reasoning:** The trip mechanism for ambulant lower-limb-weakness users at thresholds is different from the WC clearance mechanism. For a WC it is a wheel-catch problem; for an ambulant person with reduced foot clearance it is a swing-phase shortfall — the foot does not lift high enough to clear the edge. The architectural solution is the same (zero threshold) but the reasoning path is independent, meaning it applies to a different functional group. This cross-population convergence makes zero threshold a strong Tier 0 candidate.

## FDR-TDH-05 — d450 + fatigue → entrance (door weight, automation trigger)

**Sources:** RCOT HAwD 2019; OT clinical reasoning (MS, CFS/ME, OFS); ADAEZ/ATWiki

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Door weight — fatigue populations | ≤22.2 N for door opening force; automatic operator best practice for severe fatigue | CFS/ME, MS: energy envelope is fixed and finite — repeated heavy door operation depletes it; automatic door preserves energy for functional tasks within the home | RCOT HAwD 2019; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | CONFIRMS force limit; REFINES with fatigue-specific energy-conservation rationale |
| Door closer — hold-open duration | ≥5 seconds (ADA); 8–10 seconds preferred for fatigue users with mobility aids | Fatigue + mobility aid: additional time required to reposition aid after pushing door, then pass through before closure; 5 seconds often insufficient | RCOT HAwD 2019; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | REFINES — 5 seconds in BPC; adds fatigue/mobility-aid rationale for extended hold time |
| Automatic door actuator — energy cost | Push-plate operable with ≤22.2 N; wave/sensor actuation eliminates all manual force | Severe fatigue: push-plate at low force is still a measurable energy expenditure; sensor/wave activation is zero energy cost for door passage | ATWiki; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — zero-energy-cost activation as fatigue-specific provision distinct from accessibility provision |

**Clinical reasoning (energy envelope):** OT practice for CFS/ME and MS applies the *energy envelope* framework — each activity has an energy cost, and total daily capacity is limited. A heavy door at the entrance is the *first* energy expenditure of every home departure and every return. OTs consistently identify entrance door weight as a priority modification for fatigue populations because it is a mandatory, unavoidable, high-frequency expenditure. Automatic operators do not just make the door easier — they eliminate the energy cost entirely and preserve capacity for subsequent tasks.

## FDR-TDH-06 — d460 + cognitive impairment / dementia → entrance (recognition, approach, door use)

**Sources:** Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD / Alzheimer's Society Ireland); PMC8545728 (ageing/dementia-friendly design review, Tier 2); PMC8725382 (wayfinding design criteria, Tier 2); PMC6867684 (hospital dementia design review, Tier 2); Wayfinding behavioral case studies PMC11931140 (Tier 2); RCOT HAwD 2019

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Front door colour — recognition | Brightly painted; high contrast to surrounding wall; distinct from adjacent doors | Dementia: front door recognition is a prerequisite for independent return home; high-contrast, distinctive colour is the primary wayfinding cue at home entrance | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — door colour as recognition specification absent from TDH BPC |
| Entrance approach — planted/contrasting cues | Distinct plantings, gate colour, or entrance feature to differentiate home entrance from adjacent properties | Dementia: spatial orientation requires recognisable landmarks; entrance approach must contain unique visual cue visible from street approach direction | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — approach landmark specification absent from BPC |
| Interior door concealment — egress risk | Consider curtain/screen on inside face of front door to reduce door salience from interior | Dementia with wandering behaviour: visible door hardware and door frame on interior face is a visual trigger for exit-seeking; concealment reduces trigger without restricting entry | Universal Design Guidelines: Dementia Friendly Dwellings (AlzSD) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — interior door concealment as cognitive-safety provision absent from BPC |
| Signage mounting height | Lower edge ≤1220mm (4ft) AFF | Dementia: persons with dementia frequently look downward during navigation; signs at standard height (above eye level) are missed; lower mounting is within habitual visual scan range | Wayfinding & Dementia (GVSU / Benbow 2013 cited) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — lower-edge mounting height for dementia wayfinding signage absent from BPC |
| Decision points — minimised | Entrance approach should present a single clear decision: enter/do not enter. Eliminate competing doors, openings, or visual choices within approach zone | Dementia: each decision point requires intact decision-making capacity; dementia reduces this progressively. Fewer decisions = fewer failures | PMC8725382 (wayfinding design criteria SR); PMC8545728 | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — decision-point reduction as entrance approach specification absent from BPC |
| Doorway visibility — destination legibility | Key destination door (bathroom, bedroom) should be visible from point of use where possible; glazed panel in door or adjacent wall provides visual access | Dementia wayfinding case studies: participants more reliably reached destinations they could see directly vs. destinations requiring navigation through opaque space | PMC11931140 (wayfinding case studies, Tier 2) | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — destination visibility as door design specification absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (Environmental Docility Hypothesis):** The key OT/design framework for dementia and cognitive impairment at entrances is the Environmental Docility Hypothesis (Lawton & Simon 1968): people with restricted cognitive ability are more dependent on the environment to guide behaviour because they cannot adapt the environment to their own needs. Every design decision at the entrance must therefore substitute for a cognitive decision the person can no longer reliably make. The front door must be visually self-announcing (colour, contrast, landmark). The approach must remove ambiguity. The interior face must not trigger wandering. These are not aesthetic choices — they are functional substitutes for failing cognition.
