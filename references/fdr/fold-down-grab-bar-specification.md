## FDR-FGB-01 — d420 + reduced grip force → grab bar diameter and geometry

**Primary source:** UNSW Home Modification Information Clearinghouse Industry Factsheet: Selecting diameters for grabrails (Oram, Cameron & Bridge, 2006; 2nd printing 2014). Co-2 tier. This is the most comprehensive OT-authored spatial specification document on grab bar diameter selection.

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grab bar diameter — standard range | 32–38mm (1¼–1½ inch) | Provides power grasp for range of adult hand sizes; ADA mandated range | ADA §609.2; UNSW HMC Factsheet | Tier 5–6/Co-2 | CONFIRMS |
| Grab bar diameter — selection by hand size | 32mm for smaller hands; 38mm for larger hands; 38mm preferred for limited grip strength | Over-grip (too small): finger tips touch palm, increasing muscle tension; Under-grip (too large): gap between fingertips and palm, grasp fails — both reduce functional grip strength | UNSW HMC Factsheet (Oram et al.) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — hand-size-specific diameter selection rationale absent from BPC; critical clinical reasoning for OT prescription |
| Wrist neutral position during use | Grab bar orientation must maintain wrist in neutral (forearm aligned) — avoid wrist deviation | Wrist deviation from neutral reduces grip strength; OT assessment must check that installed bar geometry maintains neutral wrist for the specific transfer task | UNSW HMC Factsheet (Oram et al.) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — wrist neutrality as installation geometry requirement absent from BPC |
| Grip strength demand varies by transfer phase | Greater grip strength required at seat-off (WC transfer) than at steadying during standing; bar design should facilitate maximal grip at peak demand point | Grip deficit: if grip strength is marginal, the bar must be optimised for the moment of peak demand, not average demand | UNSW HMC Factsheet (Oram et al.) | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — phase-specific grip demand as bar specification criterion absent from BPC |
| Clearance between bar and wall | 38mm (1½ inch) from wall surface to inner edge of bar | Knuckle clearance for power grasp; less clearance prevents full hand wrap | ADA §609.3; BS 8300 | Tier 5–6 | CONFIRMS |

**Clinical reasoning (UNSW HMC):** The UNSW factsheet establishes that grab bar diameter is not a single universal specification — it is a person-specific prescription dependent on hand size, task, and wrist position. This is fundamental to OT grab bar assessment: the OT measures palm length, checks the specific transfer sequence, and selects diameter accordingly. The implication for built environment design is that DAR provisions should specify the standard ADA 32–38mm range (not a single diameter), and OT assessment at Tier 2 determines the specific selection within that range.

## FDR-FGB-02 — d440 + shoulder pain / reduced shoulder ROM → grab bar gripping under load

**Sources:** OT clinical reasoning (RA, rotator cuff, post-mastectomy); RCOT HAwD 2019; PVA SCI CPG (shoulder preservation)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grab bar height — shoulder pain | Bar height calibrated so elbow is at or below shoulder during peak grip phase | Shoulder pain/reduced ROM: reaching above shoulder for grip increases subacromial impingement load; bar too high creates painful and weak grip position | PVA SCI CPG (shoulder preservation); OT clinical reasoning | Tier 4 CPG/Co-2 | **NOVEL** — shoulder-height constraint on bar positioning absent from BPC |
| Angled grab bar (30–45°) — shoulder ROM | Angled bar allows elbow-to-shoulder approach without wrist deviation and without full shoulder elevation | Reduced shoulder ROM: horizontal bar requires full shoulder ROM to maintain neutral wrist at all grip positions; angled bar allows partial ROM users to grip at an ergonomic position | RCOT HAwD 2019; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — angled bar as shoulder-ROM-specific solution absent from BPC |
| Fold-down mechanism — spring weight | Deployment spring ≤10 N preferred; user must be able to deploy with affected limb from seated position | Shoulder pain/UPL: deploying a heavy fold-down bar from a seated position before transfer uses the same affected limb that will be compromised during transfer | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — deployment force of fold-down mechanism as OT assessment parameter absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning:** The PVA SCI CPG establishes "avoid arm above shoulder height" as a core principle for upper limb preservation. For grab bar prescription, this constrains bar height: a bar mounted at standard ADA height (840–915mm) may be appropriate for transfer mechanics but may position the hand above shoulder height during the pull phase for a shorter WC user, creating subacromial load during the moment of maximum exertion. OT assessment must check bar height against the *user's* shoulder height seated, not against a population standard.

## FDR-FGB-03 — d445 + unilateral UPL → fold-down bar deployment and use

**Sources:** France+Associates case; ATWiki; OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fold-down deployment — single-hand operable | Deployment mechanism operable with one hand without simultaneous actions | Unilateral UPL: must deploy bar, then transfer — two-hand deployment is not possible | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — single-hand deployment requirement for fold-down bar absent from BPC |
| Bar position — transfer-side selection | Bar deployed on the functional (non-affected) side for unilateral UPL | Unilateral UPL: bar on affected side cannot be gripped; bar must be on the side with functional hand — this is laterality-dependent and requires individual assessment | OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — laterality-dependent bar side selection absent from BPC |
| Fixed bar + fold-down combination | One fixed bar (structural support throughout) + one fold-down on transfer side | Unilateral UPL: the fold-down provides variable access; the fixed bar provides continuous support during the approach and departure phases when the fold-down may not be needed | RCOT HAwD 2019; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — fixed + fold-down combination as unilateral UPL provision absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning:** For unilateral UPL the design problem is laterality-dependent in a way it is not for bilateral users. The fold-down bar position must be specified relative to the user's functional side, not as a population-average bilateral provision. This is the OT reasoning: assess which side is functional → specify bar on that side. For a bilateral household, a fold-down on each side (one deployed for the current user, both available for future occupants) is the DAR best practice.

## FDR-FGB-04 — d420 + reduced grip in wet conditions → grab bar surface texture

**Sources:** Bobrick Advisory Bulletin TB-108 (ASTM F2961 testing, 2018); Canadian NBC 2015 grab bar surface requirement; GrabBars.com OT clinical resources; UNSW HMC Industry Factsheet (Oram et al.)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Surface finish — wet grip performance | Satin finish (brushed stainless) provides 10% more traction than peened finish under wet conditions (ASTM F2961 testing) | Wet bathroom conditions; reduced grip strength — counterintuitively, smooth satin outperforms textured peened under standardised wet grip testing | Bobrick Advisory Bulletin TB-108 (Bureau Veritas / ASTM F2961, 2018) | Tier 3 (manufacturer-commissioned independent test) | **NOVEL** — counterintuitive finding: satin > peened in wet grip is absent from BPC; prior guidance assumed textured = safer |
| Slip-resistant surface requirement | "Slip-resistant" surface mandatory (NBC Canada 2015); ADA does not mandate surface type but mandates presence of bar adjacent to wet plumbing fixtures | Wet areas; reduced grip — Canadian code more specific than ADA; "satin-finish, slip-resistant" now acceptable specification language replacing legacy "peened" or "knurled" | NBC 2015; ADA §609 | Tier 5–6 | CONFIRMS presence of bar; REFINES by updating surface specification language |
| Knurled vs. peened — reduced grip | Knurled (diamond pattern) provides highest dry-grip traction; peened is intermediate; satin with slip-resistant coating performs best wet | Reduced grip: in dry conditions, knurled texture may be appropriate; in wet conditions (bathroom transfer) satin with coating is preferred | GrabBars.com clinical resources; Bobrick TB-108 | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — condition-specific surface selection (dry vs. wet performance differences) absent from BPC |
| Comfort vs. safety trade-off | Smooth/satin surfaces more comfortable for prolonged grip (reduced skin irritation from texture); textured surfaces cause discomfort in extended-contact situations | Chronic condition users with skin sensitivity (RA, scleroderma, lymphoedema): rough texture causes pain during prolonged contact | GrabBars.com; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — comfort contraindication for textured bars in chronic condition contexts absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning (counterintuitive surface finding):** The Bobrick TB-108 finding reverses the traditional specification logic. Textured (peened/knurled) grab bars are universally assumed to be safer in wet conditions — this is the basis of decades of specification practice. The ASTM F2961 test data show that satin-finish bars provide 10% more wet traction because the smooth surface makes better contact with a wet palm (more contact area, more friction) while textured surfaces create a drainage effect that reduces the contact area under wet conditions. For OT grab bar prescription, this means the "rougher = safer" heuristic is incorrect for wet bathroom contexts, and surface specification must distinguish between dry and wet use conditions.

## FDR-FGB-05 — d460 + dementia → grab bar as wayfinding/orientation cue in bathroom

**Sources:** HEWI dementia orientation guide; Dementia Alliance International (O'Connor 2020); Bournemouth University (O'Malley/Wiener); Alzheimer Society NB; PMC8545728 (Marquardt et al. 2021); PMC11931140

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grab bar colour — high contrast | Signal colour (red preferred) against white/light wall; minimum 30 LRV point difference | DEM: white bar on white wall = invisible; red/dark bar on light wall = immediately identifiable | HEWI; Dementia Alliance International (O'Connor 2020) | Co-2/Tier 5 | **NOVEL** — grab bar colour contrast as DEM wayfinding specification absent from BPC |
| Toilet seat contrast | Seat contrasts with floor AND bowl; minimum 30 LRV difference on both axes | DEM: same-colour seat/floor/bowl = person cannot distinguish fixture from surface | HEWI; Alzheimer Society NB; Dementia Alliance International | Co-2 | REFINES — adds floor-to-seat as second contrast axis |
| Grab bar as landmark | Bar colour distinct from other fixtures; consistent across all bars in same bathroom | DEM: bars serve dual function — support AND visual landmark for room identification | PMC8545728 (Marquardt et al. 2021); OT reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — dual-function landmark absent from BPC |
| Matt finish — anti-glare | Matt/satin finish on all fixtures including grab bars; no reflective chrome | DEM: reflective surfaces create glare and visual confusion; chrome distorts environment | HEWI; Dementia Alliance International | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — matt finish as DEM anti-glare for grab bars absent from BPC |
| Bathroom door signage | Photographic toilet image + word at 1400mm AFF; bar colour alone not sufficient for room identification | DEM: bar visible only inside bathroom; door-level cue needed; yellow/black persists longest in visual memory | Alzheimer Society NB; PMC8545728 (Namazi/Johnson 1991); Bournemouth (O'Malley) | Co-2/Tier 1–2 | REFINES — adds residential bathroom door as specific wayfinding location |

**Clinical reasoning (dual-function grab bar):** For DEM, grab bar simultaneously serves as visual landmark identifying room function. Signal-coloured bars (red, dark blue) provide both VIS contrast AND DEM landmark — satisfying both populations. White bars on white walls fail both VIS and DEM.

**Clinical reasoning (sign format):** Realistic/photographic images outperform abstract pictograms for DEM. Namazi/Johnson 1991 found floor arrows + word "toilet" most effective. Residential specification: small well-designed sign with realistic image + word, non-institutional appearance, at decision point.
