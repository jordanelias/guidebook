## FDR-NDV-01 — d710 + sensory hypersensitivity → retreat space (quiet room, alcove geometry)

**Sources:** Autism.org.uk accessible environments (UCL 2021 study cited: 92% surveyed stated quiet space beneficial); Architizer ASD sensory design guide; DYNSEO sensory approach institutions; OT clinical reasoning

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quiet space provision — mandatory | Quiet space designated within accessible reach of all primary activity areas; permanently available without prior request | NDV hypersensitivity: sensory overload is unpredictable and non-deferrable — the space must be available on-demand, not scheduled; UCL 2021: 92% of NDV users identified quiet space as beneficial | Autism.org.uk (UCL 2021); DYNSEO | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — on-demand quiet space as design specification with NDV-specific rationale absent from BPC |
| Quiet space — acoustic isolation | Isolated from high-traffic, echo-prone areas; acoustic panels/soft furnishings to absorb ambient noise; target background noise ≤35 dB(A) | NDV auditory hypersensitivity: background noise levels that are tolerable for neurotypical users (40–50 dB) can be painful or disabling for hypersensitive users | UniqueCS; Architizer; PMC9748440 | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — NDV-specific acoustic target for retreat space absent from BPC |
| Quiet space — lighting control | Dimmable LED preferred; no fluorescent; blackout capability where possible | NDV visual hypersensitivity: fluorescent lights (flicker at 50/60Hz) are a documented trigger for sensory overload in NDV users; dimmable warm LED eliminates flicker and allows user control | Architizer; UniqueCS; PMC9748440 | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — fluorescent prohibition and dimmable LED as NDV-specific lighting specification absent from BPC |
| Quiet space — dimensions | Minimum usable area ≥4m² (approx. 2m × 2m); allows one or two persons; not a corridor or thoroughfare | NDV: retreat space that is too small triggers claustrophobia in some profiles; must accommodate the user plus companion; must not be interrupted by circulation | DYNSEO; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — minimum area specification for NDV retreat space absent from BPC |
| Quiet space — clear signposting | Signposted from main circulation areas; consistently identified across building | NDV: finding the quiet space must not itself require navigation under stress — the ability to locate it quickly is part of its function | Autism.org.uk | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — wayfinding to quiet space as functional requirement absent from BPC |
| Non-destination doors — reduced visual salience | Utility/storage/service doors visually unified with wall surface; minimise door count in circulation paths | NDV: multiple doors in a corridor are a source of decision-point confusion and visual clutter — only relevant destination doors should be visually prominent | Architizer ASD design guide; OT clinical reasoning | Co-2 | REFINES dementia finding (FDR-DEM-01); confirms cross-population application |

**Clinical reasoning (sensory overload and retreat):** Sensory overload in NDV users is not a preference — it is a neurological response to input that exceeds the processing capacity of a hypersensitive sensory system. Once overload threshold is crossed, voluntary behaviour regulation becomes severely impaired. The retreat space is not a comfort amenity — it is a functional regulatory tool that prevents escalation to meltdown or shutdown. OT sensory diet prescriptions include regular access to low-stimulation environments as a regulatory strategy. The built environment must provide this access on-demand rather than requiring the person to request it (which itself requires social interaction that may be impaired under overload).

## FDR-NDV-02 — d710 + sensory hypersensitivity → general environment (light, acoustic, visual clutter)

**Sources:** Architizer ASD sensory guide; PMC9748440 (Tier 3); DYNSEO; Autism Spectrum News OT (Rappaport OTD, OTR)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fluorescent lighting — prohibited for NDV | Fluorescent tubes/CFLs prohibited in NDV-accessible spaces; replace with flicker-free LED ≥90 CRI | NDV auditory and visual: fluorescent lights flicker at mains frequency and emit audible hum — both are triggers for NDV users; warm, flicker-free LED eliminates both | Architizer; PMC9748440; UniqueCS | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — fluorescent prohibition with NDV-specific dual (visual+auditory) mechanism absent from BPC |
| Glazing — limited large expanses | Limit uncontrolled large glazing in activity spaces; provide controllable screening (blinds, filters) | NDV visual hypersensitivity: large glazed areas create uncontrolled light variation, glare, and moving visual input (trees, traffic) — all documented distraction/overload triggers | Architizer ASD design guide | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — large glazing limitation as NDV-specific specification absent from BPC |
| Storage — concealed, clutter elimination | Provide abundant closed storage; minimise visible clutter in activity spaces | NDV visual processing: visual clutter (multiple competing visual objects) increases cognitive load and reduces ability to focus — closed storage reduces ambient visual complexity | Architizer; DYNSEO | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — closed storage provision as NDV sensory-load management specification absent from BPC |
| Acoustic performance — general circulation | Reverberation time in corridors ≤0.6s (RT60); hard floor surfaces to be offset with acoustic ceiling/wall treatment | NDV auditory: reverberant corridors amplify footstep and voice noise into a continuous sound mass that is particularly difficult to filter — NDV users cannot selectively attend to relevant sounds in reverberant environments | PMC9748440; Autism Spectrum News (Rappaport); Architizer | Co-2/Tier 3 | **NOVEL** — corridor reverberation target as NDV-specific acoustic specification absent from BPC |
| Wider personal space — corridors | ≥1500mm clear width in corridors used by NDV users; avoid narrow passages | NDV tactile hypersensitivity and personal space: narrow corridors force proximity to others and risk inadvertent contact — both are triggers; wider corridors enable avoidance of body contact | Architizer ASD design guide | Co-2 | **NOVEL** — personal-space rationale for corridor width (distinct from WC clearance rationale) absent from BPC |
