## FDR-MOB-03 — d410 + lower limb spasticity → transfers (sit-to-stand, surface height)

**Sources:** Tandfonline STS systematic review (2015); PMC3976795 stroke STS study; PVA SCI CPG (Physiopedia); Spasticity SCI review (SCIRE 2019)

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Seat/surface height — STS performance | Higher seat height reduces lower limb muscle demand for STS; recommended ≥450mm for lower limb weakness/spasticity | Lower limb spasticity: spastic muscles resist rapid eccentric load during descent; higher seat reduces ROM demand at initiation | Tandfonline STS SR (2015); PMC3976795 stroke STS | Tier 2 SR | **NOVEL** — seat height as spasticity-specific STS variable absent from mobility BPC; mechanism distinct from general WC transfer |
| Transfer surface height — level principle | Transfer surface should be level with or equal to originating surface; avoid height differential | Spasticity: sudden joint angle changes at transfer initiate spasm; level surface minimises angular demand change | PVA SCI CPG (Physiopedia) | Tier 4 CPG | REFINES — level transfer principle in BPC; adds spasticity-specific mechanism (spasm triggered by angle change) |
| Threshold / step edge — spasticity mechanism | Zero threshold mandatory for lower limb spasticity: sudden surface discontinuity triggers extensor spasm and destabilises transfer | Spastic lower limbs; ambulant users with spasticity negotiating step edges | SCIRE Spasticity review (2019); PVA CPG | Tier 4/SR | REFINES — zero threshold in BPC from WC access rationale; adds spasticity-specific neurological mechanism as additional independent justification |
| Floor surface — resistance and compliance | Firm, non-compliant surface preferred for STS; foam/soft flooring increases instability and unpredictable resistance for spastic limbs | Spasticity + STS; high-pile carpet is contraindicated | PMC3976795 (foam block STS study — compliance effect); clinical reasoning | Tier 2 | **NOVEL** — floor compliance as spasticity-specific contraindication absent from BPC |

**Clinical reasoning note:** The STS literature documents that foot position, surface compliance, and seat height all interact with STS success in neurological populations. The key mechanism for spasticity specifically is that *unpredictable surface changes trigger spasm* — this is distinct from the wheelchair access rationale and provides an independent clinical justification for firm flooring, zero thresholds, and consistent surface heights throughout the circulation path.

## FDR-MOB-04 — d450 + reduced lower limb strength → ramp / step negotiation

**Sources:** Tandfonline STS SR (2015); clinical reasoning (OT ramp assessment); PVA SCI CPG

| Parameter | Value | Condition | Source | Tier | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Seating height — STS energy cost | 450–500mm AFH seating reduces quadriceps/hip extensor demand for STS to functional range | Lower limb weakness (not spasticity); primary lever for reducing standing effort architecturally | Tandfonline STS SR (2015) | Tier 2 SR | REFINES — seat height in bathroom BPC for transfer; confirms mechanism applies broadly across furniture/seating |
| Ramp gradient — ambulant lower limb weakness | 1:20 ramp gradient (WC access standard) may remain too demanding for ambulant users with lower limb weakness walking unassisted; 1:30+ preferable | Lower limb weakness without WC: different problem from WC access; energy cost of incline still significant for weak lower limb ambulants | Clinical reasoning; no specific spatial study identified | Co-2 (inferred) | **NOVEL** — ramp gradient for ambulant lower-limb-weakness users as distinct from WC users absent from BPC; different mechanism, potentially different specification |
| Rest points — ramp and stair approach | Intermediate landing or rest point at ≤9m ramp interval for ambulant lower limb weakness | Lower limb weakness: fatigue accumulates on sustained incline; rest point before destination more functionally important than for WC users | OT home assessment clinical reasoning; BS 8300 landing intervals | Co-2/Tier 5 | REFINES — rest points in BPC; adds ambulant lower-limb-weakness as specific population where interval matters most |

**Clinical reasoning note:** The ramp gradient finding (1:20 vs 1:30) identifies a design gap: current accessible design standards are calibrated for wheelchair users, not for ambulant people with lower limb weakness. These are different mechanical problems — WC gradient is about rolling resistance and control; ambulant lower-limb-weakness gradient is about sustained eccentric/concentric muscle loading. A person with MS or post-stroke lower limb weakness walking with a stick may find 1:20 exhausting even though it is technically WC-accessible. This is a genuine specification gap warranting further research.
