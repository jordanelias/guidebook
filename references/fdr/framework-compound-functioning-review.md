## 1. Purpose

The guidebook's FDR skill decomposes functioning into single ICF d-code scenarios (e.g., d420 + hemiplegia → bathroom). This works for isolated functional deficits but fails to capture:

1. **Compound functioning** — the non-additive interaction of multiple conditions in a single person (e.g., hemiplegia + cognitive impairment + chronic pain) where functional loss is supra-additive.
2. **Whole-person occupation** — functioning as lived experience, not as a checklist of activity codes.
3. **Person-environment transaction** — the dynamic, bidirectional relationship between the person and the built environment, where the environment is not merely a barrier/facilitator but a co-constituent of functioning.
4. **Cultural assumptions** — ICF encodes Western assumptions about individual autonomy and discrete activity categories that do not transfer to collectivist contexts.

This review identifies frameworks that address these gaps and assesses their implications for guidebook Parts 1, 3, and 5.

## 2. Frameworks Identified

### 2.1 Capability Approach (CA) — Sen/Nussbaum

**Key sources:** Mitra & Shakespeare 2019 (Disability and Health Journal); Bickenbach 2013 (Alter — European Journal of Disability Research); Trani et al. 2011

**Core distinction from ICF:** ICF classifies what people *can do* (capacity) and *do do* (performance). CA classifies what people *have the real freedom to do or be* (capabilities) — incorporating individual choice, social context, and personal valuation. Disability in CA = capability deprivation, not activity limitation.

**What it adds:**
- **Choice and agency** — ICF treats functioning as binary (can/cannot); CA treats it as a space of real options. A person may be *capable* of using stairs but *choose* not to because the effort creates unacceptable pain. ICF codes this as no limitation; CA codes it as reduced capability.
- **Conversion factors** — personal characteristics (age, impairment, confidence) and environmental characteristics (built environment, social attitudes) that convert resources into capabilities. The built environment is a conversion factor, not just a barrier list.
- **Non-commensurability** — capabilities are not summable. You cannot trade mobility capability for cognitive capability. This directly challenges the additive logic of ICF core sets.

**Guidebook implication:** Part 1 should position the guidebook's specifications as expanding the *capability set* of occupants, not as eliminating *activity limitations*. Part 3's synthesis framework should acknowledge that environmental modifications change the conversion factor, not the impairment.

**Limitation for built environment:** CA is strong on justice theory but weak on operationalisation. No CA-based spatial specification methodology exists. Bickenbach (2013) argues ICF and CA are reconcilable and potentially synergetic — ICF provides the classification machinery CA lacks.

### 2.2 Person-Environment-Occupation (PEO) / PEOP / CMOP-E

**Key sources:** Law et al. 1996 (Canadian Journal of OT); Strong et al. 1999 (Canadian Journal of OT); Baum, Christiansen & Bass 2015; PMC11180417 (2024 scoping review)

**Core distinction from ICF:** These are transactional models — person, environment, and occupation are not independent variables that interact, but co-constituted elements of a single phenomenon (occupational performance/engagement). The "fit" between the three determines functioning, not any one in isolation.

**What they add:**
- **Congruence, not deficit** — functioning is not person-minus-impairment but person-environment-occupation fit. The specification target is fit optimisation, not deficit compensation.
- **Compound functioning is inherent** — PEO does not decompose into discrete activity codes. A person transfers from bed to wheelchair while managing pain while maintaining trunk balance while navigating emotional distress. This is one occupational performance, not four separate ICF codes.
- **Lifespan perspective** — PEO explicitly models functioning as changing over time. DAR provisions are PEO-native; ICF treats them as environment modifications.
- **Environment as equal component** — environment is not a modifier of person-level functioning; it is a co-equal determinant. This validates the guidebook's core premise more strongly than ICF does.

**CMOP-E specifically adds:**
- **Spirituality** at the centre of the person — meaning, purpose, identity. No ICF code captures this.
- **Engagement** (not just performance) as the outcome — a person may perform an activity but not be engaged. The guidebook's quality-of-life framing aligns with engagement, not performance.

**PEOP specifically adds:**
- **Participation and well-being** as the ultimate outcome, moving beyond occupational performance.
- **Enablers and barriers** framework that maps directly to the guidebook's facilitator/barrier language.

**Guidebook implication:** Part 1 should cite PEO/PEOP as the theoretical model that best justifies the guidebook's approach. The three-tier hierarchy (Tier 0/1/2) maps onto PEO's congruence model: Tier 0 = universal person-environment fit floor; Tier 1 = population-informed fit optimisation; Tier 2 = individual co-design for maximum congruence.

### 2.3 Kawa Model (Iwama 2006)

**Key sources:** Iwama, Thomson & Macdonald 2009 (Disability and Rehabilitation); Iwama 2006 (Elsevier monograph); PMC9851780 (2023 — Kawa in education); Ober, Newbury & Lape 2022 (Open J OT — scoping review)

**Core distinction from ICF:** Kawa (river) is a metaphor-based model from Japanese OT that rejects the Western categorical decomposition of person/environment/occupation as artificial boundaries. The river = life flow; rocks = life circumstances (including health conditions); driftwood = personal attributes and resources; riverbed/banks = social and physical environment; spaces between = opportunities for flow (well-being).

**What it adds:**
- **Non-categorical functioning** — there are no discrete activity codes. Functioning is the flow of the river. Barriers (rocks) interact with the channel (environment) and personal resources (driftwood) simultaneously and inseparably. This is the most direct model of compound functioning.
- **Collectivist cultural compatibility** — the self is embedded in social context, not autonomous. This addresses the guidebook's identified gap (project-standards: PAS 6463 private retreat model does not fit collectivist contexts).
- **Transactional, not interactional** — person and environment are not separate entities that interact; they are aspects of a single flow. The river does not interact with its banks — the river *is* the water-and-banks together.

**Guidebook implication:** Part 1 should reference Kawa as the framework that resolves the collectivist culture transfer problem. Where PAS 6463's private sensory retreat assumes individual autonomy, Kawa-informed specification would design for collective flow — shared quiet spaces, family-inclusive calm environments. Part 3 §3.8 co-occurrence resolution could reference Kawa: when specifications conflict, the resolution is not choosing which population "wins" but finding the channel configuration that allows maximum flow for all occupants.

**Limitation:** Kawa is not operationalised for spatial specification. It provides no dimensional parameters. Its value is conceptual — reshaping how we think about specification, not generating specifications directly.

### 2.4 Multimorbidity-Weighted Index (MWI) and Compound Functioning Research

**Key sources:** BMC Geriatrics 2022 (Multimorbidity and long-term disability); Aging Clinical and Experimental Research 2024 (meta-analysis of disability prevalence in multimorbidity); Disability and Rehabilitation 2020 (IADL changes with concurrent mental + physical conditions); Carr et al. 2025 (built environment + multimorbidity SR protocol — SAGE)

**Core finding:** Multimorbidity-disability relationships are **non-linear and supra-additive**. Each additional chronic condition increases disability risk by approximately 16%, but the interaction effects produce faster rates of decline than any single-condition model predicts. Concurrent mental and physical conditions are particularly predictive of persistent and incident disability.

**What this adds:**
- **Quantified non-additivity** — the guidebook's Part 5 co-occurrence framework currently treats conflicts as pairwise (POP-A vs POP-B). MWI research demonstrates that the compound functioning impact is not the sum of individual population impacts. A person with MOB + PAIN + DEM has a functional profile that cannot be predicted from MOB + PAIN + DEM specifications applied independently.
- **Built environment as multimorbidity mediator** — Clarke et al. (PMC2727170) demonstrated a 4.52× interaction effect: poor street conditions amplified the disability impact of lower-extremity impairment. The built environment is not additive with impairment — it is multiplicative.
- **Adults aging with disability (AAwPD)** — PMC10961975 (2024) identifies that age-related secondary conditions compound with primary disability, producing accelerated functional decline. The guidebook's population codes do not capture this temporal dimension.

**Guidebook implication:** Part 3 §3.8 needs a compound functioning principle: when multiple population codes apply to a single occupant, the specification response is not the union of individual population specifications but a compound assessment (Tier 2 OT) that addresses interaction effects. Part 5 should state explicitly that pairwise conflict resolution is necessary but insufficient — compound profiles require holistic assessment.

### 2.5 Mitra & Shakespeare (2019) — Remodelling the ICF

**Key source:** Disability and Health Journal 12(3): 337–339

**What it proposes:** ICF revision to address: (a) subjective experience of functioning (ICF treats functioning as observer-rated), (b) dynamic nature of functioning over time (ICF is static snapshot), (c) personal factors (ICF lists but does not classify them), (d) the relationship between disability and well-being (ICF separates them).

**Guidebook implication:** The guidebook already addresses (a) through Tier 2 co-design, (b) through DAR provisions, and (d) through its quality-of-life framing. The call to classify personal factors is relevant: the guidebook's population codes are health-condition-based, not personal-factor-based. This is a feature, not a bug — the guidebook specifies for environments, not for persons. But Part 1 should acknowledge this distinction explicitly.

## 4. Implications for Guidebook Architecture

### 4.1 Part 1 — Foundations
- Position the guidebook using PEO/PEOP as the practice model (person-environment-occupation fit) rather than ICF alone.
- Cite CA to ground the ethical framework: specifications expand capability sets.
- Cite Kawa to address collectivist culture contexts and the private-retreat transfer problem.
- Retain ICF as the classification infrastructure (activity codes, environmental factors) while acknowledging its limitations on compound functioning and subjective experience.
- State explicitly: ICF provides the taxonomy; PEO provides the theoretical model; CA provides the justice framework; Kawa provides the cultural lens.

### 4.2 Part 3 — Synthesis and Co-Occurrence
- Add a compound functioning principle: co-occurring conditions produce supra-additive functional impacts (cite MWI research). Pairwise conflict resolution (current §3.8) is necessary but insufficient.
- Add Clarke et al. multiplicative environment-impairment interaction as the evidence base for why built environment specification matters disproportionately for compound profiles.
- The synthesis methodology should model congruence (PEO), not deficit-sum (ICF additive).

### 4.3 Part 5 — Building-Level Co-Occurrence Resolution
- Acknowledge that building-level resolution addresses inter-group conflicts in shared spaces, but intra-individual compound functioning is a Tier 2 domain that the guidebook equips professionals to address, not one it resolves prescriptively.
- Reference the 4.52× Clarke interaction effect as the evidence basis for prioritising environmental quality in buildings serving compound-profile populations.

### 4.4 FDR Skill Methodology
- FDR currently decomposes by single ICF scenario. This review does not invalidate that approach — it remains the correct method for extracting spatial parameters from OT literature, which is indexed by functional deficit.
- **Addition:** After FDR extraction, a compound-functioning synthesis pass (Opus) should identify which FDR findings interact non-additively when co-occurring in a single person. This is distinct from connection-scout (which finds cross-specification links) — it asks: "when these two specifications apply to the same person simultaneously, do they conflict, compound, or interact?"

## 5. Flagged for Opus

The following require Opus-level synthesis:

1. **Part 1 theoretical framework rewrite** — integrating ICF + PEO + CA + Kawa into a coherent four-layer model. This is a complex framing task that determines how the entire guidebook is positioned.
2. **Part 3 §3.8 compound functioning amendment** — writing the decision tree node that routes compound profiles to Tier 2 rather than applying pairwise resolution.
3. **Kawa → sensory space resolution** — translating Kawa's collectivist flow model into specification language for sensory environments in non-Western contexts. This is the most novel theoretical contribution and requires careful reasoning.
4. **FDR compound-interaction audit methodology** — defining how to systematically identify which FDR findings interact non-additively. No existing methodology exists for this; it must be developed.
