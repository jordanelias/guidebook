## Synthesis 1: Economic Argument Framing

### Best-practice determination

The guidebook's economic argument should be structured as a **three-layer case**, not a single cost figure:

**Layer 1 — The design-stage near-zero finding.** This is the headline. The evidence from six independent jurisdictions (DE, BE, US, CA, SE, NL) converges: accessibility incorporated at design stage adds 0.5%–2% of construction cost, with the German TERRAGON/DStGB study demonstrating that 138 of 148 DIN 18040-2 criteria produce literally zero additional cost. The residual premium comes overwhelmingly from three items: lift provision, bathroom spatial allocation, and entrance-level transitions. This should be stated in Part 1 §1.4 or §1.5 as a factual foundation, not an argument to be won.

**Layer 2 — The retrofit penalty multiplier.** The ratio matters more than absolute numbers. The German data gives the cleanest figure: 12× (€19,100 retrofit vs €1,600 new-build per 75 m² apartment). The Belgian peer-reviewed range is 2×–15×. The US HUD data corroborates: $12,200–$15,150 average retrofit cost per unit. This layer belongs in Part 10 (Adaptable Readiness) as the economic rationale for "design it in now."

**Layer 3 — The avoided-cost dividend.** The HUD/Woodstock (2020) finding is the strongest: home modifications generate $6,668 in reduced personal care costs per participant, and only 2.5%–4.6% of recipients deferring assisted-living for 5 years is sufficient for the programme to break even. This transforms the framing from "accessibility costs money" to "inaccessibility costs more." This layer should appear in Part 1's foundational argument and be cross-referenced from Part 10.

### Specific guidance

**Part 1 (Foundations):** State the design-stage premium as settled evidence, cite the TERRAGON "138/148 zero cost" finding and the multi-jurisdiction convergence, then present the avoided-cost dividend. Do not lead with percentages — lead with the qualitative finding that accessibility is overwhelmingly a planning decision, not a cost decision. The percentages support the claim; they are not the claim.

**Part 10 (Adaptable Readiness):** Lead with the retrofit multiplier. The 12× figure from Germany is the most defensible single number. Frame it as: every dollar not spent on accessibility at design stage becomes twelve dollars of retrofit cost later. Cross-reference the Part 1 foundational statement.

**The contested French FFB figure (2–5%):** Exclude from primary argument. Note in a footnote or endnote that the French construction industry body claims higher costs, that this figure is contested by French disability organisations as unverified, and that it is not corroborated by any independent study in the assembled evidence base. The FFB has a declared commercial interest in this claim (it represents construction companies arguing for reduced accessibility requirements under Loi ELAN). This is not an even-handed disagreement — it is an industry lobby position against the weight of peer-reviewed and government evidence.

### Citation pattern for the guidebook

Do not aggregate a single "worldwide" percentage. Instead, present the pattern:

> "Studies across six countries consistently find that incorporating full accessibility at the design stage adds between 0.5% and 2% to construction cost — and in the most granular analysis available (Germany, DIN 18040-2, 148 criteria), 93% of accessibility criteria produced no additional cost whatsoever. Retrofitting the same features into existing buildings costs 2 to 15 times more."

This is defensible, multi-source, and avoids the trap of a single headline figure that can be attacked.

## Synthesis 2: Cost Data Architecture

### Best-practice determination

**(c) Both, with cross-reference** — but with clear scope separation.

**Part 4 `Retrofit cost note:` fields** should contain qualitative retrofit penalty ratings (the existing LOW/MEDIUM/HIGH — PLANNING/STRUCTURAL/SYSTEMS taxonomy already scaffolded in A-01) with a brief explanatory sentence. They should NOT contain specific cost figures in any currency. The reasons:

1. Cost figures are jurisdiction-specific, date-sensitive, and require annual verification. Part 4 items are designed to be jurisdiction-agnostic.
2. The three-tier hierarchy means a given item's cost depends on tier: Universal Mode (code compliance) is already priced into standard construction; Tier 1 (population-informed) adds the UD premium; Tier 2 (person-specific) adds the OT-assessed modification. A single dollar figure collapses this distinction.
3. The economics-researcher skill scope boundary explicitly excludes "product pricing or equipment market data." Part 4 retrofit cost notes that contain product prices would violate this boundary and create a maintenance burden.

**A standalone economics section** (likely in the Supplementary Volume or as an Appendix to Part 10) should contain:
- The three-layer economic argument (Synthesis 1 above)
- The cross-jurisdictional new-build premium evidence table
- Government programme funding comparison table
- The HUD cost-benefit methodology summary
- Per-item indicative retrofit cost ranges, explicitly labelled by jurisdiction and date, with the E-7 provisionality flag

**Cross-reference:** Part 4 retrofit cost notes should include a single-line pointer: `See Economics Appendix §[X] for indicative cost ranges by jurisdiction.`

## Synthesis 3: Three-Tier Cost-Benefit Framework

### Best-practice determination

Yes — the three-tier hierarchy maps naturally to a cost-benefit framework, and doing so strengthens both the economic argument and the conceptual architecture. But with a critical caveat.

**Universal Mode (Universal Design / Code Compliance):**
- Capital cost: Near-zero marginal at design stage (0.5%–2%)
- Benefit: Population-wide falls prevention, independent living, code compliance
- ROI: Effectively infinite — the cost is absorbed into standard construction
- Evidence: TERRAGON 138/148; multi-jurisdiction convergence

**Tier 1 (Population-Informed Inclusive Design):**
- Capital cost: Low to moderate — the "10 of 148" items plus population-specific features (hearing loops, sensory zoning, visual contrast)
- Benefit: Functional independence for specific populations; reduced need for Tier 2 intervention
- ROI: High but harder to quantify — falls on the spectrum between universal and individual
- Evidence: HUD HomeMod $6,668/participant savings; UK DFG programme economic justification

**Tier 2 (Person-Specific Co-Design / OT Assessment):**
- Capital cost: Variable — from minor (grab bar positioning: $100–350) to major (ceiling hoist: $4,500–18,000+)
- Benefit: Avoided institutional care — the HUD finding that 2.5%–4.6% deferral rate breaks even
- ROI: Individually variable; societally positive
- Evidence: HUD cost-benefit methodology; Norwegian Husbanken programme logic; Japanese Kaigo Hoken subsidy structure

**The critical caveat:** The guidebook must not frame the cost-benefit case as the primary justification for accessibility. The primary justification is rights-based (CRPD Article 9; the guidebook's own foundational principle that universal design is the floor, not the aspiration). The economic case is secondary — it removes the cost objection, it does not create the entitlement. This ordering matters because a cost-benefit framing can be weaponised: if the ROI turns negative for a specific individual or modification, a pure economic case would argue against provision. The rights-based case prevents this.

The recommended structure is:
1. State the rights-based foundation (Part 1 §1.1–1.3 — already drafted)
2. Remove the cost objection with the three-layer economic case (Part 1 §1.5 or new §1.6)
3. Map the tiers to the cost-benefit framework (Part 10 or Economics Appendix)
4. Never present step 3 without step 1.
