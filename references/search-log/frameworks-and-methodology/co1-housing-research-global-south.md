## Key Findings (Cross-Slug) — Sessions 2–3 Additions

**F-E8 — AJOL/AJOD: Africa Co-1 confirms legislation-implementation gap at housing modification level**
Nigeria Mubi (E13): home modification of entrance, restroom access, goods access produced statistically significant sense-of-place improvement. The modest scope of effective interventions (ramps, accessible restrooms, level entrances) confirms that the five accessible principles are achievable within local construction norms — but only if modification support is funded. South Africa Co-1 (E14, E15): middle-class and informal settlement residents both excluded by inaccessible built environments. Informal settlement wheelchair users navigate gravel, mud, no lifts. This is the most specific co-occurring documentation of the formal/informal divide.

**F-E9 — LILACS: LAC evidence confirms Brazil-dominant research gap and policy-implementation failure**
Systematic LAC review (E17): Brazil 61% of research; all other LAC nations severely under-researched. Mexico Co-1 (E21): government-built disabled housing fails to meet accessible standards (user-rated "medianamente accesibles"). Chile social housing guide (E18) provides low-cost adaptation framework — most actionable LAC source. Colombia: legislative framework exists (Ley 361/1997, Ley 1618/2013) but enforcement absent.

**F-E10 — CRPD concluding observations: Art. 9 accessible housing recommendations issued universally but unimplemented**
IDA compilation (E26): CRPD Committee consistently recommends comprehensive accessibility monitoring and sanctions for non-compliance across all 100+ reviewed jurisdictions. Indonesia (E24) and Nigeria (E25) both received specific recommendations. Nigeria 2024 SITAN confirms Accessibility Regulations 2023 not enforced. This is the treaty-body confirmation of the legislation-implementation gap found in all Phase 1 slugs.

**F-E11 — ZH: China has strongest Global South implementation programme (household modification at scale)**
China (E30): 无障碍环境建设法 2023 is first comprehensive national accessible environment law in China. 96.7万户 (967,000 households) of severely disabled people received accessible home modifications by June 2024. This is the largest accessible housing modification programme in the Global South by volume. Gap: no Co-1 narrative access — CDPF data is statistical, not experiential. Implementation gap: old buildings (pre-law) retrofit slow; rural areas underserved.

**F-E12 — JA: Japan is the most comprehensive non-Western residential accessible design model**
Japan (E27, E28): バリアフリー住宅 specifications include entrance ramp, bathroom step removal, handrails, sliding/outward doors — directly applicable as Tier 6 model for East Asia. Barrier-Free New Law 2006 is enforceable. DPI Japan CRPD dialogue (2022) shows disability movement is active but focused on deinstitutionalisation, not housing design per se.

**F-E13 — KO: Korea provides only non-Japanese East Asian wheelchair mobility built environment study**
Korea (E29): Busan wheelchair users mobility study (2022) — built environment quality determines mobility frequency (not just presence/absence of facilities). Korea Urban Research Institute: 14.6% of semi-basement housing residents are disabled — disproportionate concentration of disabled people in least accessible housing typology.
