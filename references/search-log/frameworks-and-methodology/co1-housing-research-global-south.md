## Key Findings (Cross-Slug)

**F-E1 — Universal pattern: legislation exists, implementation fails**
Across all researched jurisdictions (ET/TZ/ZA/IN): accessible built environment legislation exists or has been committed to (Ethiopia — CRPD ratified; Tanzania — CRPD analysis; South Africa — National Building Regulations Part S since 1987; India — RPwD Act 2016, Harmonised Guidelines 2021). In every case, implementation has failed. This is the macro-level finding that contextualises all Phase 1 slug findings.

**F-E2 — The formal/informal city divide is the primary barrier**
In Ethiopia and India (confirmed; inferred for other Global South jurisdictions): accessible design is applied in formal, funded, government-monitored buildings. Informal settlements, slum redevelopment housing, community facilities built without enforcement are almost universally inaccessible. For disabled people who are also poor — the majority globally — the formal accessible built environment is irrelevant to their lived reality.

**F-E3 — Housing as site of daily participation failure**
Ethiopia CBR research (E9): disabled people lack comfortable places to sit with family at home. Ethiopia mass housing (E3): disabled lottery winners cannot reach their own flats. South Africa (E6): built environment barriers prevent participation in health, employment, cultural life. These are not abstract policy failures — they are documented daily barriers experienced in the home and immediate community.

**F-E4 — Kenya model: DPO-government-private sector co-production**
WHO Housing and Health Guidelines (E7): National Cooperative Housing Union Kenya — linking government, disability groups, and private sector to identify land and provide technical assistance and loan capital for accessible housing construction. This is the only Global South positive model for accessible housing production identified.

**F-E5 — India slum Co-1: the most specific built environment barrier documentation**
NIUA India (E10): specific barriers in slum redevelopment buildings named by disabled residents: no ramps, no tactile, non-functional lifts, no accessible community toilets. Community toilets overcrowded; disabled people wait at end of queue. This is the most specific Co-1 documentation of built environment barriers from Global South.

**F-E6 — WHO CBR Guidelines: housing cited but not specified**
WHO CBR Guidelines (E8) name housing barriers as part of the social/livelihood components but contain no built environment design specifications. They confirm that housing barrier removal is part of CBR practice but delegate the design question. This confirms CBR is not a source of design specifications.

**F-E7 — South Africa legislative gap: implementation outpaced by poverty**
ZA (E6): even middle-class disabled people face built environment barriers despite 1987 legislation. Authors note that if middle-class disabled people face barriers, there is "little hope" for the poor majority. South Africa has the most comprehensive sub-Saharan African accessible building legislation — and it is still failing.
