## Scope

This slug documents evidence on how CRPD Art. 9 (accessibility) and Art. 19 (independent living) are being implemented — or failing to be implemented — in the built environment globally. It is distinct from co1-housing-research-global-south (which documents lived experience) and from jurisdiction-specific legislation (which is in jurisdiction-tracker). This slug captures the treaty-body monitoring layer: concluding observations, shadow reports, General Comments.

## Key Findings

**CF-1 — CRPD General Comment 2 establishes accessibility as an ex ante duty, not a reactive adjustment**
GC2 (2014): accessibility must be ensured before a person with disability requests access. States cannot use reasonable accommodation as a substitute for accessible design. Universal design is the standard. Existing buildings must be progressively made accessible — not exempt.

**CF-2 — The implementation gap is universal across CRPD-reviewed jurisdictions**
IDA Art. 9 compilation: all CRPD-reviewed jurisdictions receive the same core recommendation — establish comprehensive monitoring, enforce sanctions. The gap is not in legislation (most states have some accessible building regulation) but in enforcement, monitoring, and sanctions. This is the treaty-body confirmation of Phase 1 findings.

**CF-3 — UN-Habitat provides the only accessible design guidance for slum upgrading and reconstruction contexts**
UN-Habitat Housing Handbook (2016): the only identified international guidance source that specifically addresses accessible design in slum upgrading, reconstruction, and large-scale affordable/social housing programmes. Critical for the guidebook's scope question (formal vs informal).

**CF-4 — CRPD Art. 19 (independent living) drives deinstitutionalisation policy but not accessible housing design**
CRPD COs consistently address Art. 19 as a deinstitutionalisation obligation (move people from institutions to community). However, community living without accessible housing is contradictory. The COs do not specify built environment design standards — they mandate community-based services. The built environment design gap remains unaddressed at treaty level.
