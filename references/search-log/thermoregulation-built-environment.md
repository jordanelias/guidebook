## Key Findings

**TF-1 — Thermoregulatory impairment mechanism and population severity**
- MS: 60–80% heat-sensitive; 0.5°C core temp rise triggers Uhthoff's phenomenon; sudomotor control impaired (T1, T2)
- SCI (tetraplegia ≥C8): essentially poikilothermic — most vulnerable population; inadequate evaporative heat loss at rest in ambient >37°C confirmed (T5); warmer seasons create documented participation barriers (T6)
- POTS/MCAS/dysautonomia: heat → vasodilation → worsening orthostatic intolerance; cold → blood pooling; bidirectional sensitivity (T9, T10)
- FMS: cold pain hypersensitivity is the dominant temperature sensitivity (CPT significantly reduced in 14/17 studies); warmth relieves FMS pain — opposite risk profile to MS/SCI (T7, T8)
- **Critical conflict:** MS/SCI require cool ambient (≤20–22°C); FMS benefits from warm ambient. This is a genuine co-occurrence conflict requiring resolution at Tier 2.

**TF-2 — Ambient temperature range: evidence basis**
- MS patient preference: ~20°C (T2, Co-1 survey)
- WHO housing: 18–24°C "no demonstrable risk" for healthy sedentary persons; below 24°C recommended for vulnerable individuals (T11)
- WHO cold threshold: 18°C minimum (T12)
- Hospital standard for patients: 18–22°C (T, confirmed in hospital study)
- Clinical recommendation for SCI thermal management: 22.2–23.3°C (72–74°F) documented in hypothermia study (T, from SCI hypothermia article)
- **Working range for thermoregulation-impaired populations: 18–22°C. Below 18°C: FMS cold risk + general cardiovascular risk. Above 22°C: MS/SCI heat risk begins. Above 24°C: WHO vulnerable individual risk threshold.**

**TF-3 — Built environment design implications (EN Tier 1–5 base)**
*From clinical evidence:*
- AC is "the only long-term solution" (Co-1 patient, T3): in temperate climates, mechanical cooling is the primary intervention when passive cooling is insufficient
- "Air conditioners in the home are a must" for POTS/dysautonomia (T9)
- Cooling must be controllable to room level — individual temperature management required (T3: pwMS with motor disability most likely to use AC + fans independently)
*From passive design evidence (T14, T15):*
- Tropical passive design priorities: cross-ventilation (most effective), wall absorptance (high albedo/low solar gain), SHGC of glazing, semi-outdoor spaces, ceiling height ≥2700mm
- For thermoregulation-impaired users in tropical contexts: passive cooling reduces reliance on mechanical cooling but cannot eliminate it for SCI tetraplegia or heat-sensitive MS patients in extreme heat (>35°C ambient). Passive + mechanical hybrid is the design target.
- Cool humid tropics: ventilation is less effective at cooling than in dry climates (sweat evaporation reduced) — mechanical dehumidification + cooling required for highest-risk populations
*Gap:* No passive design specifications exist specifically calibrated to the 18–22°C target range for thermoregulation-impaired people in tropical or subtropical contexts. Australian Housing for Health (T14) is the closest available Tier 5 source but addresses general tropical comfort, not disability-specific thermal targets.

**TF-4 — The tropical/subtropical disability thermoregulation gap**
MS prevalence is low in tropical regions (less than 5 per 100,000 vs 100–300 in Northern Europe/Canada) (T, epidemiology data). However:
- SCI thermoregulatory deficit is not climate-dependent — it is equally severe in tropical settings, and potentially more dangerous (higher ambient, more humidity, less infrastructure to maintain cool environments)
- POTS/MCAS/dysautonomia: no climate-specific epidemiological data found; condition is ubiquitous
- FMS: no tropical-specific data on FMS thermal sensitivity found
- **The absence of tropical disability thermoregulation research is not absence of need — it reflects the epidemiological underrepresentation of MS in tropical regions obscuring the genuine need for SCI and OFS populations in these climates.**

**TF-5 — AOTA Home Modifications CPG: thermal environment gap confirmed**
AOTA CPG (T18) does not address thermal environment as a modification domain. No OT professional body guideline retrieved addresses indoor temperature control for thermoregulation-impaired populations. This is a structural evidence gap in the OT CPG literature.
