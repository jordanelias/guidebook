## Key Findings (for BPC)

**F-B1 — Non-Western evidence base is thin but exists:**
Egypt (Mostafa ASPECTSS design index), Malaysia (ASPECTSS application), Japan (Tsukuba calm-down space research 2021/2025), Indonesia and South Korea (1 study each in scoping review 2024), UAE (Murr Autism Center). All are education or specialist rehabilitation contexts; none are residential or public building contexts.

**F-B2 — Critical design transfer problem — collectivist culture:**
PAS 6463 model is an individualist-culture design (private, individual-occupant-controlled retreat). In collectivist cultures (JP, KR, CN, IN, MENA, Sub-Saharan Africa, most LAC): (a) stigma around withdrawal is higher; (b) autistic traits more heavily masked; (c) group-based quiet zones or family-accompanied retreat spaces may be more culturally appropriate than individual refuge rooms. No research investigates this question directly. This is the central gap.

**F-B3 — PAS 6463 principles do transfer (physics is universal):**
The physical principles (acoustic separation, lighting control, low visual complexity, user control) are confirmed to produce benefit across all jurisdictions where sensory spaces have been studied. These are not culturally variable. What is culturally variable: (a) the spatial configuration (individual vs. communal); (b) access patterns (who accompanies the person; stigma around use); (c) labelling and visibility of the space; (d) cost of implementation (Snoezelen equipment unavailable in most Global South).

**F-B4 — Low-resource implementation is in active use without research validation:**
Nigeria (Co-1 evidence): parents building sensory corners with foam mats and neutral paint — functional equivalents of PAS 6463 calming zone without acoustic specification. Colombia, Mexico: commercial Snoezelen-derived providers (Snoezelen equipment cost prohibitive for most). Chile: statutory mandate exists but no design specification for implementation.

**F-B5 — Japan Tier 1 evidence:**
Tsukuba calm-down space study (J-STAGE 2025): biodata (heart rate) confirmed tension reduction in calm-down space for sensory-sensitive individuals. Cross-national comparison (JP/USA/France) — confirms principle transfers. Japanese term カームダウンスペース is now established.

**F-B6 — Individual control remains the primary design variable:**
Confirmed across all jurisdictions studied (Unwin et al. 2022 Wales; Tsukuba 2025 JP). This is the single most important finding from the existing BPC that does transfer globally. Cultural variation does not affect the fundamental need for user agency over sensory inputs within the space.

**F-B7 — Outdoor and nature-based sensory spaces:**
Not yet researched but mentioned across practitioner literature as potentially more accessible in Global South and tropical contexts. Sensory gardens (India Autism Center West Bengal; Els Center Florida; ArchDaily 2024). No research on sensory garden design in tropical climates specifically.

## Gap Status
GAP-LRP-02: CONFIRMED P1. The gap is a design-translation gap with a cultural dimension. PAS 6463 principles transfer (physics); the implementation model (individual private retreat, Snoezelen equipment) does not transfer to collectivist cultures or low-resource settings. No alternative model exists in the literature.
