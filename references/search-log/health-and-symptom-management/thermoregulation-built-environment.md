## Key Findings (for BPC)

**F1 — Clinical thermoregulation impairment (convergent, Tiers 1–3, multi-jurisdiction):**
SCI (above T6): loss of hypothalamic thermoregulatory control; impaired sweating, vasodilation, perception of thermal strain. Core temperature rises at ambient 37°C even at rest (Griggs 2019). Tetraplegia: ~30% of normal sweating capacity; paraplegia: ~57% (Hayashi & Naito 2022). MS: 60–80% prevalence of heat sensitivity (Davis 2010, Flensner 2011); 0.5°C core temp rise sufficient to trigger pseudoexacerbation; lesions may impair hypothalamic thermoregulation.

**F2 — Zero built-environment thermal design specifications for disability populations (global, confirmed):**
No national accessible building standard (US, UK, CA, AU, DE, JP, SE, ES, IN, ZA, EU, ISO) contains indoor thermal performance specifications for SCI or MS populations. WHO HHGL 2018 names disabled persons as thermal-vulnerable but provides no disability-specific thresholds or passive design guidance. DIN 18040 (DE), ADA (US), Part M (UK), NBC India, NCC AU: all silent on thermal design for disability. This is the central finding.

**F3 — Global South compound risk (Tier 1–4, multi-jurisdiction):**
WHO HHGL Annex G: minimal risk temperature reaches 31°C in LMIC cities vs. 23°C in northern continental cities. This means SCI persons in tropical/subtropical climates face ambient conditions that chronically exceed the threshold at which thermoregulatory failure occurs in SCI populations at rest (Griggs 2019: failure at 37°C ambient at rest; but Tcore instability begins at lower ambient in tetraplegics). No study or standard addresses this intersection.

**F4 — Passive cooling as primary available strategy in Global South:**
Passive cooling (cross-ventilation, shading, materials, ceiling fans) is the primary thermally relevant building design domain for Global South housing where AC is unaffordable. Mumbai field study (Sen 2021): neutral temperature 28.3°C, comfort band 24.6–32.2°C in naturally ventilated low-income housing. ASHRAE 55 adaptive comfort model not validated for disability populations in tropical contexts.

**F5 — MS housing + thermoregulation domain confirmed:**
MS housing rapid review 2025 (AU/Multi): explicitly identifies thermoregulation as a housing design domain separate from ADL support. Air conditioning, fans, and ventilation identified by pwMS as primary thermal resilience strategies (Chaseling 2022 patient survey). This constitutes design-relevant Co-1 adjacent evidence: pwMS depend on indoor ambient temperature control for functional independence.

**F6 — Japan Tier 5 residential guidance (SCI room temperature management):**
Beppu SCI Centre residential handbook (JP, 2015): instructs SCI persons to regulate room temperature as a management strategy for thermoregulatory dysfunction. This is the only non-EN Tier 5 source that makes a direct connection between indoor built environment (room temperature) and SCI thermoregulatory need. No specific temperature targets given; no passive design specifications.

## Gap Status
GAP-LRP-01: CONFIRMED P1. The gap is not absence of evidence but absence of design translation. Clinical evidence is well-established. The specification gap — what indoor thermal parameters are required for SCI and MS populations in tropical/subtropical climates — has no evidence base in any jurisdiction. This gap requires primary research, not literature retrieval.
