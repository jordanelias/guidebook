## Key Findings (for BPC)

**F-C1 — Japan origin and leadership:**
Tactile paving (点字ブロック) was invented in Japan in 1967 by Seiichi Miyake. Japan's JIS T 9251:2001 is the original national standard. ISO 23599:2019 is explicitly derived from Japanese research and experience. Japan has the most mature TWSI system globally. This is the primary non-Western evidence base for tactile wayfinding.

**F-C2 — East Asian implementation gap (bars vs. domes):**
East Asian TWSI systems primarily use directional bars for continuous path guidance. North American and European systems primarily use truncated domes for hazard warning. ISO 23599:2019 accommodates both. GuideTWSI dataset (2025) confirms existing AI/robotics datasets are geographically biased toward East Asian bar systems, not dome-based systems. Design implication: the two system types are not interchangeable; conversion or co-implementation creates inconsistency.

**F-C3 — India implementation gap confirmed:**
India has TWSI in Delhi Metro stations (yellow tiles, Japanese-derived) but outdoor maintenance is negligible — blind commuters cannot reliably locate outdoor tactile paths. PMC12082883 (2025): 75% gap in tactile wayfinding provisions in Indian residential accessibility policies. 80% of VIS participants in Indian study prefer tactile and auditory aids. India has both the need and the national will (Harmonised Guidelines 2021 reference tactile provision) but implementation has not occurred at scale outside major metro stations.

**F-C4 — Nigeria: wayfinding gap confirmed in hospital context:**
Ahmed et al. 2022 (NG, Tier 1): hospital wayfinding study — inadequate signage, no tactile provision, disorientation for visually impaired patients at Nigerian hospital. First Sub-Saharan African peer-reviewed wayfinding study in built environment context identified.

**F-C5 — Singapore: most comprehensive Asian wayfinding code:**
BCA Code on Accessibility 2025 Chapter 8 covers signage, tactile, braille, directional signs, auditory signals — comprehensive wayfinding chapter exceeding what most National South building codes contain. Singapore is the model for Global South aspiration, not Global South current practice.

**F-C6 — Natural cues priority over TWSI:**
MDPI Buildings 2024 review: people with visual impairments primarily train using naturally available environmental cues (edges, surfaces, sound, smell). TWSI should not substitute for good universal design that provides natural guide lines. In Global South informal built environments without consistent surfaces or edges, this natural-cue priority is itself problematic — TWSI may be the only reliable cue available.

**F-C7 — Co-1 gap on wayfinding:**
No formal DPO publications on built environment wayfinding from any Global South jurisdiction retrieved. NAB India (national blindness advocacy organisation) publications not retrieved directly. Blind SA, Vision Nigeria, Kenya Union of the Blind: no built environment wayfinding advocacy documents found. Co-1 pass: THIN (not because evidence doesn't exist but because it's not published in retrievable form).
