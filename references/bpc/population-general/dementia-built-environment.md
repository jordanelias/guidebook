### Best-practice synthesis
**Most inclusive provision:** Building layouts with linear or continuous-loop circulation, minimal directional changes; minimum 300 lux in corridors / 500 lux in task areas; high-contrast luminance cues at floor/wall junctions, toilet doors, and bedroom entry points; distinctive landmark objects at all decision points; personalised door identifiers for residential rooms; no high-gloss or reflective flooring; no dark-coloured floor mats at thresholds (perceived as holes).

**Most targeted provision (Tier 2):** OT assessment of cognitive staging and specific disorientation type (landmark, route, or survey knowledge failure) informs environmental modification priority; personalised room-entry cues co-designed with resident and family; DSDC EADDAT audit used as ongoing monitoring instrument.

**Conflict resolution:** Where VIS needs (high contrast) align with DEM needs (colour-coded wayfinding), specify both simultaneously — they are complementary. Where DEM flat-floor preference conflicts with MOB threshold reduction, use flush thresholds with colour contrast band.

**Highest-ambition actionable specification:** Single-corridor or continuous-loop floor plan for dementia residential care settings; pictographic + colour-coded signage at all decision points; minimum 0.4 LRV contrast between adjacent horizontal/vertical surfaces; toilet door visible from resident bedroom (or within direct sightline from bedroom doorway); secure outdoor garden accessible without steps; all floor mats to have LRV ≥30 contrast with floor (never dark on pale).

**Opus 4.6 synthesis note (2026-03-29):** Synthesis reviewed. Four findings. (1) The synthesis correctly identifies floor plan configuration as the most influential environmental factor (Marquardt & Schmieg 2009, Bowes et al. 2019 — Tier 3). However, the specification "single-corridor or continuous-loop" is too prescriptive for non-residential settings. Loop-plan is the evidence-based provision for residential dementia care. For non-residential (hospital wards, day centres), the evidence supports legible circulation with identifiable decision points, not necessarily a loop. Reclassify: loop-plan is D-01 (residential DEM-primary); non-residential DEM provisions should specify landmark density and sightline-to-destination instead. (2) The symmetrical-vs-diverse layout divergence (noted in Divergent Findings) is genuine and unresolved. Gestalt evidence supports spatial symmetry for cognitive mapping; spatial differentiation evidence supports diversity for landmark encoding. Opus assessment: both are correct for different cognitive stages. Early-to-moderate dementia benefits from distinctive landmarks (diversity); advanced dementia benefits from spatial predictability (regularity). The guidebook should specify stage-awareness in D-01/D-04 rather than choosing one position. (3) The NO-DATA / THIN section reveals that extended passes for 9 languages have not been run. The concept boundary was clean for all 14 languages — this is a retrieval gap, not an evidence gap. Extended research pass recommended before v10 publication. Priority: DE (KDA), NO (Helsedirektoratet), FR (CEREMA/PMND), JA (MHLW group home standards). (4) CONFLICT DOMAIN: DEM lighting provisions (300/500 lux; warm colour temperature; no glare) align with VIS needs but conflict with NDV/SENS photosensitivity thresholds (lower lux preferred for sensory-sensitive users). Individual dimming control (B-06) is the Tier 1 resolution where both populations share space — but is insufficient for ambient lighting in corridor/circulation (which must serve both simultaneously). Route to cross-population-conflict-mapper LIGHT-INT domain. Evidence confidence: MODERATE-HIGH for core spatial provisions (Tier 3 systematic review base); MODERATE for lighting thresholds (DSDC practitioner guidance, not RCT); LOW for non-English jurisdictional evidence (retrieval gaps in 9 languages).
