### Best-practice synthesis
**PROVISIONAL (pre-Opus, from 2026-03-18 — will be superseded after Co-1 pass and Opus re-synthesis):**
**Most inclusive provision:** Trauma-informed design (TID) throughout — safety, choice, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural humility as spatial principles; quiet retreat room provided in all multi-occupancy settings.
**Most targeted provision:** De-escalation/safe retreat room (distinct from A-16 sensory room); predictable spatial sequence; no dead-end corridors; user control of lighting and acoustic conditions.
**Conflict resolution:** NDV/AUT muted palette vs NDV/MH (PTSD) bright-colour preference — spatial separation required; adjustable multi-mode design preferred.
**Highest-ambition actionable specification:** Dedicated quiet retreat room ≥9 m² with user-adjustable lighting (2700–6500 K), acoustic treatment (RT60 ≤0.4 s), and no through-traffic; all corridor routes loop or terminate at a named destination.

### PubMed academic pass (2026-03-27)

**Oostermeijer, Brasier, Harvey, Hamilton, Roper, Martel, Fletcher, Brophy (2021)**
Source: BMJ Open 11(7):e046647. DOI: 10.1136/bmjopen-2020-046647. [Tier 3 rapid systematic review]
[PubMed PMID: 34233981]
Populations: NDV/MH / ALL (adult psychiatric inpatient)
Design: Rapid systematic review. 35 studies. Design features reducing seclusion and restraint.
Themes: Beneficial physical environment (gardens, recreational facilities); sensory/comfort rooms; private, uncrowded, calm spaces.
Key finding: Physical environment has a role in supporting seclusion/restraint reduction through multilayered approach. Evidence quality rated low across studies.
Evidence quality: Tier 3. Evidence marker: ●

**Faerden, Rosenqvist, Håkansson, Strøm-Gundersen, Stav, Svartsund, Røssæg, Davik, Kvarstein, Pedersen, Dieset, Nyrud, Weedon-Fekjær, Kistorp (2022)**
Source: HERD 16(2):55–72. DOI: 10.1177/19375867221136558. [Co-1 / Tier 1 pre-post with control]
[PubMed PMID: 36567605]
Populations: NDV/MH (acute psychiatric, seclusion unit)
Design: User-driven service design. Pre-post evaluation with control group. NO/Oslo University Hospital.
Design concepts: Welcoming atmosphere, contact with nature, room for privacy, close contact with staff, designated smoking area.
Key finding: Environmental design changes significantly supported patients in their situation and staff in their work. Dignity design concepts applicable in acute psychiatric setting.
Evidence quality: Co-1 + Tier 1 (pre-post with control). Evidence marker: ●

**Haig, Hallett (2022)**
Source: International Journal of Mental Health Nursing 32(1):54–75. DOI: 10.1111/inm.13065. [Tier 3 systematic review]
[PubMed PMID: 36082841]
Populations: NDV/MH (adult psychiatric inpatient)
Design: Systematic review. 18 studies. Sensory rooms in psychiatric settings.
Key finding: Sensory rooms likely support reduction in patient distress. Promote emotional regulation and self-management. Positively impact patient admission experience. But insufficient evidence for reducing seclusion/restraint specifically. Further research needed on what works, for whom, and in what design configuration.
Evidence quality: Tier 3. Evidence marker: ●○

**Price, Papastavrou Brooks, Johnston, McPherson et al. (2024)**
Source: Health Technology Assessment 28(3):1–120. DOI: 10.3310/FGGW6874. [Tier 1 feasibility trial]
[PubMed PMID: 38343036]
Populations: NDV/MH (adult acute + forensic psychiatric inpatient)
Design: EDITION — co-produced de-escalation intervention. Uncontrolled pre-post feasibility. 10 wards (acute, PICU, low/medium/high secure forensic). 2 UK mental health trusts.
Interventions included: De-escalation training, reflective practice, post-incident debriefing, sensory modulation, practice changes around social AND PHYSICAL ENVIRONMENT.
Key finding: Feasibility confirmed. Signal of effect on conflict and containment rates. Physical environment changes were one component of a multicomponent intervention.
Evidence quality: Tier 1 feasibility. Evidence marker: ●○
