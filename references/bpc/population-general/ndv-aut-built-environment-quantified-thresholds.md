## ndv-aut-built-environment-quantified-thresholds

**Updated:** 2026-03-18 23:30  **Evidence tier:** 3–5 (thin base; practitioner consensus dominant)
**Opus synthesis:** YES [OPUS-SYNTHESIS]
**Consensus finding:** Evidence for specific quantitative built environment design thresholds for NDV/AUT populations is Tier 4–5 (practitioner consensus and limited empirical work). Peer-reviewed literature provides directional guidance on domains but rarely specifies validated dB or lux thresholds. The evidence is building but has not yet reached RCT-level validation for built environment specifications.
**Three primary design domains (Tola et al. 2021, Tier 1 scoping review):**
  1. Sensory quality — minimise stimuli; provide quiet/retreat spaces; manage transition zones; control acoustics first, then visual and olfactory
  2. Intelligibility — clear spatial layouts; predictable organisation; compartmentalised activity zones
  3. Predictability — consistent layouts; routine-supporting design; reduced visual complexity
**Most specific quantified evidence available:**
  - Acoustics — background noise: target <55 dB(A) in focused attention spaces for autistic students vs. neurotypical controls; disturbances at 55–60 dB(A) were disproportionately disruptive for ASD (Caniato et al. 2024 — single classroom study; PROVISIONAL — flag as directional only)
  - Acoustics — RT60: Bettarello et al. (2021) — first acoustic threshold study providing specific RT/background noise data for autism-specific spaces. Measured ASD daily-care facility; found most rooms did not comply with recommended thresholds without intervention; sound-absorbing materials achieved compliance. Recommends calibrated 3D acoustic modelling per room type (Tier 3, Applied Sciences DOI: 10.3390/app11093942)
  - Lighting: controllable preferred; avoid harsh/flickering; no specific validated lux threshold beyond general PAS 6463 guidance (Tier 5)
  - Thermal/olfactory: controllable; avoid scented materials (Tier 5, PAS 6463:2022)

**ASPECTSS Design Index (Mostafa 2008/2019/2021):** Seven criteria — acoustics, spatial sequencing, escape space, compartmentalisation, transition spaces, sensory zoning, safety. Established practitioner consensus framework. ASPECTSS 2.0 (Frontiers in Psychiatry 2021, DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2021.727353) — autism-specific design with post-occupancy evaluation data.
**PAS 6463:2022:** First BSI standard specifically for neurodiversity-inclusive design; guidance only (PAS, not mandatory BS); covers autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia; emphasises design principles and process rather than specific quantitative thresholds.
**Standing caveat for all NDV F-series items:** "Evidence for NDV sensory design is Tier 4–5 (practitioner consensus, limited empirical data). Specific quantitative thresholds remain under active investigation. All F-series specifications are expert consensus-based pending peer-reviewed threshold studies."
**Evidence gap:** No RCT-level study has established built environment design thresholds (dB, lux, mm) specifically for NDV populations. The practitioner-evidence gap is confirmed across all 14 languages searched. PAS 6463 acknowledges the evidence base is an "emerging specialism."
**Jurisdictions confirmed:** UK (PAS 6463:2022); no mandatory NDV-specific standard in any other jurisdiction

### best_practice_synthesis
**Opus synthesis:** YES [OPUS-SYNTHESIS] — 2026-03-28

**Most inclusive provision:** Design to the ASPECTSS seven-criteria framework (acoustics, spatial sequencing, escape space, compartmentalisation, transition spaces, sensory zoning, safety) with measurable targets where evidence permits, and process-based requirements (sensory audit, post-occupancy evaluation) where it does not. Acoustics is the domain with the strongest directional evidence and should be specified first.

**Most targeted provision:** Autistic individuals in focused-attention environments (classrooms, workspaces, clinical settings) where uncontrolled acoustic and visual stimuli directly impair cognitive function. Background noise < 55 dB(A) in focused-attention spaces is the only population-specific quantified threshold with even directional evidence.

**Conflict resolution:** N/A — NDV/AUT thresholds are stricter subsets of general-population comfort thresholds in every domain where data exists. No conflict with other populations.

**Highest-ambition actionable specification:** Background noise ≤ 55 dB(A) in focused-attention spaces [PROVISIONAL — Caniato et al. 2024, single classroom study, directional only; THIN]; RT60 per acoustics-speech-intelligibility-disability synthesis (≤ 0.3 s); transition buffer ≥ 1.5 m between activity zones (PAS 6463, Tier 5); quiet/retreat space ≥ 8 m² per sensory-relief-space-design synthesis; spatial layout to follow ASPECTSS compartmentalisation and sequencing principles with post-occupancy sensory audit required at RFO. Lighting: controllable intensity and colour temperature at occupant level; no quantified lux threshold defensible beyond PAS 6463 general guidance. [THIN — no RCT-level study has established built environment design thresholds for NDV populations in any searched language.] Tier 3 (Caniato et al. 2024, Bettarello et al. 2021) provides directional acoustic evidence; Tier 5 (PAS 6463:2022, ASPECTSS) provides framework; Tier 4–5 governs all other domains.

**Evidence confidence:** LOW — no RCT or controlled intervention study has validated specific built environment thresholds (dB, lux, mm) for any NDV population. The 55 dB(A) value is from a single classroom study. PAS 6463 is explicitly guidance-only and avoids quantitative thresholds by design. ASPECTSS provides a validated assessment framework but not prescriptive specifications. All quantified NDV/AUT thresholds must be marked PROVISIONAL; post-occupancy evaluation is a mandatory compensating measure.

**Opus note:** The evidence gap here is structural, not merely a literature lag. Neurodivergent sensory experience is inherently heterogeneous — population-level thresholds may never converge the way they do for wheelchair turning circles. The guidebook should frame this honestly: the highest-ambition specification for NDV/AUT is not a number but a design process — individual occupant control, sensory audit at DD and RFO, and adjustment capability built into the architecture. Specifying fixed thresholds with false precision would contradict the clinical evidence that individual variation exceeds between-group variation.
