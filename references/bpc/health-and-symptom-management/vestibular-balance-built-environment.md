## PICO

- **P** — People with vestibular disorders and others with impaired balance/postural control (incl. post-concussion, movement disorders, ageing vision) in the built environment.
- **I** — Built-environment features: handrail geometry/continuity, floor surface (compliant/anti-slip), visual environment (pattern, specularity, optic-flow), stair geometry, lighting.
- **C** — Code-minimum or unmodified environments; naive sensory "correction."
- **O** — Fall/fall-injury incidence, balance-recovery capability, vision-induced dizziness, participation.

## Best-practice synthesis (provisional)

The evidence converges on a core principle for A-STABLE: **the environment must support balance *recovery* and must not itself provoke instability** (visual-motion-induced dizziness). Design directions grounded in the collected sources:

1. **Handrails engineered for recovery, not just steadying.** Under balance loss people grasp "any available surface" and apply large multidirectional forces to rails; handrail height across the code range does not compromise reach-to-grasp timing in older adults (REF-00911, REF-00912, REF-00913). ● → Provide **continuous, both-side, power-grip handrails on all stairs and ramps**, dimensioned and mounted for recovery-level loads (WHO APS15, REF-00883 ◐).
2. **Compliant / injury-reducing flooring in high-risk zones.** Compliant flooring reduces fall-impact forces "with minimal effects on standing/walking balance" and is injury-protective in care settings (REF-00905 ●). → Specify in bathrooms, bedsides, and care settings.
3. **Do not provoke visual vertigo (the A-STABLE core).** People with vestibular disorders report dizziness in "visually charged environments" and under optokinetic / repeating-pattern / strong optic-flow stimuli; visual-motion perception is integral to balance (REF-00908, REF-00907 ●). → **Avoid large high-contrast repeating floor/wall patterns, specular/reflective floors, and strong optic-flow surfaces** in circulation; hold the visual field stable.
4. **Environmental modification is first-line and OT-led.** Home safety assessment + modification reduces falls (REF-00792 Cochrane; RR≈0.81, more effective delivered by an occupational therapist) and is strongly recommended by falls guidelines (REF-00903, REF-00902, REF-00904 ●).
5. **Beware naive "sensory correction."** A vision intervention *increased* falls, and multifocal→single-lens switching helped only active outdoor users (REF-00792). ○ → Accommodate the person's sensory reweighting; do not assume "fixing vision" reduces risk.
6. **Stairs are the critical hazard geometry.** Stair-descent balance loss ~doubles centre-of-mass velocity; a large share of older adults' home stairs fail dimensional guidelines while being *perceived* safe (REF-00913, REF-00914, REF-00917 ●). → Enforce guideline going/riser, high-contrast nosings, both-side handrails, and adequate glare-free lighting on stairs and transitions (REF-00906 ◐).

**Held for verification:** quantified specifications (handrail diameter/height bands, compliant-flooring force thresholds, illuminance targets, stair going/riser) are **not asserted here** — the collected sources are at `UNVERIFIED-1` and quantified claims require DOI + page confirmation (citation discipline). This synthesis states directions, not values; values follow source verification + non-EN/jurisdictional coverage.
