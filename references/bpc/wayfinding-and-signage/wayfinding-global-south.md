## Evidence Summary

### Cluster 1 — Japan: primary non-Western evidence base

Tactile paving (点字ブロック) origin is Japan (1967). ISO 23599:2019 derives from Japanese research. Japan's JIS T 9251:2001 preceded ISO. South Korea has comprehensive implementation (점자블록 statutory). China has 盲道 in major cities (rural: minimal). Singapore BCA Code 2025: most comprehensive Asian wayfinding code (Chapter 8 with TGSI §9.7 and tactile/braille §8.5).

East Asian systems use directional bars for path guidance (primary). Western systems use truncated domes for hazard warning (primary). ISO accommodates both. The two are not interchangeable — mixed environments create inconsistency for VIS users.

### Cluster 2 — Global South implementation gap

**India (Tier 1, PMC12082883):** 75% gap in tactile wayfinding provisions in reviewed Indian policies. 80% of VIS participants prefer tactile and auditory aids. Delhi Metro has tactile paving (Japanese-derived yellow tiles) but outdoor maintenance is negligible — commuters cannot reliably locate tactile paths. Harmonised Guidelines 2021 reference tactile provision but implementation has not occurred at scale.

**Nigeria (Tier 1, Ahmed et al. 2022):** Hospital wayfinding study — inadequate signage, no tactile provision, disorientation for VIS patients. Only Sub-Saharan African peer-reviewed built environment wayfinding study identified. No DPO wayfinding advocacy documents retrieved from Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Design implication:** The ISO 23599 / ISO 21542 specification is technically sound and confirmed effective in East Asian and Western contexts. The Global South gap is not a specification gap — it is an implementation and maintenance gap. Specifications that require specialist materials, consistent surface preparation, and ongoing maintenance cannot be specified as primary solutions in informal built environments. Natural cues (edges, surface texture changes, sound landmarks) are the primary accessible wayfinding resource in most Global South built environments.

### Cluster 3 — No Global South–specific specification exists

No national accessible building standard in any new jurisdiction (IN, ZA, NG, PH, ID, CO, CL, MX, KE, BD, EG, MA, TH, GH, TZ, ET) contains tactile wayfinding specifications beyond general reference to international standards. Singapore is the outlier — its BCA Code 2025 Chapter 8 is comprehensive and could serve as a model for ASEAN jurisdiction adoption.

### ISO 23599 / ISO 21542 Convergence Assessment

The existing wayfinding BPC entries (cognitive-wayfinding-design, detectable-gradient-protocol-sensory-zones) are built on ISO 23599:2019 and ISO 21542:2021. The Global South evidence does not challenge or modify these specifications. The core finding is:

**The specifications are sound. The problem is implementation, not design.**

This is a fundamentally different finding from the other Phase 1 slugs (thermoregulation, bathroom typology, sensory space), where the specifications themselves require adaptation for Global South contexts. For wayfinding, the ISO specifications are physically determined — the geometry of a truncated dome or directional bar is dictated by detectability under foot and under cane tip, which does not vary by culture, climate, or economic context.

### Maintenance as a Design Variable

The India experience (Delhi Metro vs. outdoor pavement) produces one design lesson: **maintenance must be designed in, not assumed.**

- Delhi Metro tactile paving (enclosed, cleaned, maintained): functional and effective.
- Delhi outdoor pavement tactile (exposed, not maintained, obstructed by vendors): non-functional within months of installation.

Design implications for Global South specifications:
1. Specify TWSI materials for durability without maintenance: precast concrete with integral pattern (cannot be removed or worn away short of destroying the paving unit). Avoid adhesive-applied TWSI overlays in uncontrolled outdoor contexts.
2. Specify TWSI placement to minimise obstruction: edge-of-path placement (300mm from wall or kerb) is less likely to be obstructed by vendors or parked vehicles than centre-of-path placement.
3. Acknowledge that no TWSI specification can guarantee function in the absence of maintenance and enforcement. The guidebook should state this explicitly rather than implying that specification alone produces accessibility.

### Natural Cues as Primary Wayfinding in Informal Environments

For built environments where TWSI installation is not feasible (unpaved surfaces, informal markets, rural paths), the guidebook should acknowledge natural cues as the primary wayfinding resource:
- Edge contrast: pathway edge distinguishable by surface texture change (gravel to packed earth, concrete to grass)
- Sound landmarks: consistent sound sources (fountain, clock, call to prayer, market activity) provide orientation reference
- Olfactory landmarks: culturally specific (bakery, spice market, fuel station) — documented in O&M training literature but not in built environment standards
- Surface texture: detectable underfoot transitions between zones (smooth to rough, hard to soft)

**These are not specifications — they are observations about how VIS people navigate informal environments.** The guidebook cannot specify natural cues because they are not designed elements. The guidebook can note that in informal environments, preserving and enhancing natural cues (maintaining edge contrast, not homogenising surface textures) supports wayfinding better than absent TWSI.
