## Evidence Summary

### Cluster 1 — Japan: primary non-Western evidence base

Tactile paving (点字ブロック) origin is Japan (1967). ISO 23599:2019 derives from Japanese research. Japan's JIS T 9251:2001 preceded ISO. South Korea has comprehensive implementation (점자블록 statutory). China has 盲道 in major cities (rural: minimal). Singapore BCA Code 2025: most comprehensive Asian wayfinding code (Chapter 8 with TGSI §9.7 and tactile/braille §8.5).

East Asian systems use directional bars for path guidance (primary). Western systems use truncated domes for hazard warning (primary). ISO accommodates both. The two are not interchangeable — mixed environments create inconsistency for VIS users.

### Cluster 2 — Global South implementation gap

**India (Tier 1, PMC12082883):** 75% gap in tactile wayfinding provisions in reviewed Indian policies. 80% of VIS participants prefer tactile and auditory aids. Delhi Metro has tactile paving (Japanese-derived yellow tiles) but outdoor maintenance is negligible — commuters cannot reliably locate tactile paths. Harmonised Guidelines 2021 reference tactile provision but implementation has not occurred at scale.

**Nigeria (Tier 1, Ahmed et al. 2022):** Hospital wayfinding study — inadequate signage, no tactile provision, disorientation for VIS patients. Only Sub-Saharan African peer-reviewed built environment wayfinding study identified. No DPO wayfinding advocacy documents retrieved from Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Design implication:** The ISO 23599 / ISO 21542 specification is technically sound and confirmed effective in East Asian and Western contexts. The Global South gap is not a specification gap — it is an implementation and maintenance gap. Specifications that require specialist materials, consistent surface preparation, and ongoing maintenance cannot be specified as primary solutions in informal built environments. Natural cues (edges, surface texture changes, sound landmarks) are the primary accessible wayfinding resource in most Global South built environments.

### Cluster 3 — No Global South–specific specification exists

No national accessible building standard in any new jurisdiction (IN, ZA, NG, PH, ID, CO, CL, MX, KE, BD, EG, MA, TH, GH, TZ, ET) contains tactile wayfinding specifications beyond general reference to international standards. Singapore is the outlier — its BCA Code 2025 Chapter 8 is comprehensive and could serve as a model for ASEAN jurisdiction adoption.
