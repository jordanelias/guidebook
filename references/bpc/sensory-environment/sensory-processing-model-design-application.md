## sensory-processing-model-design-application

**Updated:** 2026-03-29 (Opus synthesis)  **Original search:** 2026-03-18 23:00  **Evidence tier:** 2–4  **Opus synthesis:** YES [OPUS-SYNTHESIS]
**Consensus finding:** Dunn's Sensory Processing Model identifies four processing profiles that require distinct — and in some cases conflicting — environmental design responses. Accessible design specifications for acoustic (Category A), lighting (Category B), and colour/surface (Category C) must be calibrated across all four profiles simultaneously, not optimised for a single profile.
**Four profiles and design implications:**
  - Sensory seeking: seeks high stimulation; benefits from varied spatial experience, rich biophilic elements, textured surfaces, vibrant but controlled colour
  - Sensory avoiding: actively avoids stimulation; benefits from low-stimulation retreats, muted palette, acoustic attenuation, individual control to withdraw
  - Sensory sensitivity (low threshold/passive): easily distressed by stimulation but does not actively avoid; benefits from predictable, consistent sensory environment with no sudden changes; restorative elements that provide soft fascination
  - Low registration (high threshold/passive): requires stronger sensory cues to register environmental information; benefits from higher-contrast wayfinding, clearer acoustic signals, more vivid colour coding
**Design implications per category:**
  - Category A (Acoustics): achieving RT60 ≤0.4 s serves sensory-avoiding and sensory-sensitivity profiles; ensuring adequate SNR serves low-registration and hearing-impaired users; the conflict requires individual user control as the primary resolution
  - Category B (Lighting): dimmable full-range lighting (0–100%) is the only provision that serves all four profiles; fixed high-brightness fixtures serve low-registration but distress sensory-avoiding
  - Category C (Colour/Surface): muted palette serves sensory-avoiding and sensory-sensitivity; high-contrast wayfinding elements (LRV ≥30) serve low-registration and VIS users simultaneously without requiring high-saturation general palette
  - Category F/G (Sensory Zoning): the Detectable Gradient Protocol (three-zone model) implements a spatial solution — different zones serve different profiles — rather than requiring a single space to serve all four simultaneously
**Allen's CDM integration:** CDM (six cognitive levels 0–6) addresses the sequential decline of cognitive accessibility in dementia; Dunn's model addresses static sensory processing variation in NDV populations. Both operate on the same physical environment — acoustic, lighting, and colour specifications must satisfy both frameworks simultaneously where DEM + NDV co-occurrence is anticipated.
**EHP framework:** Accessible design interventions classified by EHP strategy: most alter the context (environment) rather than the person; some prevent barrier formation at source (pre-install blocking, acoustic zoning at schematic)
**Jurisdictions confirmed:** PAS 6463:2022 (UK) is the sole national standard operationalising sensory processing model principles in built environment design; no mandatory regulatory standard

**Opus 4.6 synthesis note (2026-03-29):** This BPC is a framework entry, not a specification entry. Opus review confirms it as the governing theoretical framework for all F-series and sensory-related item specifications. Three findings. (1) The four-profile model (seeking/avoiding/sensitivity/low-registration) correctly identifies that no single environmental setting serves all NDV users. The Detectable Gradient Protocol (three-zone spatial model) is the primary resolution. Individual user control is the secondary resolution. These two mechanisms together provide Tier 1 and Tier 2 coverage. (2) The Allen CDM integration note is critical for DEM+NDV co-occurrence: CDM addresses progressive cognitive decline; Dunn’s model addresses static sensory variation. In facilities serving both populations (aged care with NDV residents), specifications must satisfy both frameworks simultaneously. This is a design constraint, not a conflict — CDM-simplified environments (low complexity, consistent layout) are compatible with sensory-avoiding/sensory-sensitivity provisions. The conflict arises only with sensory-seeking provisions in dementia settings, where stimulating environments may increase agitation. Route to cross-population-conflict-mapper. (3) PAS 6463:2022 is correctly identified as the sole national standard operationalising this framework. No other jurisdiction has equivalent regulation. This means all NDV sensory specifications in the guidebook exceed any mandatory requirement — they are Tier 4–5 (beyond-code best practice). Evidence confidence: HIGH for the theoretical framework (Dunn 1997 is Tier 1 OT clinical research with extensive replication); LOW for specific built-environment applications of the framework (no RCT validating specific design thresholds derived from the model).

**Divergent findings:** Sensory seeking vs. sensory avoiding design requirements are partially irreconcilable in a single space — spatial separation (Detectable Gradient Protocol zones) is the primary resolution; individual user control is the secondary resolution
