## Evidence Summary

### Cluster 1 — Non-Western evidence base (THIN but not absent)

Bertone et al. 2021 (B1, Tier 3 scoping review, 21 studies): 9 US, 6 UK, 5 Egypt. Egypt is the only non-Western country with a peer-reviewed research base on autism built environment design. Mostafa's ASPECTSS design index (Egyptian researcher) is the primary non-Western contribution to the field.

Malaysia (Ghazali et al. 2019, B6, Tier 3): ASPECTSS application in Malaysian school context. Confirms index applies across cultural contexts without modification.

Japan (Tsukuba 2021/2025, B3/B4, Tier 1/5): カームダウンスペース research confirmed. Individual control confirmed as primary design variable across JP/USA/France comparison.

Indonesia, Korea, Italy, France (B12, 2024 scoping review): 1 study each. Insufficient for jurisdiction-specific findings; confirms global spread of sensory room practice.

UAE (B5): "Quiet and simple" design principle — natural light, neutral colours, acoustic and ventilation. No dimensional specifications. Tier 3 case study.

Chile (B9, Tier 6): Ley de Autismo N°21.545 (2023) — first LAC statutory mandate for sensory accommodation in schools. No design specifications. Statutory trigger only.

Nigeria (B13, Co-1): Autism parents implementing low-cost sensory corners (foam mats, neutral paint) in Nigerian homes. Functional equivalents of calming zone without design specification.

### Cluster 2 — Cultural transfer problem (PRIMARY GAP)

de Leeuw et al. 2020 (B7, Tier 3) and Crompton et al. 2024 (B8, Tier 1): Collectivist cultures show higher camouflaging pressure, higher autism stigma, more pressure to conform. Design implication: private individual retreat room (PAS 6463 model) assumes individualist culture. In collectivist contexts: (a) use of individual retreat room may itself be stigmatised; (b) family-accompanied or communal quiet zone may be preferred; (c) visible labelling of space as "sensory room" may deter use. No research investigates culturally appropriate sensory space design for collectivist contexts.

This gap is not addressable by literature retrieval — it requires primary research.

### Cluster 3 — Physics transfers; equipment does not

The physical design principles confirmed by PAS 6463 and research (acoustic separation, lighting control, low visual complexity, individual occupant control of sensory inputs) are not culturally variable. They produce benefit wherever implemented. What does not transfer:
- Snoezelen equipment (bubble tubes, fibre optics, LED panels) — cost prohibitive in most Global South settings (USD $10,000–$50,000+)
- AC-based thermal control — assumes mechanically conditioned indoor space; not standard in tropical Global South
- STC 50 partition — requires specialist construction; not feasible in informal or low-cost construction

Low-resource equivalents identified in practitioner literature (not research-validated): curtain partition, foam mat flooring, neutral paint, battery LED string lighting, natural ventilation, corner nook.

### Cluster 4 — Outdoor sensory spaces (horizon finding)

Multiple practitioner sources reference sensory gardens as accessible alternatives to indoor sensory rooms. India Autism Center West Bengal (52 acres, outdoor design emphasis). Els Center Florida. No research on outdoor sensory garden design in tropical climates. This is a P3 horizon gap for a future slug.
