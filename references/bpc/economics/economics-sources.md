### Depth-1 discoveries (from Keall/Clemson/Tsuchiya-Ito/Szanton network)

**D1-01** Cockayne, S. et al. (2021). Home environmental assessments and modification delivered by occupational therapists to reduce falls in people aged 65 years and over: the OTIS RCT. *Health Technology Assessment*, 25(46), 1-118. DOI: [10.3310/hta25460](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta25460) | PMID: 34254934 | PMC: PMC8287374
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** UK
**Key finding:** COUNTER-FINDING. OT-delivered home assessment and modification did NOT reduce falls in community-dwelling adults aged ≥65 at risk of falling (adjusted IRR 1.17, p=0.07). Intervention was more expensive and less effective than usual care (incremental cost £18.78/participant; −0.0042 QALY). Important nuance: the effect is population-dependent — no benefit in unselected 'at-risk' population vs. clear benefit in high-risk and vision-impaired populations (cf. Campbell 2005 VIP, Keall HAPPI). Suggests specificity of indication matters for cost-effectiveness.

**D1-02** Campbell, A.J. et al. (2005). Randomised controlled trial of prevention of falls in people aged ≥75 with severe visual impairment: the VIP trial. *BMJ*, 331(7520), 817. DOI: [10.1136/bmj.38601.447731.55](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.38601.447731.55) | PMID: 16183652 | PMC: PMC1246082
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** NZ
**Key finding:** Home safety programme delivered by OT reduced falls by 41% (IRR 0.59, 95% CI 0.42–0.83) in older adults with severe visual impairment. Cost: NZD $650 per fall prevented (2004 prices). Exercise programme not effective in this population. Supports population-specific specification of home modification programmes — VIS population shows clear benefit where general population does not.

**D1-03** Smith, R.D. & Widiatmoko, D. (1998). The cost-effectiveness of home assessment and modification to reduce falls in the elderly. *Australian and New Zealand Journal of Public Health*, 22(4), 436-440. DOI: [10.1111/j.1467-842x.1998.tb01410.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-842x.1998.tb01410.x) | PMID: 9659769
**Tier:** 3 | **Jurisdiction:** AU
**Key finding:** Decision-analytic cost model. Incremental cost: AUD $172/person/year; $1,721/fall prevented; $17,208/injury prevented at 1 year. At 10 years: cost-saving (−$92/person — dominant strategy). First published cost model for home assessment and modification; foundational for subsequent economic evaluations. Limitations: model-based, not trial-based.

**D1-04** Szanton, S.L. et al. (2011). Community aging in place, advancing better living for elders: a bio-behavioral-environmental intervention. *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society*, 59(12), 2314-20. DOI: [10.1111/j.1532-5415.2011.03698.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1532-5415.2011.03698.x) | PMID: 22091738 | PMC: PMC3245364
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** US
**Key finding:** CAPABLE pilot RCT. OT + nurse + handyman (~$1,300 home repairs) over 6 months. Effect sizes: ADL 0.63, IADL 0.62, QoL 0.89, falls efficacy 0.55. 87% completion. Confirms CAPABLE model feasibility — foundation for full RCT (PMID 24685996) and Medicaid cost-savings analysis (Szanton 2017 JAGS).

**D1-05** Szanton, S.L. et al. (2014). CAPABLE trial: rationale and design. *Contemporary Clinical Trials*, 38(1), 102-12. DOI: [10.1016/j.cct.2014.03.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cct.2014.03.005) | PMID: 24685996 | PMC: PMC4074085
**Tier:** 2 | **Jurisdiction:** US
**Key finding:** Full CAPABLE RCT protocol. Cost tracking via Medicaid claims data. 10 home visits over 4 months. Primary outcome: ADL disability reduction. Lays groundwork for Medicaid cost-savings analysis cited elsewhere in BPC.

**D1-06** Allan, L.M. et al. (2019). An intervention to improve outcomes of falls in dementia: the DIFRID mixed-methods feasibility study. *Health Technology Assessment*, 23(59), 1-208. DOI: [10.3310/hta23590](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta23590) | PMID: 31661058 | PMC: PMC6843111
**Tier:** 2 | **Jurisdiction:** UK
**Key finding:** OT+PT home-based intervention for falls in people with dementia. Feasibility confirmed; economic framework for full RCT established. DEM-population-specific economics data. Important for dementia-specific built environment cost arguments.

**D1-07** Bendixen, R.M. et al. (2009). Cost effectiveness of a telerehabilitation program to support chronically ill and disabled elders in their homes. *Telemedicine Journal and e-Health*, 15(1), 31-8. DOI: [10.1089/tmj.2008.0046](https://doi.org/10.1089/tmj.2008.0046) | PMID: 19199845
**Tier:** 3 | **Jurisdiction:** US (VA)
**Key finding:** VA telerehab including adaptive equipment and home modifications. No significant difference in post-enrolment costs between LAMP and matched control. Hospital/nursing home stays decreased; clinic visits increased. Important counter-data: isolated AT+home modification without coordinated OT management shows weak cost-effectiveness signal.

### Depth-2 discoveries (from Cockayne 2021, Campbell 2005, Szanton 2011, Robertson 2001 network)

**D2-01** Robertson, M.C. et al. (2001). Effectiveness and economic evaluation of a nurse delivered home exercise programme to prevent falls. 1: Randomised controlled trial. *BMJ*, 322(7288), 697-701. DOI: [10.1136/bmj.322.7288.697](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.322.7288.697) | PMID: 11264206 | PMC: PMC30094
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** NZ
**Key finding:** Nurse-delivered home exercise. Falls reduced 46% (IRR 0.54). Cost: NZD $1,803/fall prevented including programme delivery; NZD $155/fall prevented when hospital costs averted included. Hospital admissions due to falls: 5 (control) vs 0 (exercise). Companion paper to PMID 11264207 (multi-centre).

**D2-02** Robertson, M.C. et al. (2001). Effectiveness and economic evaluation of a nurse delivered home exercise programme to prevent falls. 2: Controlled trial in multiple centres. *BMJ*, 322(7288), 701-4. DOI: [10.1136/bmj.322.7288.701](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.322.7288.701) | PMID: 11264207 | PMC: PMC30095
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** NZ
**Key finding:** Multi-centre extension. Falls reduced 30% (IRR 0.70). Cost: NZD $418/person/year; NZD $1,519/fall prevented. Together with D2-01, establishes the Robertson/Campbell economic evidence base that underpins NZ BCR claims in HAPPI series.

**D2-03** Logan, P.A. et al. (2022). A multidomain decision support tool to prevent falls in older people: the FinCH cluster RCT. *Health Technology Assessment*, 26(9), 1-136. DOI: [10.3310/CWIB0236](https://doi.org/10.3310/CWIB0236) | PMID: 35125131 | PMC: PMC8859771
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** UK
**Key finding:** GtACH programme for care homes. Falls rate reduced 43% (IRR 0.57, p<0.01). Cost per fall averted: £190.62. Cost per QALY (EQ-5D): £4,543.69 — cost-effective at NHS threshold. Important as care-home-specific economics data.

**D2-04** Bruce, J. et al. (2021). Fall prevention interventions in primary care to reduce fractures and falls in people aged 70 years and over: the PreFIT three-arm cluster RCT. *Health Technology Assessment*, 25(34), 1-114. DOI: [10.3310/hta25340](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta25340) | PMID: 34075875 | PMC: PMC8200932
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** UK
**Key finding:** Exercise arm dominated advice and multifactorial arms. Exercise cost-effective (incremental net monetary benefit £191 at £30K/QALY threshold). Multifactorial falls prevention (which includes home assessment) was NOT cost-effective vs. exercise alone. Adds nuance: home assessment as component of multifactorial programmes may not add cost-effectiveness over exercise.

**D2-05** Gathercole, R. et al. (2021). Assistive technology and telecare to maintain independent living at home for people with dementia: the ATTILA RCT. *Health Technology Assessment*, 25(19), 1-156. DOI: [10.3310/hta25190](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta25190) | PMID: 33755548 | PMC: PMC8020444
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** UK
**Key finding:** Full AT+telecare package vs. basic (pendant alarm + smoke detectors) for dementia independent living. No significant difference in time to care home admission. AT group had −0.105 QALYs vs. control (participant-rated EQ-5D). Health and social care costs: no significant difference. COUNTER-FINDING for AT economics in dementia — full AT package not superior to basic. Clinically important for DEM population specifications.

**D2-06** Cockayne, S. et al. (2017). Clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of a multifaceted podiatry intervention for falls prevention in older people: the REFORM trial. *Health Technology Assessment*, 21(24), 1-198. DOI: [10.3310/hta21240](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta21240) | PMID: 28621259 | PMC: PMC5494515
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** UK/Ireland
**Key finding:** Podiatry intervention (footwear + orthotics + exercise). Falls rate non-significant reduction (IRR 0.88, p=0.16); proportion falling borderline significant (50% vs 55%, p=0.05). Cost per participant: £252 more than usual care. 65% probability cost-effective at £30K/QALY. Relevant to falls prevention economics methodology; less directly relevant to built environment design.

### Depth-3 discoveries (from Robertson 2001, Gathercole 2021, Smith 1998 network)

**D3-01** Robertson, M.C., Devlin, N., Scuffham, P., Gardner, M.M., Buchner, D.M. & Campbell, A.J. (2001). Economic evaluation of a community based exercise programme to prevent falls. *Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health*, 55(8), 600-6. DOI: [10.1136/jech.55.8.600](https://doi.org/10.1136/jech.55.8.600) | PMID: 11449021 | PMC: PMC1731948
**Tier:** 1 | **Jurisdiction:** NZ
**Key finding:** Otago Exercise Programme economic evaluation. Women aged ≥80. Cost: NZD $314/fall prevented at 1 year; NZD $265 at 2 years; NZD $457 and NZD $426 per fall-with-injury prevented. 27% of total hospital costs related to falls. Hospital costs: no significant difference between groups despite fall reduction. Foundational cost-effectiveness paper for falls prevention; benchmark figure referenced across the NZ/Campbell research network.

**D3-02** Williams, N.H. et al. (2017). Developing a multidisciplinary rehabilitation package following hip fracture: FEMuR RCT. *Health Technology Assessment*, 21(44), 1-528. DOI: [10.3310/hta21440](https://doi.org/10.3310/hta21440) | PMID: 28836493 | PMC: PMC5582308
**Tier:** 2 | **Jurisdiction:** UK (Wales)
**Key finding:** Enhanced community rehabilitation after hip fracture (OT + PT sessions + information workbook + goal-setting). Cost: £231/patient for enhanced rehabilitation. Medium-sized improvement in NEADL (extended ADL) vs. control (Cohen's d 0.63). Possible small relative increase in QALYs. Relevant to Part 10 (retrofit/adaptation) and hip fracture prevention economics.

**SROI-02** Watson, K.J. et al. (2017). Applying SROI to the built environment. *Building Research & Information*. DOI: 10.1080/09613218.2016.1223486 | Tier 2 | UK
Key: Stage-by-stage application to three case buildings. User-centred design generates measurable social value.

**SROI-03** CMHC. Calculating SROI for Affordable Housing. Research Insight. | Tier 4 | CA
Key: SROI framework for housing investment; rated existing studies by methodology quality.

**SROI-04** Fujiwara, D. & Mirzoev, S. (2015). SROI and public health — systematic review. *BMC Public Health*. PMC4477315 | Tier 2 | Multi

### DBL built environment evidence — GAP-S4 series

**DBL-REH-01** Warnicke, C., Wahlqvist, M., Anderzén-Carlsson, A. & Sundqvist, A-S. (2022). Interventions for adults with deafblindness — an integrative review. BMC Health Services Research, 22(1), 1594. DOI: 10.1186/s12913-022-08958-4 | PMID: 36585684 | PMC: PMC9801155
Tier: 1 | SE + international | Örebro University + Swedish National Resource Center for Deafblindness
Key: 28 studies reviewed. 14 on communication; 10 on orientation/mobility; 3 on information access; 1 on psychological adaptation. Most studies 1-5 participants. No studies addressed built environment design parameters (corridor widths, room layouts). Evidence base for DBL built environment specs is expert/practitioner consensus, NOT peer-reviewed. GAP-S4 gaps reflect genuine evidence absence.

**DBL-DESIGN-01** JJones Design Co. (2026). How to Design an Accessible Home for Deaf and HOH People. Practitioner. DeafBlind interior designer, US.
Tier: 5 | US. Key design principles: clear sightlines; consistent furniture layouts; matte surfaces; smart home vibrotactile alerts; lighting contrast at walls.

**DBL-DESIGN-02** NYSID / Helen Keller National Center student project (2022). nysid.edu. Tier: 5 | US.
Key: Interpreter must sit BESIDE person (ProTactile hand-signing). Social interaction spaces need MORE room than standard accessible design — ProTactile communication needs wider clear zones. Contrast backdrops for all skin tones.
