## Evidence Summary

### Cluster 1 — Mechanism: impaired thermoregulation is a neurological and autonomic deficit, not behavioural

**MS (NEU):** 60–80% of people with MS experience Uhthoff's phenomenon — temporary worsening of neurological symptoms with core temperature increases as small as 0.5°C. The mechanism is heat-dependent failure of nerve conduction in demyelinated axons. Ambient heat exposure (not exercise alone) augments fatigue and cognitive impairment. MS patients report preferring ~20°C ambient. Impaired sudomotor control (reduced sweating) in MS means passive evaporative cooling is less effective.

**SCI (NEU/MOB):** High-level SCI (tetraplegia ≥C8) produces near-poikilothermic thermoregulation — core body temperature varies with ambient temperature. Sympathetic pathway interruption blocks vasodilation and sweating below the lesion. Evaporative heat loss is insufficient to maintain heat balance at rest in ambient >37°C. Warmer seasonal temperatures create documented participation barriers (restricting outings, increasing physical discomfort). SCI thermodysregulation is the highest-risk scenario in this slug.

**POTS/MCAS/OFS:** Heat causes vasodilation → worsening orthostatic intolerance → increased POTS symptom severity. Mast cell degranulation is triggered by temperature extremes in MCAS. Cold causes blood pooling → cold intolerance. Both directions are clinically significant; heat is the primary trigger for most patients. AC is documented as essential home provision.

**FMS (PAIN):** Cold pain hypersensitivity is the dominant thermal sensitivity in FMS (CPT significantly reduced vs controls in 14/17 studies). Warmth relieves FMS pain. This creates a genuine conflict with the cool-ambient needs of MS/SCI populations — addressed in Cluster 3.

### Cluster 2 — Target ambient range: 18–22°C

**Evidence convergence:**
- MS patient-preferred ambient: ~20°C (patient survey)
- WHO Housing: 18–24°C "no demonstrable risk" for healthy sedentary persons; below 24°C recommended for vulnerable individuals
- WHO cold threshold: 18°C minimum (cardiovascular mortality risk below)
- Hospital clinical standard for neurological/orthopaedic patients: 18–22°C
- SCI clinical thermoregulation management: 22.2–23.3°C documented

**Working target range: 18–22°C**
- Floor (18°C): prevents cold-related cardiovascular risk; addresses FMS cold pain
- Ceiling (22°C): prevents MS Uhthoff's phenomenon onset; protects SCI thermodysregulation
- Above 24°C: WHO vulnerable individual threshold exceeded — not acceptable for thermoregulation-impaired populations at rest

**This range is narrower than typical accessible building thermal comfort standards (usually 20–26°C ASHRAE Zone 2, or equivalent).** For thermoregulation-impaired populations the upper tolerance is approximately 4°C lower than typical comfortable adult range.

### Cluster 3 — Built environment design: passive + mechanical hybrid

**Temperate climates (primary existing evidence base):**
Mechanical cooling (air conditioning) is the documented primary intervention for MS, SCI, and POTS/MCAS. OT CPG (AOTA) does not address thermal environment — the gap in OT guidance is confirmed. Passive strategies (thermal mass, shading, insulation) reduce AC burden but do not eliminate the need for mechanical cooling in thermally vulnerable populations.

**Tropical and subtropical contexts (target gap):**
Passive cooling design priorities from simulation evidence (Honduras, Australia):
1. Cross-ventilation: most effective strategy in hot-dry tropics; less effective in hot-humid tropics (reduced evaporative capacity)
2. Wall absorptance/albedo: high albedo exterior walls reduce solar heat gain
3. SHGC (Solar Heat Gain Coefficient): low-SHGC glazing on east/west/north exposures
4. Ceiling height ≥2700mm: enables thermal stratification and stack ventilation
5. Semi-outdoor spaces and verandahs: buffering zones between outdoor heat and conditioned interior
6. Building orientation: north-south main facades maximise cross-ventilation, minimise morning/afternoon solar gain

**Critical limitation for thermoregulation-impaired users in tropics:** In humid tropical climates (Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa coastal), passive ventilation cannot reliably maintain ambient ≤22°C when outdoor temperatures exceed 32°C and humidity exceeds 70–80%. At this point:
- SCI tetraplegia: mechanical cooling is medically necessary — not a comfort preference
- Heat-sensitive MS: mechanical cooling strongly indicated
- POTS/MCAS: mechanical cooling or mechanical dehumidification + fan required

**Design implication:** Accessible housing in tropical/subtropical contexts for thermoregulation-impaired populations requires mechanical cooling capability as a designed-in feature — not an optional retrofit. This is not addressed in any current accessible building standard.

### Cluster 4 — Gaps: no tropical-specific disability thermal design evidence exists

The two evidence bases (disability thermoregulation + tropical passive design) exist in separate literatures and have not been integrated. No study, standard, or guideline addresses the intersection of:
- Thermoregulation-impaired disability populations
- Tropical or subtropical climates
- Built environment design specifications

This integration gap is the primary finding of this slug. The specifications in Cluster 2 and 3 above are derived from first-principles synthesis of the two separate evidence bases, not from direct evidence.

**Opus synthesis note:** The derivation from first principles must be explicitly disclosed. This BPC entry does not constitute Tier 1–3 evidence for tropical accessible design thermal specifications. It constitutes a gap identification and a first-principles framework pending primary research or directly applicable Tier 1–3 evidence.
