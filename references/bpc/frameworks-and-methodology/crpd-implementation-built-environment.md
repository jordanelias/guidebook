## Evidence Summary

### Cluster 1 — CRPD Art. 9 as ex ante duty: universal design is the standard

CRPD General Comment No. 2 (2014): accessibility is an ex ante duty — it must be ensured in advance, not as a reactive adjustment to an individual's request. Reasonable accommodation cannot substitute for accessible design. Universal design is the required standard. Existing buildings must be progressively made accessible; there is no blanket exemption for existing stock.

Built environment implication: every building open to the public — including housing — is subject to the Art. 9 obligation. States parties must adopt mandatory accessibility standards, enforce them, and impose sanctions for non-compliance.

### Cluster 2 — The implementation gap is universal (100+ jurisdictions reviewed)

IDA compilation (Art. 9, updated September 2024): across all 100+ CRPD-reviewed jurisdictions, the Committee issues the same core recommendations: (a) establish comprehensive accessibility monitoring; (b) increase and enforce sanctions for non-compliance; (c) ensure barrier-free access in buildings. This is not a developing-country-only finding — it is universal. The pattern suggests the CRPD Art. 9 implementation gap is structural, not country-specific.

Nigeria (2024): Persons with Disabilities (Accessibility) Regulations 2023 passed — not enforced within one year. Indonesia (2022): limited OPD consultation in legislation. These are representative, not exceptional.

### Cluster 3 — Accessible housing in informal settings: UN-Habitat fills the treaty gap

The CRPD framework does not specify built environment design standards — it mandates state obligations. The design-level guidance for affordable, social, and informal housing contexts is provided by UN-Habitat Housing Handbook (2016): slum upgrading, reconstruction, and large-scale affordable housing programmes. This is the only identified source providing design guidance for the intersection of accessible design and informal/affordable housing. It bridges the CRPD obligation (rights) and the Phase 1 slug findings (contextual barriers).

### Cluster 4 — Art. 19 deinstitutionalisation focus does not resolve built environment gap

CRPD Art. 19 (independent living) drives community living policy globally (Japan, Korea, EU). However, moving people from institutions into inaccessible community housing does not fulfil the Art. 19 obligation. The CRPD COs address deinstitutionalisation through service provision, not through built environment design standards. The guidebook occupies the design-specification gap that treaty bodies leave unfilled.
