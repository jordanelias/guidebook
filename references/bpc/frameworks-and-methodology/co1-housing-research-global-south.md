## Cross-Slug Feeds — Updated Sessions 2–3

### → thermoregulation-built-environment
Global South housing for disabled people is predominantly informal/slum typology (ET/IN confirmed Session 1; ZA informal settlement Co-1 confirmed Session 2). These typologies have no passive cooling, no mechanical ventilation, no thermal mass design. SCI/MS thermoregulatory risk in these environments is extreme. China's massive household modification programme (967,000 units by June 2024) does not mention thermal performance — confirming thermal is an unaddressed gap even in the largest Global South accessible housing programme.

### → sensory-space-global-south
Communal living in informal settlements (shared rooms, shared facilities, household overcrowding) makes private sensory withdrawal impossible for NDV populations. South Africa informal settlement Co-1 (E15) confirms this: no private space at all, pathways are shared with vehicles, mud after rain. Japan's deinstitutionalisation focus (E27) does not address sensory accommodation in community housing settings.

### → wayfinding-global-south
India slum (E10): no ramps, no tactile, non-functional lifts. Nigeria Mubi (E13): access to goods and services identified as primary modification domain — confirms external wayfinding barriers prevent community participation even from home-modified accessible dwellings. Korea (E29): built environment quality determines wheelchair mobility frequency — not just facility presence.

### → bathroom-typology-global-south
South Africa informal settlement (E15): no accessible toilets mentioned, confirming the community toilet barrier is pan-African (India E10 documented; ZA E15 replicates). Mexico disabled social housing Co-1 (E21): restroom accessibility rated inadequate in government-built disability housing.

## Primary Cross-Slug Finding for Opus — Updated

**The formal/informal city divide is the meta-barrier.** All existing BPC entries specify minimum standards for formal, compliant construction. In the Global South, the majority of disabled people (who are also disproportionately poor) live in informal housing where these standards do not apply, are not enforced, and the construction typology cannot accommodate them without fundamental redesign.

**CRPD treaty body confirms the gap.** The CRPD Committee has issued Art. 9 accessibility recommendations to 100+ jurisdictions. All evidence from Indonesia (2022) and Nigeria (2024–25) confirms recommendations are not implemented. This is not a data gap — it is a structural enforcement failure confirmed at treaty-body level.

**One Global South exception at scale: China.** The 无障碍环境建设法 (2023) and CDPF household modification programme (967,000 units) represent the largest accessible housing modification programme in the Global South by volume. However: (a) Co-1 narrative is absent from indexed literature; (b) rural areas underserved; (c) pre-2023 buildings retrofit slowly; (d) thermal and sensory performance not addressed.

**The guidebook must determine scope:** (a) formal accessible housing in new construction (current scope — unchanged); (b) informal settlement retrofitting (new chapter/section needed); or (c) both with explicit scope differentiation.
