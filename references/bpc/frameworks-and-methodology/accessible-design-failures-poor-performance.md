### Scope
Documented accessible design failures, poor-performance cases, and post-occupancy regressions. Feeds Part 5 §5.3 (unresolvable conflicts register), Part 12 §12.x (methodology note), and connection register.

### Failure taxonomy

Five failure modes documented in evidence:

| Mode | Description | Primary evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Cross-population conflict | Design that serves population A creates barriers for population B | CS-17 (NHS CAMHS), CS-02 (Gallaudet corners) |
| 2. Compliance without adequacy | Building meets code but user experience is poor | Zallio & Clarkson 2021–2023; CS-09 (Hogeweyk critique) |
| 3. Programme failure | The building works; the programme around it fails | CS-25 (DFG waiting), CS-26 (Sweden 30-year persistence) |
| 4. Single-population over-specification | Design for one population excludes another via unintended consequence | CS-15 (Enabling Village operational limits) |
| 5. Post-occupancy regression | Building starts compliant; maintenance/operations degrade access | Zallio & Clarkson 2022b; ADA common violations list |

### Category 1: Cross-population conflict failures

**CS-17 — NHS CAMHS Inpatient Ward Design (UK, systematic)**
- **Failure:** Standard mental health inpatient ward design (communal areas, institutional lighting, stimulus-present environments) is systematically harmful to autistic young people admitted for NDV/AUT + NDV/MH reasons. No single building; represents a class of buildings across the NHS.
- **Evidence:** Published NHS audit literature; National Autistic Society advocacy; Rodríguez-Labajos et al. 2024 systematic review (notes autism-specific needs absent from most inpatient facility designs)
- **Population conflict:** NDV/MH design needs (social, sensory-rich, communal) ↔ NDV/AUT sensory needs (low-stimulus, predictable, enclosed)
- **Tier:** Tier 3/Co-1 (service user testimony + NHS audit + systematic review)
- **§8.5 relevance:** PRIMARY — paradigm case for unresolvable intra-NDV design conflict in shared inpatient settings

**CS-02 — Gallaudet University DeafSpace, Rounded Corners (US, 2005+)**
- **Failure:** Rounded corridor corners, specified for DeafSpace principles (eliminating sightline blind spots for signing communication), created collision hazards for MOB users (blind spots function differently for wheelchair navigation)
- **Resolution:** Post-occupancy correction — rounded corners replaced with glass corners providing sightlines without collision risk
- **Evidence:** Bauman (2015 TedxGallaudet); Studio27 Architecture documentation; NEA blog 2018
- **Tier:** Co-1 (architect self-report + community documentation)
- **§8.5 relevance:** Documents a cross-population conflict that was resolved retroactively — supports the case for design-stage conflict mapping

### Category 2: Compliance-without-adequacy failures

**Zallio & Clarkson (2021–2023) — Industry-wide POE deficit**
- **Zallio, M. & Clarkson, P.J. (2021).** Inclusion, Diversity, Equity and Accessibility in the Built Environment: A Study of Architectural Design Practice. *Building and Environment*, 206, 108352.
- **Zallio, M. & Clarkson, P.J. (2022b).** The IDEA Audit: A Post-Occupancy Evaluation Method to Help Design the Buildings of Tomorrow. *Building and Environment*, 217, 109058.
- **Zallio, M. & Clarkson, P.J. (2023).** A Study to Depict Challenges and Opportunities Building Industry Professionals Face When Designing Inclusive Buildings. *Architectural Science Review*, 67(3), 268–279.
- **Key failure finding:** 78.1% of building industry practitioners (n=89) have limited knowledge of POE tools for accessibility; 41.6% of clients request only minimum legal compliance; only 10.6% of clients seek future-proof inclusive design beyond physical accessibility (sensory/cognitive inclusion)
- **Failure mechanism:** Compliance ≠ adequacy. Standards calibrate to majority needs; minority populations (NDV, DEM, DEAF, OFS) systematically underserved even in nominally compliant buildings
- **Tier:** Tier 3 (Cambridge/UCL surveys, validated questionnaire)
- **§8.5 relevance:** Structural failure — industry-wide. Confirms that compliance-based design process produces a class of failures invisible to standard QA

**CS-09 — De Hogeweyk, "Truman Show" Critique (NL, 2009+)**
- **Failure:** De Hogeweyk's simulated normalcy environment (dementia village with actors and scripted activities) critiqued by disability rights advocates as "deceptive" and inconsistent with social model of disability. Addressed by Hogeweyk team in public response
- **Evidence:** International journalism; rights-based design critique literature; Hogeweyk team response documentation
- **Tier:** Co-1/Co-2 (advocacy critique)
- **Note:** Not a failure of built environment specifications; a failure of rights-based framing. Relevant to §8.5 as an "unresolvable" tension: therapeutic environment design for DEM vs rights-based autonomous living

### Category 3: Programme-level failures

**CS-25 — UK Disabled Facilities Grant (DFG) Programme Failures**
- **Failure:** DFG documented failures include: (1) average waiting time 20+ weeks for assessment-to-completion; (2) means-testing systematically disadvantages low-income disabled people; (3) private tenants face landlord refusal barriers not faced by owner-occupiers; (4) 2018 Parliamentary review found 53% increase in unmet need; (5) described as "clunky" by Parliamentary committee
- **Evidence:** MHCLG DFG programme data; House of Commons Housing Committee reports; Habinteg DFG analysis; RCOT advocacy submissions
- **Tier:** Tier 5/Co-2 (government programme data + OT professional body submissions)
- **§8.5 relevance:** Programme design failure. The building spec is adequate; the delivery mechanism fails the populations it serves

**CS-26 — Swedish Housing Adaptation Programme: 30-Year Persistence**
- **Failure:** Swedish government investigation (2015) found no evidence that recommended accessibility upgrade measures had come into effect. Slaug et al. (2025, *Disability and Rehabilitation*): accessibility problems documented in 1995 persist unchanged in 2025 — 30 years of non-implementation
- **Evidence:** Slaug et al. 2025 (30-year longitudinal); Swedish government investigation 2015
- **Tier:** Tier 3 (longitudinal study) + Tier 2 (government investigation)
- **§8.5 relevance:** Long-term programme failure. Documented design specifications exist; retrofit rollout fails systematically. Strongest evidence for Part 11 economics of non-implementation

### Category 4: Legal/litigation failures (US ADA/FHA)

**DOJ v. Days Inn / Howard Johnson (US, 1993–1997)**
- **Failure:** 15 newly-constructed senior living/hotel properties built after ADA effective date with systematic accessible design failures: inaccessible pedestrian routes, inaccessible parking, doors too narrow for wheelchairs, environmental controls out of wheelchair reach, inaccessible bathrooms/kitchens, inadequate visual alarms for deaf users, inaccessible signage for blind users
- **Source:** US DOJ Civil Rights Division press releases; archived ADA enforcement records
- **Outcome:** Consent decrees; remediation required; owners + contractors + architects entered agreements
- **Tier:** Tier 5 (DOJ enforcement records — primary source)
- **Pattern:** New construction failure — buildings designed after ADA compliance date were still systematically non-compliant. Demonstrates architect knowledge gap at time of ADA adoption

**Paralyzed Veterans of America v. Ellerbe Beckett Architects (US, 1996)**
- **Failure:** MCI Center sports arena (Washington DC) — wheelchair seating location and sightlines non-compliant; ADA required comparable viewing angles for wheelchair users
- **Source:** Mazumdar & Geis (2002) *Journal of Architectural and Planning Research*; DOJ enforcement
- **Tier:** Tier 3 (peer-reviewed case analysis) + Tier 5 (DOJ records)
- **Pattern:** Sightline compliance failure — wheelchair seating placed at front of arena (worst sightlines) while non-disabled seating used full stadium distribution; stadium-style seating was the central ADA litigation domain of the 1990s

### Category 5: Post-occupancy regression

**Common ADA compliance violations (US, documented pattern)**
- Buildings compliant at handover lose compliance through operations: sink understorage (wheelchair knee clearance blocked by storage items), lowered counter obstruction (bric-a-brac on accessible counters), grab bar obstruction (paper towel dispensers), exterior route icing at curb cuts
- Source: Swinter Group audit practice documentation; ADA National Network guidance
- Tier: Co-2/Tier 5 (professional practice documentation)
- Pattern: Design-stage compliance does not guarantee operational compliance; maintenance protocols required
