## 3. Frameworks Identified as Missing — HIGH Priority

### 3.1 Social Model of Disability
**Sources:** Oliver 1983; Shakespeare & Watson 2002; Barnes & Mercer 2005
**Evidence base:** Foundational to CRPD (which §1.7 grounds the guidebook in). The social model is the political-theoretical foundation of the disability rights movement. Validated through decades of disability studies scholarship and enshrined in international law.
**Current gap:** Not named anywhere in §1.4.8 despite the guidebook operating from a relational model (PEO). The social model does *political* work that PEO's *practice* work depends on.
**Integration target:** §1.4.8 — as framework #0 (political foundation preceding the four analytical frameworks)
**What it adds:** Explains why MOB-only codes are structurally insufficient (they encode medical-model assumptions about which barriers count). Grounds expansion to NDV, MH, PAIN, OFS populations.

### 3.2 Lynch's Imageability + Passini's Wayfinding Model
**Sources:**
- Lynch K (1960) The Image of the City. MIT Press.
- Passini R (1984) Wayfinding in Architecture. Van Nostrand Reinhold.
- Arthur P & Passini R (1992) Wayfinding: People, Signs, and Architecture.
**Validation studies:**
- Wiener & Pazzaglia 2021 (Cognitive Processing, PMC8545728) — systematic integration of cognitive psychology, neuropsychology, and environmental psychology into dementia-friendly wayfinding design guidelines. Explicitly connects Lynch's 5 elements and Passini's 3-step cognitive model to residual navigation abilities in dementia.
- Zali et al. 2026 (SAGE, doi:10.1177/19375867251391361) — first scoping review of wayfinding for people with sensory impairment. Framework grounded in Arthur & Passini 1992 and Lynch 1960.
- Van der Linden et al. 2025 (Frontiers in Dementia, PMC11931140) — Passini's spatial-problem-solving framework validated for dementia wayfinding case studies.
- Davis & Ohman (PMC5358323) — salient, colorful, personally meaningful cues at decision points help Alzheimer's wayfinding.
- Li & Klippel 2020 (Frontiers in Psychology, PMC7677306) — integrative review of 84 studies on interior wayfinding.
- Passini & Proulx 1988 (Environment and Behavior) — wayfinding without vision.
**Lynch's 5 elements:** paths, edges, districts, nodes, landmarks
**Passini's 3 cognitive steps:** (1) information processing, (2) decision-making, (3) decision execution
**Current gap:** D-category specs lack a theoretical model for *why* wayfinding strategies work. Specs are physical (tactile indicators, signage dimensions) without cognitive process grounding.
**Integration target:** §1.8 as new framework; D-category restructure; Part 3 §3.8 sequencing
**What it adds:**
- Population-specific failure analysis: DEM loses decision-making first; VIS loses information processing (visual channel); NEU/ABI may lose all three in different patterns
- Vocabulary for specifying decision points: "Does this corridor provide a landmark at the node?"
- Restructures D-category from physical signage specs to cognitive process support

### 3.3 Norman's Signifiers (expanding Gibson)
**Sources:**
- Norman D (1988/2013) The Design of Everyday Things.
- Gibson JJ (1979) The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception.
**Distinction:** Affordance = what the environment *actually offers*. Signifier = what the environment *communicates* it offers. Gibson didn't distinguish these. Norman did.
**Validation:** Norman's framework validated through 35+ years of HCI and industrial design research. Petrucci et al. 2024 (Frontiers in Psychology, PMC11212831) confirmed affordances are body-scaled and individually variable. Morgenthaler et al. 2024 (AJOT) used affordance theory to build environmental taxonomies.
**Current gap:** §1.8.7 is one paragraph conflating affordance and signifier. Grab bar shape is a signifier issue as much as an affordance issue.
**Integration target:** Expand §1.8.7 from 1 paragraph to proper treatment
**What it adds:**
- VIS: affordance of grab bar identical regardless of vision; *signifier* (visual contrast, tactile profile) is what fails
- DEM: lever handle affords opening; *signifier* may fail if handle looks decorative
- NDV: sensory profiles interact with signifiers differently — visual busyness is a signifier problem
- Separates "can they physically do it?" from "do they perceive they can?" — sharpens conflict resolution

## 4. Frameworks Identified as Missing — MEDIUM Priority

### 4.1 Salutogenesis (Antonovsky 1987)
**Sources:**
- Antonovsky A (1987) Unraveling the Mystery of Health.
- Golembiewski J (2010, 2012, 2017) — systematic application to healthcare, emergency, aged care, dementia care architecture.
- Rakhshani & Khakzand 2025 (HERD) — systematic review confirmed architectural elements support all three SOC dimensions.
**Three dimensions of Sense of Coherence (SOC):** comprehensibility, manageability, meaningfulness
**Current gap:** ART/SRT covers nature-restoration only. Salutogenesis covers the *whole environment* as health-promoting.
**Integration target:** §1.8 as new framework
**What it adds:**
- Comprehensibility → wayfinding legibility (D-category)
- Manageability → user control hierarchy (§1.4.4, C-category)
- Meaningfulness → dignity provisions (strengthens PEOP with health-outcome evidence)
- Reframes accessible design from "removing barriers" to "actively promoting health"

### 4.2 ASPECTSS Spatial Sequencing / Transition Zones
**Sources:**
- Mostafa M (2008, 2014) — ASPECTSS Design Index
- Mostafa M (2023) — expanded to 18 criteria
- Dordolin 2021; Limoncin 2021; Kinnaer et al. 2015
- 2025 sensory taxonomy (Tandfonline) — 6 design qualities: safety, sensory balance, adjustability, predictability, controllability, recovery
**Current gap:** A-16 is an *item*. Transition zones and spatial sequencing are a *synthesis principle* that belongs in Part 3 — tells you how to *arrange* items in space.
**Integration target:** Part 3 §3.8 decision tree
**What it adds:** Operationalizes Dunn's 4 sensory profiles into spatial organization. Answers "how do items interact in space?" not just "what should each item specify?"

### 4.3 ASA Deficits by Dementia Subtype
**Source:** PMC12739567 (2025) — novel framework
**Finding:** Different dementia subtypes have different bottom-up (primitive) vs. top-down (schema-based) auditory processing deficits. Alzheimer's loses top-down first; Lewy body has bottom-up primitive segregation deficits.
**Current gap:** Generic "DEM" acoustic specs (STI ≥ 0.60) don't differentiate by subtype.
**Integration target:** A-category acoustic specs; Part 5 conflict resolution
**What it adds:** Subtype-specific acoustic specification at Mode P; flags subtype as Mode S resolution parameter.

### 4.4 Sensory Design Quality Taxonomy (6 qualities)
**Sources:** 2025 Tandfonline taxonomy; 2026 ScienceDirect scoping review (11 environmental factors × 6 design qualities)
**6 qualities:** safety, sensory balance, adjustability, predictability, controllability, recovery
**11 environmental factors:** lighting, color, visual complexity, material, thermal, spatial configuration, acoustic parameters, sound source attributes, functional area odors, wayfinding, natural elements
**Current gap:** No cross-item quality vocabulary in Part 3. "Predictability" isn't an item — it's a quality that D, B, A, F categories all serve.
**Integration target:** Part 3 cross-item synthesis vocabulary
**What it adds:** Names what multiple items collectively achieve, enabling better synthesis assessment.

## 5. Frameworks Assessed — LOW Priority or Not Recommended

### 5.1 Soundscape as Active Design
**Sources:** Devos et al. 2019 (PMC6950055); AcustiCare RCT (NCT04809545); PMC12739567
**Assessment:** Expands A-category from defensive (noise control) to active (acoustic cuing). Evidence is emerging (RCT ongoing) but insufficient for specification. Flag for future research.
**Recommendation:** Mention in §1.8 alongside ART/SRT. Do not generate items until RCT evidence available.

### 5.2 Kaplan's Preference Matrix
**Sources:** Kaplan & Kaplan 1989; Stamps 2004 meta-analysis
**Assessment:** Coherence and legibility variables cross-validate Lynch and salutogenesis. Considerable heterogeneity in built environment evidence (Stamps 2004). No standalone value.
**Recommendation:** Brief mention alongside Lynch. Do not add as separate framework.

### 5.3 Proxemics (Hall 1966)
**Sources:** ACM 2024 wheelchair proxemics study
**Assessment:** Social comfort dimension of spatial specifications. Already covered by biomechanical frame (physical space) and PEO (relational dimension).
**Recommendation:** Brief mention in §1.8 note. Does not generate specifications.

### 5.4 Prospect-Refuge Theory (Appleton 1975)
**Sources:** Dosen & Ostwald 2016 meta-analysis
**Assessment:** Inconsistent evidence, especially for interiors. Already covered by ART/SRT and MH de-escalation room spec.
**Recommendation:** Do not add.

### 5.5 Neuroarchitecture
**Sources:** MDPI 2025 systematic review; ANFA
**Assessment:** "Limited practical application" per systematic review. Validated findings already captured by Dunn, ART/SRT, Competence-Press.
**Recommendation:** Do not add as framework. Individual neuroscience findings already flow through existing frameworks.

### 5.6 Embodied/Enactive Cognition
**Sources:** Jelić et al. 2016 (Frontiers in Psychology, PMC4815679)
**Assessment:** Theoretically rich. No disability-specific validation. Connects Gibson to Merleau-Ponty but doesn't generate specifications.
**Recommendation:** Do not add.

### 5.7 Matching Person & Technology (Scherer 1986)
**Assessment:** Validated for assistive technology selection, not built environment specification. Overlaps with Mode S/OT pathway.
**Recommendation:** Do not add.

## 6. Colour/Contrast Research (Confirming Signifier Theory)

LRV research confirms DEM/VIS conflict and validates signifier framework:
- VIS: ≥30 LRV points contrast needed (RNIB; BS 8300:2018; Equality Act Part M)
- DEM: ≤10 LRV change between adjacent floors (BS 8300:2009; Forbo dementia design guidance)
- Dark floors misperceived as holes by DEM = failed signifier (perceptual miscommunication)
- Insufficient LRV contrast for VIS = absent signifier
- Norman's signifier framework explains *why* the same physical surface communicates differently to different perceptual systems

Sources: Heckmondwike 2025; Realm Access 2025; Forbo dementia guidance; Grestec LRV guidance; Wilkinson (Architects' Journal 2020); Belroc Group dementia care interiors.
