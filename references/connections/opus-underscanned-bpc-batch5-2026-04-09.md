**Status:** PENDING

**Target item(s):** D-02, Part 3 §3.x
**Target population(s):** DEM, NEU/ABI, NDV/AUT, VIS
**Evidence tier:** Tier 3 (O'Neill 1991, Sweller 1988, Passini 1984)
**Filed:** 2026-04-09
**Applied:** —

**Connection:** The cognitive-science BPC establishes "≤2 decision points visible simultaneously" as a cognitive load specification. The detectable-gradient-protocol specifies multi-sensory zone boundaries (LRV change, RT60 change, luminance change) at every transition. Zone boundary signals occurring at a wayfinding decision point ADD to information load — creating the maximum cognitive load condition (simultaneous navigation decision + sensory transition). For DEM/NEU/ABI: exceeds degraded executive function. For NDV/AUT: sensory overload at the moment requiring focused attention. For VIS: contrast changes at junctions may mask directional cues.

**Evidence basis:** Sweller 1988 (extraneous cognitive load reduces available processing capacity); O'Neill 1991 (spatial configuration outperforms signage); detectable-gradient-protocol BPC (multi-sensory redundancy at transitions). The interaction is a direct application of cognitive load theory — never stated in either BPC.

**Action required:** Part 3 §3.x spatial planning principle: "Sensory zone transitions shall not coincide with wayfinding decision points. Zone boundaries to be located on single-route straight segments between decision points." Cross-reference D-02 ↔ detectable-gradient-protocol items. ○ marker (expert synthesis — no empirical study validates the separation rule).

**Disposition notes:** — Novel spatial coordination rule derived from combining two independent BPC entries.

### CON-0149

**Status:** PENDING
**Confidence:** HIGH
**Opus-reviewed:** true
**Source BPC slug(s):** stair-ramp-threshold-biomechanics-accessibility, luminance-contrast-lrv-evidence-base
**Target item(s):** C-04, B-items (stair nosings)
**Target population(s):** VIS, MOB, DEM
**Evidence tier:** Tier 3 (Harper 2022, Brown 2023, Thompson 2022, Templer 1992)
**Filed:** 2026-04-09
**Applied:** —

**Connection:** Stair descent fall risk = 3× ascent (Simoneau 1991); 6mm riser irregularity = +18% misstep (Templer 1992). 30% LRV is a regulatory floor with no empirical basis; ~65% Michelson needed for reliable surface boundary detection (Dain/Manandhar 2022). Harper 2022 bridges both: combined step-face AND edge contrast enhancement reduced fall events (OR 2.87). The highest-risk built environment feature (stairs) has the weakest contrast specification (general 30% LRV). Stair nosings should carry a SEPARATE, HIGHER contrast specification.

**Evidence basis:** Simoneau 1991 (3× descent risk), Templer 1992 (18% per 6mm), Thompson 2022 (30% invisible at moderate VI), Harper 2022 (OR 2.87 for enhanced contrast), Manandhar 2022 (65% Michelson for severe VI).

**Action required:** Stair-nosing-specific contrast: ≥65% Michelson (≥75% LRV difference), not general ≥50% LRV target. Width ≥50mm. Part 1 evidence exemplar: biomechanical risk directly driving specification above code minimum.

**Disposition notes:** — Strongest risk-evidence convergence for a single specification in the guidebook.

### CON-0154

**Status:** PENDING
**Confidence:** MODERATE
**Opus-reviewed:** true
**Source BPC slug(s):** floor-vibration-wheelchair-disability, residential-kitchen-and-task-surfaces
**Target item(s):** A-09, kitchen/laundry flooring
**Target population(s):** MOB (wheelchair), VIS
**Evidence tier:** Tier 3 (Larivière 2024)
**Filed:** 2026-04-09
**Applied:** —

**Connection:** Floor type is the single largest determinant of WBV in wheelchair users (Larivière 2024). Hard tile (common in kitchens/laundries) transmits maximum WBV. Wheelchair users spend extended time in one position during meal prep/laundry. Water-resistant resilient flooring (sheet vinyl with foam backing, sealed rubber) satisfies both water resistance AND WBV reduction. Hard tile is the worst material and should be flagged. VIS compatibility: plain colour, low pattern (per CON-0069).

**Evidence basis:** Larivière 2024 (floor type = largest WBV determinant). Kitchen BPC specifies task surfaces but not floor material. Laundry BPC specifies clear floor space but not material.

**Action required:** Kitchen/laundry flooring: water-resistant resilient flooring in wheelchair-primary spaces. Hard tile contraindicated. Cross-reference A-09.

**Disposition notes:** — Low-cost specification change with high WBV reduction benefit.

### CON-0155

**Status:** PENDING
**Confidence:** HIGH
**Opus-reviewed:** true
**Source BPC slug(s):** therapeutic-lighting-design, circadian-lighting-melanopic-edi, sensory-processing-model-design-application
**Target item(s):** B-01, B-06, B-07, A-16
**Target population(s):** DEM, NDV/AUT, NEU
**Evidence tier:** Tier 1 (circadian RCTs) + Tier 3 (Dunn model)
**Filed:** 2026-04-09
**Applied:** —

**Connection:** Circadian lighting requires ≥250 melanopic EDI daytime (DEM: reduced agitation). A-16 sensory room needs calming function during daytime use — low melanopic EDI (≤10, warm CCT ≤2700K). If A-16 is on the building's circadian lighting programme, a mid-morning user receives ≥250 melanopic EDI that contradicts the room's calming function. A-16 lighting must be INDEPENDENT from the building circadian system.

**Evidence basis:** Brown 2022 (≥250 melanopic EDI daytime target). Dunn model (sensory-avoiding profiles need lower input). Unwin 2022 (occupant-controlled dimming as primary A-16 requirement). Therapeutic-lighting BPC metric note: use melanopic EDI ≥250 as unified daytime threshold.

**Action required:** A-16 lighting: independent from building circadian system. Maximum melanopic EDI in A-16: ≤10 (occupant may increase). CCT: ≤2700K default. Dimmer to 1% minimum. No automatic circadian programme in A-16. Cross-reference B-01 with A-16 exclusion note.

**Disposition notes:** — Prevents circadian system from undermining sensory room function.
