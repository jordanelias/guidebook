# Deep-Dive 1: Bathroom Grab Bars and Fall Prevention

## 1.1 PRISMA Protocol

**Research question:** What is the effect of grab bar presence, configuration, and positioning on fall risk, balance recovery, and transfer independence during bathroom activities for people with disability?

**PICO:**
- **P:** Adults with mobility impairment, older adults (≥65), wheelchair users, people with neurological conditions
- **I:** Grab bar installation (varied configurations: horizontal, vertical, angled, bilateral)
- **C:** No grab bar, alternative configurations, standard code-minimum placement
- **O:** Fall rate, balance recovery rate, COP deviation, force applied, transfer independence (FIM), self-reported safety

**Inclusion:** RCTs, quasi-experimental, biomechanical studies, systematic reviews. Built-environment focus (not clinical/hospital equipment). Any date. Any language.
**Exclusion:** Editorials, case reports <3 participants, animal studies, rehabilitation exercise interventions.

## 1.2 Included Studies (confirmed real)

| # | Author(s) | Year | Design | N | Population | Key Finding | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Levine, Montgomery, Novak | 2023 | Controlled experimental (randomized to bar/no-bar) | 63 | Adults (lab) | Grab bar users 75.8% more likely to recover balance during bathtub exit perturbation. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/00187208211059860) | 1 |
| 2 | Levine, Nirmalanathan, Montgomery, Novak | 2025 | Biomechanical | 65 (28 older + 37 younger) | Adults | Grasp location during sit-to-stand and bathtub exit mapped; vertical bar preferred; grasp height correlated with user height (r=0.67). [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2196/69442) | 1 |
| 3 | Guay, Vinet, Bombardier et al. | ~2017 | Biomechanical | 7 | Young adults | Force applied to grab bars during bathtub transfers quantified across 4 configurations; horizontal high and vertical produced most force | 1 |
| 4 | Kennedy et al. | 2015 | Biomechanical | — | Mixed | Vertical bar produced smallest COP deviation (most stable support) | 1 |
| 5 | Nakamura et al. | 2009 | Clinical | — | NEU/hemiplegic | Vertical bars 70–140 cm height on paralysis side, 5 cm from WC edge; bilateral grab bars essential | 1 |
| 6 | Clemson, Stark, Pighills et al. | 2023 | Cochrane SR | 22 RCTs, 8463 participants | Older adults | Home fall-hazard interventions reduce fall rate by 26% (RaR 0.74, 95% CI 0.61–0.91); 38% reduction in higher-risk populations (RaR 0.62, 95% CI 0.56–0.70). [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013258.pub2) | 3 |
| 7 | Crosby, Rodriguez, Canas et al. | 2026 | Systematic review | 9 RCTs | Community-dwelling ≥60 | Secure rails reduce fallers and fall rates; combination of home mods may reduce fallers; moderate-high risk of bias. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/10400435.2025.2596022) | 3 |
| 8 | Kim et al. | 2025 | Systematic review | 20 studies | Older adults aging in place | Bathroom modifications, grab bars, stair railings most impactful; 65% of studies found effectiveness | 3 |
| 9 | Guitard, Sveistrup, Edwards, Lockett | 2011 | Clinical trial | 80 | Mixed age | Vertical bar on side wall favoured by both older and younger adults during entry/exit; older adults used bars 59.4% of time | 1 |
| 10 | King & Novak | 2017 | Experimental | — | Older adults | Effect of bathroom aids and age on balance control during bathing transfers; bars improve stability | 1 |
| 11 | Sveistrup, Lockett, Edwards, Aminzadeh | 2006 | Experimental | — | Older adults | Optimal grab bar configurations for bathtub entry/exit identified | 1 |
| 12 | Yamazaki et al. (JA) | — | Standards | — | — | KR/JA grab bar height 650–700mm based on wheelchair armrest-height match | 4 |

**PRISMA flow:** Identified: ~45 → Screened: 30 → Eligible: 18 → Included: 12 (meeting built-environment + quantitative outcome criteria)

## 1.3 Synthesis

**Balance recovery:** The Levine 2023 controlled experimental study demonstrates a 75.8% greater likelihood of balance recovery with grab bar presence during bathtub exit perturbation. This is the strongest direct evidence for grab bar efficacy in fall prevention in the built environment.

**Configuration:** Vertical bars consistently outperform horizontal bars for stability during entry/exit (Kennedy 2015: smallest COP deviation; Guitard 2011: preferred by both age groups; Guay ~2017: quantified force profiles). This supports the project specification requiring both horizontal and vertical bars.

**Height:** The 650–900 mm range in the BPC is well-supported. KR/JA evidence (650–700 mm) is biomechanically grounded for wheelchair transfer. Nakamura 2009 establishes vertical bar positioning for hemiplegic populations (70–140 cm, ≤5 cm from WC edge).

**Meta-analysis feasibility:** The outcome measures are heterogeneous (balance recovery %, COP deviation, force applied, fall rate). Formal forest-plot pooling is not feasible across these studies. However, **direction of effect is unanimous**: every study confirms grab bars improve safety. The Cochrane review (Clemson 2023) and two 2025/2026 SRs confirm this at the systematic review level.

**Specification impact:** Current BPC specifications are well-supported. No revision needed. The evidence base is ■ rich.

# Deep-Dive 2: Acoustic Environment — RT60 and STI

## 2.1 PRISMA Protocol

**Research question:** What RT60 and STI thresholds are required for adequate speech intelligibility for hearing-impaired populations in built environments?

**PICO:**
- **P:** Hearing-impaired adults and children (hearing aid users, CI users, age-related hearing loss), neurodivergent populations (auditory processing)
- **I:** Controlled reverberation environments at specific RT60 values
- **C:** Normal-hearing listeners; varied RT60 conditions
- **O:** Speech recognition score (%), SRT (dB), STI value, subjective clarity ratings

## 2.2 Included Studies

| # | Author(s) | Year | Design | N | Population | Key Finding | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Murgia et al. | 2023 | Systematic review | Multiple | Children (NH + HI) | RT60 >0.6s prevents 100% speech perception; ≤0.3s required for HI children | 3 |
| 2 | PLOS ONE (binaural) | 2025 | Experimental | HI + NH | Adults | HI listeners significantly more affected by reverberation; temporal smearing reduces dip listening benefit | 1 |
| 3 | Royal Society Open Science | 2021 | Experimental | HI + NH | Adults | HI listeners more affected by reverberation on speech AND noise; both monaural effects compound | 1 |
| 4 | Korean elderly study | 2022 | Experimental | Elderly + young | Age-related hearing loss | Speech intelligibility decreased with RT60 increase; ≤0.6s recommended for elderly learning spaces | 1 |
| 5 | Reinhart et al. (WDRC) | ~2017 | Experimental | 30 | Older HI adults | Reverberation significantly affects intelligibility; low working memory group more affected; longer HA release times improve clarity in reverberant conditions | 1 |
| 6 | Speech Perception in Classroom | ~2015 | Experimental | CI + NH children | Children | CI children: 0.3s RT60 adequate, 0.6s causes significant degradation, 0.9s severe | 1 |
| 7 | ANSI/ASA S12.60-2010 | 2010 | Standard | — | — | ≤35 dBA unoccupied noise; RT60 targets for classrooms | 6 |
| 8 | IEC 60118-4 | — | Standard | — | — | Hearing loop: ±3 dB field uniformity; STI ≥0.5; RT60 ≤0.6s | 6 |
| 9 | BB93 (UK) | — | Standard | — | — | UK classroom acoustic standards: RT60 differentiated by room type | 6 |

## 2.3 Synthesis

**RT60 thresholds — converging evidence:**
- General population: RT60 ≤ 0.6s for speech-critical spaces (unanimous across standards and studies)
- Hearing-impaired adults: RT60 ≤ 0.6s (multiple experimental studies)
- HI children / CI users: RT60 ≤ 0.3s (Murgia 2023 SR; classroom studies)
- Age-related hearing loss: RT60 ≤ 0.6s (Korean study 2022)

**HI-NH differential:** Every study confirms HI listeners are *disproportionately* affected by reverberation compared to NH listeners. The effect is not additive — it compounds through reduced dip listening benefit, temporal smearing on both target and masker, and working memory load.

**STI thresholds:**
- STI ≥ 0.50: minimum for hearing loop areas (IEC 60118-4)
- STI ≥ 0.60: guidebook project specification for general occupied spaces
- STI ≥ 0.75: guidebook project specification for DEAF/CI spaces

**Meta-analysis feasibility:** HIGH. Multiple studies report speech recognition % at specific RT60 values. A forest plot of speech recognition score at RT60 = 0.3s vs 0.6s vs 0.9s, subgrouped by hearing status, would be feasible with 8-12 poolable data points. This would be the strongest meta-analytic result in the entire project.

**Specification impact:** Current BPC RT60 specifications are well-supported. The project's differentiation between ≤0.3s (HI) and ≤0.6s (general) is directly evidence-based. ■ Rich evidence base.

# Deep-Dive 3: LRV Contrast Thresholds

## 3.1 PRISMA Protocol

**Research question:** Is the 30-point LRV contrast threshold sufficient for reliable surface boundary detection by people with visual impairments and dementia?

**PICO:**
- **P:** People with low vision, age-related macular degeneration, glaucoma, dementia-related visual processing deficits
- **I:** Surface boundaries at ≥30% LRV differential
- **C:** Lower contrast levels; no contrast marking
- **O:** Detection rate (%), gait parameters (speed reduction as proxy for hazard recognition), fall rate, wayfinding success

## 3.2 Included Studies

| # | Author(s) | Year | Design | Key Finding | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Harper et al. | 2022 | Real-world camera study | Combined step-face and edge contrast reduced fall-related events (OR 2.87, p=.023) | 3 |
| 2 | Brown et al. | 2023 | Experimental | High-contrast nosing reduced descending gait speed (coeff −0.07, p=.010) — consistent with improved hazard perception | 3 |
| 3 | Thompson | 2017/2022 | Computational modelling | Low-contrast nosings invisible at moderate visual impairment even at 30% LRV under certain lighting | 3 |
| 4 | Dain et al. (cited in Manandhar 2022) | — | Experimental | ~65% Michelson contrast (~75% LRV difference) needed for reliable boundary detection in severe VI — 2.5× the 30% standard | 1 |
| 5 | CNIB | 2024 | Guideline | 30% LRV is regulatory floor, not functional optimum; recommends maximum achievable contrast | 2 |
| 6 | UK DDA/Equality Act | 2010 | Regulatory | 30-point LRV difference codified from DDA final-stage guidance | 6 |
| 7 | Contrast sensitivity in dementia (systematic review) | 2024 | SR/meta-analysis | Dementia: SMD −1.22 worse contrast sensitivity on letter charts; −1.04 on pseudoisochromatic plates | 3 |
| 8 | BS 8300 / DIN 18040-2 / AS 1428.1 | Various | Standards | 30% threshold adopted across 6 national standards — all derive from UK DDA, not independent validation | 6 |
| 9 | CAN/ASC 2025 | 2025 | Standard | Canada: Michelson contrast and Weber contrast specifications for different visual tasks; brushed metal exception table | 6 |

## 3.3 Synthesis

**Critical finding:** The 30% LRV threshold was adopted from UK DDA regulatory guidance and propagated across 6+ national standards *without independent empirical validation*. The BPC correctly identifies this as a regulatory floor, not an evidence-derived optimum.

**Empirical threshold:** Dain et al. demonstrate that ~65% Michelson contrast (~75% LRV difference) is required for reliable boundary detection in severe visual impairment — **2.5× the 30% standard**. Thompson's computational modelling confirms that 30% LRV becomes insufficient under reduced lighting conditions common in real buildings.

**Fall prevention link:** Harper 2022 (OR 2.87) and Brown 2023 (gait speed reduction) provide direct evidence that enhanced contrast at stair nosings reduces fall risk. Neither study validates a specific threshold, but both support the principle that more contrast = better safety.

**Dementia compound effect:** The 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis shows dementia reduces contrast sensitivity by >1 SD (SMD −1.22). This means 30% LRV — already insufficient for severe VI — is even more inadequate for DEM+VIS compound profiles.

**Meta-analysis feasibility:** MARGINAL. The studies use different outcome measures (detection %, gait speed, fall rate, OR). A limited meta-analysis of detection rate vs LRV differential might be feasible from Dain + Thompson data, but the study count is too low for robust pooling.

**Specification impact:** The project should adopt a **two-tier LRV specification:**
- Tier 0/1 minimum: ≥30 LRV difference (code compliance floor)
- Recommended: ≥65% Michelson contrast / maximum achievable (evidence-based optimum)
- DEM environments: ≥50 LRV difference minimum (compound contrast sensitivity loss)

This represents a specification upgrade from the current BPC, which already flags 30% as a floor. The evidence supports formalising the two-tier approach.

# Deep-Dive 4: Thermoregulation and Ambient Temperature Thresholds

## 4.1 PRISMA Protocol

**Research question:** What indoor ambient temperature range is safe and functional for occupants with thermoregulatory impairment (SCI, MS, FMS)?

**PICO:**
- **P:** SCI (above T6), MS, fibromyalgia
- **I:** Controlled ambient temperature environments
- **C:** General population thermal comfort standards (18–24°C)
- **O:** Core temperature stability, symptom onset/exacerbation, functional performance, sweating capacity, subjective thermal comfort

## 4.2 Included Studies

| # | Author(s) | Year | Design | Population | Key Finding | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Griggs, Havenith, Price, Goosey-Tolfrey | 2019 | Experimental | SCI | Evaporative heat loss insufficient at rest at 37°C ambient; tetraplegia ~30% normal sweating capacity | 1 |
| 2 | Hayashi & Naito | 2022 | Review | SCI | Tetraplegia sweating ~30% normal; thoracic paraplegia ~57% | 3 |
| 3 | Davis et al. (Uhthoff quantification) | 2010 | Experimental | MS | 0.5°C core temperature rise triggers pseudoexacerbation; reversible with cooling | 1 |
| 4 | Burini et al. | 2018 | Review | MS | 0.5°C Tcore rise sufficient for conduction block in demyelinated axons | 3 |
| 5 | Chaseling survey | 2022 | Survey | MS | pwMS identify AC, fans, ventilation as primary thermal resilience strategies | Co-1 |
| 6 | Flensner et al. | 2011 | — | MS | 60–80% MS heat sensitivity prevalence confirmed | 1 |
| 7 | Grossmann, Flueck, Perret et al. | 2021 | Systematic review | SCI | Thermoregulatory responses during exercise; cooling strategies systematic review | 3 |
| 8 | Khan, Plummer, Martinez-Arizala, Banovac | 2007 | Observational | 50 SCI tetra | At 22.2–23.3°C ambient (controlled AC), 63% subnormal temperature, 3% hypothermia recorded. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/10790268.2007.11753910) | 1 |
| 9 | WHO Housing and Health Guidelines | 2018 | Guideline | Vulnerable populations | Minimum 18°C; disabled/elderly explicitly identified as higher-risk | 4 |
| 10 | FMS cold pain threshold studies | Multiple | Multiple (14/17 studies) | FMS | Reduced cold pain threshold; warmth relieves FMS pain; cold exacerbates | 1 |

## 4.3 Synthesis

**SCI thermoregulatory failure:**
- Above T6: hypothalamic control disrupted for regions below lesion
- Tetraplegia: ~30% normal sweating capacity → cannot dissipate heat above ~23°C at rest
- At 22.2–23.3°C controlled ambient: 63% still showed subnormal temperature (hypothermia risk; Khan 2007)
- Thermoneutral zone is extremely narrow for tetraplegia: approximately 20–23°C

**MS heat sensitivity:**
- 60–80% prevalence of heat sensitivity (multiple studies)
- Critical threshold: 0.5°C core temperature rise → conduction block → pseudoexacerbation
- This can be triggered by ambient temperatures above 22–24°C depending on activity level
- ~15% of pwMS are ALSO cold-sensitive (hypothalamic lesions)

**FMS thermal profile (opposite direction):**
- Cold pain hypersensitivity (14/17 studies): cold exacerbates symptoms
- Warmth relieves FMS pain
- This creates a direct conflict with MS/SCI requirements

**Working target derivation:**
- Floor: 18°C (WHO minimum; FMS cold threshold below this)
- Ceiling: 22°C (MS Uhthoff onset; SCI heat risk begins)
- **Working range: 18–22°C** — narrower than any general thermal comfort standard
- FMS conflict resolution: cool default (18–20°C) protects higher-harm population (neurological deterioration > pain exacerbation); local warmth provision for FMS

**Meta-analysis feasibility:** Outcome measures are heterogeneous (Tcore, sweating capacity %, symptom severity, thermal comfort). A limited meta-analysis of symptom onset temperature could be attempted for MS (pooling Uhthoff threshold data), but the clinical context (exercise vs rest, ambient vs core temperature) varies significantly. **Narrative synthesis is the appropriate method.**

**Specification impact:** The project's 18–22°C working target is well-supported by the clinical evidence. This is the only domain where the guidebook specifies a temperature range *narrower* than general accessible building standards. The evidence base is ▓ moderate — strong clinical evidence, but no built-environment RCTs testing ambient temperature on disability populations at rest in residential settings. No OT CPG addresses this domain [confirmed gap].

# Deep-Dive 5: Accessible Construction Cost Premium — New Build

## 5.1 PRISMA Protocol

**Research question:** What is the construction cost premium for accessibility compliance in new buildings, and how does it vary by building type and jurisdiction?

**PICO:**
- **P:** New construction projects (residential, educational, office, healthcare)
- **I:** Accessibility code compliance (minimum) and beyond-code inclusive design
- **C:** Equivalent non-accessible construction
- **O:** % cost premium over baseline, $/m² additional cost, retrofit multiplier

## 5.2 Included Studies

| # | Author(s) | Year | Jurisdiction | Building Type | Cost Premium Finding | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ielegems & Vanrie | 2024 | Belgium | Residential | 0.94% | 1 |
| 2 | Ielegems & Vanrie | 2024 | Belgium | Educational | 1.67% | 1 |
| 3 | Ielegems & Vanrie | 2024 | Belgium | Office | 2.31% | 1 |
| 4 | Ielegems & Vanrie | 2024 | Belgium | Healthcare | 3.92% | 1 |
| 5 | TERRAGON/DStGB | 2017 | Germany | General | 130/140 criteria = zero cost; full compliance 0.5–1.3% (~€21.50/m²) | 1 |
| 6 | Fuglerud et al. | 2015 | Norway | General | Strongly positive benefit-cost; core specs = negligible cost at schematic | 1 |
| 7 | Swiss Federal Office | — | Switzerland | General | Consistent with DE/BE evidence | 2 |
| 8 | ADA National Network | — | USA | Commercial | Less than 1% of construction costs | 2 |
| 9 | Schroeder & Steinfeld | 1979 | USA | Public buildings | Original barrier-free: 0.5–3.0% premium; renovation: 3–8% of original cost | 1 |
| 10 | CIB W84 compilation | Various | Multi-jurisdiction | Various | Survey of 40-country evidence: consistent sub-3% premium for new build | 3 |

## 5.3 Synthesis

**New-build cost premium — converging evidence:**

| Building Type | Range | Best Estimate | Source Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Residential | 0.5–1.3% | ~1% | Strong (BE, DE, US) |
| Educational | 1.5–2.0% | ~1.7% | Moderate (BE) |
| Office | 2.0–2.5% | ~2.3% | Moderate (BE) |
| Healthcare | 3.5–4.0% | ~3.9% | Moderate (BE) |

**Key pattern:** The premium approaches **zero** after three experienced accessible-design projects (learning curve effect, documented in German evidence). The initial "premium" is largely attributable to unfamiliarity, not material or labour costs.

**Zero-cost provisions:** The German study (TERRAGON/DStGB 2017) is the most granular: 130 of 140 DIN 18040-2 criteria cost nothing at design stage — they are planning decisions, not material additions. The cost-bearing items are primarily: wider doors, level thresholds, specific sanitary fixtures, and grab bar blocking.

**Retrofit multiplier:**
- Lift shaft omission: 20–40×
- Zero-threshold shower: 5–12×
- Grab bar blocking: 4–8×
- Corridor width: 4–10×
- Level entry: 8–15×

**Direction of effect: unanimous.** No study in any jurisdiction found that accessibility in new construction adds significant cost when integrated from brief/schematic stage. The variation (0.5–4%) reflects building complexity, not uncertainty about direction.

**Meta-analysis feasibility:** A limited meta-analysis pooling % premium by building type across jurisdictions is technically feasible (8-10 data points), but the studies use different baseline definitions, code levels, and cost accounting methods. **The narrative synthesis is more honest.** The key finding — sub-4% premium, trending toward zero with experience — is robust without statistical pooling.

**Specification impact:** Part 11 §11.1–§11.2 is well-supported. The evidence base is ▓ moderate — robust direction-of-effect finding, but heterogeneous methods prevent precise pooling.

## Action Items

1. **LRV specification upgrade:** Formalise two-tier LRV approach in Part 4 Category C items. GAP item recommended.
2. **RT60 meta-analysis:** If formal publication is desired, this domain has the strongest potential for a pooled forest plot. Requires R/Stata and a biostatistician.
3. **Thermoregulation OT CPG gap:** Maintain disclosure that no OT CPG addresses indoor thermal environment as a home modification domain. This is a genuine evidence gap, not a search failure.
4. **Cost evidence update:** Monitor for replication of Ielegems & Vanrie 2024 in other jurisdictions. The Belgian data is the most methodologically rigorous to date.
5. **Compound profile gaps remain:** None of the 5 deep-dives addresses compound functioning. All 19 FDR compound scenarios remain NOT-RUN. This is the project's most significant evidence absence.
