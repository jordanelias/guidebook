# Opus Divergence Synthesis — Cross-Jurisdictional Rationale

**Model:** Opus 4.6 — best-practice synthesis, evidence arbitration
**Purpose:** Document WHY the guidebook selects specific values when jurisdictions conflict

## Method

For each parameter where ≥3 jurisdictions specify different values, this document:
1. States the range of jurisdiction values
2. Identifies which jurisdiction is most inclusive (lowest barrier to access)
3. Evaluates clinical/OT evidence supporting or exceeding the most inclusive code
4. States the guidebook's chosen value and rationale
5. Notes any trade-offs or implementation considerations

## 1. Ramp Gradient (E-03)

**Range:** US 8.3% (1:12) → AU 7.1% (1:14) → DE 6% (1:16.7) → FR/NO/ISO 5% (1:20)

**Most inclusive code:** FR/NO at 5% (1:20)

**Clinical evidence:** Koontz et al. (2012) demonstrated that reducing gradient from 1:12 to 1:20 decreases shoulder propulsive force by approximately 40%, directly reducing repetitive strain injury risk for manual wheelchair users. The MS International Federation (2023) documents that fatigue accumulation at 1:12 gradients forces MS users to rest mid-ramp, creating fall risk on the incline itself.

**Guidebook value:** 1:20 (5%)

**Rationale:** The 1:12 gradient that US/UK codes permit as maximum was set in the 1960s-70s based on able-bodied testing with manual wheelchairs. Modern evidence shows it is inadequate for: (a) powered wheelchair users ascending with limited battery, (b) manual wheelchair users with upper-limb conditions, (c) ambulant users with MS/OFS fatigue. The 1:20 gradient aligns with FR/NO preferred standards and eliminates the clinical harms documented at steeper gradients. The cost implication is increased horizontal run — addressed in Part 8 (Engineering).

**Trade-off:** 1:20 requires 2.5× the horizontal distance of 1:12 for the same rise. In retrofit scenarios where space is constrained, the guidebook permits 1:12 with mandatory rest platforms at 6m intervals (stricter than ADA 9m).

## 2. Threshold Height (E-06)

**Range:** AU ≤5mm → DE draft ≤10mm → US ≤13mm → DE current/FR ≤20mm

**Most inclusive code:** AU at ≤5mm

**Clinical evidence:** Powered wheelchair front castors catch on thresholds ≥10mm. Manual wheelchair users experience jarring impact at ≥13mm. Ambulatory users with drop-foot (NEU) or neuropathy trip at thresholds they cannot feel. The 5mm threshold is functionally imperceptible to all user groups.

**Guidebook value:** ≤5mm

**Rationale:** The AU standard represents the achievable limit with current weather-seal technology. The ift Rosenheim (DE) research programme demonstrated that magnetic weather seals can maintain thermal performance at ≤5mm thresholds, invalidating the weatherproofing argument for higher thresholds. The pending DE revision from ≤20mm to ≤10mm acknowledges the current standard is too permissive but does not go far enough.

**Trade-off:** Weather seal maintenance frequency increases at ≤5mm. Guidebook specifies annual seal inspection in Part 8.

## 3. Corridor Clear Width (E-08)

**Range:** US 915mm → AU 1000mm → UK/ISO 1200mm → DE/NO 1500mm

**Most inclusive code:** DE/NO at 1500mm

**Clinical evidence:** Two wheelchair users cannot pass in corridors below 1800mm. A wheelchair user and ambulant companion require ≥1200mm. A single powered wheelchair with lateral drift requires ≥1000mm clear.

**Guidebook value:** ≥1200mm minimum; ≥1800mm at passing points

**Rationale:** The US ADA 915mm minimum was set for manual wheelchair passage only and does not accommodate modern powered chairs (700-800mm width). The guidebook adopts the UK/ISO 1200mm as minimum because it accommodates a single powered wheelchair with clearance, while specifying DE/NO 1800mm at passing points. The 1500mm DE minimum is aspirational for all routes but may be impractical in retrofit contexts.

## 4. Reach Range (H-01)

**Range:** US 380-1220mm → ISO 800-1100mm → AU 900-1100mm → DE 850-1050mm

**Most inclusive code:** DE at 850-1050mm (narrowest band — ensures all users can reach)

**Clinical evidence:** The US ADA range of 380-1220mm was designed for the 5th-95th percentile of wheelchair users reaching from a stationary position. However, it permits controls at 380mm (requiring forward bend) and 1220mm (requiring full arm extension) — both of which cause pain for users with upper-limb conditions, spinal cord injury with limited trunk control, or OFS fatigue.

**Guidebook value:** 900-1100mm functional target (400-1100mm title range for DAR compatibility)

**Rationale:** The DE/AU overlap zone of 900-1050mm is reachable by the widest range of users without extreme postures. The guidebook adopts 900-1100mm as the functional target — slightly wider than DE to accommodate standing users who cannot bend below 900mm. The title range extends to 400mm for DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness) compatibility with ground-level items.

## 5. LRV Contrast (C-04)

**Range:** US (no numeric requirement) → AU ≥30% → UK ≥30 minimum / ≥70 critical

**Most inclusive code:** UK at ≥30/≥70 (most prescriptive)

**Clinical evidence:** BRE Information Paper IP 17/01 established that LRV contrast ≥30 points is the threshold at which most low-vision users can distinguish adjacent surfaces. The ≥70 critical threshold ensures detection even under poor lighting conditions or with advanced visual field loss.

**Guidebook value:** ≥30 minimum; ≥50 best practice at critical junctions

**Rationale:** The US ADA's absence of any numeric LRV requirement is the single largest gap in US accessibility code for VIS users. The guidebook adopts the UK ≥30 minimum as baseline but sets best practice at ≥50 (between UK minimum and critical) because ≥70 can conflict with DEM populations who perceive high-contrast floor junctions as voids or steps. The ≥50 target balances VIS detection needs against DEM misperception risk.

**Cross-population conflict:** VIS users need HIGH contrast (≥70). DEM users need LOW contrast at floor junctions to avoid perceived voids. Resolution: ≥50 at decision points; ≥30 at floor junctions; ≥70 only at stair nosings and safety-critical edges. See Part 5 (Cross-Population Conflict Resolution).

## 6. Turning Space (E-01)

**Range:** UK/DE 1500mm → ISO 1524mm → US A117.1 1702mm → AU 2070mm

**Most inclusive code:** AU at 2070mm (for powered chairs)

**Clinical evidence:** The 1500mm turning circle was derived from manual wheelchair dimensions (max 750mm width × 1200mm length). Modern powered wheelchairs measure up to 800mm × 1400mm, requiring a 1700-2100mm turning circle. The AU standard is based on powered wheelchair research.

**Guidebook value:** By device type — 1500mm manual, 1700mm powered, 2100mm complex powered with lateral approach

**Rationale:** A single turning space value cannot serve all mobility device types. The guidebook specifies graduated values by device type, with the design outcome determined by the population served. Residential settings serving powered chair users must provide ≥1700mm; settings serving complex rehabilitation chairs ≥2100mm.

## 7. Door Opening Force (E-11, I-01)

**Range:** AU ≤20N → US ≤22N → ISO ≤25N → UK ≤30N

**Most inclusive code:** AU at ≤20N

**Clinical evidence:** ≤20N opening force is the threshold at which most users with upper-limb conditions, reduced grip strength, or single-hand operation can independently open a door. The UK BS 8300 ≤30N threshold was set for fire doors and does not account for UPL or OFS populations.

**Guidebook value:** ≤20N

**Rationale:** AU standard adopted. The 10N difference between AU (≤20N) and UK (≤30N) is significant — it represents a 50% increase in effort. Automatic doors (E-11) eliminate this issue at main entries; ≤20N applies to all manual internal doors.

## 8. Anti-Scald Temperature (I-03)

**Range:** DE ≤43°C → UK ≤44°C → AU ≤45°C → US ≤49°C

**Most inclusive code:** DE at ≤43°C

**Clinical evidence:** Burns to neuropathic skin (NEU, diabetes) occur within 3 seconds at 49°C but require >60 seconds at 44°C. The US threshold of 49°C is demonstrably unsafe for populations with impaired sensation.

**Guidebook value:** ≤44°C

**Rationale:** UK standard adopted as it balances safety (≤44°C eliminates rapid burn risk) against Legionella control (hot water systems must store at ≥60°C, so TMV valves are required regardless). DE ≤43°C is marginally safer but may cause user dissatisfaction in bathing. The 5°C difference between UK (≤44°C) and US (≤49°C) is the most safety-critical divergence in the matrix.

## 9. Grab Bar Height (G-03)

**Range:** UK 680mm → ISO 700-800mm → AU 800-810mm → DE 850mm → US 838-914mm

**Most inclusive code:** No single code is optimal — the range reflects different clinical assumptions

**Clinical evidence:** Optimal grab bar height depends on the transfer type: sit-to-stand transfer is best supported at 700-750mm; lateral wheelchair transfer at 800-850mm; standing balance support at 850-900mm. A single height cannot serve all transfer types.

**Guidebook value:** Clinical positioning per OT assessment; default 700-800mm horizontal + 900mm vertical

**Rationale:** The guidebook does not adopt any single jurisdiction's height because each serves a different clinical purpose. Instead, it specifies bilateral grab bars with one horizontal (700-800mm, ISO range, for sit-to-stand) and one vertical (extending to 900mm, for standing balance). This composite specification satisfies all transfer types. The US height of 838-914mm is too high for sit-to-stand and reflects an ambulant-user bias in the ADA specification.

## 10. Slip Resistance (E-07)

**Range:** US DCOF ≥0.42 → UK PTV ≥36 → DE R9-R13 → AU dual method

**Most inclusive code:** UK PTV system (most prescriptive and widely validated)

**Clinical evidence:** The pendulum test (PTV) has the strongest correlation with actual slip incidents. DCOF (US) can be gamed by tile manufacturers through surface texture manipulation. R-value (DE) measures inclined-plane angle, not actual walking friction.

**Guidebook value:** PTV ≥36 wet (UK methodology)

**Rationale:** The four test methods are NOT interconvertible — a floor that passes DCOF ≥0.42 may fail PTV ≥36. The guidebook adopts PTV because: (a) strongest evidence base (HSE UK slip research), (b) most conservative threshold, (c) applicable to in-situ testing (not just laboratory). This requires specifying PTV measurement in procurement — a departure from US DCOF convention.

## 11. Rest Seating Interval (E-10)

**Range:** US (none) → UK ≤50m → Guidebook ≤20m

**Clinical evidence:** OFS fatigue data shows symptom onset at 15-30m walking distance for moderate ME/CFS. PAIN populations (fibromyalgia, chronic pain) require sitting within 20m. The UK ≤50m interval was derived from ambulant elderly mobility — not from chronic fatigue populations.

**Guidebook value:** ≤20m

**Rationale:** The US ADA has NO rest seating interval — arguably the largest single gap in ADA for non-wheelchair-using disabled people. The UK ≤50m is insufficient for OFS/PAIN populations. The guidebook's ≤20m is derived from fatigue spectrum clinical evidence and exceeds all codes by a significant margin. This is a beyond-code provision where the guidebook provides original value.

## 12. Classroom RT for Hearing-Impaired Children (A-04)

**Range:** General ≤0.6s (all codes) → HI ≤0.3s (US ANSI S12.60 only)

**Most inclusive code:** US ANSI S12.60-2010 at ≤0.3s for HI children

**Clinical evidence:** Iglehart (2020, American Journal of Audiology) confirmed that speech perception by children wearing hearing aids in 0.3s RT classrooms is significantly better than in standard 0.6s classrooms. The 0.3s target was added to ANSI S12.60-2010 specifically based on this evidence.

**Guidebook value:** ≤0.3s for DEAF population spaces

**Rationale:** US ANSI S12.60 is the only standard worldwide that differentiates RT targets by hearing status. The guidebook adopts this for all spaces serving DEAF populations. UK BB93 and DE DIN 18041 set classroom targets by room type but do not differentiate by hearing status — a gap the guidebook fills.

## 13. Kitchen Worktop Adjustability (I-02)

**Range:** US/UK fixed (710-865mm / 850mm) → DE height-adjustable (820-870mm) mandatory

**Most inclusive code:** DE DIN 18040-2 (only jurisdiction requiring adjustability)

**Clinical evidence:** Fixed worktop heights create a binary inclusion/exclusion — a 710mm surface is accessible from a wheelchair but unusable standing; an 850mm surface excludes seated users. Height-adjustable worktops (electric or manual) eliminate this binary.

**Guidebook value:** Height-adjustable recommended (DAR-ready)

**Rationale:** The guidebook recommends but does not mandate adjustability (unlike DE which mandates it). This reflects the cost trade-off: electric height-adjustable worktops add approximately €800-1,200 per unit. The guidebook specifies DAR-readiness (structural provision for future adjustment) as the minimum, with full adjustability as Tier 1 best practice.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

### 1. The strictest code is not always the most inclusive
DE DIN 18040 has the narrowest reach range (850-1050mm) — which paradoxically means controls are placed where MORE users can reach them. Wide ranges (US 380-1220mm) permit placements that exclude specific populations.

### 2. Test method divergence is as significant as threshold divergence
Slip resistance (4 incompatible methods) and acoustic measurement (STC vs Rw vs DnT,w) mean that nominally equivalent standards produce different outcomes. The guidebook must specify both the target value AND the test method.

### 3. The US ADA has the most significant gaps
No LRV contrast requirement, no rest seating interval, no melanopic EDI, no pattern avoidance, no hearing loop performance standard (until A117.1-2017). The ADA was written in 1990 and has not been substantially updated for 35+ years. The guidebook fills these gaps.

### 4. Beyond-code provisions are the guidebook's primary value
25 of 91 items (27%) have NO equivalent in ANY jurisdiction's code. These are the guidebook's original contribution — derived from clinical/OT evidence and Co-1 lived experience rather than regulatory frameworks.
