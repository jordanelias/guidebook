## Part C: GAP-077 LRV Two-Tier Specification Upgrade

### Current state
C-04 specifies ≥30% LRV difference at all critical junctions. This is a regulatory floor with no empirical basis for sufficiency (per BPC luminance-contrast-lrv-evidence-base.md).

### Evidence basis for upgrade
From DD-3 (LRV deep-dive) and BPC:
- 30% threshold derived from UK DDA guidance, not peer-reviewed research (BPC consensus finding)
- ≥65% Michelson (~75% LRV difference) needed for reliable surface boundary detection at moderate visual impairment (Dain et al., cited in Manandhar 2022, Tier 3)
- Step edge contrast enhancement reduced fall events OR 2.87 (Harper 2022, Tier 3) — based on PubMed PMID 36314061
- High-contrast nosing reduced descending gait speed (Brown 2023, Tier 3) — PMID 36314061
- CNIB 2024 explicitly states 30% is a minimum regulatory floor, not a functional optimum (Co-1)
- ≥50% LRV recommended by NIHR-funded review and consistent with US Access Board guidance

### Proposed specification (for ISW pass)

**C-04 — LRV Contrast at All Critical Junctions**

**Universal Mode (code compliance minimum):** ≥30% LRV difference between adjacent surfaces at all critical junctions, circulation boundaries, and hazard locations. ●

**Tier 1 (guidebook best-practice target):** ≥50% LRV difference at all critical junctions, zone boundaries, and wayfinding decision points. Contrast strip width ≥50mm. ○ [evidence supports direction but specific threshold not validated by RCT]

**Stair nosings and platform edges (enhanced specification per CON-0161):** ≥65% Michelson contrast (approximately ≥75% LRV difference). Width ≥50mm. This is the highest-risk feature in the built environment (stair descent fall risk = 3× ascent, Simoneau 1991). The general 30% LRV floor is empirically insufficient at this location. ○

**Mode S:** OT assessment resolves specific contrast levels based on individual visual profile. The 30% regulatory floor may be substantially insufficient for the most severely impaired users.

**Evidence disclosure:** The 30% LRV minimum adopted by six national standards (UK BS 8300, DIN 18040-2, CEREMA France, Swedish BFS, Norwegian TEK17, Japanese MLIT) derives from UK Disability Discrimination Act guidance. No peer-reviewed study has validated 30% as sufficient for people with visual impairment. Emerging evidence suggests it is substantially insufficient for moderate-to-severe VI. This guidebook recommends exceeding the 30% floor wherever achievable.

## Part D: DD-8 Realist Review Protocol Design

### Protocol for external execution

**Title:** What built-environment features work, for whom, in what circumstances, and through what mechanisms? A realist review of disability-inclusive architectural design.

**Background:** The guidebook's central question — how should buildings be designed to support people with different disability profiles in different contexts — is inherently a realist question (Pawson & Tilley 1997). No existing review addresses this across the full population and typology scope of the guidebook.

**Method:** Realist synthesis following RAMESES quality standards (Wong et al. 2013).

### Initial Programme Theory (from guidebook structure)

**Context-Mechanism-Outcome configurations (candidate):**

1. **C:** Occupant has mobility impairment + bathroom is designed with grab bars at biomechanically correct height → **M:** Occupant can execute transfer using trained technique → **O:** Reduced fall rate (Levine 2023: 75.8% more likely to recover balance)

2. **C:** Occupant is hearing-impaired + room has RT60 ≤0.3s → **M:** Temporal fine structure of speech preserved; dip-listening benefit maintained → **O:** Speech perception ≥95% (Iglehart 2020)

3. **C:** Occupant is neurodivergent + environment provides user-controlled sensory inputs → **M:** Self-regulation capacity maintained; sensory overload prevented → **O:** Sustained participation (Unwin 2022/2023; Watchorn 2025)

4. **C:** Occupant has dementia + building uses loop floor plan with visible toilets → **M:** Spatial recognition replaces recall-based wayfinding → **O:** 94% wayfinding success vs 34% in non-loop layout (De Hogeweyk POE)

5. **C:** Building is designed with accessibility from brief stage → **M:** Accessibility provisions are planning decisions (zero marginal cost) rather than additions → **O:** 0.94-3.92% cost premium vs 10-40× retrofit (Ielegems 2024)

6. **C:** Occupant has co-occurring conditions (e.g., MOB+PAIN) → **M:** Individual specifications may conflict; compound functional impact is supra-additive → **O:** Resolution requires Mode S OT co-design; Tier 1 pairwise resolution is necessary but insufficient

### Search strategy
- **Theory-driven:** Search for evidence that confirms, refutes, or refines each CMO configuration
- **Databases:** PubMed, CINAHL, Scopus, Avery Index to Architectural Periodicals, J-STAGE
- **Grey literature:** Standards bodies (ISO, BSI, DIN, JIS), DPO publications, OT professional body guidelines
- **Forward citation mining:** From all Tier 1 sources in BPC files

### Scope limitation
This protocol is designed for execution by a research team, not within the guidebook project. Estimated effort: 6-12 months, 2-3 researchers. The protocol design is the project's contribution; execution is external.

### Value proposition
A completed realist review would provide the theoretical spine connecting all 125+ specifications in Part 4 to context-specific outcomes. It would answer the question the guidebook implicitly asks but cannot formally answer within its current evidence synthesis methodology: *does this work, and for whom?*

## Part E: DD-9 Co-1 Meta-Synthesis Protocol Design

### Protocol for MOB/DEAF/DEM lived experience synthesis

**Title:** Lived experience of disability-inclusive built environments: A qualitative meta-synthesis of Co-1 evidence across mobility, deaf, and dementia populations.

**Method:** Thematic synthesis (Thomas & Harden 2008)

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Qualitative or mixed-methods studies reporting disabled people's experiences of built environments
- Population: MOB (wheelchair users, ambulatory mobility impairment), DEAF (Deaf/hard of hearing), DEM (people with dementia + carers)
- Setting: Residential, public buildings, workplaces, healthcare
- Date: 2010-2026

**Search terms:**
```
(wheelchair OR "mobility impairment" OR deaf OR "hard of hearing" OR dementia OR "cognitive impairment")
AND (built environment OR housing OR home OR building OR architecture OR design)
AND (experience OR perspective OR perception OR narrative OR "lived experience" OR qualitative)
```

**Databases:** PubMed, CINAHL, PsycINFO, Scopus, Social Care Online

**Quality appraisal:** CASP Qualitative Checklist

**Data extraction:** Participant quotes and researcher interpretations relating to built-environment features

**Synthesis steps:**
1. Line-by-line coding of results/findings sections
2. Development of descriptive themes
3. Generation of analytical themes mapped to guidebook item categories (A-I)
4. Cross-population comparison: where do experiences converge (universal design) vs diverge (population-specific design)?

**Expected yield:** Based on BPC Co-1 inventory:
- MOB: 15-25 qualitative studies (housing adaptation experiences, wheelchair user testimonies)
- DEAF: 10-15 studies (DeafSpace narratives, Gallaudet documentation, hearing device user experiences)
- DEM: 20-30 studies (carer narratives, POE qualitative components, De Hogeweyk documentation)

**Scope limitation:** Like DD-8, this protocol is designed for execution over 3-6 months by a research team. The guidebook project can design the protocol and identify seed studies from the BPC corpus.

## Part F: GRADE-Equivalent Confidence Ratings

### Proposed framework

The project's evidence hierarchy (§1.5) is a source classification system. GRADE adds a confidence-in-recommendation layer. Proposed adaptation for architectural specifications:

| Rating | Definition | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **HIGH** | Very confident the specification value is correct | ≥2 Tier 1 studies with consistent findings; OR Tier 1 SR confirms; AND ≥3 jurisdictions confirm |
| **MODERATE** | Moderately confident; true value likely close to specification | 1 Tier 1 study OR ≥2 Tier 3 studies; AND ≥6 jurisdictions confirm; minor inconsistencies possible |
| **LOW** | Limited confidence; true value may be substantially different | Tier 4-6 only; OR single study; OR significant inconsistency across jurisdictions |
| **VERY LOW** | Very little confidence; specification is extrapolated | No built-environment-specific evidence; specification derived from adjacent clinical evidence or expert consensus |

### Application to deep-dive domains

| Specification | Current evidence basis | GRADE rating |
|---|---|---|
| RT60 ≤0.3s (hearing-impaired) | Tier 1 SR (Murgia 2023) + Tier 1 primary (Iglehart 2020) | **HIGH** |
| Grab bar bilateral, 650-900mm | Tier 1 ×3 (Levine, Kennedy, Nakamura) + Cochrane SR | **HIGH** |
| LRV ≥30% (general) | Tier 4-6 (standards convergence); NO empirical validation | **LOW** |
| LRV ≥65% Michelson (severe VI) | Tier 3 (Dain/Manandhar); computational + observational | **MODERATE** |
| Ambient temp 18-22°C (thermoregulation) | Tier 1-3 ×6 clinical studies; no built-env-specific study | **MODERATE** |
| Cost premium 0.94-3.92% | Tier 3 ×3 (Ielegems, TERRAGON, Fuglerud) | **MODERATE** |
| Sensory room ≥8m² | No empirical basis (NCC AU statutory minimum = 3m²) | **VERY LOW** |
| OFS built-env specs (all) | Tier 5 all provisions — extrapolated | **VERY LOW** |
| DBL built-env specs (all) | Zero Tier 1-2; Tier 4-5 practitioner guidance only | **VERY LOW** |
| Compound functioning resolution | Zero primary studies; clinical reasoning + harm asymmetry | **VERY LOW** |

### Implementation plan
Apply GRADE ratings to all ~125 Part 4 item specifications during the evidence-marker pass. Each specification gets: ● or ○ (existing marker) + GRADE rating (HIGH/MODERATE/LOW/VERY LOW). This transforms the ● / ○ binary into a four-level confidence disclosure.

Estimated effort: 3-4 sessions (1 session per ~35 items, cross-referencing BPC evidence basis).
