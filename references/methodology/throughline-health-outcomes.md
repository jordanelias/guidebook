**The convergence finding:** Low-cost modifications ($500-$1,300 per person) consistently produce 9-10× returns across NZ (RCT) and US (quasi-experimental) data. This cross-jurisdictional convergence strengthens confidence beyond what either study alone provides.

**The curb-cut effect (Chandola 2022):** External housing modifications (ramps, widened doorways, automatic doors, parking, lift) benefit even people WITHOUT mobility impairments: 6% reduction in social isolation (95% CI 5-7%) and 4% reduction in need to move (95% CI 2-5%). This is the strongest empirical evidence for universal benefit of accessible design in residential housing.

**Methodological note — targeted vs untargeted interventions:** The OTIS RCT (Cockayne et al. 2021, UK, NIHR HTA, pragmatic RCT, n=2,300+) found **no evidence** that OT-delivered home assessment and modification reduced falls in **general-population** adults 65+. This null result is consistent with Lektip et al. 2023 (PeerJ, SR+MA of 12 RCTs), which found only a 7% falls reduction (RR 0.93) when including general-population studies. The distinction is critical:

- **Targeted programmes** (high-risk fallers, previous fall history, functional limitation): Clemson SR 21% reduction, Keall 26-31%, CAPABLE 10:1 BCR
- **Untargeted general-population programmes**: OTIS null, Lektip 7%

The BCR calculations in this project (Keall 9:1, CAPABLE 10:1) are valid because they represent targeted programmes. Part 11 §11.3 health evidence should be framed as applying to targeted interventions, not universal screening of the general population.

## Aging-in-place intersection

Aging-in-place reframes accessibility for a vendor audience because:

**Demand structure shifts from niche to mainstream.** Disability-only framing addresses ~15–27% of populations across project jurisdictions. Aging-in-place framing addresses the cohort currently 50+ planning their last housing transition (consistently 30–45% of household-forming adults across developed-economy jurisdictions [verify per jurisdiction]), plus their adult children buying homes that can accommodate a parent. The product becomes "a home that works at every life stage."

**Holding-period economics change.** A purchaser planning to age in place is buying 25–40 year tenure rather than 7–10 year tenure. They will pay a premium for features they will actually use. Hedonic studies on energy-efficiency premiums show this pattern: features valued for long-term use earn premiums; features valued only at sale don't.

**Move-up vs age-in-place purchaser segmentation.** The mainstream residential market has distinguished move-up buyers from first-time buyers for decades. The aging-in-place segment is functionally a third buyer type with distinct preferences (single-level living, accessible, low-maintenance, near services). Developers who explicitly target this segment can command pricing power; developers who treat it as a sub-set of move-up market under-sell.

**Caregiver / multi-generational household demand.** Census data across jurisdictions documents rising multi-generational households — fastest-growing household type in several OECD jurisdictions [verify each]. This segment has explicit accessibility demand for elder family members.

**Conversion potential as a sale feature.** Suite-conversion potential — basement legal suite, garden suite, secondary unit, granny flat, ADU, *Einliegerwohnung*, *Anbau*, *bijwoonhuis*, *附属住宅* — is itself an aging-in-place feature. It allows the purchaser to bring in a caregiver, generate income to fund care, or downsize within their own property. Intersects with current housing policy across multiple jurisdictions (as-of-right zoning reforms) and creates significant purchaser appeal at modest construction cost.

### 16.7 Aging-in-place lived-experience (Co-1)

**[Co-1 — Tier 3, lived-experience survey]**
**Wiesel, I.** (2020). *Living with disability in inaccessible housing: social, health and economic impacts.* Melbourne: University of Melbourne School of Geography / Summer Foundation. — Survey of 1,178 Australians with disability + 40 in-depth interviews. **74% of respondents living in housing that does not adequately meet their accessibility needs.** Documents social isolation, reduced employment, increased care costs, and mental health impacts of inaccessible housing. Co-sponsored by Melbourne Disability Institute and Summer Foundation. Submitted to ABCB as evidence for NCC minimum accessibility standards.

**[Co-1 — Tier 3, peer-reviewed lived-experience study]**
**Goodwin, I., Davis, E., Winkler, D., Douglas, J., Wellecke, C., D'Cruz, K., et al.** (2022). *Making homes more accessible for people with mobility impairment: A lived experience perspective.* Australian Journal of Social Issues, 57, 956-969. doi: 10.1002/ajs4.214. — Builds on Wiesel 2020. Priority modifications from lived-experience perspective: step-free external access, internal bathroom access, wider doorways. Accessible features perceived to lead to social inclusion, increased functioning and independence, and increased quality of life.

**[Co-1 — Tier 3, mixed-methods OT perspective]**
**Wellecke, C., D'Cruz, K., Winkler, D., Douglas, J., Goodwin, I., Davis, E., & Mulherin, P.** (2022). *Accessible design features and home modifications to improve physical housing accessibility: A mixed-methods survey of occupational therapists.* Disability and Health Journal, 15(3), 101281. doi: 10.1016/j.dhjo.2022.101281. — OT perspective complementing Goodwin/Wiesel lived-experience data. Step-free external access and internal bathroom access identified as key to accessible housing design. Home modifications are frequently needed but time-consuming to implement.

**[Co-1 — Tier 3, critical review]**
**Best, K. L., Noreau, L., Gagnon, M.-A., Barthod, C., Hitzig, S. L., & Routhier, F.** (2022). *Housing, Transportation and Quality of Life among People with Mobility Limitations: A Critical Review.* Disabilities, 2(2), 204-218. doi: 10.3390/disabilities2020015. — Canadian review linking housing, transport, QOL, and home-and-community-based services for people with mobility limitations.

**Co-1 network note:** The Wiesel→Goodwin→Wellecke→Winkler cluster forms a coherent Australian Co-1 evidence network spanning user, clinician, and practitioner perspectives. This network comprehensively resolves GAP-ECON-02 (Co-1 lived-experience economic evidence).
