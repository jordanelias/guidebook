## The question a designer arrives with
"Carpet softens reverberation and footfall — and it's cheap and comfortable. So why is the item literally named *'where VIS-navigation is maintained'*? Because carpet is the clearest case in the whole set where **one technique serves one functional capacity by harming another** — and best practice is the honest adjudication, not the carpet."

Unlike A-02 (where the naive move backfires acoustically), A-05's tension is *between capacities*. It is a genuinely conditional technique.

---

## 1 · What carpet actually delivers (performance)

| Effect | Magnitude | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Reverberation absorption | modest — NRC ~0.15–0.55 by pile/pad | **supplementary**, not primary; the ceiling (A-02) does the heavy lifting |
| Impact / footfall noise | meaningful reduction | carpet's genuine acoustic strength is *impact* sound, not airborne absorption |
| Whole-body vibration (WBV) | lower than hard floors via underlay damping | floor type is the single largest WBV determinant (Larivière et al. 2021, REF-00398, T3) |

Carpet is therefore not an alternative to ceiling treatment for RT60/STI — it is a *supplementary* absorber whose distinctive value is footfall and vibration, and whose cost lands on other capacities.

## 2 · The adjudication — one technique, four capacities, opposite signs

This is the spine of the record. Carpet's sign flips depending on which functional capacity you're serving:

| Functional capacity | Carpet's effect | Who it lands on | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Auditory clarity** under reverberation | **+ benefit** (supplementary absorption + footfall) | Deaf/HoH · autism · dementia | established; carpet modest vs ceiling |
| **Visual wayfinding** | **− harm *if patterned*** — busy / high-contrast patterns are misperceived as steps, holes, water; in dementia, swirls read as snakes, block patterns as crevasses to fall into | low-vision · dementia | strong design-guidance consensus (Tier 2–3) |
| **Mobility — comfort/pain (WBV)** | **+ benefit** — carpet-on-underlay is a *low-vibration* surface (damping); low-pile carpet groups with rubber as low-intensity | wheelchair — pain/spinal; the clean win is **powered / attendant-pushed** users | T3 (Larivière 2021; Misch & Sprigle 2022) |
| **Mobility — propulsion** | **− cost** — higher rolling resistance, greater upper-limb load, shorter stroke | manual wheelchair users | T3 rolling-resistance + **T1 upper-limb overuse (Sawatzky 2015)** |
| **Air quality / infection** | **− cost** — retains particulates, allergens, moisture, pathogens; harder to clean | MCAS/allergy · immunocompromised · clinical | CDC *Environmental Infection Control in Health-Care Facilities* |

A **manual** wheelchair user carries the WBV benefit *and* the propulsion cost at once — so even within one person the sign is split. There is no setting where carpet is unconditionally best practice. The design act is choosing **whether**, **where**, and **which carpet** so the served capacity outweighs the harmed ones — and mitigating the harms.

*(On the vibration figures: floor **type** is the largest WBV determinant — Larivière 2021; surface *roughness* alone spans up to ~8× across floor types (Chadefaux 2023) — that spread is across floor types generally, not a carpet-vs-hard-floor delta. Carpet's benefit is the underlay's damping, a different mechanism.)*

## 3 · The technique — how to do it *right*, when you do it

1. **No busy pattern.** The visual harm is specifically *pattern*, not carpet. Use plain or very-subtle low-contrast field; avoid large-scale, swirled, or high-contrast block patterns (misperceived as level changes). This is the single most important move.
2. **Keep the wall–floor contrast.** Non-patterned floor *plus* a clear tonal contrast at the floor/wall junction actively *aids* dementia and low-vision wayfinding — you lose the pattern, keep the boundary cue.
3. **Low-pile, on resilient underlay.** Low-pile balances traction, trip-safety and propulsion; the underlay is what delivers the WBV benefit.
4. **Washable carpet tile in infection-sensitive settings.** Replaceable tiles (not broadloom), impervious backing — lets the acoustic/vibration benefit survive the cleanability requirement (CDC permits carpet in corridors/waiting rooms under a cleaning protocol; contraindicated in high-risk/immunocompromised areas).
5. **Adjudicate by dominant capacity.** Where manual-wheelchair propulsion effort or clinical infection control dominates, *don't* carpet — treat reverberation at the ceiling (A-02) and footfall with resilient hard floors (cushioned vinyl, rubber, cork).

## 4 · Products — manufacturer-neutral

| Option | Serves | Costs | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low-pile loop carpet **tile**, plain, impervious backing | acoustic · footfall · WBV · cleanable | some propulsion resistance | preferred carpet form |
| Broadloom carpet | acoustic · footfall · WBV | cleanability · propulsion | avoid in clinical / high-soiling |
| Cushion-backed vinyl / LVT | footfall · cleanable · low resistance | less airborne absorption | hard-floor compromise where carpet contraindicated |
| Rubber / cork | footfall · WBV · resilient · cleanable | cost | good middle path |

## 5 · Failure modes
- **Patterned carpet as a fall/agitation hazard** — busy or high-contrast patterns misread as steps/holes; in dementia, as hallucinated obstacles. The named reason the item is conditional.
- **High-pile** — trip risk at thresholds and increased propulsion load.
- **Broadloom in clinical / allergy settings** — infection and air-quality cost with no easy remediation.
- **Carpeting a manual-propulsion-dominant route** — trading a modest acoustic gain for a daily upper-limb cost the user can't opt out of.
- **Treating carpet as the primary acoustic fix** — it isn't; without ceiling treatment (A-02) the RT60/STI target won't be met.

## 6 · Functional-capacity map — honest winners and losers

| Population | Net for them | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Deaf / autism / dementia** (auditory) | **+** with plain low-pile | reverberation + footfall down; *provided* no busy pattern (dementia is also a visual loser if patterned) |
| **Low-vision · dementia** (visual) | **− if patterned / + if plain + contrasted** | the whole conditional lives here |
| **Wheelchair — pain/spinal, powered/attendant** (WBV) | **+** | lower vibration than hard floor |
| **Wheelchair — manual propulsion** | **− (and + on WBV at once)** | higher rolling resistance + upper-limb load |
| **MCAS / allergy / immunocompromised** | **−** unless washable tile | retention of allergens/particulates |

## 7 · Evidence & honesty trail
- **Verified this session / in review:** the visual-wayfinding harm of *patterned* carpet (dementia/low-vision design-guidance consensus — misperception as steps/holes/hallucination; non-patterned + wall-floor contrast preferred); the mobility trade-off (carpet higher rolling resistance / upper-limb load vs hard floors' higher vibration); and the infection cost — **CDC *Environmental Infection Control in Health-Care Facilities*** documents carpet harbouring bacteria/fungi, poor post-spill cleanability, and contraindication in high-risk/immunocompromised areas while permitting it in corridors under a cleaning protocol.
- **In-corpus:** Larivière et al. **2021** (REF-00398, T3) — floor type the largest WBV determinant; Misch & Sprigle 2022 — low-pile carpet a low-vibration surface; Sawatzky 2015 (T1, MOB) — manual-propulsion upper-limb overuse. *(The frozen MOB.md prose mis-dates Larivière "2024"; the corrected year is 2021.)*
- **DB gap surfaced:** A-05's stored population links are the acoustic set (NDV/DEM/PAIN/MH/OFS) and do **not** include VIS, MOB or MCAS — the very capacities this adjudication turns on (DEAF is absent too, logged GAP-053). This record reasons across them regardless, which *surfaces* the gap rather than inheriting it.
- **Grade:** carpet as a technique is **provisional / contingent** — there is no `stated` "use carpet" best practice. The best-practice *statement* is the adjudication rule (plain + low-pile + contrast + washable-tile + dominant-capacity test), supported by convergent Tier 2–3 design guidance and Tier 3 primary studies (upper-limb cost Tier 1). Held behind the banner.
