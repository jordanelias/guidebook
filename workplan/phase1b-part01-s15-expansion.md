### §1.5 Evidence Hierarchy

#### §1.5.1 Why This Guidebook Needs Its Own Evidence Framework

Three established clinical evidence-grading frameworks — GRADE, JBI, and SIGN — inform the hierarchy used in this guidebook, but none can be adopted wholesale. These frameworks were designed to evaluate whether a health intervention causes a measurable patient outcome. Built environment specification operates in a fundamentally different domain.

The unit of analysis in accessible design is a space, not a patient. A corridor width, a door handle height, or an acoustic absorption target is not an intervention administered to a person — it is a spatial parameter that mediates interaction between a person's functional capacity and their physical environment. Randomisation, the methodological bedrock of clinical evidence hierarchies, is structurally impossible for most built environment parameters: one cannot randomise occupants to corridor widths in a double-blind trial. The evidence base for spatial specifications is necessarily observational, quasi-experimental, participatory, or derived from ergonomic and anthropometric measurement.

The counterfactual is also different. In clinical research, the control group receives no treatment or a placebo. In built environment research, the counterfactual to an accessible specification is a specification that excludes — a barrier. The ethical framework governing this guidebook is rights-based, grounded in the CRPD, not equipoise-based.

Population heterogeneity is the design problem, not a confound to be controlled. Clinical hierarchies treat individual variation as noise. Accessible design treats it as the specification target. A range in this guidebook is not imprecision — it is the bridge between Tier 1 (population-level) and Tier 2 (person-specific co-design). See §1.4.5.

These differences mean that the evidence hierarchy below draws on GRADE, JBI, and SIGN while departing from each where the built environment domain requires it. A full crosswalk mapping every tier to its analogue in all three frameworks is maintained in the internal methodology reference and is available on request.

#### §1.5.2 The Hierarchy

Source prioritisation in this guidebook follows a tier structure. Where sources conflict, higher-tier evidence governs regardless of jurisdiction, language, or number of citations.

**Tier 1 — Occupational Therapy Clinical Research (Highest Authority)**

OT evidence is intervention-tested: specifications are derived from observation of actual people performing actual tasks in actual environments, with outcome measurement. OT evidence sits at Tier 1 because it is directly person-environment-occupation tested, calibrated to actual functional capacity ranges, and intervention-verified. *Primary Tier 1 sources:* CAOT (2018); JOTA (2022); COTEC/WFOT position statements; AOTA (2023); Housing Enabler (Iwarsson & Slaug, Lund University CASE; r = 0.74 reliability; validated across residential and institutional environments); RCOT (2019).

**Co-1 — Lived Experience and Participatory Design (Co-Primary with Tier 1)**

This guidebook treats the documented lived expertise of disabled people as co-primary evidence alongside OT clinical research. This is its most significant departure from clinical evidence frameworks, none of which grant co-primary status to experiential evidence. The departure rests on three independent grounds.

*Legal-normative ground.* CRPD Article 4.3 requires States Parties to closely consult with and actively involve persons with disabilities in all decision-making processes concerning issues relating to them. General Comment No. 7 (2018) extends this obligation to the design and implementation of standards and policies. A guidebook that claims CRPD alignment cannot relegate lived experience to the lowest evidence tier. Doing so would structurally ensure that the voices Article 4.3 mandates are heard could be overridden by any clinical study, however indirect its relevance to spatial design.

*Epistemological ground.* People with disabilities hold knowledge about their interaction with the built environment that is not accessible to researchers or clinicians observing from outside that experience. A wheelchair user navigating a bathroom transfer knows things about grab bar placement that a biomechanical study measuring force vectors does not capture — the accumulation of fatigue across a day, the fear of falling that alters movement strategy, the adaptation sequences that make a theoretically suboptimal layout workable. An autistic person in a sensory environment knows whether a specification reduces distress in ways that a sound-level meter cannot measure. This is not a claim of infallibility. It is a claim of epistemic access.

*Methodological ground.* For many built environment specifications, no clinical trial exists and none is feasible. The question "What is the optimal bedroom layout for a person with ME/CFS who spends 18 hours per day in bed?" cannot be answered by a randomised controlled trial. In these domains — and they constitute a substantial proportion of this guidebook's specification scope — lived experience is not a weak substitute for unavailable research. It is the only form of direct evidence that exists.

Where Co-1 and Tier 1 evidence agree, the specification is well-supported. Where they diverge, both are presented and the divergence is noted — neither automatically overrides. Where Tier 1 evidence is absent but Co-1 evidence exists, the specification proceeds with Co-1 as primary basis. Where Co-1 evidence is absent but Tier 1 evidence exists, the specification proceeds on Tier 1 alone with a gap flag noting the absence of participatory validation.

*Primary Co-1 sources:* The Kelsey IDS co-design methodology; Gallaudet DeafSpace participatory design process; Motionspot neuroinclusive design co-design protocols; patient-led research collectives including ME Association (UK) and Patient Led Research Collaborative.

**Tier 2 — NGO and Advocacy Organisation Best-Practice Guidelines**

NGO and advocacy guidelines are developed by and for disabled communities. They incorporate aggregated lived experience — a form of validity that clinical and technical standards frequently lack. Clinical evidence frameworks would classify these as lowest-quality evidence. This guidebook elevates them because DPOs are the closest proxy to aggregated lived experience when individual participatory research is unavailable. *Primary Tier 2 sources:* CNIB (Canada); Alzheimer Society / BrainXchange (Canada); DeafSpace Project, Gallaudet University (USA); HLAA (USA); The Kelsey IDS (USA); Motionspot (UK); NAS (UK); APF France Handicap; CERMI; ONCE Foundation (Spain); CAWaB; SgEnable (Singapore); KODDI (South Korea); DbI Nordic programme; Sense UK Collaborative (USA/UK).

**Co-2 — Occupational Therapy Clinical Practice Guidelines**

OT CPGs from CAOT, AOTA, RCOT, COTEC/WFOT, and OT Australia synthesise and interpret the Tier 1 evidence base, translating findings into practice recommendations. They sit below Tier 1 because they depend on the research they summarise, and at the same level as systematic reviews because CPGs typically conduct or incorporate systematic evidence reviews. Their consensus-derived recommendations are the primary translation mechanism between research and specification. *Primary Co-2 sources:* CAOT CPGs; AOTA CPGs; RCOT CPGs; COTEC/WFOT joint practice guidelines.

**Tier 3 — Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses**

In clinical evidence hierarchies, systematic reviews of randomised trials sit at the highest level. In this guidebook, they sit below direct OT research. The reason is indirectness. Most available systematic reviews synthesise clinical outcomes — fall rates, independence scores, quality-of-life measures — rather than spatial parameters. A systematic review finding that "grab bars reduce falls" does not specify optimal grab bar mounting height. The leap from clinical outcome to spatial specification requires the kind of direct person-environment-occupation research that Tier 1 provides. Where a systematic review does directly address a spatial parameter, it functions at Tier 1 for that parameter. *Primary Tier 3 sources:* Bettarello et al. (2021); Marquardt (2011); Koontz et al. (2012); Ulrich (1984, 1991); Petaccia et al. (2021); Wellecke et al. (2022); Sherrington et al. (2019); Räty et al. (2021).

**Tier 4 — International Standards with Evidence Basis**

Standards are not research. They are consensus-derived technical specifications. No clinical framework treats them as evidence. This guidebook does because built environment practice treats standards as the primary specification source — and practitioners need to understand where those standards sit in relation to the research evidence. Positioned below research evidence to prevent standards from overriding more direct population evidence. CRPD Article 9 (UN, 2006) — 185 States Parties; foundational international legal framework for accessibility. PAS 6463:2022 (BSI, UK). WELL v2 (2024). ISO 23599:2019. IEC 60118-4:2014+AMD1:2017. ISO 21542:2021. EN 17210:2021.

**Tier 5 — National Beyond-Code Frameworks**

National frameworks that exceed statutory minimums, encoding accumulated professional knowledge. Below international standards because they are jurisdiction-specific. BS 8300:2018 (UK); The Kelsey IDS (USA, 2023); ABCB Silver/Gold/Platinum Livable Housing (Australia); RHFAC v4.0 (Canada, 2024); NS 11001-1:2018 (Norway); DIN 18040-1/2/3 (Germany); BCA Universal Design Index (Singapore); BF certification framework (South Korea); SBi-anvisning 230 (Denmark); Esteettömyysopas / ESKEH (Finland).

**Tier 6 — Statutory Codes (Reference Baseline Only)**

Legal minimum. Design that meets code only is not best practice and in many cases produces environments that remain inaccessible to significant proportions of disabled users. Every specification in this guidebook is calibrated above code. See §1.2.

#### §1.5.3 Evidence Markers

Every specification sentence in Part 4 carries one of two evidence markers:

● (filled circle) — **Evidence-based specification.** The specification value is directly supported by at least one source from Tier 1 through Tier 6 that addresses this specific spatial parameter for this population. The tier of the supporting source is recorded in the BPC Key sources table.

○ (empty circle) — **Inferred specification.** The specification value is derived from clinical reasoning, expert consensus, analogy from adjacent parameters, or extrapolation from evidence for a different population. The basis for inference and the gap are disclosed.

An unmarked specification is an error.

The ● marker tracks whether evidence directly addresses the spatial parameter in question — not the tier of the evidence. A Tier 6 statutory code that specifies a minimum corridor width provides direct evidence for the parameter "corridor width" and receives ●. That same code does not provide evidence for optimal corridor width for two wheelchair users passing — a specification for that scenario derived by extrapolation receives ○.

Practitioners need to know whether a specification is evidence-based or inferred. The evidence tier is available in the source record for those who need it — but it is not the marker's function. The marker answers one question: is there direct evidence for this value, or is it an inference?

#### §1.5.4 Scope, Limitations, and the Guidebook's Relationship to Professional Judgment

This guidebook is a starting framework for professional judgment, not a substitute for it. Population-level specifications — at Universal Mode and Tier 1 — are necessary but insufficient. They establish the evidence-informed envelope within which design decisions are made. They do not replace the assessment of a specific building, a specific site, or a specific person.

At Tier 2, conflict resolution is the domain of the OT and the individual through co-design. The guidebook's Tier 2 role is to equip non-OT professionals with decision frameworks and structured approaches, not prescriptive answers.

The CRPD mandates obligations, not specifications. Treaty bodies identify what States Parties must achieve; they do not prescribe how built environments should be dimensioned to achieve it. This guidebook fills the specification gap that treaty bodies leave unfilled — translating rights-based obligations into spatial parameters that practitioners can implement.
