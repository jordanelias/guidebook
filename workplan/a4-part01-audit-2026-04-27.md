## 1. Audit scope

This audit covers Part 1 (Foundations of Accessible Design) prose against the two doctrinal rules enshrined 2026-04-26:

1. **Advocacy identity rule** — the guidebook is an advocacy project, not an authority; does not teach professional judgment, does not confer competence, is not a registered body or regulatory instrument.
2. **Specification language rule** — tier-appropriate constructions; blanket "shall be" retired; "DAR is mandatory" patterns must locate the mandate (in the Guidebook's recommendation, not in non-existent external authority).

The audit does NOT cover: voice-style violations unrelated to advocacy framing (those are routine prose-style work for any pass); structural/formatting issues (Part 1 §1.8 has line-break corruption — flagged separately as 🟢 LOW for typographic fix).

## 3. Flagged items

### F-01 · §1.2 · "operates above the code floor" · 🟡 MED
**Original text (line 50):** *"This document operates above the code floor throughout."*

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM. Voice-style §8 explicitly retires "operates above" in favor of "recommends provisions above."

**Proposed reframe:** *"This guidebook recommends provisions above the code floor throughout."*

**Rationale:** "Operates above" implies the document is a thing that does things — an active regulatory instrument. "Recommends provisions above" locates the speech act correctly: the guidebook makes recommendations.

---

### F-02 · §1.4.3 · "DAR (...) is mandatory at every tier without exception" · 🔴 HIGH
**Original text (line 98):** *"This guidebook applies a three-tier design hierarchy. DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness, §1.6) is mandatory at every tier without exception."*

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM (unlocated mandate). The Guidebook has no authority to make DAR mandatory. This phrasing implies external regulatory force that does not exist.

**Proposed reframe:** *"This guidebook applies a three-tier design hierarchy. DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness, §1.6) is treated as mandatory at every tier of this guidebook — not because external authority requires it, but because the construction-stage evidence is unambiguous (see §1.6 and Part 11)."*

**Rationale:** Locates the mandate in the guidebook's own voice (per voice-style §8.1 worked example). Acknowledges the absence of external force while preserving the guidebook's normative position.

---

### F-03 · §1.4.3 table · "Mandatory" column header (×4) · 🔴 HIGH
**Original text (lines 100–104):** Table column "DAR" with values "Mandatory" repeated four times — once per tier row.

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM. Same issue as F-02, replicated across the table. In tabular form the violation is concentrated and visible.

**Proposed reframe:** Column header `"DAR (this guidebook)"` with values `"Mandatory throughout"` (Tier 0/1/2 rows). Alternative: keep the tabular cell as `"Mandatory"` and add a footnote: `"Mandatory in this guidebook's recommendation; no external authority mandates DAR at any tier in any reviewed jurisdiction."`

**Rationale:** Footnote approach is preferred — preserves table density while locating the mandate. The existing table layout cannot accommodate full sentence reframes without column-width problems.

---

### F-04 · §1.4.7 · "DAR is mandatory above that baseline" · 🔴 HIGH
**Original text (line 174):** *"DAR is mandatory above that baseline, since future occupants will include people with disabilities whose specific needs cannot be anticipated."*

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM. Same pattern as F-02.

**Proposed reframe:** *"This guidebook treats DAR as mandatory above that baseline, since future occupants will include people with disabilities whose specific needs cannot be anticipated."*

**Rationale:** Three-word insertion ("This guidebook treats") fixes the mandate location.

---

### F-05 · §1.4.7 · "DAR remains mandatory" · 🔴 HIGH
**Original text (line 174):** *"DAR remains mandatory to preserve adaptability for future occupants and for changes in the current occupant's functional capacity over time."*

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM. Same pattern as F-02 and F-04.

**Proposed reframe:** *"DAR remains mandatory in this guidebook's recommendation, to preserve adaptability for future occupants and for changes in the current occupant's functional capacity over time."*

**Rationale:** Co-occurs with F-04 in the same paragraph; both must be remediated together. The "in this guidebook's recommendation" phrasing varies the construction so the paragraph doesn't read as repetitive.

---

### F-06 · §1.6 · DAR mandatory implicit · 🟡 MED
**Original text (line 218):** *"Building codes that do not require DAR provisions impose these multiplied costs on ageing homeowners and disability communities."*

**Flag:** None — this passage is descriptive and policy-critical. **No remediation.** Listed here only because it is the section where DAR's mandate weight is most heavily implied; the prose is already advocacy-aligned ("Building codes that do not require DAR" makes the absence of external mandate explicit).

**Disposition:** Pass. Use this section's voice as a model for F-02 / F-04 / F-05 reframes.

---

### F-07 · §1.9.5 · "this document governs" over statutory requirements · 🟡 MED
**Original text (line 313):** *"Where this document conflicts with statutory requirements, comply with the statutory requirement and document the conflict as a Tier 6 baseline limitation. Where this document requires more than statutory requirements, this document governs."*

**Flag:** AUTHORITY_CLAIM. Voice-style §8 permits "this document governs" for **internal document hierarchy** (e.g. Part 4 governs over Part 8 where they conflict). Using "governs" to describe the document's relationship to **external statutory requirements** exceeds that allowance.

**Proposed reframe:** *"Where this document conflicts with statutory requirements, comply with the statutory requirement and document the conflict as a Tier 6 baseline limitation. Where this document recommends more than statutory requirements, this guidebook's recommendation should be followed in addition to the statutory baseline."*

**Rationale:** "This document governs" claims authority over compliance choice; the Guidebook has no such authority. "Should be followed in addition to" describes the additive relationship without overclaiming. The reader retains professional responsibility for the final compliance decision (per §1.9.4 which is already advocacy-aligned).

## 4. Section-level commendations

Two sections of Part 1 already exemplify the post-2026-04-26 advocacy voice and should be used as anchors for any reframing work elsewhere in the guidebook:

### §1.7.3 — "The Guidebook as Design Response to the CRPD"
> "The CRPD establishes *what* states must achieve (accessible built environments). It does not establish *how* environments should be designed to achieve accessibility. This guidebook occupies the design-specification gap that treaty bodies leave unfilled..."

This passage locates the guidebook's role precisely: it is the technical content that gives external obligations material form. It does not claim authority; it claims utility. **Model voice for advocacy identity.**

### §1.9.4 — "This Guidebook as a Framework for Professional Judgment"
> "This guidebook provides a starting framework for professional judgment — not a substitute for it... Designers, planners, and regulators using this guidebook retain full professional responsibility for the environments they design, approve, or specify. The guidebook's provisions represent the best available evidence, synthesised across multiple populations and jurisdictions; they are not a guarantee of accessibility for any individual case."

This passage is the operative statement of advocacy identity in Part 1. It should be cross-referenced from §1.4.7, §1.6, and §1.9.5 once those sections are reframed — the disclaimer here covers the mandate language elsewhere.
