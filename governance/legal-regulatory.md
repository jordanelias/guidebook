## 0. What this document covers and what it does not

The guidebook is a synthesis of accessibility evidence and best practice. It is published into a regulatory environment where building codes, professional standards, and disability-rights statutes operate. This document specifies the project's posture toward that environment: how the guidebook positions itself in liability terms, how it discloses its limits to readers, how it is licensed for use and reuse, where it sits relative to formal jurisdictional adoption, and what questions remain for external counsel review.

The document does not provide legal advice and does not substitute for counsel review of the live disclaimer language in any specific jurisdiction. Counsel review is an external dependency named at A11 in `workplan/workplan-co0007-v3.md`. The framework itself — the doctrinal positioning, the disclaimer structure, the licensing approach, the trajectory decision, and the counsel review queue — is determined in this session. Specific operative phrasing of the front-matter disclaimer is provisional pending counsel review (§3.4); all other decisions are final.

The document covers all guidebook deliverables: Volumes I and II, the Supplementary Volume on Body Sizes, Appendices, Bibliography, Glossary, and any accompanying website or distribution channel. Counsel review is required per major release and on any material change to the disclaimer or licensing language.

## 1. Foundational legal posture: "teaches not prescribes"

### 1.1 The doctrine

The guidebook teaches accessible-design judgment; it does not prescribe specific legal conformance. A reader who follows the guidebook's specifications in a building project may produce a design that exceeds code in the relevant jurisdiction, that meets code, or — in rare specifications where evidence supports a parameter range that includes values below code — that fails code. The guidebook does not relieve the designer from code-conformance responsibility; it informs the designer's judgment within whatever code framework applies.

This posture is the mission's L139 statement made operative: "not a legal authority. It does not replace jurisdictional code compliance (which is Tier 6 — the floor)." The teaches-not-prescribes formulation captures the same boundary in liability terms. The guidebook's specifications are pedagogical: they educate the practitioner about evidence-based ranges, convergence and divergence in the literature, population-specific needs, and the synthesis between Co-1 evidence and Tier 1–6 sources. They are not contractual specifications. They are not standard-of-care declarations. They are not jurisdictional substitutes.

### 1.2 What this means in practice

A practitioner consulting the guidebook for a corridor-width specification reads: a code-floor value (Tier 6), a best-practice range with convergence assessment (Tier 1–5 synthesis), the populations the value supports, and the divergent positions where they exist. The practitioner then exercises professional judgment to select a value appropriate to the project, the jurisdiction, and the people the building serves. The guidebook supports that judgment; it does not replace it.

A reader using the guidebook to prepare a brief, evaluate a competitor's design, advocate for accessibility in their own home, or argue in a planning consultation does so within their own context. The guidebook offers the evidence; the application is theirs. The guidebook's authors do not warrant that any specific application is correct, complete, or compliant with any specific legal framework.

### 1.3 What this is not

The teaches-not-prescribes posture is not an abdication. It does not mean the guidebook is "just opinions" or that its specifications are equivalent to other claims with weaker evidence. The guidebook's evidence-state machine (A6) records convergence assessments per cell; the seven-tier evidence hierarchy (A6) ranks source strength; the Co-1 priority (A5) recognises lived-experience evidence as co-primary with Tier 1. Within those structures, specifications are well-grounded recommendations carrying the warrant of the synthesis behind them. Teaches-not-prescribes describes the legal relationship between the guidebook and the reader's design decisions, not the epistemic strength of the specifications themselves.

Equally, teaches-not-prescribes is not an argument that nothing in the guidebook is mandatory. Where statutory codes apply, code values are mandatory in their jurisdictions. The guidebook's role in those cases is to surface the code value (Tier 6, the floor) alongside any evidence-based value that exceeds it. Mandatory-by-law content remains mandatory regardless of how the guidebook teaches it.

## 2. Liability framing

### 2.1 Author posture

The guidebook is authored as best-effort synthesis under explicit methodological constraints declared throughout the project (mission-and-epistemics §Operational reality; A5 §1 pre-launch solo authorship; A6 evidence-state machine; A7 population-as-organising-principle). The author's claim is that the synthesis is faithful to the evidence engaged, that limits are declared, and that gaps and divergences are surfaced rather than hidden. The author's claim is not that the synthesis is exhaustive, that all relevant evidence has been engaged, or that any specification is correct for any specific design context.

Reader use is therefore constructed-as-informed: a reader engaging the guidebook is presumed to read the methodology, observe the evidence-state declarations, and apply professional judgment in their context. Liability framing follows this construction: the guidebook supplies information faithfully synthesised; downstream application is the reader's responsibility.

### 2.2 Risks the framework addresses

The framework is designed to address four identifiable liability vectors:

**Misuse-of-recommendation risk.** A reader follows a guidebook recommendation; the resulting design produces harm or fails to meet a contextual need; the reader cites the guidebook as authority for the design choice. The framework's mitigation: front-matter disclaimer (§3) explicitly states that the guidebook is not legal authority and does not replace professional judgment; voice-style §8.1 tier-located authority makes the source of each specification visible (Universal Mode = code; Tier 1 = evidence; Tier 2 = OT-assessed); evidence-state machine (A6) makes the synthesis state of each cell available to the reader. A reader following a Tier 2 (person-specific) specification as a Universal Mode (mandatory) specification is using the guidebook outside its scope, and the documentation makes that visible.

**Adversarial-citation risk** (`governance/adversarial-use-framework.md` V-08). A defendant in disability-discrimination litigation cites the guidebook's Tier 2 language to argue a contested provision is "discretionary." The framework's mitigation: front-matter disclaimer explicitly states that pending/provisional cells reflect synthesis status, not entitlement status; §3.3 below provides the operative wording; counsel review queue §6 includes confirmation that this language defends against adversarial use.

**Currency risk.** The guidebook's standards register and code citations age; a reader relies on a citation that has been superseded between the guidebook's publication and the reader's use. The framework's mitigation: A9 time-model freshness windows (Tier 4–5 standards: 5 years; Tier 6 codes: 2 years; Co-1: 7 years; Tier 1–3 lit: 10 years) plus the standards-supersession-tracker. The disclaimer cross-references the publication date and the reader's responsibility to check current code in their jurisdiction.

**Mis-jurisdiction risk.** A reader applies a UK-specific specification in an Australian context, or an EU specification in a US context, without attending to jurisdictional divergence. The framework's mitigation: A8 jurisdiction-philosophy and the 24/46/49 jurisdiction structure surface jurisdictional differences explicitly per specification; the front-matter disclaimer notes that jurisdictional code-compliance remains the reader's responsibility.

### 2.3 Risks outside the framework's reach

The framework does not address: deliberate fraud (a reader citing the guidebook for a position the guidebook does not support, with intent to deceive); third-party misappropriation of guidebook content into other products (handled by licensing, §4); off-label use as a personal-classification instrument (handled by adversarial-use V-03 surveillance vector). These are mitigated elsewhere in the methodology or accepted as inherent to publishing information.

## 3. Front-matter disclaimer structure

### 3.1 Where the disclaimer lives

A front-matter disclaimer appears in every public release of the guidebook. The canonical location is the opening pages of Volume I, with the same text appearing on the project website's main entry pages, in any printed edition's introductory matter, and in the front matter of any extracted derivative permitted by the licensing model (§4). The disclaimer is not optional in any reasonable presentation of the work; reproducing the guidebook without the disclaimer is a licensing violation.

### 3.2 What the disclaimer contains

The disclaimer carries five required elements. The exact wording is provisional pending counsel review (§3.4); the elements are fixed by this framework.

| Element | Function |
|---|---|
| 1. Not legal authority | States plainly that the guidebook is not a legal authority and does not replace jurisdictional code compliance. Mirrors mission L139. |
| 2. Not professional advice | States that the guidebook does not provide professional design or accessibility-consultancy advice for any specific project. Mirrors mission L137. Cross-references Tier 2 (OT-resolved) for person-specific design. |
| 3. Synthesis-state transparency | States that pending/provisional/divergent cells reflect synthesis status — what the evidence supports as of the publication date — not entitlement status under any disability-rights framework. Closes the V-08 adversarial-citation vector explicitly. |
| 4. Currency notice | States the publication date, references the standards-supersession-tracker, and reminds the reader that code currency in the reader's jurisdiction is the reader's responsibility. |
| 5. Jurisdictional scope | States that the guidebook covers 24 jurisdictions per A8 and that application in jurisdictions outside that set requires comparable judgment about applicability. |

### 3.3 Provisional disclaimer language

The following text is the provisional front-matter disclaimer. **This wording is subject to counsel review (§6) and will be revised before any release labeled v10.0 or later.** All five §3.2 elements are present.

> **About this guidebook.** This guidebook is a synthesis of accessibility evidence and best practice produced by an independent author. It is published as a teaching and reference work. It is not a legal authority and does not replace the building codes, accessibility standards, or disability-rights statutes that apply in your jurisdiction. Where the guidebook's recommendations exceed your jurisdiction's code, the code remains the regulatory minimum and the guidebook's value remains a recommendation. Where the guidebook's recommendations and your jurisdiction's code differ, the code is mandatory; the guidebook informs your judgment about how to design above that minimum.
>
> The guidebook does not provide professional design or accessibility-consultancy advice for any specific building or project. Population-level specifications are necessary but insufficient; person-specific design questions resolve through occupational-therapy assessment in the project context. The guidebook supports the practitioner's judgment; it does not replace it.
>
> Some specifications are marked as pending, provisional, or divergent. These markings reflect the state of the evidence synthesis as of the publication date. They do not say anything about disabled people's entitlements under any disability-rights framework. A specification marked pending is pending in the synthesis; it is not pending in law. A reader citing this guidebook in a legal or regulatory proceeding should engage qualified counsel; the guidebook is not designed for that use and should not be construed as authority for the rights or duties of any party.
>
> This guidebook covers 24 jurisdictions explicitly (see Part 1). Application of guidance in jurisdictions outside that set requires the reader's judgment about comparability. Building codes and accessibility standards change; the guidebook's currency at the reader's date of use is the reader's responsibility. The supersession tracker at `references/standards-supersession-tracker.md` records known changes since publication.
>
> Published [date]. Authored by [name], synthesising published Co-1 evidence and Tier 1–6 sources per the methodology in Part 1.

### 3.4 Counsel review status

The above wording is **PROVISIONAL — pending counsel review**. Counsel review queue §6 includes the specific questions for counsel. Until counsel review completes, releases shipped under this framework include the provisional disclaimer plus a release-notes annotation: "Disclaimer language pending counsel review; current wording is provisional and may change at v10.0 or later."

## 4. Licensing model

### 4.1 The decision space

The guidebook's licensing model determines what others may do with its contents: read-only access, derivative works, commercial use, redistribution, translation, integration into other works. Four broad options are commonly available:

| Option | Allows | Requires |
|---|---|---|
| All-rights-reserved (proprietary) | Reading; not derivatives, redistribution, or commercial use without permission | Per-use permission negotiation |
| Creative Commons BY 4.0 | Any use including derivatives and commercial | Attribution to the author |
| Creative Commons BY-SA 4.0 | Any use including derivatives and commercial | Attribution + share-alike (derivatives must use the same licence) |
| Creative Commons BY-NC-ND 4.0 | Reading and non-commercial sharing without modification | Attribution + non-commercial only + no derivatives |

### 4.2 Decision criteria

The guidebook's mission frames it as a teaching reference for practitioners and disabled people. The teaching mission is best served when the work circulates widely and can be referenced freely. The mitigation against misappropriation (e.g., a defendant excerpting the guidebook to argue against accessibility) is not licensing — it is the integrity of the front-matter disclaimer, the integration of the catalogued vectors per A10, and counsel-reviewed release language. Restrictive licensing does not prevent this misuse; it only makes legitimate beneficial use harder.

The teaching mission is also well-served by allowing translations (which BY-NC-ND would prevent) and adaptations for specific contexts (e.g., an OT producing a client-facing summary based on the guidebook). Both fall under "derivatives" and are blocked by ND licences.

The teaching mission is not well-served by allowing repackaging of the guidebook as a paid commercial product without attribution or share-alike — which BY 4.0 alone would permit. A commercial vendor could, in principle, sell a product that is substantially the guidebook with cosmetic changes and no attribution back. BY-SA prevents this by requiring derivatives to remain under the same licence; a BY-SA derivative cannot become proprietary.

### 4.3 Decision

**The guidebook is published under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International (CC BY-SA 4.0).**

This licence:
- permits derivatives, translations, and adaptations
- permits commercial use
- requires attribution to the project
- requires share-alike: derivatives must be released under CC BY-SA 4.0
- preserves the front-matter disclaimer requirement (§3.1) as a licensing condition: any derivative or substantial excerpt must include the disclaimer
- aligns with widespread accessibility-knowledge sharing practice (W3C accessibility resources, several DPO publications, academic open-access conventions)

The licence text is reproduced in full in the guidebook's licensing appendix, with a project-specific notice clarifying:
- Co-1 evidence cited in the guidebook is licensed by its original publishers; the guidebook's CC BY-SA does not extend to the underlying Co-1 sources, which retain their original licensing
- Standards and codes cited in the guidebook are likewise the property of their issuing bodies (BSI, ISO, AS/NZS, and so on); citation under fair dealing/fair use is the basis for inclusion, not relicensing
- Images, diagrams, and figures may have separate licensing per item; the licensing appendix includes a per-asset register

### 4.4 Counsel review

Counsel review queue §6 includes confirmation that:
- the CC BY-SA 4.0 selection is consistent with the guidebook's published-citations posture under fair-dealing / fair-use frameworks in the relevant jurisdictions
- the share-alike condition is enforceable against derivative works produced in any of the 24 covered jurisdictions
- the licensing-appendix carve-outs for cited Co-1 sources, standards, and codes are correctly framed

## 5. Trajectory positioning: jurisdictional adoption and certification

### 5.1 What is at stake

A guidebook of accessibility specifications can pursue several relationships with formal regulatory frameworks:

1. **Pursue adoption.** Engage with code-writing bodies (model-code organisations, standards committees, jurisdictional regulators) to integrate guidebook recommendations into code or standards. Outcome at saturation: guidebook content becomes Tier 4–5 or Tier 6.
2. **Pursue referencing.** Engage to be cited as a recognised reference by codes and standards (e.g., "see [guidebook] for evidence base"). Outcome: guidebook remains independent but acquires institutional acknowledgement.
3. **Pursue certification.** Develop a credentialing or certification system (e.g., "certified accessible-design-aware practitioner") tied to the guidebook content.
4. **Decline pursuit.** Position the guidebook as an independent practitioner reference, neither seeking adoption nor certifying users. Engage with regulators only when invited.

### 5.2 Mission default

`governance/mission-and-epistemics.md` L141 declares the default: "The guidebook does **not pursue universal jurisdictional adoption** as its primary trajectory; it pursues equipping practitioners to make informed decisions in any jurisdiction." This default rules out (1) and (3) as primary trajectories. It does not rule out (2) — independent referencing is consistent with equipping practitioners — and it does not rule out (1) responsively (engaging with a regulator who initiates contact) as distinct from (1) actively (the project pursuing regulators).

### 5.3 Decision

**The guidebook formally adopts the "decline pursuit / accept invitation" trajectory.**

Specifically:
- The project does **not actively pursue** jurisdictional adoption of guidebook recommendations into code or standards. It does not lobby regulators. It does not seek formal endorsement.
- The project **does not pursue certification or credentialing** of practitioners. The guidebook is read by practitioners under their own professional judgment; the project does not warrant any practitioner's competence.
- The project **accepts invitations** from regulators, standards bodies, model-code organisations, or DPOs who wish to reference the guidebook or engage with the synthesis. Engagement is responsive, not initiating.
- The project **welcomes independent referencing** of the guidebook in code or standards, where such referencing is initiated by the code-writing body. Independent referencing does not require project-side action.

### 5.4 Reasons

The decline-pursuit trajectory is consistent with the teaches-not-prescribes posture (§1) and the mission's framing of authority (mission §Authority and validation; voice-style §8.1). A guidebook actively pursuing adoption would acquire a stake in its specifications being formally enacted, which would distort the synthesis. A guidebook that holds its specifications as evidence-warranted recommendations, available to whomever finds them useful, retains the synthesis's integrity.

The decline-pursuit trajectory is also consistent with the project's pre-launch resource state. Active pursuit of jurisdictional adoption requires the kind of sustained organisational engagement (committee participation, regulatory comment, position-paper drafting) that a single-author pre-launch project cannot conduct credibly. Declining pursuit is the honest reflection of the project's actual capacity.

The accept-invitation clause leaves room for the trajectory to evolve post-launch if conditions change. If a DPO requests the project's referenced inclusion in their guidance, the project can respond. If a standards committee invites comment on a draft, the project can comment. The clause defends against the over-restrictive case of refusing all engagement.

### 5.5 What this affects

The trajectory decision affects:
- The guidebook's voice (no positioning as "the standard"; no claims of regulatory weight)
- The licensing model (CC BY-SA 4.0 supports the decline-pursuit trajectory by enabling independent referencing without project-side gatekeeping)
- The CS coverage list (no new cross-stage thread for "regulator engagement"; CS9 adversarial-use review remains the active accountability mechanism)
- A12 decision-capture protocol scope (decisions about specific regulator interactions are recorded if invitations arrive; no proactive decisions are recorded because none are made)
