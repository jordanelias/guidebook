# How This Project Thinks

**Status: PROPOSED — pending owner ratification.** This is the render source for the
public "Grounds" section of the website (Phase E renders `governance/*.md`; placement
and per-page instruments are specified in §R at the end). It is the public-facing
companion of `governance/held-tensions.md` and is written in plain language on
purpose: if this page is hard to read, it is failing its own subject.

---

## What this is

This guidebook helps people ask better questions about designing buildings and
places that work for disabled people.

It is not a rulebook. It does not tell you what to value. It does not replace your
own judgment, or a professional's. It is a thinking tool.

We collect research and real-world evidence about accessible design, we weigh it
openly, and we show you our working — including where the evidence is weak, where
experts disagree, and where we simply do not know.

## Who made this, and the limits that come with that

This project is currently researched and written by **one person, working with AI
assistance**, before launch. That matters, and we will not hide it:

- No panel of disabled people has reviewed this work yet. We use published
  lived-experience evidence — writing and research by disabled people and their
  organizations — and we treat it as equal in authority to clinical research. But
  reading about people is not the same as working with them. The UN disability
  convention (CRPD, Article 4.3) says decisions about disabled people should be made
  with them. Right now this project honors that only partly, through the published
  record. If the project grows, the form that collaboration takes will be decided
  *with* disabled people, not for them. It is also possible this project stays a
  solo work permanently — we have committed to being honest in that case too.
- Most of our research so far was found and read **in English**, with partial
  coverage in German, Japanese, French, Chinese, Korean, and several other
  languages, and almost none yet in Arabic, Hindi, Bengali, or Swahili. Our
  jurisdiction coverage is wide on paper and still thin in practice. Where we make
  claims, we tag what they rest on — and a claim built only from English-language
  sources should be read as exactly that.
- This guidebook specifies for **formal construction** — new buildings and planned
  retrofits. Housing in informal settlements is outside what we can responsibly
  specify. We record this as the single biggest exclusion in our scope, not a
  small footnote: the divide between formal and informal cities is the largest
  barrier to accessible housing worldwide.

**How to correct us:** every page shows its evidence. If you know better evidence,
a missing perspective, or an error — especially from lived experience or from
outside the English-language world — the project treats that as a contribution,
not a complaint. Corrections are logged, versioned, and visible.

## How we grade what we tell you

Every recommendation carries a strength mark:

- **● full strength** — supported by strong direct evidence: high-quality research,
  or documented lived experience, or both agreeing.
- **◐ partial** — supported, with real gaps.
- **○ weak** — the best we can currently say, honestly flagged. This includes values
  that many building codes agree on but that no one has properly tested. Codes
  agreeing with each other is not proof — they may all be copying the same old
  assumption. We show such values, marked, because pretending not to know them
  would help no one.

Lived experience is **not** a lesser kind of evidence here. Research by and with
disabled people sits at the top tier alongside clinical studies. Where the two
agree, that agreement is itself evidence. Where they disagree, we show you the
disagreement instead of quietly picking a side.

Building codes are the **floor** — the legal minimum — never the goal. Where
evidence supports doing better than code, we say so, and we show the gap. That gap
is not an embarrassment; it is the to-do list for advocacy and innovation.

## Where we stand

No single theory of disability is enough to design from, so this project uses a
declared stack and shows its work:

- **Rights** (the human-rights model and the CRPD) tell us *why*: access is an
  obligation, not a favor.
- **Function and environment** (the WHO's ICF framework, and
  occupational-therapy models including PEO and the Japanese Kawa model) tell us
  *where to look*: disability happens in the meeting of a person and an
  environment — which is the part designers control.
- **Capability thinking** (from Sen and Nussbaum) tells us *what to aim for*: not
  minimum compliance, but environments that genuinely expand what people can do
  and choose, to a threshold of dignity.
- **Care thinking** reminds us that most lives are interdependent: buildings are
  used by people together — with family, carers, assistance animals — not only by
  a lone individual, and design that only imagines solo use fails most of the
  world.
- **Lived experience** is the ground truth the other layers answer to.

We also refuse one thing plainly: this project will never use cost-per-quality-of-
life arithmetic that prices disabled lives below other lives. The evidence is clear
that disabled people rate their own lives far higher than outside observers assume.
Our economics volume argues from costs and value — never from discounting anyone's
life.

## Eight tensions we hold on purpose

Some contradictions in this field cannot be solved, only handled honestly. We keep
eight of them deliberately open. Each one gives you a question worth asking of any
design — including ours.

1. **Every body / every culture.** Bodies share physiology; lives differ by
   culture, building tradition, and climate. *Ask: would this claim survive
   translation — does it hold at the level of the body, the building type, or only
   one culture?*
2. **Disability / function.** "Disability" carries rights, history, and community.
   "Function" includes everyone, diagnosed or not. We keep both doors open. *Ask:
   am I asking what this body needs here, or what this community knows — and can I
   ask both?*
3. **Frameworks / lived experience.** Frameworks see patterns across thousands of
   people; a person sees what actually happened. *Ask: what does the framework see
   that no witness can — and what did the witness see that no framework can?*
4. **Categories.** Every category helps find evidence and cuts someone at its
   edges. Ours are organizing conveniences, never definitions of people, and they
   can be revised. *Ask: who made this box, who is cut by its edges, and what
   would I miss without it?*
5. **Five chairs.** A disabled person, an advocate, a policymaker, a designer, and
   a therapist all mean something different by "best practice." This site shows the
   same record differently per chair — and tells you what each view underweights.
   *Ask: which chair am I reading from, and what does it hide from me?*
6. **Protection / dignity.** Designing *for* people risks deciding *about* them.
   We specify what must be possible, not what people must do — and where a
   provision trades someone's freedom for safety, we flag the trade. *Ask: does
   this expand what the person can choose, or what others can prevent?*
7. **Universal / specific / individual.** One-size-fits-all meets real conflicts —
   the contrast that helps one group can harm another. We show such conflicts at
   parity instead of quietly picking a winner. *Ask: who is the "everyone" this
   design claims, and who becomes the exception it manages?*
8. **Done / possible.** The best that has been built is not the best that evidence
   supports. We show both numbers and the gap between them. *Ask: is this the best
   anyone has done, or the best anyone has shown — and what lives in the gap?*

## How you can read this

The same record renders for different readers — a designer, a disabled person or
advocate, a therapist, a policymaker, a carer. Switching view never changes the
data; it changes emphasis and language. Each view also states what it
underweights, so no reader mistakes their window for the whole house.

If you are checking whether this project represents people like you fairly: the
lived-experience markers, the disagreement displays, and this page are yours. They
exist so you can check us — and so that when we are wrong, it is findable.
