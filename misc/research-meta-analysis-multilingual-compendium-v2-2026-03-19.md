## 0.2 Structural Biases Found and Corrected

### Bias 1: English-first concept sequencing
v1.0 listed EN first and treated other languages as translations. v2.0 introduces a **Legislative Vocabulary** section (Part 0.4) that begins from each jurisdiction's own legal framework, not from English equivalents. Many searches must begin with the statutory instrument, not the concept.

### Bias 2: Absent participation concept
Every language has a distinct, non-translatable concept for disability *participation* as distinct from physical *accessibility*. v1.0 does not capture this. v2.0 adds it as Concept Group 0.

### Bias 3: Latin American / Iberian conflation
v1.0 treats Spanish (ES) as a single entry. ES-Spain, ES-Mexico, ES-Colombia, ES-Argentina use different standards, different statutory terms, and different sign languages. v2.0 distinguishes these where material.

### Bias 4: Brazilian APO tradition presented as absent POE
Brazil has the most developed non-English POE tradition in the world (*Avaliação Pós-Ocupação*). v1.0 implied POE is universally absent in non-English contexts. Corrected in v2.0.

### Bias 5: Missing legislative vocabulary layer
Every jurisdiction generates accessible design evidence primarily through its statutory instrument and subordinate guidance, not through academic databases. The correct primary search term for French research is *ERP* + *PMR*, not "accessibility" + "France". For Japanese research it is *移動等円滑化* not "barrier-free" + "Japan". v1.0 misses this entirely. v2.0 adds the legislative vocabulary layer as a prerequisite to all language-specific searches.

### Bias 6: Under-representation of East Asian and Korean discourse
JA and KO entries in v1.0 were the thinnest despite both jurisdictions having substantial indigenous research traditions. Corrected in v2.0 with expansion of JA content (Japan has the richest non-English accessible design regulatory tradition) and KO content.

### Bias 7: Missing concept groups
v1.0 covered only a fraction of the design elements in the guidebook. Missing entirely: turning radius / manoeuvring space, door width / opening force, ramp gradient, wayfinding/signage, luminance contrast, acoustic performance metrics, dementia design, and age-inclusive design. v2.0 adds these as Concept Groups 6–9.

## 0.3 Syncretic Findings — What Only Emerges Across Languages

These findings are invisible from English alone. They require cross-language analysis to surface.

### Syncretic Finding S-01: The Participation Gap
Every jurisdiction has a term for disability *participation* — the right to full social engagement with the built world — that is distinct from, and superior to, physical *accessibility*. These terms are not synonyms for each other and generate entirely different bodies of evidence:

| Language | Term | Concept | Legislative anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| NO | *brukermedvirkning* | Statutory user participation in planning; mandatory, not aspirational | Plan- og bygningsloven §5-1 |
| DE | *Teilhabe* | Social participation; the governing goal of Bundesteilhabegesetz (BTHG 2016) | SGB IX |
| FI | *osallisuus* | Participation/belonging; STM disability policy goal distinct from accessibility | Vammaispalvelulaki |
| JA | *参画* (sankaku) | Genuine stakeholder involvement — stronger than mere participation (*参加*); disability persons must lead | CRPD ratification guidance |
| KO | *자립생활* (jarib saengwhal) | Independent living — disability movement's built environment demand | 장애인복지법 |
| IT | *vita indipendente* | Independent life — equivalent of IL movement's spatial demands | 아직 legge quadro pending |
| FR | *participation sociale* | Social participation as disability rights concept; MDPH-governed | Loi 2005-102 |
| SV | *delaktighet* | Participation as key outcome; LSS-governed | Socialtjänstlagen, LSS |
| PT | *inclusão social* | Social inclusion; LBI 2015 governing concept | Lei 13.146/2015 |

**Search implication:** Searches for accessible design evidence in German MUST include *Teilhabe* + *Barrierefreiheit*; in Japanese MUST include *参画* + *バリアフリー*; in Finnish MUST include *osallisuus* + *esteettömyys*. Without the participation term, the statutory guidance and DPO publications will not surface.

### Syncretic Finding S-02: Non-Western Framings of Disability in the Built Environment
Three East Asian discourses generate conceptual frameworks with no English equivalent:

- **Japanese** *共生社会* (kyōsei shakai = symbiotic/coexisting society): Japan's post-CRPD social integration concept; used in Cabinet Office disability white papers; not a disability-specific term but frames all accessible design policy since 2011 CRPD ratification. Without this term, you cannot find Japanese government built environment policy documents.
- **Chinese** *残障* (cánzhàng): The contemporary progressive Chinese disability term that structurally encodes the social model — *残* (impairment) + *障* (barrier). Used in academic and advocacy discourse to distinguish from the official/legal *残疾* (cánjí). Searches using *残疾* alone miss the emerging Chinese CDS literature.
- **Korean** *장애학* (jangaehak = disability studies): The academic field; distinct from *재활의학* (rehabilitation medicine). Korean disability studies produces built environment critiques using CDS frameworks not recoverable via medical or building science databases.

### Syncretic Finding S-03: Nordic Statutory Participation as Co-1 Evidence
Norway's *brukermedvirkning* is legally mandated in planning processes. This means Norwegian municipal planning documents contain disability community input (Tier Co-1 evidence) embedded in official records — planning reports, *konsekvensutredninger* (impact assessments), and *reguleringsplaner* (zoning plans). No other jurisdiction systematically mandates this at this level. Norwegian planning archives are an underexplored Co-1 source.

### Syncretic Finding S-04: Brazil's APO Is Not Absent POE
The claim that POE is "universally absent" for accessible design is false for Portuguese-language research. Brazil's *Avaliação Pós-Ocupação* (APO) methodology, developed primarily at FAU-USP and IPT since the 1980s, includes accessibility-specific evaluation tools. NUTAU (Núcleo de Pesquisa em Tecnologia da Arquitetura e Urbanismo, USP) has produced APO studies of accessible housing, healthcare facilities, and public buildings. These are entirely invisible to English-language searches.

### Syncretic Finding S-05: The Italian *Barriere Architettoniche* Tradition
Italian accessibility research organises itself around the term *barriere architettoniche* (architectural barriers), not *accessibilità* or *progettazione universale*. Every Italian municipality is legally required to produce a *PEBA* (Piano di Eliminazione delle Barriere Architettoniche — Plan for the Elimination of Architectural Barriers). These plans constitute a national database of real-world accessibility gap evidence, surveyed at municipal level, entirely unpublished in English. *Barriere architettoniche* must be the primary Italian search term; *accessibilità* alone misses most of this body of evidence.

### Syncretic Finding S-06: Divergent Deaf Community Space Standards by Sign Language
The 1800mm corridor figure derives from ASL proxemics. Sign languages with different spatial grammar may require different dimensions. Evidence:
- **ASL** (American): uses larger signing space; facial grammar encoded at greater distance
- **BSL** (British): uses comparatively smaller signing space; classifier predicates more compact
- **DGS** (Deutsche Gebärdensprache): signing space research conducted at Humboldt-Universität Berlin (Leuninger group) suggests DGS spatial grammar is intermediate
- **LSF** (French): systematic signing space research at Université Paris 8 (Cuxac group)
- **NGT** (Dutch): signing space research at Radboud University (Nijmegen Institute for Cognition and Information)
- **JSL/NSL/KSL**: minimal spatial grammar research for built environment implications
**Implication:** The 1800mm figure is likely an ASL-derived value. Research in other signed languages may yield different or narrowed specifications. This is an unresolved cross-language divergence.

### Syncretic Finding S-07: Missing Dementia Design Tradition in German and Japanese
The document's treatment of dementia design (DEM) is overwhelmingly Anglo-Scottish (Stirling/DSDC, Alzheimer's Society UK). However:
- **German**: *Deutsche Alzheimer Gesellschaft* produces building guidance; *Kuratorium Deutsche Altershilfe (KDA)* has produced *Wohnkonzepte für Menschen mit Demenz* (housing concepts for people with dementia) — a major German-language design reference
- **Japanese**: *認知症* (ninchishō = dementia) design research is substantial; *老人福祉施設* (elderly welfare facilities) standards include dementia-specific provisions; *グループホーム* (group home) model has extensive Japanese built environment research

### Syncretic Finding S-08: Korean Welfare Facility Standard as Underutilised Source
The Korean *장애인·노인·임산부 등의 편의증진법* (Convenience Facilities Promotion Act) produces detailed technical specifications for all building types. The *편의시설 설치 기준* (installation standards) are more granular than ADA in several categories (toilet manoeuvring space, grab bar positioning, ramp gradient). This is a significant under-used source that would strengthen multiple items.

## 1.1 Structural Biases in Current Search Practice

### Bias A: Clinical Pyramid Default
The prior multilingual research runs (Batches 1–3) used a PRISMA clinical pyramid — Tier 1 systematic reviews → RCTs → observational studies. This systematically underweights:
- **Co-1 lived experience research** (disability-led participatory studies, netnographic testimony, DPO publications)
- **Tier 2 NGO/advocacy guidelines** (community-developed; lived experience embedded)
- **Grey literature from disabled-led organisations** (national Deaf associations, DeafBlind organisations, OFS/PAIN patient communities)

**Fix:** Every multilingual-research run must begin with a dedicated Co-1/Tier 2 pass before any academic database — and in each language, with the *national* disability organisation, not just the English-language equivalent.

# PART 6: NON-ENGLISH RESEARCH AVENUES — EXCLUSIVE TO SPECIFIC LANGUAGES

*These research streams are only accessible in the native language. English searches will not surface them.*

## 6.1 Japanese-Only Research Streams

- **都道府県福祉のまちづくり条例** (Prefectural Welfare City Ordinances) — 47 prefectures each have a welfare city ordinance with built environment requirements; collectively a massive sub-national evidence base with no English equivalent
- **重点整備地区** (Priority Improvement Zones) — Japanese cities designate areas for enhanced accessibility investment; published plans contain disability needs assessments (Co-1 evidence)
- **建築審査会** (Building Review Boards) — prefectural building review boards that make accessibility exception decisions; published decisions create case law equivalent
- **日本建築学会大会** (AIJ Annual Conference Proceedings) — Japanese architectural conference papers on accessibility; not indexed in English databases
- **福祉のまちづくり研究** (Journal of JAUD) — Japanese Association for Urban Accessible Design journal; primary publication venue for Japanese accessibility research

## 6.2 German-Only Research Streams

- **Behindertenbeauftragte reports** — each German state (16 Länder) publishes a disability commissioner's report with built environment sections; unindexed outside Germany
- **Bericht der Bundesregierung über die Lage von Menschen mit Behinderungen** — Federal disability situation report; comprehensive built environment barrier assessment
- **VDI-Berichte** — Verein Deutscher Ingenieure conference proceedings; accessible design engineering papers
- **Berichte zur BTHG-Umsetzung** — BTHG implementation reports; monitor accessibility in housing and public buildings

## 6.3 Italian-Only Research Streams

- **PEBA reports** — Every Italian municipality must produce an annual report on its Piano di Eliminazione delle Barriere Architettoniche; collectively the largest surveyed built environment accessibility dataset in any jurisdiction; never published in English
- **Relazioni di accessibilità** — Accessibility statements submitted with building permits; Italian administrative archive
- **Rivista REQUADRO** — Italian accessibility design trade journal

## 6.4 Brazilian Portuguese-Only Research Streams

- **APO dissertations** — hundreds of Brazilian graduate theses on accessible design POE; indexed in BDTD; never appear in English-language databases
- **Relatórios de acessibilidade** — Brazilian accessibility audit reports for public buildings (Lei 10.098/2000); municipal archives
- **ABNT CB-40 standards working papers** — Brazilian standards committee accessibility work programme

## 6.5 Korean-Only Research Streams

- **장애인 편의시설 실태조사** (Survey on Accessibility Facility Status) — Korea's national accessibility facility audit; comprehensive building-type evidence; published in Korean only
- **지방자치단체 장애인 편의증진계획** — Municipal accessibility promotion plans; equivalent to Italian PEBA; nationwide coverage
- **한국장애인개발원 연구보고서** (KODDI Research Reports) — annual research output; primarily Korean language

## 6.6 French-Only Research Streams

- **Bilan de l'accessibilité des ERP** — Ministry annual report on ERP accessibility compliance; national building-type data
- **Agendas d'Accessibilité Programmée (Ad'AP)** — Programmed accessibility plans; building-by-building gap documentation; French administrative record
- **CEREMA études et rapports** — CEREMA technical studies; majority French-language only
