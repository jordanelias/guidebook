## Part 4: Synthesis and Sequencing

### 4.1 Why Synthesis Matters

The Part V technique library contains 71 items. No building project requires all 71 simultaneously, and no project fails for lack of knowledge of individual items. Projects fail in accessible design because: 1.

**Stage failure:** Structural items (E-01 lift shaft, G-03 grab bar blocking, D-01 loop floor plan) are not decided at schematic stage and cannot be incorporated later without structural intervention.

**Sensory incoherence:** Individual items are specified correctly but their interaction produces a hostile sensory environment. A room with excellent acoustics but flickering lighting defeats the acoustic work for NDV users. A bathroom with correct grab bars but no heated floor defeats the thermal provisions for MS users. 3.

**Population omission:** Design addresses MOB and VIS requirements but misses NDV, OFS, and DEM — the populations for whom the sensory environment is the primary barrier. 4.

**Checklist mentality:** Items are treated as a compliance list rather than as a design discipline. An item on a list is not the same as an item on a drawing. Synthesis is the practice of treating the entire library as a single integrated design system, applied through a staged process from brief to handover.

### 4.2 The Layered Environment

Every building produces a three-layer accessible environment:

**Layer 1 — Physical access layer:** Route, entry, level change, turning space. Items: E-01 through E-12, G-01, J-01/J-02. Stage: schematic and structural design. Cannot be added later.

**Layer 2 — Sensory environment layer:** Acoustic, visual, thermal, olfactory, tactile. Items: A-01 through A-17 (merged), B-01 through B-11 (merged), C-01 through C-05 (merged), F-01 through F-07, TC-01 through TC-05. Stage: technical design (finishes, MEP, lighting). Can be partially retrofitted but at significant cost.

**Layer 3 — Information and technology layer:** Controls, signage, wayfinding, communication, automation. Items: D-01 through D-11, H-01 through H-05, I-01 through I-03. Stage: technical design and construction. Partially retrofittable (signage, hardware) but infrastructure elements (conduit, blocking) cannot be added later.

A building that achieves Layer 1 but not Layers 2 and 3 is physically accessible but sensorially hostile and informationally inaccessible — this describes the majority of DDA-compliant buildings in the current stock.

### 4.3 The Four Synthesis Principles

**Principle 1 — Design Stage Primacy**

Every Part V item has a design stage at which it must be decided. Items not decided at the correct stage are deferred into a risk register. The risk is not just cost — it is physical impossibility. A lift shaft not placed at schematic design cannot be added at technical design. A grab bar blocking specification not on a structural drawing at construction stage will not be installed. The design stage column in Part D is not a suggested timeline — it is a constraint.

**Principle 2 — Sensory Coherence**

The acoustic environment, visual environment, thermal environment, and olfactory environment must be designed as a coherent whole. They are not separate specification packages — they are one sensory experience. An acoustic treatment that uses high-VOC products defeats the olfactory specification. A fragrance-free policy that permits fragrant cleaning products defeats the construction specification. A circadian lighting system that uses flickering LED defeats the NDV acoustic work. The synthesis deliverable is a Sensory Coherence Check at the end of technical design, confirming that all sensory provisions are mutually compatible.

**Principle 3 — Population Co-occurrence**

Design for the realistic population, not isolated cases. A residential care building serves: DEM + MOB (concurrent at high prevalence), plus staff who are NDV + OFS (workplace prevalence data). An office serves NDV + PAIN + OFS as co-occurring conditions in the same building user. The conflict register in Parts E and F identifies the standard co-occurrences and their resolutions. Apply the relevant conflict resolutions at the start of technical design, not when they are noticed.

**Principle 4 — Temporal Continuity**

An accessible building is not a collection of accessible rooms. It is a continuous journey from arrival to departure. A person in a wheelchair should be able to arrive at a parking space, enter the building, navigate to their destination, use all facilities, and depart without encountering a single inaccessible link in the chain. One inaccessible link — a step at the accessible toilet door, a control above reach height in the lift — breaks the chain for the entire journey. Synthesis maps the journey, not the individual rooms.

### 4.4 Stage-by-Stage Process

**Stage 0 — Brief**

Deliverables:

- Population brief: identify which disability categories will use this building; estimate co-occurrence patterns
- NOT-RETROFITTABLE register: flag D-01, D-02, D-03, D-07, E-01, F-01, A-09 as schematic-stage constraints
- Conflict register: pre-populate with standard conflicts from Parts E/F relevant to building type
- Consultant briefs: include disability population brief in initial brief to all consultants — not an afterthought Specific brief requirements:
- HVAC brief: temperature maximum ≤24°C (TC-01); MERV 13 filtration (F-04); acoustic NC targets (A-08)
- Structural brief: grab bar blocking provision (G-03); lift shaft (E-01); through-floor lift zone (R-STA DAR)
- Acoustic brief: programme adjacency diagram (A-04); RT60 targets by space type
- Lighting brief: circadian lighting (B-01); no fluorescent (B-03/04); EML targets (WELL v2)

**Stage 1 — Schematic Design** Deliverables at end of schematic:

- Sensory gradient diagram on floor plan (F-01 sensory zones annotated)
- Acoustic zoning diagram (A-04: high/low stimulation zones; buffer zones ≥1.5 m)
- Natural light strategy (B-09: sDA simulation or daylight factor calculation)
- Physical access route confirmed (E-06, E-07, E-08 clear widths, E-01 lift shaft position)
- DAR structural provisions committed to structural engineer brief (G-03 blocking, hoist zone, stairlift channel) Schematic-only check: if the following items are not on the schematic drawings, they cannot be added later:
- Lift shaft (E-01)
- Loop floor plan (D-01)
- Toilet position relative to bedroom or day room (D-03)
- Sensory room position (F-01, A-16)
- A-09 HVAC plant room location (vibration path to occupied spaces)

**Stage 2 — Technical Design** Deliverables at end of technical design:

- Room-by-room specification tables (using Parts E and F matrices)
- MEP briefs issued with Part C/H coordination requirements
- Sensory coherence check completed (Principle 2 cross-check)
- Conflict resolutions documented in specification preamble
- DAR provisions on structural, electrical, and M\&E drawings (not only in specification text)
- Commissioning schedule drafted

**Stage 3 — Procurement and Construction** Deliverables:

- VE protection register: list of accessibility specifications that cannot be value-engineered without design team sign-off (acoustic products, flicker-free LED specification, TMV specification, grab bar blocking)
- Substitution control: no product substitution for acoustic, lighting, or MEP accessibility items without written approval
- Site quality assurance: photographic record of grab bar blocking installation before wall lining

**Stage 4 — Handover and POE** Deliverables:

- O\&M manual accessibility section: fragrance-free maintenance policy, thermostat settings, grab bar load ratings, TMV service schedule, BLE beacon battery monitoring, accessible area plans
- PEEP (Personal Emergency Evacuation Plan) protocol drafted and provided to building manager
- Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) schedule: minimum POE at 6 months and 12 months post-occupation; measure RT60 in key spaces, lux at task heights, temperature at peak summer, air quality (CO2, TVOC)

### 4.5 Synthesis by Building Strategy

**Strategy A — Baseline-Plus**

For most commercial and workplace buildings. Start from the Universal Non-Residential Provisions (Part F §F.0). Then identify the primary disability categories for the building's user population and apply the relevant building-type matrix from Part F. Conflict resolutions from Part F §NR.3 apply as standard.

**Strategy B — Universal Design**

For public buildings, cultural buildings, and healthcare. Start from the assumption that the full disability spectrum will use the building daily. All 15 Universal NR Provisions apply. Apply all relevant building-type matrices and the Population Co-occurrence principle. This is the appropriate strategy for buildings where excluding any disability category is not acceptable.

**Strategy C — Specialist Design**

For DEM care homes, autism-specific schools, MS neurology wards, and other single-population-focused buildings. Universal provisions plus the full sensory environment specification for the primary population. The sensory environment specification is the design driver — not physical access alone.

### 4.6 Decision Framework for Sequencing Techniques

When faced with a specific design decision, the architect should ask four questions in sequence:

**Q1: Is this item NOT-RETROFITTABLE?**

If yes: this is a schematic design constraint. Commit it now. If it cannot be committed at schematic (e.g., programme area constraint), escalate to the client as a risk.

**Q2: Does this item require a consultant brief?**

If yes (Part C/H items): issue the brief at the stage indicated in Part C. Do not wait until construction to involve the consultant.

**Q3: Does this item appear in the conflict register for this room or building type?**

If yes: apply the resolution documented in Parts E/F before specifying the individual item.

**Q4: What drawing does this item appear on?**

If no drawing is identified: the item is not specified. An accessibility requirement that appears only in a narrative specification but not on a drawing will not be built.

### 4.7 Worked Examples —

#### Worked Example 1: Memory Care Residential Unit (DEM Primary; MOB Secondary)

**Building:** 20-bed residential memory care unit. Single-storey. Suburban. All residents have Alzheimer's or LBD diagnosis.

**Population brief:** DEM primary (all residents); MOB secondary (60% use walking aids or wheelchairs); OFS tertiary (1 resident with known chemical sensitivity).

**Stage 0 priorities:**

- D-03 (toilet visibility from bedroom and day room): STRUCTURAL — confirmed on schematic floor plan
- F-01 (sensory gradient: activity lounge → circulation corridor → quiet room → individual bedrooms): SCHEMATIC
- A-09 (HVAC plant room location: away from bedrooms): STRUCTURAL + SCHEMATIC
- NOT-RETROFITTABLE items committed: D-01 (loop floor plan), D-03, D-07, E-01 (lift or single storey confirmed), F-01

**Critical synthesis decisions:**

1. Toilet visibility: D-03 requires toilet visible from bed position. Floor plan must show direct sightline line from bed to toilet door — not into bathroom, just to the door. This is a planning constraint confirmed at schematic.
2. Sensory gradient: activity lounge (high stimulation) → circulation (medium) → bedroom corridor (low) → individual bedroom (lowest). Each transition uses a F-01 buffer zone ≥1.5 m. Position sensory/quiet room adjacent to circulation but not at activity lounge end.
3. Acoustic treatment throughout: RT60 ≤0.5 s in all common areas; NC-30; no hard floor surfaces in bedrooms or corridors (A-05/A-15).
4. Lighting: circadian (B-01) in all occupied spaces; warm CCT ≤2700 K throughout; no fluorescent (B-03/04); night-light path in all corridors (B-05/motion-activated ≤10 lux warm amber).
5. Flooring: C-05 plain floor throughout (no pattern); LRV ≥30 at all door frames and step edges (C-04); no high-LRV differential at carpet-to-hard transitions.
6. Heated bathroom floors: TC-05 in all bathrooms (DEM + MOB fall prevention).
7. OFS resident: fragrance-free policy throughout unit (F-02 + F-06) — FM brief specifies fragrance-free cleaning products for entire building.

**Conflict resolution applied:**

- DEM requires consistent, simple layout (D-02) vs. NDV staff may require sensory-reduced workstation: provide the sensory staff area in the staff office, which is not on the resident circuit — no conflict in resident areas.
- DEM plain floor (C-05) vs. VIS resident (contrast needed): plain floor satisfies both; contrast at perimeter (skirting LRV ≥30).

**Key DAR provisions:**

- Grab bar blocking in all bathrooms (G-03 ×75 cost multiplier)
- Ceiling hoist blocking in at least 4 of 20 bedrooms (likely MOB proportion) + all bathrooms
- Stairlift provision not applicable (single storey) —

#### Worked Example 2: NDV/OFS Co-working Office (NDV Primary; OFS Secondary)

**Building:** 60-person co-working office with private studios. Urban. 3-storey. Named client: technology company with disclosed NDV/ADHD workforce prevalence.

**Population brief:** NDV/AUT/ADHD primary (disclosed 25% of staff); OFS secondary (8% chronic fatigue/pain/MCS); MOB tertiary (2 wheelchair users, 1 power chair).

**Stage 0 priorities:**

- F-01 (sensory gradient: collaborative open area → quiet pods → sensory-reduced private studios): SCHEMATIC
- A-04 (acoustic zoning: no meeting rooms adjacent to quiet studios): SCHEMATIC
- A-09 (HVAC plant room not below or adjacent to quiet studio zone): STRUCTURAL
- B-09 (natural light strategy: sDA simulation at schematic): SCHEMATIC

**Critical synthesis decisions:**

1. Acoustic zoning: A-04 — adjacency matrix confirmed at schematic. Open collaboration zone (NC-40 acceptable) → transition corridor (NC-35) → quiet studio (NC-25). No meeting rooms share a wall with quiet studios without STC ≥45 partition.
2. Quiet pods: ≥3 acoustic pods per floor (RT60 ≤0.3 s; NC ≤25; A-03 acoustic door; ≥1 pod per 20 staff).
3. Masking: prohibited in quiet zones (A-13). Where masking requested for open plan: ambient natural sound at ≤40 dBA (birdsong/water feature at building threshold); no white noise in NDV areas.
4. Lighting: no fluorescent anywhere (B-03/04); individual dimming at every desk (B-06); no cool white CCT in quiet zones (B-11 warm CCT); natural light strategy with south-facing workstations and 30% roof lights.
5. Air quality: MERV 13 filtration (F-04); fragrance-free building policy (F-02/F-06 for OFS staff); CO2 monitoring in all enclosed meeting rooms (TC-01 cross-ref).
6. Temperature: individual thermostats at all private offices and pods (TC-02); ≤24°C maximum (TC-01) for OFS/MS staff; USB power at all desks for personal cooling fans (TC-03).
7. MOB provisions: E-01 lift confirmed at schematic; G-05 height-adjustable desks at all 3 wheelchair-user workstations + ≥20% total (G-05); G-01 turning circle at each desk.

**Conflict resolution applied:**

- Open plan high-SPL (for communication) vs. NDV quiet requirement: hearing loop (A-11) in collaboration zone; CART (H-03) in meeting rooms; personal receiver (A-12 Auracast) as infrastructure option. Room SPL set for general communication; personal tools are the NDV solution.
- Biophilic planting (BIO-01) vs. OFS fragrance: select low-fragrance, low-pollen species (succulents, snake plant, pothos); no flowering plants in OFS-populated areas. —

#### Worked Example 3: Accessible Primary School (NDV Primary; MOB Secondary; DEAF Tertiary)

**Building:** 420-student primary school. Urban. 2-storey. 6 classrooms per floor.

**Population brief:** NDV/AUT/ADHD (estimated 15–20% student population, Autism Europe 2022); MOB (8 wheelchair users, 3 walking aids); DEAF (4 hearing aid users, 1 low-vision student); DEM not applicable (age range).

**Stage 0 priorities:**

- A-04 acoustic zoning: music room and gym not adjacent to classrooms — STRUCTURAL constraint
- F-01 sensory gradient: active corridor → decompression zone → sensory room — SCHEMATIC
- D-03 toilet position: accessible WC accessible from classroom corridor at ≤30 m — SCHEMATIC
- Sensory room provision: ≥1 per 150 students = ≥3 sensory rooms on this building — SCHEMATIC

**Critical synthesis decisions:**

1. Classroom acoustics (DIN 18041:2016 Category A): RT60 ≤0.4 s; background noise ≤35 dBA; this requires acoustic ceiling tiles NRC ≥0.85, no hard parallel surfaces, and confirmed A-04 programme adjacency at schematic.
2. Sensory rooms: 3 sensory rooms (A-16 spec), positioned per F-01 gradient — adjacent to main corridor but with buffer zone at entry. One on each floor; one accessible from EYFS/KS1 zone.
3. No fluorescent: B-03/04 throughout; flicker-free LED with TLA ≥90 in all classrooms — critical safety spec for NDV and photosensitivity students.
4. Height-adjustable furniture: ≥20% of student desks height-adjustable (G-05); all teacher workstations height-adjustable.
5. Hearing loop: A-11 in all classrooms ≥20 seats + assembly hall + SENCO consultation rooms.
6. Visual fire alarm: B-10 strobe in all occupied spaces including all toilet cubicles.
7. MOB: E-01 lift to all floors; E-08 corridor ≥1500 mm clear; D-03 accessible WC on each floor adjacent to accessible route.

**Conflict resolution:**

- Playground/exterior noise vs. classroom quiet: confirm A-03 acoustic performance at all classroom windows (double or triple glazing with acoustic laminate; STC ≥35 at windows facing playground/road). —

#### Worked Example 4: Hospital Ward — Older Adults Orthopaedic (MOB Primary; DEM Secondary

)

**Building:** 24-bed orthopaedic ward, older adults, urban hospital. All patients post-surgical or awaiting surgery.

**Population brief:** MOB primary (all patients; post-surgical mobility limitation); DEM secondary (estimated 30–40% of older adults on ward have concurrent dementia diagnosis, NICE NG97 2018); large body size/BAR (→ Supp Vol IV) tertiary (estimated 15% of patients require large body size provision (BAR → Supp Vol IV)s, NHS NHSE 2023).

Structural engineer confirmation at schematic for floor loading and grab bar blocking.
3. DEM provisions: D-04 landmarks at corridor junctions; C-06 plain flooring; B-11 warm CCT ≤3000 K throughout ward; sensory room (A-16 or F-03) adjacent to main corridor — for agitation management and post-operative disorientation.
4. Acoustic: NC-25 in all patient areas; A-14 STC ≥50 at partition between patient bay and corridor (speech privacy); A-08 HVAC noise NC-25 in patient sleeping areas.
5. Heating: TC-01 18–22°C in all clinical areas; TC-05 heated bathroom floors (fall prevention + MS patient provision); I-03 TMV ≤38°C (all patient bathrooms), TMV ≤35°C (neurology patients if ward includes any).
6. Emergency evacuation: E-12 PEEP for all patients; refuge at each stair core; evacuation lift confirmed with fire engineer. —

#### Worked Example 5: Supported Housing — Mixed Needs (MOB; DEM; NDV)

**Building:** 12-apartment supported housing development. Urban. 4-storey with lift. Residents: mix of young physically disabled adults (MOB), older adults (MOB + DEM), and adults with autism (NDV/AUT).

**Population brief:** MOB primary (all 12 residents); DEM secondary (4 residents); NDV/AUT tertiary (3 residents); PAIN + OFS (2 residents with ME/CFS).

**Stage 0 priorities:**

- E-01 lift: confirmed at schematic (2 lifts for 4 floors)
- G-03 grab bar blocking in all 12 apartments: CONSTRUCTION-CRITICAL (×75 cost multiplier)
- F-01 sensory gradient in communal areas: SCHEMATIC (NDV residents use communal lounge and laundry — sensory zoning critical for inclusion)
- D-03 toilet visibility: STRUCTURAL in all apartments (DEM provision standard applied to all units)

&#x20; **\*\*Critical synthesis decisions:\*\***

1. Uniformity of DAR provisions: all 12 apartments receive identical DAR provisions regardless of current occupant — grab bar blocking, hoist blocking, through-floor lift zone provision for future. This is not a cost increase: it is the most cost-effective accessible design decision across the lifespan of the building.
2. Communal sensory gradient: activity room (communal lounge, kitchen) → circulation corridor (medium stimulation, artwork/planting) → quiet corridor zone → individual apartments (lowest stimulation). F-01 buffer zone ≥1.5 m at each corridor transition.
3. NDV apartment specification: A-02 acoustic ceiling in all apartments; A-03 STC ≥35 acoustic door at each apartment entry (not only fire door); F-02 fragrance-free communal policy in FM brief.
4. OFS/ME-CFS provisions: TC-01 ≤24°C in all apartments (individual thermostat TC-02); F-04 MERV 13 in central ventilation system; F-02/F-06 fragrance-free policy.
5. DEM provisions: D-04 distinctive door colour at each apartment entry (DEM spatial memory); consistent corridor layout (D-09); plain floor throughout communal areas (C-05); warm amber night-lighting on circulation routes.

### 4.8 From Approach to Application

*Accessible Built Environments: Utopian Best Practice Guidebook — v8.8*
*Follows §I.7 (Worked Examples). Readers should be familiar with §I.1–§I.7 and Volume 1 §1.4a.*

---

The four synthesis principles in §I.3 — design stage primacy, sensory coherence, population co-occurrence, temporal continuity — describe how to work with this volume correctly. They do not describe how to arrive at the project brief that makes that work meaningful. The Volume 1 approach (§1.4a) is the prior step: it establishes who the building must serve, what they need to do, and what the environment must therefore be. Without it, §I.1–§I.7 is applied to the wrong questions.

This section states the connection explicitly and gives the practitioner the minimal set of decisions that must be made before the entry paths, matrices, and item library are consulted.

---

### The prior question

The entry paths (Entry Paths I–III, preceding Part I) and the room matrices (Parts II–III) answer specification questions: which items, at which stage, for which population. They assume the designer already knows which populations the building serves, what activities it must support, and which tier applies. [Vol 1 §1.4.3]

These assumptions are not always warranted. The most common failure mode documented across the guidebook's worked examples (§I.7) is not incorrect specification — it is correct specification of the wrong problem: a bathroom that passes all matrix checks but cannot be used by the occupant because the co-occurring conditions were not identified at brief stage; an office with excellent acoustic treatment and no quiet room because NDV was not in the population brief; a dementia care unit with every D-category item specified and toilet visibility omitted because D-03's NOT-RETROFITTABLE status was not read before the floor plan was fixed.

The approach does not add new content to the brief. It disciplines the questions that produce the brief.

---

### Before the entry paths: four decisions

These are to be made in writing, held in the project file, and reviewed at each stage gate. None requires external research — each draws on what the designer already knows or can quickly establish.

**Decision 1 — Population brief.**
Name every disability population the building has an obligation to serve. For each, state their primary functional need in this building type in one sentence. Work through the full population code list explicitly: MOB, MOB/UPL, VIS, VIS/DEAF, NEU, DEM, NDV, NDV/MH, PAIN, OFS, DBL. [Vol 1 Part II; Vol 2 Entry Path II] Absence without stated reason is a gap, not a decision. The most commonly omitted populations — NDV, OFS, PAIN, DBL — are precisely those for whom Layer 2 (sensory environment) is the primary barrier, and Layer 2 decisions are made at schematic and technical design stages where the opportunity to correct omissions is limited.

**Decision 2 — Journey map.**
For each population in the brief, write the sequence: arrival → approach → entry → orient → move → use → secondary facilities (toilet, rest) → depart → emergency egress. The purpose is not to produce a document — it is to force the question of which transitions carry the highest failure risk before the floor plan constrains the answer. The four highest-risk transitions in most building types are: public realm to entry; entry to vertical circulation; vertical circulation to destination; destination to sanitary facilities. If any of these cannot yet be traced on the proposed layout, that is a design problem, not a documentation gap. [Vol 1 §1.4a; Synthesis Principle 4, §I.3]

**Decision 3 — Tier.**
Confirm whether this project operates at Tier 0 (unknown occupancy — apply maximum DAR, use matrices as population coverage check), Tier 1 (identified population — matrix medians as working specification), or Tier 2 (named individual — OT assessment governs, matrices used for cross-check). Different parts of the same project may operate at different tiers. Commission OT assessment before technical design begins if Tier 2. [Vol 1 §1.4.3, §1.3]

**Decision 4 — NOT-RETROFITTABLE commitment.**
Before schematic design is fixed, every applicable NOT-RETROFITTABLE item [Vol 2 NOT-RETROFITTABLE table] must be committed to the structural and spatial brief. This is Synthesis Principle 1 (§I.3) applied at the earliest possible stage. The minimum commitment before schematic: E-01 (lift shaft), D-01 (loop floor plan, DEM buildings), D-03 (toilet visibility, DEM buildings), E-06 (level entry), A-08/A-09 (HVAC plant location), I-03/I-04 (grab bar blocking and wet room drainage). If the client brief makes any item impossible, escalate before schematic design — not after. An item identified as impossible after schematic is a cost and programme risk; before schematic it is a brief negotiation.

---

### Using the entry paths and matrices with the approach brief

Once the four decisions are recorded, open the entry paths in the order appropriate to this project: Entry Path I if the stage is the organising question; Entry Path II if the population is the primary concern; Entry Path III if a room or building type is the starting point. The entry paths assume the approach brief exists. Where it does not, the entry paths produce a specification without a governing question — items will be correctly specified and the wrong items will be omitted.

**Reading the room matrices.** Each matrix opens with a criticality note. Read it before the item table — it names the populations for whom this room is highest-risk and states what failure in this room costs functionally. The item table is a derivation from that note, not an independent list. An item in the table that does not map to a population in the project brief does not apply. A population in the brief that does not map to an item in the table is a specification gap requiring escalation to the item library (Part IV) or to OT advice.

**Transitions between rooms are not in any matrix.** The matrices specify rooms. The approach brief maps journeys. At the end of each room specification, the transition into and out of that room must be verified against the journey map: does the corridor width maintain what the room achieved? Does the lighting strategy continue across the threshold? Does the acoustic zone change abruptly? The entry and circulation matrices [§II.1 R-ENT] and the sensory gradient item [F-01] are the primary instruments for transitions; both require the journey map to be used correctly.

**Co-occurrences and the conflict register.** Population co-occurrences identified in Decision 1 map directly to the conflict register in Part E. Pre-populate the project conflict register from Part E before technical design begins. Common co-occurrences by building type are documented in §I.5 (Building Strategies) and §I.7 (Worked Examples). A conflict encountered at construction documents stage requires design team sign-off and specification revision; the same conflict identified at brief stage is a brief annotation.

**The sensory coherence check.** At the end of technical design, list every Layer 2 item and review each pair for conflict [§I.3, Principle 2]. The check is grounded in the approach brief: it is only possible to evaluate coherence against a population brief that names whose sensory environment is being protected. A sensory coherence check without a population brief is a cross-referencing exercise without a governing criterion.

---

### When no matrix exists

The matrices cover the most common building types and rooms. For spaces not covered — specialist clinical rooms, reception desks, prayer rooms, rooftop terraces, specialist educational spaces — apply the four decisions above to the specific space and cross-reference Part IV directly by population code. Identify the applicable items, confirm their design stages, apply the sensory coherence principle, and verify the transitions. This is the matrix method without a pre-built template. When this process produces a coherent specification for a novel space, the output should be submitted to the editorial register. Post-occupancy evidence from novel spaces is how the matrix coverage expands.

---

### What the approach changes in how this volume is used

The approach does not change any specification in this volume. It changes the sequence in which the volume is consulted and the governing question that is brought to each section.

Without the approach: a designer consults the entry paths and matrices and produces what the matrices require — a set of items correctly specified for the room types listed.

With the approach: a designer arrives at the entry paths and matrices knowing who the building must serve, what journeys must work, at what tier, and which structural decisions are already irrevocable. The matrices confirm and complete the specification. The approach determines whether the right specification has been produced for the right people.

The distinction is between producing an accessible building and producing a building that is accessible for the people who will use it. The second is harder and more specific. It is what this guidebook is for.
