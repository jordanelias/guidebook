## Section IV: Accessible Environments for Persons of Large Body Size (BAR)

---

## IV.0 Purpose, Scope, and Language

This section addresses the design of built environments for persons of large body size — persons whose weight, body width, or physical dimensions exceed the standard design parameters used in most building specifications.

**Large body size is not a disability.** This section is presented as a dedicated chapter rather than as a disability code because large body size is not an impairment, limitation, or disability under any reviewed legal framework. A person of large body size is not, by reason of body size alone, a person with a disability under the ADA (USA), the Equality Act 2010 (UK), the CRPD, or equivalent legislation in any reviewed jurisdiction. The former placement of BAR as a population code within the main guidebook's disability taxonomy was an error of categorisation that this volume corrects. The design provisions for persons of large body size are a matter of inclusive and equitable design, not disability access.

This section does not use the language of disability, accommodation of impairment, or disability equality law in relation to body size. Bariatric design provisions are design quality provisions that reduce physical barriers for a significant user population that has been systematically excluded from standard design parameters.

**Language note:** "Large body size" is the preferred descriptor in this section. "Bariatric" is used where it refers specifically to clinical or healthcare contexts where the term has an established and appropriate technical meaning (bariatric equipment, bariatric surgery, bariatric healthcare). In general design contexts, "large body size" or "larger body size" is preferred. Terms such as "obese," "morbidly obese," and "overweight" are not used in this section as design descriptors; where referenced in clinical literature, they are reproduced as cited.

---

## IV.1 Design Rationale

Standard building specifications have been developed around assumed body dimensions derived from population anthropometric surveys of mid-20th century populations. These dimensions — including standard seat widths, turning circles, aisle clearances, structural load ratings, and equipment specifications — do not accommodate the full range of current population body sizes.

The consequences of designing exclusively to mid-range body size parameters include:

- Seating without armrests, or with armrests too narrow, in public waiting areas, theatres, and transport.
- Inadequate turning circle in toilet facilities for ambulant large-body users.
- Structural loading of floors, beds, examination tables, and seating that fails under body weights above 150–160 kg.
- Accessible toilet grab bars specified at 300 kg load that are not provided because the standard is 200 kg.
- Insufficient aisle and door widths to permit comfortable passage without sideways orientation.
- Weight-rated medical equipment (examination tables, imaging equipment) with insufficient load capacity.
- Dignity barriers: being unable to sit comfortably, navigate a space, or use toilet facilities independently.

These are design failures, not the person's failure to fit a standard.

## IV.2 Anthropometric and Population Context

**Population prevalence:** The World Health Organization (2024) estimates that globally, more than 1 billion people are living with obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m²). In the UK, the Health Survey for England (2022) found approximately 26% of adults are obese and a further 38% overweight. In the USA, CDC NHANES data (2017–2020) documents adult obesity prevalence at approximately 42%. These are not fringe populations — they are approximately one quarter to one third of the adult population in high-income countries.

**Design-relevant body dimensions:** Body width (shoulder width and abdominal width) and weight are the primary design variables. Relevant reference data:

| Parameter | Population 95th percentile (male, European) | Design target for large body size |
|-----------|--------------------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Shoulder width | \~530 mm | ≥600 mm clear passage |
| Seated hip width | \~420 mm | ≥550 mm seat width |
| Body weight | \~107 kg | Equipment to ≥250 kg; designated to 300 kg |
| Abdominal projection (seated) | \~300 mm forward | Desk knee clearance ≥350 mm |

Sources: DIN 33402-2 (2005) German anthropometric data; Pheasant \& Haslegrave (2006) *Bodyspace*; NHS/NHSE bariatric design guidance (2023).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Body dimension data for persons above the 99th percentile is sparse in published anthropometric literature. The 250–300 kg structural load target is derived from NHS bariatric design guidance and clinical engineering standards, not from population anthropometric surveys.

## IV.3 BAR Design Item Specifications

---

### BAR-01 — Entry Doors and Clear Opening Width

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook entry door items; E-06

**Rationale:** Standard accessible door clear opening (850–900 mm in most European standards; 812 mm per ADA) is insufficient for comfortable passage by persons of large body size, who may require 950 mm or more clear opening.

**Specifications:**

- All new public buildings: primary entry door clear opening minimum 950 mm.
- Where large body size populations are a significant user group (bariatric healthcare, community health centres, pharmacies, GP practices, supermarkets): minimum 1000 mm clear opening.
- Power-assisted or automatic doors strongly preferred — eliminates the need to manage door weight while negotiating clearance.
- Revolving doors: not appropriate as sole entry where large body size users are anticipated. Bypass door minimum 1000 mm clear opening must always be provided.
- Accessible parking bays serving large body size users: bay width minimum 4500 mm (vs standard accessible bay 3600 mm + 1200 mm) to allow door opening without tight lateral clearance.

**Standards cited:** BS 8300:2018 §9.4; ADA §404; main guidebook entry items.

---

### BAR-02 — Corridor and Circulation Width

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook item E-08; corridor specifications

**Specifications:**

- Minimum corridor clear width: 1500 mm for primary circulation in buildings where large body size users are a significant population (all healthcare settings, all public buildings).
- Preferred: 1800 mm in healthcare settings to permit two-way ambulant passage without contact.
- Where equipment (beds, trolleys, hoists) is moved through corridors in healthcare settings, minimum 1800 mm is required for BAR-designated patient areas.
- Aisle widths between fixed objects (shelving, seating rows, displays): minimum 1200 mm in retail and public assembly settings.

**Standards cited:** Main guidebook E-08; BS 8300:2018; NHS Estate Design Guidance for Bariatric Care (NHSE, 2023).

---

### BAR-03 — Turning Circle

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook item I-01

**Rationale:** The standard 1500 mm turning circle for wheelchair users is based on a standard manual wheelchair. Larger body size, with or without a mobility aid, may require an increased turning circle.

**Specifications:**

- Standard accessible facilities serving large body size users: minimum turning circle 1800 mm.
- Accessible toilet cubicles (BAR-designated): minimum 1800 mm turning circle, measured from door leaf to opposite wall in closed-door configuration.
- Hospital rooms (bariatric-designated): minimum 1800 mm unobstructed floor space around all sides of the bed.

**Standards cited:** Main guidebook I-01; NHS NHSE bariatric design guidance (2023); BS 8300:2018.

---

### BAR-04 — Seating and Public Furniture

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook seating provisions

**Problem:** Standard fixed public seating — with or without armrests — typically provides 480–520 mm between armrests. This is insufficient for many persons of large body size, creating a dignity and access barrier in waiting areas, transport, theatres, and public buildings.

**Specifications:**

- All public waiting areas and waiting rooms: minimum 10% of seating to be armrest-free, with seat width ≥600 mm.
- At least two adjacent seats of appropriate width should be available without armrests in any seating bank of 10 or more.
- Seat depth: minimum 450 mm.
- Seat structural load: minimum 250 kg per seat in all public seating. Specify minimum 300 kg for seating in healthcare waiting areas.
- Bench seating (no individual armrests): ensure seating is continuous with a minimum 700 mm per seated person when fully occupied by large body size users.
- Chairs with armrests: minimum 580 mm between armrests.
- Avoid chairs with inward-tapering seats; all seats should maintain consistent width at seat surface.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Seating specifications derived from NHS NHSE bariatric design guidance and clinical equipment standards. No OT study specifically addressing public seating provision for large body size persons was identified.

**Standards cited:** NHS NHSE bariatric patient environment guidelines (2023); BS EN 1335 (office furniture seating, load capacity).

---

### BAR-05 — Accessible Toilet Facilities

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook items I-03, I-04; K-03

This is the highest-priority item for persons of large body size in public buildings.

**Specifications:**

- Cubicle dimensions: BAR-accessible WC cubicle minimum 1800 mm × 2200 mm (versus standard accessible 1500 mm × 2000 mm). This provides the 1800 mm turning circle (BAR-03) within the closed cubicle.
- Toilet seat height: 430–480 mm AFF. Higher seat height (480 mm) recommended to assist rising from the toilet for persons with limited mobility in addition to large body size.
- WC pan and floor fixings: structural weight-rated. WC pan and fixings: minimum 300 kg rated.
- Grab bars: minimum 300 kg rated. This must be specified explicitly — standard accessible grab bars are typically rated at 200 kg; bariatric grab bars at 300 kg are available and must be specified as the standard in all BAR-accessible toilet facilities.
- Wall backing/noggins: solid structural noggins in all walls of a BAR-accessible WC cubicle at grab bar positions (700–800 mm AFF throughout the cubicle) to permit future bar installation at any position.
- Drop-down rear grab bar: 300 kg rated.
- Wash basin and tap: standard wheelchair provision (760 mm AFF) — appropriate for large body size users.
- Floor: structural loading per BAR-06.
- Door: minimum 950 mm clear opening; outward-opening preferred, or sliding. Power-assisted opening recommended.

**Standards cited:** BS 8300:2018 (adapted); NHS NHSE bariatric design guidance (2023); main guidebook I-03/I-04.

---

### BAR-06 — Structural Floor Loading

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook structural notes; sessions\_3to6.md structural provisions

**Rationale:** Standard structural floor loading in residential and commercial buildings is typically designed to 1.5–2.0 kN/m² for general occupation, with furniture and equipment superimposed loads specified separately. Persons of large body size create point loads that may exceed standard assumptions at specific positions — at drain points, grab bar anchor positions, and examination table/bed locations.

**Specifications:**

- Structural floor review required in all BAR-designated areas (accessible WC, bariatric hospital rooms, bariatric examination rooms).
- Minimum point load capacity at drain position: 300 kg.
- Minimum point load capacity at all grab bar anchor positions: 300 kg.
- Bariatric hospital beds: structural engineer to review floor capacity for bed + patient combined load. NHS standard bariatric bed weight: 150–250 kg tare; patient weight: up to 300 kg; combined point load at bed leg positions may exceed 500 kg.
- Note: this is a structural engineering specification, not an architectural one. A structural engineer must sign off floor capacity in all bariatric-designated areas before completion.

**Standards cited:** BS EN 1990–1994 structural Eurocodes (load calculations); NHS NHSE bariatric design guidance (2023).

---

### BAR-07 — Clinical and Medical Equipment (Healthcare Settings Only)

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook healthcare building typology

**Note:** This item applies specifically to healthcare settings where clinical equipment is used.

**Specifications:**

- Examination tables / treatment couches: minimum weight capacity 300 kg. Height-adjustable to ≤450 mm for patient mounting. Minimum width 600 mm (standard 500 mm is inadequate).
- Imaging equipment (CT, MRI, X-ray): standard CT bore diameter (\~700 mm) is insufficient for many persons of large body size. Where BAR patients are served, specify bore diameter minimum 800 mm and table weight capacity 300 kg. At least one bariatric-capable imaging suite per hospital campus is considered minimum provision in NHS and NHSE guidance (2023).
- Hoist systems: overhead tracked hoists in bariatric patient rooms minimum 300 kg rated. Floor hoist minimum 300 kg rated.
- Operating tables (bariatric surgical suite): minimum 450 kg capacity; width 550–600 mm; powered positioning throughout.
- Shower and bathing: bariatric shower trolley capacity 300 kg; bariatric bath hoist if provided 300 kg; level-access shower base 300 kg rated.
- Patient weigh scales: minimum 300 kg capacity in all clinical areas. At least one 500 kg capacity scale per hospital campus.

**Standards cited:** NHS NHSE bariatric design guidance (2023); MHRA guidance on bariatric medical devices (2022); BS EN 1970:2000+A1:2013 (adjustable beds for disabled persons).

---

### BAR-08 — Signage and Dignity Provisions

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook wayfinding items

**Note:** This item addresses the dignity and social aspects of large body size provisions in public and healthcare buildings.

**Specifications:**

- BAR-accessible toilet facilities should be provided with neutral signage that does not single out the user's body size. Where a facility is designated for both large body size and mobility impairment users, "Accessible Toilet" signage (not "Bariatric Toilet") is appropriate.
- Gown and clothing storage in healthcare settings: provide gowns in a full range of sizes, stored accessibly. Gown storage at 1200 mm AFF maximum.
- Privacy provisions in healthcare: curtain tracks, privacy screens, and examination room dimensions must permit full privacy for patients of large body size. Standard cubicle curtains that do not reach the floor create dignity barriers.

## IV.6 OT Practice Notes: Large Body Size

### Assessment Framework

OT practitioners working with persons of large body size should apply the PEO model (Law et al., 1996), examining:

1. **Seating:** Can the person sit comfortably without contact constraint at home, in the workplace, and in public settings?
2. **Toilet facilities:** Can the person use toilet facilities independently and with dignity in their primary environments?
3. **Structural adequacy:** Are the floors, fittings, and equipment in the person's home and healthcare environments structurally adequate for the person's weight?
4. **Transfer and mobility:** Where large body size is combined with mobility impairment, hoist and transfer provisions must be rated to the individual's weight. This is a safety-critical item.
5. **Healthcare encounters:** Is clinical equipment (examination table, imaging) weight-rated and sized for the person? Inadequate equipment creates both safety risks and dignity barriers that may deter healthcare attendance.

### Escalation Threshold

Where an existing healthcare setting is used by patients of large body size and:

- Examination tables are not 300 kg rated
- Accessible WC grab bars are not 300 kg rated
- Hoists (where used) are not 300 kg rated

These are P1 items representing direct patient safety risk. Log as Gap Register entries and escalate to facilities management immediately.
