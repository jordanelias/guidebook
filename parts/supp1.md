## About This Volume

This supplementary volume addresses four distinct population groups whose access requirements are either absent from, or insufficiently represented in, mainstream international building accessibility standards and the main guidebook.

Each section in this volume is self-contained. The populations addressed are different in body size, medical profile, lived experience, and design requirements. They are presented together in a single volume because they share a structural challenge: existing built environment standards and design guidance have been developed around an assumed adult body of average height and average weight. People who fall outside that assumed norm — whether significantly smaller, significantly taller, or significantly larger in body mass — encounter systematic design failures in the built environment.

This volume does not treat these populations as equivalent, comparable, or interchangeable. Each section stands alone and should be read on its own terms.

## How to Use This Volume

**For architects and designers:** Each section provides dimensioned technical requirements, building typology matrices, and item specifications. Use the section relevant to the population you are designing for. Where a building serves multiple populations from this volume (e.g., a hospital serving both CHD and BAR users), read each section independently and apply the requirements from each without substitution.

**For OT practitioners:** Each section includes OT practice notes specific to the population. Evidence notes flag where the evidence base is thin or where recommendations are derived from first-principles or expert consensus rather than peer-reviewed OT studies.

**For reviewers and specifiers:** Cross-reference with the main guidebook using the item codes listed in each section. Where this volume extends or supersedes a main guidebook item, the revision is noted explicitly.

## Section I: Accessible Environments for Children (CHD)

---

## I.0 Purpose and Scope

All existing items in this guidebook — and in the international standards and clinical literature from which they derive — are specified against adult anthropometric norms. This produces systematic design failures in any built environment where children are primary users.

The purpose of this section is to provide architects, designers, and OT practitioners with:

1. A developmental-age framework for environment design (the CHD Age-Stage Framework)
2. Population sub-codes for prescribing age-appropriate specifications
3. Dimensioned technical requirements by age group, drawing on available standards
4. Building-typology matrices for the five primary child-use building types
5. Design item specifications (CHD-01 to CHD-11) cross-referenced to existing guidebook items
6. OT practice notes on the child-environment relationship

**Out of scope:** This section does not address paediatric clinical interventions, developmental therapy programmes, or educational pedagogy. It addresses the physical built environment as it enables or impedes a child's independent occupation, participation, and safety.

**Population included:** Children aged 0 through 17 years, inclusive of children with disabilities and children who use mobility aids. Children with disabilities cross-reference the main guidebook's MOB, VIS, NEU, and NDV population codes as appropriate; the CHD code addresses the scale and developmental dimension that those codes do not capture.

---

## I.1 Why Existing Standards Are Insufficient

Current accessibility standards are adult-centred. The majority of international standards — including ISO 21542:2021, ADA 2010, BS 8300:2018, DIN 18040-1, NS 11001-1, BCA 2025, CSA B651:2023 — base all technical dimensions on adult anthropometric data, typically the 5th to 95th percentile adult range. Children fall entirely outside this range during the years that matter most for built environment encounters.

The consequences are predictable and well-documented:

- Grab bars, handrails, and controls positioned for adults are out of reach or unsafe for children.
- Reception counters, desks, and service points are above children's sightlines and reach ranges.
- Toilet facilities designed for adults create hygiene, safety, and independence risks for young children.
- Wayfinding and signage systems assume literacy and reading heights appropriate for adults.
- Door hardware assumes adult grip strength and hand size.
- Playground and recreational equipment designed under safety standards may simultaneously fail accessibility requirements for children with mobility impairments.

The notable exception is the United States ADA, which includes advisory (non-mandatory) children's specifications for selected elements. These are documented and extended in this section. No equivalent consolidated children's provision exists in any other reviewed jurisdiction's main accessibility standard.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Evidence-based design for paediatric facilities, as a field, is acknowledged to be substantially less developed than for adult facilities. Fricke et al. (2019) found in a systematic review that evidence-based design knowledge is focused on adult patients and not consistently adjusted for children. This supplementary section synthesises the best available evidence; architects should commission post-occupancy evaluations (POEs) in completed children's facilities to contribute to the evidence base.

## I.2 Population Sub-Codes: CHD Age-Stage Framework

The CHD (Children) population uses a five-band age-stage framework. The bands are drawn from developmental psychology, OT developmental literature, and the age groupings used in the ADA children's provisions, WHO growth standards, and paediatric OT practice.

**Table I.2.1 — CHD Sub-Codes**

| Sub-code | Age band | Developmental characterisation | Key design drivers |
|----------|----------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| CHD/INF | 0–2 years | Infant/toddler; pre-ambulatory to early walking; entirely dependent on caregiver for environment navigation | Caregiver–child dyad design; pram/stroller access; nappy-change provisions; feeding areas; floor-level safety |
| CHD/PRE | 3–5 years | Preschool; full ambulatory; spatial exploration; early independence; fine motor emerging; limited reading literacy | Scaled sanitary provisions; low-reach storage and controls; sensory-rich environments; wayfinding by colour and form (not text) |
| CHD/ESA | 6–8 years | Early school age; fine motor refining; spatial cognition developing; beginning independent wayfinding | Dual-height provisions; pictographic and text wayfinding; scaled toilet provisions; accessible play equipment |
| CHD/MCA | 9–12 years | Middle childhood; approaching adolescent physical dimensions; independent environment navigation; peer group important | Transitional dimensions (child → adult); accessible lockers, controls, drinking fountains |
| CHD/ADO | 13–17 years | Adolescent; majority approaching adult dimensions by 15–16; design may converge with adult provisions above 15 | Privacy provisions; mixed-age facility design; adult-transitional specifications |

**Note on use:** The sub-codes are applied to technical specifications. Where a building serves a specific age group (e.g., a primary school: predominantly CHD/PRE and CHD/ESA), specifications should be selected for the appropriate age band. Where a building serves mixed ages (e.g., a children's hospital: CHD/INF through CHD/ADO), provide graduated or adjustable provisions.

**Note on children with disabilities:** A child who uses a manual wheelchair, for example, requires BOTH the MOB wheelchair-user clearances from the main guidebook AND the CHD/ESA (or appropriate band) scaled dimensions. The narrower or more demanding requirement of the two applies for each element — typically the adult-wheelchair clearance floor plan but the child-scaled heights for all operable elements.

## I.4 Developmental Environment Theory: OT Framework

Occupational therapists working with children apply a set of theoretical frameworks that directly inform built environment design.

**Person-Environment-Occupation (PEO) Model** (Law et al., 1996): Occupational performance emerges from the transaction between a person (the child), their environment (the built setting), and the occupation (what the child is trying to do). Misfit between environment and child capacity produces barriers to participation.

**Ecological Psychology — Affordances** (Gibson, 1979): The environment offers affordances to users — possibilities for action relative to the user's body and capacities. A step that affords climbing for a 10-year-old does not afford climbing for a 3-year-old. Design must provide affordances appropriate to the intended age groups.

**Sensory Integration** (Ayres, 1972; reviewed Frontiers in Psychology, 2024): The built environment is the primary context in which children develop and regulate sensory processing. Environmental characteristics — acoustic treatment, lighting, texture, spatial complexity — directly shape sensory integration outcomes.

**Developmental Stage–Environment Matching** (Moore, 1994, cited Frontiers in Psychology, 2024): Environments should match developmental stage. Preschool environments supporting exploratory, play-based learning differ fundamentally from middle-childhood environments supporting focused task performance.

**OT Practice Note:** OT practitioners commissioned to advise on children's facilities should apply the PEO Model to assess fit between the child population's developmental profile, the intended occupational roles (play, learning, healthcare, rest), and the environment. CHD specifications in this section provide minimum technical baselines; the OT practitioner's role is to identify where individual children require further environmental adaptation beyond these baselines.

## I.5 Acoustic Environment — Children-Specific Considerations

**Signal-to-Noise Ratio in Classrooms:** Speech intelligibility requires higher signal-to-noise ratios for children than adults because children's auditory processing is not fully mature. ANSI/ASA S12.60 (Acoustical Performance Criteria for School Facilities) specifies maximum background noise levels of 35 dB(A) in core learning spaces and 45 dB(A) in ancillary spaces.

**Reverberation Times:** ANSI/ASA S12.60 specifies maximum reverberation times of 0.6 seconds for classrooms ≤283 m³ and 0.7 seconds for larger rooms. Building Bulletin 93 (BB93, England and Wales, Department for Education) sets equivalent requirements (unoccupied RT ≤ 0.6 s in classrooms ≤250 m³). DIN 18041 (Germany) and NS 8175 (Norway) establish comparable requirements.

**Children with Hearing Impairment:** Children with hearing impairment are more significantly affected by poor classroom acoustics than adults with equivalent hearing loss, because they simultaneously manage hearing deficit, auditory processing development, and language acquisition. Hearing loops (main guidebook item A-10) are required in all formal learning spaces serving children with hearing impairment.

**Sensory Environment for Neurodivergent Children:** Settings serving children with autism spectrum conditions, sensory processing differences, or ADHD require particular acoustic management (cross-reference NDV specifications). The ASPECTSS design index (Mostafa, 2021) includes acoustics as a primary design variable.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Lersilp et al. (2021, *Child Care in Practice*, Chiang Mai University) identified acoustic and visual overstimulation as the two most common environmental barriers to learning and participation for preschool children with sensory processing differences. This is consistent with OT clinical practice but is based on a small-scale study in Thailand; cross-cultural validation recommended.

## I.6 Lighting — Children-Specific Considerations

**Paediatric Healthcare:** Multiple studies confirm that natural light access reduces anxiety and stress in hospitalised children. Litwin et al. (2023), in an OT-led co-design study at a tertiary-care children's hospital (*HERD* Journal), identified natural light as one of the primary co-designed features. Fricke et al. (2019) confirmed that light, colour, and view to nature are among the few design elements with consistent positive outcome evidence in paediatric settings.

**Education and Childcare:** Children in daylit classrooms demonstrate improved attention and reduced cortisol stress responses compared to artificially lit environments (Hathaway et al., 1992). North-facing or diffuse daylight is preferable to avoid glare.

**Lighting Levels:** For children aged 3 and above, recommended classroom illuminance is 300–500 lux at task surface; healthcare procedure rooms for children may require up to 1000 lux at the procedure area.

**Wayfinding Signage:** Children in CHD/PRE and CHD/INF bands cannot read text; wayfinding must rely on colour, pictogram, floor graphics, and landmark design at child eye-height (approximately 880–1000 mm for ages 3–5). This is addressed in design item CHD-05.

### CHD-01 — Children's Sanitary Provisions: Water Closet

**Population:** CHD/PRE, CHD/ESA, CHD/MCA

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook items I-03, E-06

**Rationale:** A toilet seat at adult height (430–485 mm AFF) leaves a child's feet entirely unsupported, creates a tipping risk, and prevents independent use of flush controls and toilet paper dispensers. ADA §604.9 is the only confirmed international provision addressing children's WC dimensions; no equivalent mandatory provision exists in BS 8300, DIN 18040, ISO 21542, or other reviewed standards.

**Table I.7.1 — Age-Band WC Specifications**

| Parameter | Ages 3–4 (CHD/PRE) | Ages 5–8 (CHD/ESA) | Ages 9–12 (CHD/MCA) |
|-----------|--------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| WC seat height (AFF) | 280–305 mm | 305–380 mm | 380–430 mm |
| WC centreline from side wall | 305 mm | 380 mm | 380–455 mm |
| Grab bar (side wall), top of gripping surface AFF | 455–510 mm | 510–635 mm | 635–685 mm |
| Grab bar (rear wall), top of gripping surface AFF | 455–510 mm | 510–635 mm | 635–685 mm |
| Flush control max height AFF | 915 mm | 1015 mm | 1115 mm |
| TP dispenser outlet AFF | 355–485 mm | 355–485 mm | 355–485 mm |

**Technical notes:**

- Where a facility serves a single age group, select all specifications from one column consistently.
- Where a facility serves mixed ages, provide both CHD/PRE and CHD/ESA cubicles in at least one accessible toilet facility per floor.
- Specify an integral footstool at 150 mm height for CHD/PRE to provide foot support — this also improves defecation posture.
- Grab bars at children's heights do NOT satisfy adult grab bar requirements. An accessible toilet facility serving adult carers accompanying children must also meet adult specifications (main guidebook I-03).
- Automatic flush is preferred for all children's cubicles.

**Standards cited:** ADA 2010 §604.9; ADA Final Rule on Children's Facilities (61 FR 37964, 1996).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No equivalent mandatory provision confirmed in BS 8300:2018, DIN 18040-1/2, ISO 21542:2021, NS 11001-1/2, BCA 2025, CSA B651:2023.

### CHD-04 — Stair and Ramp Handrail — Child Height

**Population:** All CHD bands where stairs or ramps are present

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook vertical circulation items

**Rationale:** Standard handrail height (865–1000 mm AFF per ISO 21542 and most national standards) is too high for children to grip safely. Children's falls on stairs and ramps are a significant injury risk exacerbated by inaccessible handrail heights.

**Specification:**

- Where a stair or ramp serves children as primary users, a secondary (child-height) handrail is required in addition to the standard adult-height rail.
- Child handrail: 600–750 mm AFF (Sensory Trust UK guidance; consistent with ADA §505 which permits handrail heights between 510–710 mm on playground ramps).
- Both rails must be continuous along the full run, with 300 mm horizontal extension beyond top and bottom.
- Diameter: 25–32 mm (smaller than adult 35–50 mm to suit smaller hand grip).
- Material: smooth continuous surface, no protrusions, rounded returns at each end.
- Dual-rail configuration: adult rail at 865–1000 mm (standard); child rail at 600–750 mm.
- For facilities serving CHD/INF: a third rail at 400–500 mm may be appropriate for toddlers learning to use stairs independently — structural engineering clearance required.

**Standards cited:** ISO 21542:2021 (adult standard, 865–1000 mm); ADA §505; ADA §1002.5 (play areas: 510–710 mm); Sensory Trust UK (mid-rail 600–750 mm).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No confirmed mandatory dual-rail standard in reviewed national codes.

**Caution:** Dual handrails increase obstruction risk at landings. Ensure landing width accommodates both rails without reducing accessible turning circle.

### CHD-05 — Child-Scale Wayfinding

**Population:** CHD/PRE, CHD/ESA

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook wayfinding items

**Rationale:** Wayfinding systems are designed at adult eye level (\~1500–1700 mm). Children in CHD/PRE have eye levels of 880–970 mm. CHD/ESA eye levels are 1000–1120 mm. Text-based signage is inaccessible to pre-literate children.

**Specifications:**

- Primary wayfinding signage: lower edge at ≤900 mm AFF for CHD/PRE areas; ≤1100 mm for CHD/ESA areas.
- Floor-level wayfinding: colour-coded floor-path systems at ≤50 mm above floor (floor vinyl or inlay); particularly effective in paediatric hospital corridors. Colour must contrast ≥30 LRV with floor surface.
- Pictograms mandatory (not text-only) in all areas serving CHD/PRE. Minimum 100 mm × 100 mm.
- Room identification: combine pictogram and text at child eye level + adult eye level (two-level mounting).
- Landmark design: distinctive visual landmarks (colour accent, sculptural element, mural) at key decision points. Evidence: wayfinding by landmark is more effective than directional signage for populations with limited cognitive mapping capacity (Marquardt, 2011).
- In paediatric hospitals: colour-coding by department/zone is consistently rated positively by children and caregivers (Yu et al., 2024, *HERD Journal*).

**Standards cited:** ADA §703 (general principles); Yu et al. (2024) scoping review; Marquardt (2011) landmark wayfinding evidence.

### CHD-06 — Safe Floor Surfaces for Children

**Population:** CHD/INF, CHD/PRE

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook floor specifications

**Specifications:**

- Play areas (CHD/INF, CHD/PRE): impact-attenuating surface required. ASTM F1292 critical fall height tested material below all elevated play components.
- Indoor floor surfaces (CHD/PRE and above): coefficient of friction ≥0.6 (wet).
- Hard flooring (concrete, ceramic tile) without cushioning: not appropriate as primary floor material in CHD/INF rooms where floor play is primary occupation. Cork, rubber, and quality vinyl are preferred.
- Transition strips between floor materials: maximum 6 mm height.
- Carpeted areas: loop-pile carpet at ≤8 mm pile height. Deeper piles impede wheelchair and walking frame access for children with MOB.

**Standards cited:** ASTM F1292; main guidebook floor specifications.

### CHD-07 — Changing Facilities (Infant/Toddler)

**Population:** CHD/INF, and parents/carers of CHD/INF

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook sanitary provisions

**Rationale:** Nappy-changing facilities are absent from all reviewed international accessibility standards, yet are critical environmental provisions in any building regularly used by families with infants.

**Specifications:**

- Required in any building where CHD/INF populations are a significant user group. Minimum one dedicated changing facility per building; locate on every occupied floor.
- Location: within or immediately adjacent to accessible toilet facility; accessible to wheelchair-using parents and carers.
- Changing table: height-adjustable 450–900 mm AFF to accommodate seated (wheelchair-using) and standing carers.
- Minimum table dimensions: 600 mm wide × 900 mm deep.
- Structural load: 100 kg minimum (table surface).
- Side protection: raised edge ≥75 mm on all sides except approach side.
- Approach space: minimum 900 mm clearance on approach side; 1500 mm turning circle for wheelchair-using carer.
- Waste disposal and nappy bag dispenser: within arm reach of changing table (maximum 1200 mm AFF).
- Handwash basin: immediately adjacent; sensor tap; maximum 1100 mm AFF.

**Standards cited:** BS 8300:2018 Annex D (guidance, not mandatory); ASTM F2285-04 (Standard Consumer Safety Performance Specification for Diaper Changing Tables).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No confirmed mandatory international standard for baby changing facilities exists in reviewed accessibility codes.

### CHD-08 — Drinking Fountain / Water Dispenser

**Population:** CHD/PRE, CHD/ESA, CHD/MCA

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook items

**Rationale:** Standard drinking fountain spout height (890–1115 mm AFF per ADA; typically 800–1000 mm in European standards) is inaccessible for CHD/PRE children without step assistance.

**Specifications:**

- Children's fountain: spout maximum 760 mm AFF (ADA §602.6 children's exception — parallel approach where spout ≤760 mm).
- Minimum one adult-height fountain must be provided in addition (ADA §602 requires minimum one of each where fountains are provided).
- Alternatively: water dispensers with front-fill or low spout at 600–760 mm AFF; lever or push-button activation at 700–900 mm AFF.
- Operating force: ≤22 N.

**Standards cited:** ADA 2010 §602.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No equivalent children's fountain provision confirmed in BS 8300, DIN 18040-1, or ISO 21542.

### CHD-10 — Sensory Environment Design in Children's Settings

**Population:** All CHD bands; specifically NDV children

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook NDV items; item G-01

**Rationale:** The sensory environment has documented impacts on children's learning, wellbeing, and stress responses. This applies universally, and is intensified for children with sensory processing differences, autism spectrum conditions, anxiety disorders, or post-traumatic stress.

**ASPECTSS Design Index** (Mostafa, 2021, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*):

| ASPECTSS variable | Design principle |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| **A**coustics | Controlled reverberation; acoustic absorbers; quiet zones |
| **S**pace sequencing | Predictable space sequence; clear circulation logic |
| **P**ace escape | Accessible sensory retreat/break-out space within each zone |
| **C**ompartmentalisation | Visual and acoustic subdivision of large spaces |
| **T**ransition | Transition zones between environments (lobbies, decompression corridors) |
| **S**ensory zoning | Low-stimulation vs high-stimulation zones clearly delineated |
| **S**afety | Reduced hazard; predictable spatial boundaries |

**Key minimum provisions for all children's buildings:**

- At least one designated low-stimulation room or alcove per zone.
- Acoustic treatment to ANSI/ASA S12.60 or BB93 in all formal learning spaces.
- Lighting: dimmable; no high-frequency fluorescent flicker.
- Avoid high-contrast clashing patterns in main circulation; reserve visual richness for play zones.

**Standards cited:** Mostafa (2021); ANSI/ASA S12.60-2010; Department for Education UK BB93 (2015); Lersilp et al. (2021).

### CHD-11 — Outdoor Play Accessibility

**Population:** CHD/PRE, CHD/ESA, CHD/MCA

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook recreational provisions

**Key specifications:**

- Accessible route: minimum 1524 mm wide main route through play area; sub-routes 915 mm minimum.
- Surfacing: unitary (poured-in-place rubber, tiles, or play turf) preferred over loose fill for accessible routes. Engineered wood fibre acceptable if maintained to ASTM F1951-99 firmness standard.
- Elevated play component access: ramps preferred over transfer systems (ADA §1008.2).
- Ramp handrail on elevated structures: 510–710 mm AFF (ADA §1002.5).
- Ground-level play components: minimum 50% of all ground-level components on accessible route.
- Transfer platform height: 280–455 mm (ADA §1008.3.2 default 355–455 mm; lower range supports CHD/PRE).
- Shade: required over critical play areas in jurisdictions where UV index regularly exceeds 3.

**Standards cited:** ADA §1008; ASTM F1951-99; AS 4685 (Australia).

### CHD-BT-03: Childcare Facility (Nursery / Crèche / Preschool)

**Key considerations beyond school standards:**

- All sanitary facilities scaled for CHD/INF and CHD/PRE exclusively.
- Nappy changing facilities (CHD-07) are primary, not secondary provisions.
- Floor-level activities are primary occupation: impact-attenuating floor surfaces throughout.
- No standard adult-scale elements in primary child areas (except adult staff workpoints).
- Sleep/rest area: dedicated room with individual cots; acoustic separation from active play; blackout capability; accessible to wheelchair-using parents.
- Outdoor play: required daily access; accessible outdoor space per CHD-11.

**Evidence:** Lersilp et al. (2021); European Commission ECEC framework quality guidelines (2014).

---

### CHD-BT-04: Indoor Recreational Facility (Sports / Leisure / Swimming)

**Key additional provisions:**

- Swimming pools: steps or ramp pool access (not ladder-only). Handrail at child height (CHD-04) on pool steps. Pool lift for children with mobility impairment (cross-reference MOB items).
- Changing rooms: child-height changing benches (320–380 mm AFF); lockable doors operable at child height; hooks within CHD reach range (CHD-03).
- Sports halls: accessible from main entrance with no steps to court level.
- Spectator seating: sightlines that accommodate seated wheelchair users and children — avoid solid perimeter barriers that block children's sightlines.

---

### CHD-BT-05: Children's Library

**Key provisions:**

- Shelving: accessible to CHD/ESA at maximum reach (1015 mm AFF top shelf). Higher shelves for adult staff use only — clear differentiation required.
- CHD/PRE floor-level seating and reading areas: carpet or cushioned mat area; low-table reading spaces.
- Computer/digital terminals: child-height fixed provision (530–680 mm AFF desk surface for CHD/ESA) or adjustable height.
- Wayfinding: pictographic shelf identifiers at child eye level (CHD-05 standard).
- Quiet zone: designated low-stimulation reading area with acoustic treatment (CHD-10).

## I.9 OT Practice Notes: Children's Environments

### Assessment Questions — Children's Facility

1. **Age-band match:** Do the facility's fixed dimensions (WC, basin, furniture, handrails, controls) match the age band(s) of the children primarily served?
2. **Caregiver-child dyad:** Where children require caregiver support (CHD/INF, children with complex disabilities), can the caregiver operate alongside the child without spatial conflict?
3. **Sensory environment:** Does the acoustic, lighting, and visual complexity of the environment suit the sensory profile of the child population? Are sensory retreat spaces available?
4. **Literacy-independent wayfinding:** Can a pre-literate child navigate to the toilet, the play area, and the exit independently?
5. **Outdoor play inclusion:** Can children with mobility impairments access outdoor play on an equivalent basis to ambulant peers?
6. **Transition support:** Are there physical transition zones (decompression lobbies, low-stimulation corridors) between busy and quiet spaces?

### Escalation Threshold — Existing Buildings

Where an existing building used by children fails on items CHD-01 (WC height), CHD-04 (handrail), or CHD-10 (no quiet space), these are P1-level gaps. Children's welfare depends on baseline sanitary independence, fall prevention, and sensory regulation capacity.

Gap Register entry recommended where any CHD-BT-01 building fails to have age-appropriate WC provisions or a sensory quiet area — these represent direct risk to child wellbeing during healthcare encounters.
