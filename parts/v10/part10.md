### §10.0 Scope

Part 10 addresses buildings designed to adapt to changing functional needs without full reconstruction. The core argument is asymmetric: the cost of not designing for adaptability is never zero. The most critical accessible features are prohibitively expensive as retrofits but negligible at construction when embedded in schematic design.

The DAR principle rests on a single empirical observation confirmed across all reviewed jurisdictions: a DAR provision absent from a named construction drawing will not be built. Specification text without drawing reference is not executed by contractors. Every DAR provision in this Part must therefore appear on a named structural, electrical, or floor plan drawing by Construction Documentation stage.

For the full international cost evidence base, see Part 11. For case study evidence of DAR provisions in practice, see Part 12.

### §10.1 The DAR Cost Multiplier Framework

All figures are approximate industry estimates (UK/CA data, Tier 4–5). The ×75 multiplier for grab bar blocking is widely cited but UNVERIFIED — no formal cost study has been identified; direction is unambiguous, magnitude is approximate.

| Provision | At construction | Retrofit cost | Multiplier | Drawing required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grab bar blocking (structural plywood, all WC/shower/bath positions) | £40 / CAD $68 | £3,000 / CAD $680–$5,100 | ×75 | Structural/blocking drawings |
| Ceiling hoist tracking blocking (continuous run ≥3600 mm, centred on bed zone) | £25 / CAD $43 | £3,000–£6,000 / CAD $5,100–$10,200 | ×20–40 | Structural drawings |
| Through-floor lift structural zone (≥900×1200 mm, same position each floor) | £2,000–£5,000 / CAD $3,400–$8,500 | £20,000–£50,000 / CAD $34,000–$85,000 | ×50+ | Structural drawings (SE) |
| Wider bathroom (+1 m²) | £1,500 / CAD $1,360–$2,550 | £15,000–£40,000 / CAD $25,500–$68,000 | ×10–25 | Floor plan |
| Level entry (zero-step approach) | £2,000 / CAD $850–$3,400 | £5,000–£30,000 / CAD $8,500–$51,000 | ×2.5–15 | Section drawings |
| Stairlift structural channel (≥850 mm clear stair width + conduit) | £500 / CAD $340–$850 | £2,000 + unit / CAD $1,360–$3,400 + unit | ×15–30 | Structural drawings |
| DAR framing for wider door (≥950 mm structural opening) | £200/door / CAD $340/door | £1,500–£5,000/door / CAD $2,550–$8,500/door | ×5–15 | Floor plan/door schedule |
| Smart lock/control conduit | £100 / CAD $170 | £800–£1,500 / CAD $1,360–$2,550 | ×8 | Electrical drawings |
| EV charger conduit (garage/parking) | £200 / CAD $340 | £1,000–£2,000 / CAD $1,700–$3,400 | ×5 | Electrical drawings |

**Commissioning verification protocol:** Photographic record of all blocking before wall lining; structural drawings filed with O&M manual; conduit test-pull before plastering; signed-off checklist from site accessibility inspector at each DAR milestone. (Source: residential-dar-provisions-priority-register BPC.)

**Policy implication:** DAR provision in new construction is the highest-return accessible design investment available. The combined DAR register above costs approximately £5,000–£10,000 per dwelling at construction — less than the cost of a single grab bar retrofit in an existing bathroom.

### §10.2 CAN/ASC 2.8:2025 DAR Core Provisions

Canada's CAN/ASC 2.8:2025 specifies minimum DAR provisions for all new accessible-ready housing. Each provision is a rough-in at construction enabling future modification without structural demolition.

| Code | Provision | Standard reference |
|---|---|---|
| DAR-01 | Entry: step-free approach; ≥950 mm structural opening; automatic door conduit; covered entry provision | Clause 7.2 |
| DAR-02 | Ground floor / elevator-accessible floor: all essential rooms on one lift-accessible level OR through-floor lift shaft zone ≥900×1400 mm with structural provision | Clause 7.4 |
| DAR-03 | Bathroom: bilateral grab bar blocking at 800–900 mm AFF; shower framed for future wet room; floor loading ≥135 kg; bathroom ≥1200×1800 mm | Clause 7.5 |
| DAR-04 | Ceiling hoist: structural provision along transfer path (bedroom → bathroom); ceiling joists ≥135 kg point load per bracket at 600 mm spacing; electrical conduit to accessible panel | Clause 7.6 |
| DAR-05 | Stairlift: stair clear width ≥850 mm; electrical conduit from bottom to top landing; structural blocking at head and foot | Clause 7.7 |
| DAR-06 | Controls/technology: conduit to all major control points; smart home hub provision; WiFi AP conduit | Clause 7.8 |

### §10.3 Lifetime Homes 16 Criteria (UK)

The UK Lifetime Homes standard (Habinteg 2010; now superseded by Part M4(2) in building regulations but remains influential) specifies 16 design criteria. M4(1) to M4(2) adaptation cost is approximately £27,000 per household (grab rail + stairlift + wet room) — the cost that DAR provisions at construction would have avoided.

Key statistics (Habinteg Forecast 2025): only 12.6% of England's housing stock has basic access features. M4(3) wheelchair standard homes: 3.3% of new homes planned. 41% of new homes will not meet M4(2) or M4(3).

### §10.4 Visitability: The Minimum Standard

Visitability (Concrete Change, Eleanor Smith, 1987) proposes three core requirements for all new residential construction:

1. At least one zero-step entrance
2. Interior doors ≥815 mm (32 inches) clear passage on the main floor; hallways ≥915 mm (36 inches)
3. At least one accessible toilet room on the main floor

**Cost:** $100–600 per dwelling (<0.5% of typical construction cost). This is the documented minimum below which a dwelling functionally excludes wheelchair users from social participation at private homes.

**Jurisdiction matrix:**

| Jurisdiction | Standard | Status | Key provisions |
|---|---|---|---|
| US | Fair Housing Act + local visitability ordinances (Atlanta, Arizona, Texas, Illinois) | Partial (federal: multi-family only; local: varies) | Zero-step entrance; 815 mm doors; ground-floor WC |
| UK | Part M4(1) Building Regulations (mandatory); M4(2) optional; M4(3) wheelchair standard | M4(1) mandatory all new dwellings | Level approach; accessible WC |
| AU | NCC 2022 (mandatory — Livable Housing Australia Silver) | Mandatory all new Class 1a dwellings from 1 Oct 2023 | Step-free entrance; wider corridors; reinforced walls |
| CA | CAN/ASC B652; provincial codes vary | Provincial adoption varies; ON/BC leading | Visitability requirements in B652 |
| NO | TEK17 (mandatory) | Mandatory all new dwellings | Level entry; accessible bathroom; lift in ≥3 storeys |
| SE | BFS 2024:12 (Boverket) (mandatory) | Mandatory all apartments in multi-dwelling | Future conversion without structural modification |
| DE | DIN 18040-2 (voluntary; state adoption varies) | Building regulation in most Länder | Barrier-free residential (some include DAR) |

Visitability policy has produced an estimated 10,000+ visitable homes globally (Concrete Change). The evidence confirms that mandatory adoption (AU, NO, SE) produces higher population-level accessibility than voluntary standards.

### §10.5 Aging in Place: OT Evidence

The systematic review of home modifications for aging in place (PMC, 2024; literature 2010–2024) ranks evidence by impact:

1. **Bathroom modifications** (grab bars, shower seat, wet room): highest evidence for fall reduction, ADL independence, and care home admission prevention.
2. **Entry and threshold modifications** (level entry, ramp, automatic door): directly enable social participation.
3. **Lighting modifications** (motion-activated, night-light path, dimmable): high evidence for fall prevention; low cost; most commonly deferred until after a fall.
4. **Kitchen modifications** (lowered counters, pull-out storage, lever taps): high evidence for meal preparation independence.
5. **Environmental controls** (accessible thermostat, smart controls): moderate evidence; highest self-reported impact for episodic disability (OFS, PAIN).

The Australian evidence (Ainsworth, Aplin, de Jonge & Bennett 2022, Disability and Rehabilitation, Tier 1 qualitative) confirms that outcomes are co-produced by environment AND OT process quality — the modification alone is insufficient without OT assessment driving the specification. This supports the guidebook's Tier 2 framework: DAR provisions at Tier 1 (population-informed); individual specification at Tier 2 (OT assessment).

The Summer Foundation SDA evidence (Douglas et al. 2024, BMJ Open, Tier 1) provides the strongest available finding: "quality of support provided in the home is a greater determinant of tenant quality of life than the built environment." This does not reduce the case for DAR — it clarifies that DAR is necessary but not sufficient. Part 9 OT involvement is the complement.

### §10.6 Nordic Models

**Sweden — BFS 2024:12 (Boverket).** All apartments in multi-dwelling buildings must be designed for future conversion to fully accessible without structural modification. Bathroom structural blocking: mandatory. Entry door: ≥850 mm clear. Lift: required in all buildings ≥3 storeys.

**Norway — NS 11001-2:2018 (Standard Norge) + TEK17.** All new dwellings: accessible-ready specification. Multi-dwelling buildings: lift required from ground to all floors. Lever hardware, accessible electrical heights, bathroom structural support: all mandatory.

Both countries report significantly lower retrofit costs and higher aging-in-place rates in the decades after mandatory adaptable housing standards were adopted. The Nordic evidence provides the strongest available national-scale data for the policy value of mandatory DAR.

### §10.7 Case Study Evidence for DAR

The residential-accessible-home-case-studies BPC documents 15+ case studies with DAR-relevant findings across UK, AU, US, CA, and Nordic jurisdictions. Key findings:

**The Kelsey Ayer Station (San Jose, CA, 2024):** All 115 units built with DAR provisions; inclusive design community with 25% disability-designated tenancies. Demonstrates that DAR provisions in market-rate housing are commercially viable. (Source: The Kelsey / Enterprise Community Partners, Tier 2.)

**Summer Foundation SDA (Australia, ongoing):** >2,000 SDA apartments enrolled nationally. Purpose-built accessible apartments replacing group homes and residential aged care. BMJ Open peer-reviewed outcomes: transition support quality is as important as built environment specification. (Source: Douglas et al. 2024 BMJ Open, Tier 1.)

**Habinteg Housing Association (UK, ongoing):** Wheelchair-accessible homes with adjustable counters, wider entrances, wet rooms. Tenant-reported outcomes: independence in ADL, reduced anxiety, improved social participation. Part of mainstream integrated housing — not segregated disability housing. M4(1) to M4(2) adaptation cost ~£27,000 per household — the cost that construction-stage DAR would have prevented. (Source: SCIE 2023; Habinteg 2024/2025, Co-1/Tier 2.)

**BC Housing HAFI (Canada):** Programme evaluation of retrofit outcomes. Average CAD $7,500 modification cost per home represents ~30–40% of what would have been required without any DAR provisions. (Source: BC Housing, Tier 4.)

**Singapore HDB:** Grab bar blocking in all new HDB bathrooms. Flat-to-care-home transfer rate significantly lower in UD-specified blocks versus standard blocks (longitudinal, n>5,000). (Source: HDB programme data, Tier 4.)
