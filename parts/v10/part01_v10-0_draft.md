## Part 1: Foundations of Accessible Design

### §1.1 Definitions

**Accessibility.** Accessibility is the condition in which any person — regardless of physical, sensory, cognitive, or psychological characteristics — can independently, safely, and with dignity reach, enter, use, and exit a built environment and all its facilities.

This definition has three operative components:

**Independence.**

The person does not require another person's physical assistance to navigate or use the environment. Accessible design removes environmental barriers that create dependency. A well-designed accessible bathroom, for example, allows a wheelchair user to transfer, shower, and dress without assistance from a carer — which has profound implications for privacy, autonomy, and the economics of care.

**Safety.**

The environment does not create hazards specific to the person's impairment. A wet room floor that drains incorrectly is not merely inconvenient — it creates a fall hazard that can be catastrophic for a person with reduced balance. A fire alarm that delivers only an audio signal is not merely inadequate — it is life-threatening for a deaf occupant. Accessible design is, in significant part, safety design.

**Dignity.**

The accessible route, feature, or facility does not stigmatise, other, or degrade the person using it. A separate 'accessible entrance' at the back of a building next to a service area is a dignity failure even if it meets dimensional compliance. Accessible features that are aesthetically indistinguishable from the mainstream environment serve users without marking them as different. Functional access extends beyond physical dimensions.

True accessibility encompasses:

- **Physical access:** step-free routes, clear widths, turning space, lever hardware, reach zones
- **Sensory access:** visual contrast, tactile information, hearing loops, visual fire alarms, vibrotactile alerting
- **Cognitive access:** predictable layouts, clear wayfinding, plain language, sensory calm
- **Communicative access:** hearing loop coverage, captioning, pictogram signage, interpreter seating
- **Temporal access:** provisions remain accessible throughout the full range of time that people occupy the building
- **Emergency access:** evacuation provisions for all building users, not only ambulant non-disabled occupants (see Appendix D)

### Key Terms

**Impairment:** a condition of the body or mind. An impairment is a characteristic of the person.

**Disability:** the mismatch between a person's impairment and an environment that has not been designed to accommodate it. Disability is a characteristic of the person-environment interaction, not of the person alone.

**Accessible design / inclusive design:** design that eliminates the mismatch between impairment and environment — or reduces it where elimination is not achievable — restoring function, independence, and dignity.

**Universal design:** design that works for the widest possible range of users without adaptation or specialist provision.

**DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness):** construction-stage provisions that allow future accessibility modifications at minimal cost, without structural intervention. See §1.6.

**OT (Occupational Therapist):** a registered healthcare professional whose discipline is concerned with the relationship between person, environment, and occupation. OT is the highest-tier evidence source for accessible design in this document. See §1.5.

### §1.2 The Code-as-Floor Principle

Building codes represent legal minimums — not design targets, not expressions of best practice, and not statements about what constitutes a good environment for disabled people. This document operates above the code floor throughout. Statutory requirements are referenced as baselines only (Evidence Tier 6). Where codes conflict with higher-tier evidence, higher-tier evidence governs. The multilingual endorsements of the code-as-floor principle confirm that this position is not a minority view, but the unanimous position of every major national accessibility body and advocacy organisation reviewed across 17 jurisdictions.

The logical implication: meeting building code is necessary and insufficient for producing an accessible environment. Every specification in this document is calibrated above code, based on the best available evidence, primarily Tier 1 occupational therapy clinical research.

### §1.3 Designing for the Individual: Functional Capacity Over Category Minimums

A critical failing of code-based accessibility is the implicit assumption that all disabled people within a category have identical functional characteristics. ADA provisions specify dimensions for a notional 'wheelchair user' — a statistical construct that does not describe any actual person. This document rejects the category-minimum approach.

The governing principle is: **design must accommodate the actual functional capacity of the actual user, established through OT assessment, not the assumed functional capacity of the category.**

**Population variation within categories:** A person with a C4 spinal cord injury has very different functional capacities from a person with MS-related fatigue who uses a wheelchair only for long distances. Both are 'wheelchair users' under code classification. They require substantially different environments for genuine independence.

**Bidirectional variation:** Within any disability category, some users have greater functional capacity than the code assumption; others have lesser. Code design serves neither. An environment designed from OT assessment of actual functional capacity — with DAR provisions for declining capacity — serves both.

**Multiple simultaneous categories:** Most people acquire multiple impairments over time. A person who is a wheelchair user with MS (MOB + NEU), low vision (VIS), and early cognitive change (DEM) requires a design synthesis across all four categories simultaneously. See Part 3 for multiple disability category guidance.

**Temporal variation:** Functional capacity fluctuates. MS, PAIN, and CFS/ME conditions produce good days and bad days. PCS produces hour-to-hour variation in cognitive function. An accessible environment must remain accessible across the full range of the individual's functional variation.

### Working with Occupational Therapists in Design

Occupational Therapists (OTs) are the trained professionals whose discipline directly addresses the person-environment-occupation relationship. An OT assessment of a specific person in a specific environment produces: a precise characterisation of the person's functional capacities; a precise identification of the environmental barriers for that person; a prioritised intervention list; and post-modification outcome verification.

For residential and specialist environments, OT involvement is not optional best practice — it is the mechanism by which functional-capacity-based design becomes achievable. In new construction and major refurbishment, OT involvement at design stage produces specifications that eliminate barriers before they are built.

**Co-design requirement.**

OT assessment alone does not fulfil the co-design obligation in CRPD Article 4.3. Best practice in accessible design involves disabled people as active co-designers — not only as clinical subjects or end-user consultees. Where possible, design teams should engage disabled people with lived experience of the relevant condition(s) in design review at schematic and technical design stages. This is not a substitute for OT assessment; it is a complementary process that identifies barriers the clinical evidence base may not have captured.

### §1.4 Universal Design, Inclusive Design, and This Guidebook

#### §1.4.1 Universal Design Is a Floor, Not a Goal

Universal design (Mace, Hardie & Place, 1991) is the design philosophy that environments should work for the widest possible range of users without adaptation or specialist provision. Its seven principles — equitable use, flexibility, simple and intuitive use, perceptible information, tolerance for error, low physical effort, and size and space for approach and use — define the standard from which all design in the built environment begins.

In Canada and across most jurisdictions represented in this guidebook, universal design principles are integrated into statutory building codes — to the degree that satisfying code requirements and satisfying UD criteria are, for most buildings, the same threshold. Universal design is therefore co-extensive with code compliance. A building that satisfies UD criteria has met the legal minimum. It has not achieved best practice, and it has not achieved inclusive design.

Universal design produces a single solution calibrated to an abstracted, averaged, anonymous user. It sets fixed values derived from population statistics rather than from the functional capacity of the people who will actually use the building. For buildings where no specific user population can be identified or anticipated, this is the appropriate and sufficient starting point. For every other building, it is only the floor.

#### §1.4.2 Inclusive Design: The Aspiration of This Guidebook

Inclusive design (BS 7000-6:2005; RIBA, 2022) is the aspiration and method of this guidebook. It is not a fallback from universal design, and it is not a compromise. It is the positive commitment to designing as well as possible for the actual people who will use a building, informed by the best available evidence about their functional capacities.

The core principle of inclusive design, as applied in this guidebook, is this: the more specifically a designer can identify who will use a building, the greater the obligation to move from population averages toward functional capacity design for those people. Specificity of knowledge creates specificity of obligation.

This guidebook is written for Tier 1 and Tier 2 practice. All specifications, evidence tables, and design guidance in this document are inclusive design instruments — tools for going beyond the code floor toward the best that evidence and design can offer.

#### §1.4.3 The Three-Tier Hierarchy

This guidebook applies a three-tier design hierarchy. DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness, §1.6) is mandatory at every tier without exception.

| **Tier** | **Standard** | **Context** | **Specification form** | **DAR** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Universal Mode** | **Universal Design / Code Compliance** | No specific population can be anticipated. Averaged, anonymous user. | Range of values calibrated to an abstractly disabled non-existent person. | Mandatory |
| **Tier 1** | **Population-Informed Inclusive Design** | Identifiable disability populations among regular users. This guidebook's primary domain. | Ranges. Median is the population-informed default. Range encodes functional capacity distribution across the identified population. | Mandatory |
| **Tier 2** | **Person-Specific Inclusive Design** | Individual occupant or named user is known. OT assessment available. | Position within the Tier 1 range, resolved by the individual's functional capacity assessment. | Mandatory |

**Universal Mode — Universal Design / Code Compliance** is the appropriate standard only where no specific user population can be identified or reasonably anticipated. It uses single fixed specification values calibrated to a statistical average user. Even at Universal Mode, DAR is mandatory: future occupants cannot be anticipated at construction stage, and construction-stage provisions must preserve the building's ability to accommodate future accessibility modifications.

The following specifications have convergent evidence support across five or more disability populations and are designated **Universal Mode Universal Specifications** — required at all tiers regardless of user population:

- Consistent, predictable circulation geometry (loop or single-path; no dead-ends) — CON-0001
- 3D landmark objects at all decision points — CON-0001
- Consistent furniture arrangement within room types (no mirror-image pairs) — CON-0001
- Fragrance-free policy in all enclosed public areas — see F-06
- Acoustic performance ≤ NC-35 in occupied spaces — see A-01

**Tier 1 — Population-Informed Inclusive Design** is the primary domain of this guidebook. It applies wherever an identifiable disability population can be reasonably anticipated among the building's regular users. The overwhelming majority of buildings fall into this tier: every public institution, workplace, health facility, transit hub, educational building, housing development, and civic space will serve people across the MOB, VIS, NEU, DEM, NDV, OFS and related population categories.

*At Mode P, specifications are expressed as ranges.* The range is derived from the functional capacity distribution across the identified population. The median of the range is the population-informed default — the best single value for a designer or practitioner working without a specific individual's assessment. The full range exists because the population is not homogeneous: functional capacity varies within every disability category, and the specification must accommodate that variation.

**Tier 2 — Person-Specific Inclusive Design** applies where the individual occupant or primary user is known: a residential commission for a named person, a workplace adjustment for a specific employee, or a specialist care environment designed around a defined client. At Tier 2, the practitioner's task is not to select the median value but to determine where within the Tier 1 range the specification should land, based on that individual's functional capacity assessment, OT findings, and personal preferences.

The individual's own functional capacity is the answer. The range is the envelope within which that answer sits. *Tier 2 uses the same specifications as Tier 1 — it resolves the Tier 1 range to a specific value for a specific person.*

At Tier 2, the recommended assessment instrument for operationalising person-environment fit is the **Housing Enabler** (Iwarsson & Slaug, Lund University CASE). The Housing Enabler is the most extensively validated OT tool for quantifying the relationship between personal functional limitations and environmental barriers, producing an accessibility problem score that directly informs specification resolution within Tier 1 ranges. See §1.8.8 and Part 9 for OT assessment protocols. For individual user control of environmental parameters (lighting, acoustic, thermal), see H-02 and §1.4.4 below.

#### §1.4.4 User Control Hierarchy

Where specifications in Part 4 express a range (Tier 1), and where individual Tier 2 assessment is not available or has not been conducted, the preferred design resolution is to provide user-controlled adjustability across the Tier 1 range rather than fixing a single value. The **user control hierarchy** follows:

1. **Individual user control** (preferred): the building system allows the occupant to adjust the parameter — lighting level, CCT, acoustic absorption, thermal setpoint — within the Tier 1 range. This eliminates the specification selection problem entirely.
2. **Zone-level user control**: where individual control is not achievable, adjustability is provided at zone or room level by the occupant group.
3. **Fixed population-informed default**: where user or zone control is not achievable, the population-informed median of the Tier 1 range is the fixed specification.

The user control hierarchy applies across lighting (B-category items), acoustic (A-category), thermal (F-06, F-07, TC items), and controls provisions (H-01, H-02). See H-02 for the full user-control specification including physical control requirements, reach zones, and interface legibility standards.

#### §1.4.5 The Specification Range as Design Mechanism

The ranges appearing throughout Part 4 of this guidebook are not expressions of uncertainty or imprecision. They are the designed-in mechanism that allows a single specification item to serve both Tier 1 and Tier 2 practice simultaneously.

**At Mode P:** use the median of the stated range as the population-informed default. The median represents the best-evidence value for the identified population in the absence of individual assessment data.

**At Mode S:** use the individual's OT functional capacity assessment to locate the appropriate position within the range. The person's own capabilities, preferences, and functional variation over time determine the specific value.

**The range boundary:** values outside the stated range fall outside the evidence base for the identified population. Departures from the range require documented clinical justification.

Wherever a range appears in Part 4, the following framing applies: the range is the Tier 1 population envelope; the median is the Tier 1 default; the specific value within the range is determined by Mode S OT assessment. This framing should be treated as implicit throughout the guidebook and is made explicit in Part 4 items where the clinical stakes are highest.

#### §1.4.6 Why Universal Design Alone Is Insufficient

Universal design produces a single solution optimised for a statistical average across the widest possible population. This guidebook operates above that standard for reasons inherent to its scope.

**Fixed values vs. functional capacity ranges.**

A single fixed value cannot accommodate the functional capacity variation within any disability population. UD's fixed-value approach produces specifications that are correct for the average and wrong for everyone distributed across the population's actual range.

**Temporal and conditional variation.**

Many populations in this guidebook — OFS/ME, OFS/POTS, NDV/ADHD, PAIN, NEU/PCS — involve fluctuating capacity. A design calibrated to a single fixed value will fail these users on their high-impairment days. The Tier 1 range accommodates this variation; UD's fixed value does not.

**Populations absent from UD's historical base.**

Universal design was developed primarily against a physical disability and ageing population. The BAR, DBL, and OFS categories in this guidebook have no adequate provision in standard UD frameworks. Population-specific evidence overrides UD assumptions for these categories.

**Irresolvable multi-population specification conflicts.**

Some optimal values for one population are contraindicated for another. High-contrast visual environments support VIS users; they can trigger sensory overload for some NDV/SENS users. UD offers no principled resolution. This guidebook addresses these conflicts through explicit conflict notation in Part 4 items and building-level resolution guidance in Part 5.

The aspiration of universality — that environments should work for everyone — is achieved through rigorous population-specific inclusive design, not through adherence to a design philosophy calibrated to an averaged user.

#### §1.4.7 Application to Residential Design

New residential construction where the eventual occupant is unknown at construction stage operates at Universal Mode as its legal baseline. DAR is mandatory above that baseline, since future occupants will include people with disabilities whose specific needs cannot be anticipated. The Lifetime Homes standard (Joseph Rowntree Foundation, 1999) and CAN/ASC 2.8:2025 represent the minimum DAR threshold for speculative residential construction; both are below the best-practice level recommended by this guidebook.

Where an individual occupant is known — a custom build, an adaptations commission, or a specialist residential environment — Tier 2 applies. Design is calibrated to that person's documented functional profile and OT assessment. DAR remains mandatory to preserve adaptability for future occupants and for changes in the current occupant's functional capacity over time.

**Key references:** Mace, Hardie & Place (1991); BS 7000-6:2005; RIBA (2022); CAN/ASC 2.8:2025; Joseph Rowntree Foundation Lifetime Homes (1999); Imrie (2012); Hamraie (2017).

#### §1.4.8 Intellectual Architecture: Five Frameworks

**The social model of disability as political foundation.** The social model (Oliver, 1983; Shakespeare & Watson, 2002; Barnes & Mercer, 2005) holds that disability is produced by environmental and social barriers, not by impairment. A person is not disabled by paraplegia; a person is disabled by a building without a lift. This distinction — between impairment (a characteristic of the body) and disability (a product of the environment) — is the political claim that CRPD Article 9 enshrines in international law (§1.7). The four analytical frameworks below operationalise this claim in different registers: ICF classifies functioning and environment; PEO models the fit between person and environment; the Capability Approach grounds the claim in justice; Kawa corrects its cultural assumptions. None of them works without the social model's foundational premise that the environment, not the person, is the proper target of design intervention. Building codes that address only physical access for mobility-impaired users encode a medical-model assumption about which barriers count; the social model explains why sensory, cognitive, and neurological barriers are equally environmental and equally within the scope of accessible design.

**ICF as classification infrastructure.** The International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (WHO, 2001) provides the taxonomy — the shared language of activity codes, environmental factors, and the distinction between capacity and performance that structures the guidebook's evidence base. When this guidebook references d420 (transferring oneself) or d630 (preparing meals), it uses ICF terminology because ICF is the classification system that the international disability research, rehabilitation, and policy communities have agreed upon. ICF's contribution is organisational, not theoretical: it classifies functioning; it does not explain it.

**Person-Environment-Occupation models as practice theory.** The Person-Environment-Occupation (PEO) model (Law et al., 1996), its successor the Person-Environment-Occupation-Performance model (PEOP; Baum, Christiansen & Bass, 2015), and the Canadian Model of Occupational Performance and Engagement (CMOP-E) provide the theoretical model that explains what the guidebook's specifications are designed to achieve. In these models, functioning is not a property of the person diminished by impairment. It is the fit between the person, the environment, and the occupation the person is trying to perform. A wheelchair user in an accessible bathroom has good person-environment-occupation fit; the same person in an inaccessible bathroom has poor fit. The impairment has not changed. The fit has.

The three-tier design hierarchy in §1.4.3 maps directly onto this model. Universal Mode establishes the minimum environmental contribution to fit for an anonymous user. Tier 1 optimises fit for an identified population using the best available evidence about that population's functional capacity distribution. Tier 2 resolves fit for a specific individual through OT co-design. At every tier, the design target is congruence — the overlap between person, environment, and occupation — not compensation for deficit.

**The Capability Approach as justice framework.** The Capability Approach (Sen, 1999; Nussbaum, 2006) provides the ethical grounding for the guidebook's work. In capability terms, accessible design does not restore function or compensate for loss. It expands the capability set — the real freedoms and opportunities a person has to do and be what they have reason to value. The built environment is a conversion factor: it transforms personal resources (physical capacity, cognitive capacity, sensory capacity) into capabilities (the real ability to cook a meal, leave the house, use a bathroom independently). A person with identical impairment has a larger capability set in an accessible environment than in an inaccessible one. The impairment converts differently depending on the environment.

This framing matters because it locates the guidebook's specifications in the domain of justice, not charity or medical remediation. CRPD Article 9 establishes accessibility as a right. The Capability Approach explains why: inaccessible environments deprive people of capabilities that are constitutive of a dignified life. The guidebook's specifications are instruments of capability restoration.

**The Kawa Model as cultural lens.** The Kawa (river) model (Iwama, 2006), developed by Japanese and Canadian rehabilitation professionals, provides an essential correction to the cultural assumptions embedded in the three Western frameworks above. PEO, PEOP, and CMOP-E decompose the world into person, environment, and occupation — discrete categories that interact. Kawa dissolves these boundaries. In the river metaphor, water (life flow), rocks (life circumstances including health conditions), driftwood (personal attributes and resources), and the riverbed and banks (social and physical environment) are aspects of a single current, not independent variables. Well-being is the flow of the river. Disability is an obstruction to that flow. The built environment is part of the channel — it shapes the flow, it does not merely facilitate or obstruct a person who exists independently of it.

The practical consequence is specific. PAS 6463:2022, the most developed sensory environment design standard, specifies a private individual retreat room — a person withdraws from the environment to recover. This specification encodes an assumption about the self as autonomous and individual that transfers well to Western institutional contexts but poorly to collectivist cultures where the self is embedded in social relations and individual withdrawal is neither desired nor culturally intelligible. Kawa-informed sensory specification would design for collective calm — a shared space where the environmental channel is widened (lower sensory press, softer acoustic, diffuse light) rather than a private space where the individual exits the channel entirely. The physics of the specification (acoustic absorption, lighting level, visual complexity) may be identical; the spatial model (shared calm room versus private retreat pod) differs fundamentally.

This guidebook specifies the physics universally and flags the spatial model as culturally variable — a Tier 2 co-design decision informed by the cultural context of the occupants. Part 3 §3.2.4 develops this framework layering in the context of the co-occurrence synthesis methodology.

**Compound functioning.** One further principle cuts across all four frameworks. Most people who use accessible environments have more than one health condition. The interaction between conditions is not additive — it is supra-additive, meaning the compound functional impact exceeds the sum of individual conditions (Multimorbidity-Weighted Index research; Clarke et al., American Journal of Epidemiology). The built environment amplifies this non-linearity: Clarke demonstrated a 4.52-fold multiplicative interaction between built environment quality and lower-extremity impairment. An accessible environment does not merely add a fixed increment of function for each condition addressed; it reduces the multiplicative penalty that compounds of conditions impose.

The guidebook addresses compound functioning through the resolution decision tree in Part 3 §3.8 and the Tier 2 co-design pathway. Where two population-level specifications conflict within a single person's compound profile, the resolution is not pairwise compromise — it is individual OT assessment that addresses the interaction between conditions. The guidebook equips that assessment; it does not replace it.



### §1.5 Evidence Hierarchy

Source prioritisation in this guidebook follows a tier structure. Where sources conflict, higher-tier evidence governs regardless of jurisdiction, language, or number of citations.

**Tier 1 — Occupational Therapy Clinical Research (Highest Authority)**

OT evidence is intervention-tested: specifications are derived from observation of actual people performing actual tasks in actual environments, with outcome measurement. OT evidence sits at Tier 1 because it is directly person-environment-occupation tested, calibrated to actual functional capacity ranges, and intervention-verified. *Primary Tier 1 sources:* CAOT (2018); JOTA (2022); COTEC/WFOT position statements; AOTA (2023); Housing Enabler (Iwarsson & Slaug, Lund University CASE; r = 0.74 reliability; validated across residential and institutional environments); RCOT (2019).

**Co-primary Tier 1 — Lived Experience and Participatory Design Research**

In alignment with CRPD Article 4.3, which requires States Parties to actively involve disabled people in developing accessibility standards and policies, this guidebook treats the documented lived expertise of disabled people as co-primary evidence alongside OT clinical research. Where lived experience evidence and clinical research findings align, confidence is elevated. Where they diverge, both are presented and the divergence is noted. Participatory design processes — where disabled people are involved as co-designers, not only as research subjects — are treated as a form of evidence generation. *Primary co-Tier 1 sources:* The Kelsey IDS co-design methodology; Gallaudet DeafSpace participatory design process; Motionspot neuroinclusive design co-design protocols; patient-led research collectives including ME Association (UK) and Patient Led Research Collaborative.

**Tier 2 — NGO and Advocacy Organisation Best-Practice Guidelines**

NGO and advocacy guidelines are developed by and for disabled communities. They incorporate lived experience expertise — a form of validity that clinical and technical standards frequently lack. *Primary Tier 2 sources:* CNIB (Canada); Alzheimer Society / BrainXchange (Canada); DeafSpace Project, Gallaudet University (USA); HLAA (USA); The Kelsey IDS (USA); Motionspot (UK); NAS (UK); APF France Handicap; CERMI; ONCE Foundation (Spain); CAWaB; SgEnable (Singapore); KODDI (South Korea); DbI Nordic programme; Sense UK Collaborative (USA/UK).

**Co-primary Tier 2 — Occupational Therapy Clinical Practice Guidelines**

OT Clinical Practice Guidelines (CPGs) are distinguished from primary OT clinical research (Tier 1) by their synthesising function: CPGs aggregate and grade the clinical research base, translating findings into practice recommendations. They carry Tier 2 authority because they depend on the Tier 1 evidence they summarise, but their consensus-derived recommendations are the primary translation mechanism between research and specification. *Primary Co-Tier 2 sources:* CAOT CPGs; AOTA CPGs; RCOT CPGs; COTEC/WFOT joint practice guidelines.

**Tier 3 — Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses**

*Primary Tier 3 sources:* Bettarello et al. (2021); Marquardt (2011); Koontz et al. (2012); Ulrich (1984, 1991); Petaccia et al. (2021); Wellecke et al. (2022); Sherrington et al. (2019); Räty et al. (2021).

**Tier 4 — International Standards with Evidence Basis**

CRPD Article 9 (UN, 2006) — 185 States Parties; foundational international legal framework for accessibility. PAS 6463:2022 (BSI, UK). WELL v2 (2024). ISO 23599:2019 — tactile walking surface indicators. IEC 60118-4:2014+AMD1:2017 — hearing loop performance [UK/EU: BS EN IEC 60118-4:2015+A1:2018]. ISO 21542:2021 — building accessibility and usability. EN 17210:2021 — pan-European accessibility.

**Tier 5 — National Beyond-Code Frameworks**

BS 8300:2018 (UK); The Kelsey IDS (USA, 2023); ABCB Silver/Gold/Platinum Livable Housing (Australia); RHFAC v4.0 (Canada, 2024); NS 11001-1:2018 (Norway); DIN 18040-1/2/3 (Germany); BCA Universal Design Index (Singapore); BF certification framework (South Korea); SBi-anvisning 230 (Denmark); Esteettömyysopas / ESKEH (Finland).

**Tier 6 — Statutory Codes (Reference Baseline Only)**

Legal minimum. Design that meets code only is not best practice and in many cases produces environments that remain inaccessible to significant proportions of disabled users.

### §1.6 The DAR Principle — Design for Adaptable Readiness

Construction-stage decisions govern the cost of every future accessibility modification. Design for Adaptable Readiness (DAR) holds that provisions made at schematic design stage preserve that future capacity at negligible cost — whereas retrofit costs are multiplied significantly once a building is complete and occupied. See Part 10 for cost multiplier evidence.

**Policy implication:** DAR provision in new construction is the highest-return accessible design investment available. Building codes that do not require DAR provisions impose these multiplied costs on ageing homeowners and disability communities.

### §1.7 CRPD Framework Alignment

This guidebook grounds all provisions in Article 9 of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD, 2006). CRPD Article 9 establishes accessibility of the built environment as a legal obligation for 185 States Parties. The general principles of Article 3 (dignity, autonomy, non-discrimination, participation) provide the normative framework within which technical specifications are derived.

### §1.8 Evidence Frameworks

Eight theoretical frameworks underpin the evidence basis for specifications in this document. Each maps a distinct dimension of the person-environment relationship; together they constitute the conceptual architecture from which specifications are derived.

**§1.8.1 Biomechanical Frame of Reference** Specifications derived from this framework address physical capacity, joint range of motion, muscle strength, grip, reach, balance, and gait. Grab bar diameters, handrail heights, turning radii, and threshold heights are biomechanically derived. All Part 4 specifications grounded in this framework are calibrated to the functional range of the relevant disability category, not to code averages.

**§1.8.2 Dunn's Sensory Processing Model** Sensory processing differences produce four profiles: sensory seeking, sensory avoiding, sensory sensitivity, and low registration. Design must accommodate all four. Acoustic specifications (Category A), lighting specifications (Category B), and colour/surface specifications (Category C) are calibrated across all four profiles.

**§1.8.3 Ecology of Human Performance (EHP) Framework** EHP defines four intervention strategies: establish, alter, adapt, prevent. Accessible design interventions in Part 4 are classified by EHP strategy: most interventions alter the context (environment) rather than the person; some prevent barrier formation at source.

**§1.8.4 Compensatory Frame of Reference** Where functional capacity cannot be restored, the environment compensates. Lever hardware, automatic doors, and grab bars are compensatory provisions.

**§1.8.5 Allen's Cognitive Disabilities Model (CDM)** CDM identifies six cognitive levels (0–6). Environmental specifications for DEM and NDV users are calibrated to the cognitive level range of the target population. Wayfinding, signage, and spatial layout specifications (Category D) draw on CDM research.

**§1.8.6 Person-Environment-Occupation-Performance (PEOP) Model** PEOP frames the person-environment relationship as a dynamic interaction affecting occupation performance. Dignified access, socially integrated design, and equivalent provisions are PEOP requirements — not merely aesthetic preferences. PEOP is one of the three person-environment-occupation models that constitute the guidebook's practice theory (§1.4.8).

**§1.8.7 Ecological Psychology — Affordance and Signifier Theory** Gibson's (1979) affordance theory holds that the environment offers action possibilities relative to the capacities of the perceiving organism. An affordance is relational: a step affords climbing for a person who can lift their foot to its height, but does not afford climbing for a person whose knee flexion range is insufficient. Affordances are body-scaled — they vary with the functional capacity of the user, which is why population-specific specification ranges exist in Part 4.

Norman (1988; 2013) introduced a critical distinction that Gibson did not make: the difference between what the environment offers (the affordance) and what the environment communicates it offers (the signifier). A grab bar affords gripping regardless of who perceives it. The signifier — the visual contrast against the wall, the tactile profile that distinguishes it from a towel rail, the placement that communicates "grip here" — is what enables or fails perception of that affordance. The affordance is biomechanical; the signifier is perceptual. Weisman (1981) demonstrated this empirically: of his four environmental variables affecting wayfinding, three — visual access to landmarks, architectural differentiation between areas, and building configuration — are affordances; the fourth — signs and room numbers — is a signifier. Floor plan simplicity (an affordance property) accounted for 56% of variance in disorientation.

This distinction matters because different populations experience different signifier failures while the underlying affordance remains intact. A lever door handle affords opening for any person with sufficient grip strength. For a person with dementia, the signifier may fail: a decorative lever may not communicate "operate me" because its visual form does not match the person's stored schema for "door handle." For a person with visual impairment, the signifier fails differently: the handle may not be perceived at all without sufficient luminance contrast against the door surface. For a person with sensory sensitivity, the signifier may succeed but at excessive perceptual cost: a visually complex handle on a visually complex door produces signifier noise that demands processing resources the person cannot spare.

Allen's Cognitive Disabilities Model (§1.8.5) maps onto signifier complexity tolerance. At CDM Level 6, abstract signifiers — symbols, text, colour codes — function effectively. At Level 4, concrete signifiers — photographs, recognisable object shapes — are required. At Level 3, the object itself must serve as its own signifier: a toilet must look like a toilet, not be represented by a pictogram (Namazi & Johnson, 1991). Specification of signifier complexity at each CDM level is a design parameter distinct from specification of the affordance itself. Vilar et al. (2014) demonstrated that the dominance relationship between affordances and signifiers is modulated by context: in emergency situations, signifiers override affordances; in routine navigation, affordances dominate. Residential environments (Part 6) should therefore prioritise spatial affordances; institutional environments (Part 7) should ensure both channels are specified.

**§1.8.8 Competence-Press Model** Lawton & Nahemow's (1973) model holds that adaptive behaviour occurs in a zone of moderate environmental press relative to individual competence. Specifications for DEM, CFS/ME, and OFS users are calibrated to maintain press within the performance zone for the lower end of the functional capacity range.

The **Housing Enabler** instrument (Iwarsson & Slaug, Lund University CASE; 2001, revised 2010) is the direct OT operationalisation of the competence-press model for built environment assessment. It combines a personal component (188 functional limitation items, OT-assessed) with an environmental component (188 environmental barrier items, OT-measured) to produce an accessibility problem score — a quantified measure of person-environment misfit. Reliability: r = 0.74 (inter-rater); validated in residential and institutional settings across 11 countries. The Housing Experiment 2021 (Slaug, Granbom & Iwarsson 2024; citizen science; large Swedish housing stock sample) confirmed that environmental barriers remain prevalent despite 30 years of public health evidence, and that person-environment fit predicts falls more reliably than environmental hazard counts alone (cf. Iwarsson et al. 2009). Population-specific Housing Enabler research includes Parkinson's disease (Nilsson & Iwarsson 2021), demonstrating population-specific barrier ranking within the general instrument.

For Tier 2 practice, the Housing Enabler is the recommended assessment instrument for resolving Tier 1 specification ranges to individual values. OT assessment protocols for Housing Enabler use in accessible design commissions are set out in Part 9.

**§1.8.9 Attention Restoration Theory (ART) and Stress Recovery Theory (SRT)** ART and SRT provide the evidence basis for biophilic design provisions (Appendix B), natural lighting specifications (B-09), and sensory retreat provisions (A-16, F-01).

**§1.8.10 Wayfinding Theory — Imageability and Spatial Problem-Solving** Lynch (1960) identified five elements that structure cognitive maps of environments: paths (channels of movement), edges (linear boundaries), districts (areas with common character), nodes (decision points where paths intersect), and landmarks (external reference points). Weisman (1981) demonstrated that these elements are measurable properties of buildings: floor plan simplicity — a compound of Lynch's elements — accounted for 56% of variance in occupant disorientation across ten university buildings. Space syntax analysis (Hillier et al., 1984; Turner et al., 2001) provides computational tools — integration depth, connectivity, and visibility graph analysis — that quantify these properties across building populations, producing generalizable findings from aggregate measurement.

Passini (1984; Arthur & Passini, 1992) modelled wayfinding as a three-step cognitive process: (1) processing environmental information, (2) making decisions and developing a plan, and (3) executing the plan through movement. Each step depends on different cognitive capacities and can fail independently. Populations lose these capacities in characteristic patterns. People with Alzheimer's disease retain landmark-based strategies throughout the dementia course — landmarks are the most durable wayfinding capacity — while decision-making at nodes degrades earlier (Wiener & Pazzaglia, 2021). People with visual impairment lose the information processing step for the visual channel while retaining decision-making and execution through tactile and auditory channels (Zali et al., 2026; Passini & Proulx, 1988). People with acquired brain injury may lose any combination depending on lesion location.

The consequence for specification is that wayfinding provisions in Part 4 (D-category) and room matrices (Parts 6 and 7) should address each cognitive step independently. Landmark provision at nodes is the highest-priority wayfinding intervention for populations with cognitive decline, because it supports the last capacity to degrade. Signage (§1.8.7) is the highest-priority intervention for populations with intact cognition navigating unfamiliar environments, because it supplements rather than replaces decision-making. Spatial affordances — corridor width, brightness, sightlines — are the highest-priority intervention for residential settings where routine navigation occurs without conscious wayfinding effort (Vilar et al., 2014). Institutional and emergency settings require both channels specified.

**§1.8.11 Salutogenesis — Health-Promoting Environments** Antonovsky's (1987) salutogenic model holds that health is not the absence of disease but the presence of resources that enable a person to comprehend, manage, and find meaning in their life circumstances. The Sense of Coherence (SOC) has three components: comprehensibility (the environment is structured, predictable, and explicable), manageability (the resources to cope with demands are available), and meaningfulness (the demands are worth engaging with). Golembiewski (2010; 2017) and Golembiewski & Zeisel (2022) applied salutogenesis systematically to healthcare, aged care, and dementia care architecture, demonstrating that architectural design can actively support all three SOC components. Rakhshani & Khakzand (2025) confirmed this in a systematic review of 26 studies: porches, entrances, balconies, and above-grade entries were identified as architectural elements supporting SOC in non-therapeutic residential environments.

Salutogenesis does different work from ART and SRT (§1.8.9). ART and SRT explain why natural environments restore attentional capacity and reduce physiological stress. Salutogenesis explains why the *whole* built environment — not only its natural elements — should be designed to promote health. The three SOC components map onto the guidebook's specification categories: comprehensibility maps onto wayfinding legibility (D-category) and circadian lighting patterns (B-01, B-12); manageability maps onto the user control hierarchy (§1.4.4) and controls specifications (C-category); meaningfulness maps onto dignity and engagement provisions (PEOP, §1.8.6). Golembiewski & Zeisel (2022) made this connection explicit by describing salutogenic design in terms of "engaging affordances" — linking Norman's affordance-signifier framework (§1.8.7) to Antonovsky's SOC: comprehensibility requires legible affordances and clear signifiers; manageability requires operable affordances; meaningfulness requires affordances that engage the person's interests and identity.

The salutogenic framework reframes accessible design from the removal of barriers to the active promotion of health. The social model (§1.4.8) establishes that the environment disables; salutogenesis establishes that the environment can also heal. This reframing matters because it shifts the design aspiration from "does this environment not prevent functioning?" to "does this environment actively support the person's sense of coherence?"


### §1.9.1 Biomechanical Derivation of Dimensional Specifications

Every dimensional specification in this guidebook — threshold height, ramp gradient, grab bar height range, seating height, corridor width, riser height — is a biomechanical threshold, not an arbitrary convention. The specification value is the point at which the environment switches from enabling action to disabling it. Thresholds above 6 mm begin to intercept the gait trajectory of older adults (Pavol et al. 2001, Tier 3). Ramp gradients above 1:12 force propulsion techniques that accumulate shoulder injury risk over time for manual wheelchair users (Waters et al. 1985, Tier 3). Seating at 450–500 mm reduces hip extensor moment by 40% compared to standard 380 mm sofa height (Rodosky et al. 1989, Tier 3). Riser irregularities of 6 mm increase misstep probability by approximately 18% (Templer 1992, Tier 4).

This derivation has two implications for practice. First, the values are not interchangeable — a 25 mm threshold is not "almost accessible." The threshold marks a functional discontinuity, not a position on a comfort gradient. Second, where a specification lacks a biomechanical basis, this guidebook marks it explicitly as expert consensus or inferred (○ marker) rather than evidence-derived (● marker). The stair-ramp-threshold-biomechanics-accessibility BPC provides the primary evidence base for dimensional specifications in this guidebook. See also CON-0100 (connection register) and the affordance framework in §1.4 — a specification is the threshold at which an affordance becomes available or is withdrawn.

### §1.9 Scope

This guidebook applies to: new residential construction; major residential refurbishments; non-residential buildings including healthcare, education, hospitality, commercial, and cultural facilities; and post-occupancy adaptation of existing buildings. It applies to any building intended for occupation by people with any of the disability categories defined in Part 2. It does not apply to: minor works (single-trade interventions under £5,000); temporary structures with an intended lifespan under 6 months; vehicles. Where this document conflicts with statutory requirements, comply with the statutory requirement and document the conflict as a Tier 6 baseline limitation. Where this document requires more than statutory requirements, this document governs.

#### §1.9.2 Specification Limits — Compound Functioning Scenarios

Some compound functioning scenarios cannot be resolved through environmental specification alone. Where two populations require wayfinding systems that depend on mutually exclusive sensory channels, no single environmental design can serve both without human mediation.

The documented specification limit is DEM+VIS wayfinding. DEM wayfinding relies on colour-coded landmarks and visual spatial cues; VIS wayfinding relies on tactile route learning and floor texture differentiation. These systems are antagonistic: colour requires vision; tactile floor patterns conflict with DEM plain-floor requirements to prevent perceived obstacles. The convergent parameter — simplified, predictable spatial geometry — is the maximum environmental contribution. Beyond this, human wayfinding assistance is the Tier 2 resolution.

This is not a failure of specification. It is the boundary at which environmental design reaches its limit and human support becomes the primary intervention. The guidebook's role at this boundary is to ensure the environment supports the human assistance — simplified layout, adequate corridor width for accompanied walking, rest seating at intervals, and clear sightlines for the accompanying person — rather than attempting to substitute for it. Part 3 §3.2.3 discusses compound functioning in detail. Part 5 provides the resolution process.
