## Part 3: Synthesis, Sequencing and the Co-Occurrence Framework

*Guidebook for Accessible Design v10.0*

---

### §3.1 Why Synthesis Matters: Co-Occurrence as the Design Norm

Accessible design is not the sum of eleven separate disability category specifications applied independently. Most buildings serve multiple populations simultaneously. Most individuals experience multiple impairments concurrently or acquire them sequentially over a lifetime. Co-occurrence — the condition of a single building serving users from two or more population categories at the same time — is not an exception to be managed; it is the normal condition of every institutional, commercial, and multi-resident building.

Designing for one population category at a time and then attempting to reconcile the results produces inferior environments. Some specifications that are optimal for one population are directly contraindicated for another: high-contrast visual environments support VIS orientation and DEM fall prevention, but can trigger sensory overload for NDV/SENS users. Ambient temperatures ≤18°C are safety-critical for NEU/MS users, but aggravate PAIN/fibromyalgia. Acoustic amplification in hearing loops enables DEAF communication, but violates the acoustic calm requirements of NDV/AUT. These conflicts do not disappear when categories are designed independently — they collide in the finished building.

Synthesis is the practice of designing for co-occurrence from the outset. It requires: understanding which populations will occupy a given building; identifying which specifications conflict and which converge; resolving conflicts using an explicit evidence-based hierarchy before they are embedded in construction; and building in adaptability for configurations that cannot be resolved at population level.

### §3.2 Theoretical Framework

#### §3.2.1 ICF and Intersectional Disability

The International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF, WHO 2001) frames disability as the interaction between body function/structure impairment and environmental and social barriers. This guidebook applies the ICF framework: disability is not located in the person but in the person-environment mismatch. Co-occurrence in this framework means the mismatch operates on multiple dimensions simultaneously — a wheelchair user with low vision experiences not the sum of MOB and VIS barriers but a compound person-environment mismatch that single-category specifications fail to address.

#### §3.2.2 Competence-Press Model at Compound Strain

Lawton and Nahemow's (1973) competence-press model holds that adaptive behaviour occurs in a zone of moderate environmental press relative to individual competence. Environmental press that exceeds the competence zone produces maladaptation — the environment disables rather than enables. For compound co-occurrence, the press accumulates: a DEM+MOB+OFS user in a high-stimulation, thermally warm, long-corridor building faces compound press that defeats each individual population's threshold.

Synthesis applies the competence-press model across co-occurring populations simultaneously. The Tier 1 specification for any given parameter must be calibrated to the most constrained population in the space — the population whose adaptive threshold is first exceeded by environmental press.

#### §3.2.3 Co-Occurrence as Norm, Not Exception

Epidemiological data confirms that the design assumption of a single-category user is a statistical fiction. Multimorbidity (two or more chronic conditions) affects 42.5% of the UK adult population (Barnett et al. 2012). Among people with disabilities, multimorbidity rates are substantially higher. The built environment's design tradition of addressing one population at a time is not evidence-based — it is an artefact of regulatory and professional silos that the clinical evidence does not support.

---


#### §3.2.3 Compound Functioning and Non-Additive Disability

The ICF and competence-press models operate on individual functional dimensions. Multimorbidity research demonstrates that co-occurring conditions produce supra-additive disability: each additional chronic condition increases disability risk by approximately 16%, and the interaction between conditions accelerates functional decline faster than any single-condition model predicts (BMC Geriatrics 2022, MWI). Clarke et al. (American Journal of Epidemiology) demonstrated a 4.52x multiplicative interaction between built environment quality and lower-extremity impairment — the environment does not add to impairment; it multiplies it.

For the guidebook, this means: when multiple population codes apply to a single occupant, the specification response is not the union of individual population specifications but a compound assessment. At Tier 1, the pairwise resolution in §3.8 is necessary but insufficient. At Tier 2, the OT co-design assessment addresses the interaction between conditions — not each condition independently.

This principle is operationalised in §3.8 Step 0 (compound functioning check) and in Part 5 §5.1.

#### §3.2.4 Theoretical Framework Layering

This guidebook integrates four complementary frameworks:

1. **ICF (WHO 2001)** provides the classification infrastructure — activity codes, environmental factors, the taxonomy of functioning. It is the shared language between this guidebook and the international disability research and policy community.

2. **Person-Environment-Occupation models (PEO, Law et al. 1996; PEOP, Baum et al. 2015; CMOP-E)** provide the theoretical model. Functioning is not person-minus-impairment but person-environment-occupation *fit*. The guidebook's specifications aim to optimise this fit. The three-tier design hierarchy maps directly onto the PEO congruence model: Tier 0 = universal fit floor; Tier 1 = population-informed fit optimisation; Tier 2 = individual co-design for maximum congruence.

3. **The Capability Approach (Sen 1999; Nussbaum 2006)** provides the justice framework. Accessible design expands the *capability set* of occupants — the real freedoms and opportunities they have to do and be what they value. The built environment is a *conversion factor* that transforms personal resources into capabilities. A wheelchair user with an accessible bathroom has a larger capability set than the same person with an inaccessible one, even though the impairment is unchanged.

4. **The Kawa Model (Iwama 2006)** provides the cultural lens. Kawa rejects the Western decomposition of person/environment/occupation as artificial and instead models life as a river whose flow (well-being) depends on the inseparable interaction of water (life force), rocks (circumstances), driftwood (personal attributes), and riverbed (environment). This model is essential for contexts where PAS 6463-style private individual retreat specifications do not transfer to collectivist cultures, and where the guidebook must specify for shared calm rather than individual withdrawal.

No single framework is sufficient. ICF provides the taxonomy but not the theory. PEO provides the theory but not the classification. CA provides the ethics but not the operationalisation. Kawa provides the cultural correction but not the spatial parameters. Together they constitute the guidebook's intellectual architecture.

### §3.4 The Layered Environment and Synthesis Principles

The layered environment model organises accessible design provisions by their physical scale and persistence:

**Layer 1 — Structural and fixed:** floor plan geometry, structural grid, slab penetrations, ceiling heights, ramp gradients, stair configuration. These decisions are made at schematic design and are costly or impossible to modify post-construction. They establish the conditions within which all other layers operate.

**Layer 2 — Services and systems:** HVAC systems, lighting circuits, loop cabling, acoustic treatment, fire alarm infrastructure, structural blocking for future fittings. Services decisions are made at technical design stage; they can be modified with moderate cost if properly detailed.

**Layer 3 — Fit-out and finishes:** flooring, wall finishes, signage, grab bars, hardware, furniture. These are made at detailed design and are the most reversible — DAR provisions at Layers 1 and 2 make Layer 3 modifications less disruptive.

**Layer 4 — Individual adaptation:** assistive technology, personalised assessments, post-occupancy adjustments. This is the Tier 2 domain — OT-mediated, person-specific, operating within the envelope created by Layers 1–3.

**Four synthesis principles.** These govern how population-specific specifications are combined across layers:

1. **Resolve at the earliest layer.** Conflicts that require a structural resolution cannot be addressed by signage or fit-out. Identify conflicts at Layer 1 and resolve them there. A conflict left to Layer 3 will either be unresolved or require expensive structural remediation.

2. **Converging specifications produce Tier 0 provisions.** Where five or more populations require the same or compatible provisions independently, the specification becomes a Tier 0 universal: loop circulation geometry (DEM, VIS, DBL, NDV/AUT, DEM/NDV[IntD-proxy] — CON-0001); quiet retreat space (NDV/AUT, NDV/MH, OFS — CON-0002); fragrance-free policy (OFS/MCAS, NDV/SENS — CON-0007). These should be specified as universal provisions without population conditioning.

3. **Safety-critical provisions establish the floor.** Where one population's provision addresses a safety outcome (fall, scald, fire evacuation, Uhthoff's neurological worsening) and another population's provision addresses a comfort or preference outcome, the safety provision governs the shared environment. Comfort requirements are met through individual supplemental provision.

4. **Zoning is a resolution mechanism, not a workaround.** Sensory gradient zoning (F-01) separates irreconcilable ambient parameters into distinct spatial zones without compromising either population. Zoning is not a compromise — it is the principled design response to specifications that cannot be reconciled in a single ambient parameter.

### §3.5 Stage-by-Stage Process

The synthesis process runs in parallel with the standard RIBA Plan of Work or equivalent national project delivery framework. Synthesis is not a post-design audit; it is a concurrent design activity from Stage 1.

**Stage 0 — Strategic Definition:** Confirm the building's anticipated user populations. For each identified population, identify the primary Part 4 categories. Cross-reference the co-occurrence matrix (§3.3) for each population pair. Flag conflict domains for resolution in Stage 2.

**Stage 1 — Preparation and Brief:** Develop the Population Approach Brief (see §3.12). Record: population codes anticipated; co-occurrence pairs identified; conflict domains flagged; OT assessment requirement identified. Issue brief to design team including OT if required (see Part 9 §9.2 appointment triggers).

**Stage 2 — Concept Design (Schematic Design):** Resolve all Layer 1 conflicts: floor plan geometry, circulation type, structural grid, slab provisions. Apply the resolution decision tree (§3.8) to each flagged conflict. Record resolution rationale. Specialist consultant appointments confirmed (see Part 9).

**Stage 3 — Spatial Coordination (Technical Design — DD):** Resolve all Layer 2 conflicts: HVAC zoning, lighting circuit design, loop wiring routes, acoustic treatment specification. Issue engineer briefs (see Part 8). Apply CON connections relevant to this project from the connection register.

**Stage 4 — Technical Design (Construction Issue — CD):** Specify all Layer 3 items: hardware, flooring, signage, grab bars, furniture. Apply population-specific specifications from Part 4. Confirm DAR provisions are built into structural and services details (see Part 10).

**Stage 5 — Manufacturing and Construction:** No new accessibility decisions should arise at this stage. If they do, escalate to Stage 2 process before proceeding.

**Stage 6 — Handover and Close-out (RFO — Ready for Occupancy):** Accessibility systems commissioning (loop testing per IEC 60118-4; lighting level verification; HVAC temperature setpoint verification). OT post-occupancy assessment for Tier 2 users. DSDC EADDAT audit where DEM is a primary population. Prepare FM brief for ongoing operational provisions (fragrance-free policy, furniture permanence, sensory room maintenance).

**Note on terminology:** DD = Design Development; RFO = Ready for Occupancy. These are the terms used throughout this guidebook for the design stage immediately preceding construction documentation and for the handover stage respectively.

### §3.6 Synthesis by Building Strategy

Different building types present different co-occurrence profiles. This section identifies the primary synthesis challenge for each major building category and the governing resolution approach.

**Residential care (DEM-primary):** The co-occurrence challenge is DEM+MOB+OFS — the three most prevalent co-occurring conditions in residential care populations. Layer 1 resolution: loop-plan circulation serves all three simultaneously. Layer 2: circadian lighting and HVAC zoning. Layer 3: plain flooring, contrast-band door frames, bilateral grab bars throughout. See Part 5 §5.4.1 worked example.

**Educational:** NDV is the most prevalent population in mainstream education (ADHD prevalence 5–7%; autism 1.5–2%; dyslexia 10%). Co-occurrence challenge: NDV+DEAF (hearing loop vs acoustic quiet). Resolution: independent loop audio input circuit; RT60 ≤0.3 s serves both simultaneously. Sensory quiet room mandatory on every floor plate (A-16).

**Healthcare:** MOB+DEM+NEU is the dominant co-occurrence in hospital settings. VIS is a significant secondary population in older adult wards. Resolution: plain flooring on primary routes (DEM+VIS alignment); tactile wayfinding on secondary routes (VIS); bilateral grab bars throughout (MOB+NEU/hemiplegia); individual environmental controls in patient rooms (NEU/MS temperature; PAIN warmth; NDV/MH control).

**Co-working/office:** NDV is the fastest-growing primary population in contemporary workplace design (PAS 6463 adoption driving employer provision). DEAF is significant (loop infrastructure increasingly standard). Resolution: graduated sensory zones from open-plan collaboration spine (DEAF sightlines) to enclosed acoustic pods (NDV quiet); independent loop audio.

**Transport:** MOB is the primary population; DEAF and VIS are significant secondaries. All spaces serve transient populations with no opportunity for individual adaptation. Tier 0 provisions govern. Loop infrastructure, TWSI, LRV contrast, and visual fire alarms are mandatory rather than conditional.

### §3.7 Decision Framework for Sequencing Techniques

When multiple population-specific specifications apply to the same parameter in the same space, apply the sequencing framework below in order. Stop at the first step that produces a resolution.

**Step 1: Identify the conflict type.**
- INTRA-INDIVIDUAL: same person — both specifications must be satisfied simultaneously or the individual cannot use the space.
- INTER-GROUP: different users — zoning may separate specifications spatially.
- BOTH: both conditions apply.

**Step 2: Is either provision safety-critical?**
A provision is safety-critical if its absence creates a risk of injury, falls, fire evacuation failure, or medical deterioration (Uhthoff's mechanism, scald, choking). If one provision is safety-critical and the other is not: the safety-critical provision governs the shared ambient environment. The non-safety-critical population's need is met through individual supplemental provision (radiant panel, personal control, assistive device).

**Step 3: Where both are safety-equivalent, which provision has stronger evidence?**
A provision supported by Tier 1–2 OT clinical evidence governs over a provision supported by Tier 3–5 consensus or standards. If evidence quality is equivalent, proceed to Step 4.

**Step 4: Where evidence is equivalent, which population is most constrained?**
The most constrained population is the one for which non-compliance creates the greatest functional consequence with the fewest available compensatory strategies. Deaf users without a hearing loop have no alternative in a looped-room space. NDV/AUT users without hearing loop augmentation have earplugs, headphones, and withdrawal. The most constrained user's provision governs.

**Step 5: Can a range or zone specification serve both?**
If the conflict is about a point value (a single fixed specification), test whether a range that falls within the acceptable zone for both populations exists. The grab bar diameter range (32–45 mm) resolves MOB/UPL simultaneously. Where a range resolves the conflict, specify the range and note both populations.


If specifications are irreconcilable in a single ambient environment, apply the sensory gradient model (F-01): assign each population's specification to a spatial zone. Zoning works for: illuminance (Zone 1: circadian DEM/VIS; Zone 3: low-stimulation NDV); acoustic level (Zone 1: loop/PA; Zone 3: quiet NDV); stimulation level generally.

**Step 6: Is this an irresolvable conflict?**
If Steps 1–5 fail to produce a resolution for a shared ambient parameter in a non-zoned space, the conflict is irresolvable at Tier 1. Document it as a Tier 2 conflict — resolvable only through individual OT assessment and co-design. Record in the project's conflict register. Flag for the OT appointment trigger (Part 9 §9.10). See Part 5 §5.3 for documented irresolvable conflicts.

### §3.8 Resolution Decision Tree

The resolution decision tree operationalises §3.7 as a practitioner tool. It applies to any parameter conflict identified during the Stage 2 synthesis process.

```
START: A specification conflict is identified between Population A and Population B
for parameter [X] in space [Y].

Step 0: Preliminary checks
├── Variable conflation check (per project-standards rule 2026-03-30)
│     Are the opposing needs operating on the SAME physical variable?
│     If not → not a true conflict. Specify both independently.
│
└── Compound functioning check
      Does the conflict involve an INTRA-INDIVIDUAL compound profile
      (same person experiences both populations)?
      ├── YES, and the functional interaction is non-additive
      │     → This conflict cannot be resolved by pairwise population logic.
      │       Route to Tier 2 OT co-design assessment.
      │       Document: "Compound profile — Tier 2 resolution required.
      │       Individual specifications: [A-spec], [B-spec].
      │       Interaction mechanism: [brief clinical reasoning].
      │       OT assessment resolves: [what the OT determines]."
      │       → EXIT decision tree. Do not proceed to Step 1.
      │
      └── NO, or interaction is additive (specs can co-exist)
            → Proceed to Step 1.

Step 1: Conflict classification
├── Is it INTRA-INDIVIDUAL? (Same person experiences both populations)
│     → Zoning cannot resolve it. Proceed to Step 2.
├── Is it INTER-GROUP? (Different users in same space)
│     → Zoning is available. Flag as option in Step 5.
└── Is it BOTH?
      → Both paths apply. Test each.

Step 2: Safety-criticality check
├── Is Population A's provision safety-critical? (Injury, fall, evacuation, medical)
│     AND Population B's provision is NOT safety-critical?
│     → Population A governs ambient environment.
│       Population B's need met by supplemental individual provision.
│       → RESOLVED. Document: governing provision + supplemental provision.
│
├── Is Population B's provision safety-critical?
│     AND Population A's provision is NOT safety-critical?
│     → Population B governs. Same process.
│       → RESOLVED. Document.
│
└── Both safety-critical? OR neither safety-critical?
      → Proceed to Step 3.

Step 3: Evidence-tier comparison
├── Is Population A's specification supported by higher-tier evidence?
│     → Population A governs. Residual gap for B noted.
│       → RESOLVED-EVIDENCE. Document tier reference.
│
├── Is Population B's specification supported by higher-tier evidence?
│     → Population B governs.
│       → RESOLVED-EVIDENCE.
│
└── Evidence-equivalent or inconclusive?
      → Proceed to Step 4.

Step 4: Constraint comparison
Who faces the greater functional consequence with fewer compensatory strategies?
├── Population A is more constrained
│     → Population A governs.
│       → RESOLVED-CONSENSUS. Document constraint rationale.
│
└── Population B is more constrained
      → Population B governs.

Step 5: Range or zone test (runs in parallel with Steps 2–4 where conflict is INTER-GROUP)
├── Does a parameter range exist that satisfies both populations' evidence basis?
│     → Specify the range.
│       → RESOLVED-RANGE. Document the range and both populations served.
│
└── Can spatial zoning separate the specifications?
      → Apply F-01 sensory gradient zones.
        → RESOLVED-ZONING. Document zone assignment for each population.

Step 6: Irresolvable conflict
If Steps 1–5 produce no resolution for a shared non-zoned ambient parameter:
→ IRRESOLVABLE at Tier 1.
→ Record in project conflict register.
→ Flag as Tier 2: OT assessment mandatory (Part 9 §9.10 trigger).
→ Enter in Part 5 §5.3 register if it is a known systemic conflict.
```

**Worked example — strobe vs seizure (CON-0014):**
DEAF and DBL users require visual fire alarm strobes throughout all occupied spaces. NEU (epilepsy) and NDV users face seizure or photosensitive response risk from the same strobes. Step 2: Both provisions are safety-critical (DEAF fire evacuation; NEU seizure). Step 3: evidence-equivalent. Step 4: constraint comparison — DEAF user in a non-strobe building has no alternative fire alarm channel; NEU user can take medication, position away from line of sight, or use vibrotactile alternative. Resolution: strobes provided throughout (DEAF/DBL safety-critical); vibrotactile alerting devices added in sleeping areas as alternative channel for photosensitive populations (CON-0014). Type: RESOLVED-SUPPLEMENTAL (supplemental provision, not ambient compromise).

### §3.9 Resolution Strategies Menu

Eight resolution strategies are available. Strategies are not mutually exclusive and can be combined. Each strategy is referenced by a short code for use in conflict registers and design documents.

**IEC — Individual Environmental Control.**
Provide user-operated adjustment over the parameter (lighting level, CCT, temperature, acoustic setpoint). Eliminates the specification selection problem entirely at Tier 2. Best applied where: (a) individual users can identify their preference and operate controls; (b) the parameter is adjustable without affecting adjacent spaces. Items: H-01, H-02, B-06, F-07. Evidence: Tier 1 for user control of lighting and temperature (Faerden et al. 2022).

**SZ — Sensory Gradient Zoning.**
Assign population-specific ambient parameters to distinct spatial zones using the F-01 three-zone model. Zone 1: high-activation (circulation, assembly); Zone 2: moderate (workspace, consultation); Zone 3: low-activation (sensory retreat, quiet rooms). Design principles: clear zone boundaries; predictable transitions; ≥1.5 m buffer at zone transitions (PAS 6463). Best applied to: illuminance, acoustic level, stimulation density, colour intensity. Items: F-01, A-16, D-05.

**TS — Technical Separation.**
Provide the same provision through separate technical channels that do not interfere with each other. Example: hearing loop (IEC 60118-4) on independent audio input circuit — amplifies direct speech input without broadcasting ambient PA or background music into NDV quiet zones. Example: vibrotactile alerting in sleeping areas (CON-0014) provides DEAF fire alarm channel without strobes visible from sleeping positions. Items: A-10, A-11, B-10, K-04.

**DAR — Design for Adaptable Readiness.**
Build in the structural, services, or fit-out provisions that allow future individual adaptation without structural intervention. Where a specification cannot be resolved at population level (Tier 1), DAR provisions preserve the building's ability to be adapted at Tier 2 (individual OT assessment). Items: all items carrying DAR notation; Part 10.

**SRW — Safety Rule Wins.**
Apply the safety-critical provision to the shared environment without compromise; deliver the comfort/preference provision to the non-safety-critical population through individual supplemental means. Example: ambient temperature ≤18°C (Uhthoff's safety-critical floor); individual radiant panels at fixed seating for PAIN users. This is the most commonly applied strategy for TEMP-RANGE conflicts.

**PP — Plain Provision.**
Where a single plain/undecorated/unstructured specification serves the most constrained population without violating other populations' evidence basis, specify it universally. Example: plain flooring on primary routes (DEM fall prevention, VIS orientation via perimeter contrast rather than floor pattern, NDV/SENS reduced visual noise). Plain provision is a Tier 0 strategy — it produces the simplest specification that satisfies the maximum number of populations simultaneously.

**T0 — Tier 0 Universal Specification.**
Where five or more populations require the same or compatible provisions from independent clinical rationales, designate the provision as a Tier 0 universal and specify it without population conditioning. This eliminates the need for conflict resolution because the convergence is genuine. Tier 0 candidates identified from the connection register: loop circulation geometry (CON-0001); sensory quiet room (CON-0002); fragrance-free policy (CON-0007); prospect-refuge spatial balance (CON-0024). See §1.4.3 for the three-tier hierarchy.

**RS — Range Specification.**
Specify a parameter range that falls within the acceptable zone for both populations, rather than selecting a point value that favours one. The range boundaries are set by the evidence-based limits of each population. Example: grab bar diameter 32–45 mm serves both MOB standard grip (minimum 32 mm) and UPL pathological grip (maximum 45 mm before grip degrades). Range specification eliminates the conflict without compromise to either population. Best applied where: both populations have empirically defined tolerance ranges that overlap. Items: G-03 (grab bar diameter), reach-range items.

**OT-REF — OT Referral (Tier 2 escalation).**
Where population-level resolution is not achievable (Steps 1–5 of §3.8 produce no resolution for a shared non-zoned parameter), refer to Tier 2: OT assessment of the specific individual(s) and OT-mediated co-design. OT-REF is not a fallback from design failure — it is the correct outcome for genuinely irresolvable conflicts where individual variation must govern. The design task at Tier 1 is to ensure OT referral is available, accessible, and actioned through the design brief (Part 9 §9.10 OT threshold).

### §3.10 Co-Occurrence Design Guidance — Synergies

The following pairs share provisions that are compatible or reinforcing rather than conflicting. These are not conflicts requiring resolution; they are convergences that can be specified as single provisions serving multiple populations.

| Pair | Shared domain | Converging specification | Tier 0 candidate? |
|---|---|---|---|
| MOB + OFS | Rest seating | ≤25 m interval (OFS governs — stricter than MOB 50 m); seat 430–480 mm; armrests | YES (5+ populations benefit) |
| MOB + DEM | Plain flooring | Plain, non-patterned flooring on primary routes; flush thresholds | YES |
| NEU + PAIN | Thermal control | Individual supplemental heating at fixed seating positions | No (supplemental, not ambient) |
| NDV + MH | Retreat space | Separate dedicated rooms required (not combinable — different clinical functions); both require RT60 ≤0.4 s | No |
| VIS + DBL | Tactile wayfinding | Continuous ISO 23599 TWSI; tactile maps at entry | YES |
| DEM + OFS | Loop circulation | Loop/single-path plan with rest seating throughout loop route | YES |
| MOB + NEU | Grab bars | Bilateral vertical bars; 32–45 mm diameter; ≥1.3 kN load rating | YES |
| MOB + PAIN | Insulated grab bars | Coated/insulated grab bar surface on all PAIN-primary routes | No |
| NEU + OFS | Photosensitivity | Gradual illuminance transition zones ≥5 m (B-05); eliminate fluorescent | YES |
| MH + PAIN | Thermal control + warmth | Individual radiant supplement; warm ambient preferred; individual control | No |
| MH + OFS | Low-stimulus retreat | Shared retreat space valid — no conflict between OFS and NDV/MH spatial requirements | YES |

### §3.11 Worked Examples — Methodology Level

The following examples demonstrate the synthesis method at building-design level. They trace the §3.7–3.8 process through a specific building type's co-occurrence profile to produce a set of design decisions. For building-scale conflict examples with full worked specifications, see Part 5 §5.4.

**Worked Example 1: Memory Care Residential Unit (DEM primary; MOB secondary)**

Populations: DEM (all residents); MOB/AMB (18 of 20 residents — ambulant with walking aids); MOB/WC (2 residents — manual wheelchair).

Layer 1 decisions: Loop-plan floor plan with no dead-ends (DEM+MOB convergence — T0 strategy). All doors on loop to be automatic sliding with ≥900 mm clear opening (MOB+DEM door conflict — SZ not applicable; TS: automatic with 3 m sensor warning, auditory chime, glazed leaf per §3.9). Secure garden with continuous loop path and bilateral handrails (DEM wandering freedom + MOB fall support — T0 strategy).

Layer 2 decisions: Circadian lighting circuit (≥300 lux daytime, ≥250 EML — DEM Tier 3–5); dimmable zones in staff areas (NDV/SENS staff — IEC strategy). HVAC with individual apartment thermostats (if NEU/MS residents anticipated — SRW: cool ambient + individual supplemental heating).

Layer 3 decisions: Plain flooring throughout resident areas (DEM T1 — PP strategy; VIS orientation via wall/skirting contrast). Bilateral grab bars all corridors ≥15 m (MOB+NEU convergence). High-contrast door frame bands ≥30 LRV (DEM+VIS — convergence).

**Worked Example 2: NDV/OFS Co-Working Office**

Populations: NDV/AUT + NDV/ADHD (25% of headcount — disclosed); DEAF (2 staff — hearing aid users); OFS/ME (8% — self-disclosed).

Layer 1 decisions: Open-plan collaboration spine ≥1500 mm (DEAF signing + OFS circulation — T0); enclosed acoustic pods with RT60 ≤0.3 s (NDV quiet zones — SZ); proximity of pods to open spine (graduated transition — SZ + F-01).

Layer 2 decisions: Hearing loop throughout collaboration spine and all meeting rooms, independent audio input (DEAF — TS: loop without broadcast audio into NDV quiet zones). Fragrance-free HVAC filtration (OFS/MCAS — PP). Individual desk dimming (NDV + OFS photosensitivity — IEC). Rest seating at ≤25 m intervals, including recline option (OFS — T0 candidate; no conflict with NDV or DEAF).

Layer 3 decisions: Muted palette in shared areas (NDV/AUT — PP for shared spaces; NDV/MH warm palette in breakout areas — SZ). No fluorescent luminaires (NDV+OFS convergence — PP). F-06 fragrance-free operational policy in FM brief (OFS/MCAS+NDV/SENS — T0).

**Worked Example 3: Accessible Primary School**

Populations: NDV/AUT + NDV/ADHD (approximately 15% of student body); MOB (3 students — one power wheelchair, two ambulant with aids); DEAF (1 teacher — hearing aid user).

Layer 1 decisions: Sensory quiet room on every floor plate (NDV — A-16, T0); single-path circulation with predictable layout (NDV+MOB — T0); power wheelchair turning space ≥1800 mm at all accessible destinations (MOB — governs over code minimum).

Layer 2 decisions: Hearing loop in every classroom and assembly space (DEAF — IEC 60118-4, TS); RT60 ≤0.3 s in all classrooms (NDV governs over DEAF ≤0.4 s — NDV stricter target is governing); no sound masking (A-13 — NDV, T0); visual fire alarm strobes throughout (DEAF — B-10; NDV seizure risk — vibrotactile in quiet rooms per CON-0014 TS strategy).

Layer 3 decisions: Muted, low-chroma palette in classrooms (NDV — PP); high-contrast TWSI at all stair heads (VIS — mandatory); lever hardware ≤22 N throughout (MOB/UPL — T0).

**Worked Example 4: Hospital Ward — Older Adults Orthopaedic**

Populations: MOB (all patients — post-operative); DEM (estimated 40% of ward — comorbid); NEU/MS (3 patients — confirmed Uhthoff's risk); OFS (POTS confirmed, 2 patients).

Governing conflicts: TEMP-RANGE (NEU/MS vs OFS/PAIN warmth preference). Step 2: NEU/MS is safety-critical. Resolution: SRW — ambient ≤18°C governs; individual radiant panel at each bed position for PAIN/OFS warmth. Plain flooring throughout ward (DEM+MOB — PP strategy). Bilateral grab bars at all patient transfer points (MOB+NEU/hemiplegia — T0 convergence). Circadian lighting daytime (DEM ≥300 lux) with individual dimming at each bed (OFS/PCS photosensitivity — IEC).

**Worked Example 5: Supported Housing — Mixed Needs**

Populations: MOB (4 units — power wheelchair); DEM (3 residents); NDV/MH (2 residents — PTSD); OFS/ME (1 resident).

Layer 1: Loop-plan internal circulation (DEM+MOB — T0); ≥1800 mm turning space at all destinations (MOB/power WC); enclosed apartment entries with exit sightlines from inside to exit (NDV/MH — PTSD geometry requirement — TS: does not conflict with DEM loop plan because loop plan does not create ambush geometry).

Layer 2: Individual thermostats per apartment (OFS/ME requires ≤20°C; MOB/DEM residents may prefer warmer — IEC at apartment level resolves; shared areas at ≤18°C where MS risk applies). Fragrance-free common areas (OFS/MCAS — PP).

Layer 3: Insulated grab bars throughout (OFS/PAIN cold-contact trigger — no conflict with MOB or DEM). Plain flooring in common areas (DEM+VIS — PP).

### §3.12 From Approach to Application

#### §3.12.1 The Prior Question

Before using the matrices in Parts 6 and 7, the designer must answer: which population codes apply to this building, and in what combinations? The answer determines which Part 4 items are mandatory, which conflicts require resolution, which consultant appointments are triggered, and which matrix rows are active.

This question has two forms:

- **New construction for a known client group:** The client can identify specific populations (a dementia care home; a DEAF-majority workplace; a supported living scheme for residents with complex needs). Part 4 items are selected, conflicts resolved, and specifications calibrated to those populations. OT assessment is triggered where individual users are known.

- **Speculative construction for an unknown user group:** The population cannot be specified. Tier 0 provisions apply universally (see §1.4.3). DAR provisions preserve the building's ability to be adapted when users become known. The matrix rows for ALL apply to every speculative building; population-specific rows apply where the eventual user profile is anticipated.

#### §3.12.2 Before the Entry Paths: Four Decisions

Before using the entry paths (§3.12.3), make four decisions that establish the application framework:

**Decision 1: Population identification.** Which population codes apply? Use Part 2 to confirm. A single building may have multiple primary populations (DEAF-primary school also serving MOB students and NDV staff). All active population codes must be carried through the matrix.

**Decision 2: Co-occurrence profile.** What population pairs are present? Cross-reference §3.3. Identify all active conflict domains. Record the resolution strategy to be applied to each (§3.9).

**Decision 3: Design tier.** Is this Tier 0 (speculative, unknown user), Tier 1 (population-identified), or Tier 2 (individual-specific)? Most institutional buildings are Tier 1; speculative residential is Tier 0 with DAR; individual commissions and specialist residential are Tier 2.

**Decision 4: OT appointment.** Is OT assessment required? Use Part 9 §9.2 appointment triggers. For Tier 2 buildings, OT assessment is mandatory. For Tier 1 buildings with complex co-occurrence profiles, OT input at schematic design is best practice. For Tier 0 buildings, OT is not required at design stage but DAR provisions must preserve the building's ability to accommodate OT-assessed modifications later.

#### §3.12.3 Using the Entry Paths and Matrices with the Approach Brief

Three entry paths are available depending on the design question. Each path leads to the same destination (Part 4 specifications and Part 6/7 matrices) by a different route suited to the designer's starting point.

**Entry Path I — By Design Stage:** Use this path when you need to know what decisions to make at a specific design stage (Stage 2 vs Stage 3 vs Stage 4). The Part and Section Map identifies which Part 4 items are Layer 1 (schematic), Layer 2 (services), or Layer 3 (fit-out). Apply in stage order.

**Entry Path II — By Population Code:** Use this path when you have identified a specific population code and need to know all relevant Part 4 items. Each population code in Part 2 carries cross-references to the relevant Part 4 category headings and items.

**Entry Path III — By Building or Room Type:** Use this path when you have a specific building type and need to know which matrix applies. Part 6 (residential matrices) and Part 7 (non-residential matrices) are organised by building type. Select the relevant matrix; cross-reference to Part 4 item specifications for each active population row.

#### §3.12.4 When No Matrix Exists

Parts 6 and 7 do not cover all building types. For building types without a matrix: (1) identify the closest analogous matrix; (2) apply Entry Path II (by population) to generate the specification set from Part 4; (3) apply the co-occurrence synthesis process in §3.7 to resolve conflicts; (4) record the synthesis process and outcomes in the project's approach brief.

#### §3.12.5 What the Approach Changes in How This Volume Is Used

The synthesis approach changes how the guidebook is used in two ways. First, no Part 4 item should be specified in isolation — each item exists in a co-occurrence context, and that context determines whether the item's specification stands as written or requires modification through the resolution decision tree. Second, the conflict register is a design deliverable, not a diagnostic tool. It should be started at Stage 1 and maintained through Stage 5. At Stage 6 (RFO), it is the basis for the FM brief's operational accessibility provisions.
