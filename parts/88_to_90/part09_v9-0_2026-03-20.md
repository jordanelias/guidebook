## Part 10: Working with Specialist Consultants

## 10.0 Overview

Part 10 addresses the architect's coordination role with specialist consultants whose input is required for accessible design that cannot be delivered through engineering briefs alone. The engineering disciplines covered in Part 9 — acoustic, mechanical, electrical, and structural — operate on measurable physical systems. The consultants addressed here operate on the relationship between people and environments: a different domain, requiring a different coordination logic.

The central specialist is the occupational therapist. OT practice is the primary evidence base for this entire guidebook (Volume 1, §1.4 Evidence Hierarchy — Tier 1). An architect working with this guidebook is, in effect, working from a distillation of OT evidence. When the individual client or user population is known, that distillation must be supplemented by direct OT involvement.

Other specialist consultants addressed in this Part — dementia design specialists, DeafSpace consultants, sensory design specialists, and accessibility auditors — each operate on an evidence base with a specific population scope. The appointment triggers and scope of services for each are defined below.

**What this Part does not cover.** Hearing loop specialists and audiologists are addressed in Part 9 §9.1 as acoustic engineering sub-specialists. Post-occupancy evaluation protocols are addressed in Part 13. Legal compliance review by access consultants operating under code frameworks is addressed in Appendix A (Global Standards Catalogue). Fire engineers' involvement in emergency evacuation for disabled occupants is addressed in Appendix D.

## 9.1 The Three-Tier Hierarchy and Specialist Consultant Appointment

The Three-Tier Design Hierarchy (Volume 1, §1.6.3) governs when specialist consultant input is required and what its scope must be.

| Tier | Standard | User Knowledge | Specialist Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 0 | Universal Design / Code Compliance | Anonymous | None required. Design from this guidebook's library items at their stated medians. |
| Tier 1 | Population-Informed Inclusive Design | Identified population (not individual) | Required where the building's primary population includes NDV, DEM, NEU, OFS, or DBL, or where two or more primary populations are present. Required where co-occurrence patterns produce conflicts that population-level medians cannot resolve. Recommended for all other Tier 1 projects. |
| Tier 2 | Person-Specific Inclusive Design | Named individual | OT assessment mandatory. Design cannot proceed to Tier 2 specification without a completed functional capacity assessment for the named person. Co-design process required. |

DAR (Design for Adaptable Readiness) is mandatory at all three tiers regardless of specialist involvement. DAR provisions, once confirmed in the structural and engineering briefs, do not require specialist sign-off — they are standard construction-stage provisions documented in Part 10.

The boundary between tiers is a project decision, not an option. A residential commission for a named client with a known disability is Tier 2 by definition. A dementia care facility serving an identified population is Tier 1. A speculative apartment building where no user population can be characterised is Tier 0. Misclassifying a project at a lower tier than the evidence warrants is a design failure, not a cost saving.

## 9.2 Occupational Therapist

### 9.2.1 Role and Scope

Occupational therapists are the trained professionals whose discipline directly addresses the person-environment-occupation relationship. OT practice is built on the assessment of how a specific person performs specific activities in specific environments — and what environmental modifications would enable greater occupational performance and independence. This is precisely the evidence domain this guidebook draws on.

An OT engaged in a building project is not providing an accessibility audit in the compliance sense. The OT's role is to bring functional capacity evidence — either for a named individual (Tier 2) or for a defined population (Tier 1) — into the design process in a form that the architect can translate into spatial and technical specifications.

The OT does not produce drawings or engineering specifications. The OT produces clinical knowledge that the architect must translate. This translation responsibility sits with the architect and is the reason this guidebook exists.

### 9.2.2 Appointment Triggers

Appoint an OT when any of the following conditions apply:

- **Tier 2 project** — a named individual is the primary occupant or user. Mandatory. OT assessment is the mechanism by which Tier 1 specification ranges are resolved to person-specific values. Without OT assessment, Tier 2 design is not achievable; median Tier 1 values must be used instead, with explicit documentation that individual assessment has not been completed.
- **Residential adaptations to an occupied dwelling** — where a current occupant has a disability and the design brief is to improve that person's independence in their own home. OT referral may precede architectural involvement; the OT assessment report is the primary brief document.
- **Tier 1 project with NDV, DEM, NEU, OFS, or DBL as a primary population** — these populations require environmental calibration beyond the standard design library medians. Sensory zoning (F-01 through F-07), acoustic environment, and individual control provisions (H-01, H-02) require OT review of the proposed environment against population-specific functional profiles before technical design is finalised.
- **Healthcare, supported housing, or specialist education environments** — where the gap between code compliance and genuine occupational independence is at its greatest, and where OT involvement is standard professional practice in most jurisdictions.
- **Post-occupancy evaluation** — where the building has been completed and occupied, and the design team or building owner requires evidence of whether the environment is meeting its accessibility intent. See Part 13.

### 9.2.3 Scope of Services by Design Stage

The following table sets out the minimum scope of OT involvement at each design stage where appointment has been triggered. The scope expands at Tier 2 and contracts at Tier 1; the minimum for each tier is stated.

| Stage | Tier 1 Minimum OT Scope | Tier 2 Minimum OT Scope |
|---|---|---|
| Brief / Pre-Design | Review population brief. Confirm which population codes are primary and which are secondary. Flag any population-specific provisions that fall outside the standard library medians. Identify Tier 2 triggers if any. | Complete functional capacity assessment of named individual. Produce assessment report structured for architectural use (see VI.2.4). Confirm DAR provisions required for anticipated functional trajectory. |
| Schematic Design | Review room adjacency diagram against sensory gradient strategy (F-01). Confirm NOT-RETROFITTABLE items relevant to the population are on the schematic floor plan. Flag any spatial layout decisions that will compromise occupational performance for the identified population. | Review schematic floor plan against assessment report. Confirm toilet position (D-03), grab bar blocking zones (G-03), hoist track zones (I-05), and circulation widths (E-08). Issue schematic-stage specification inputs to architect. Co-design session with named occupant. |
| Design Development | Review item specifications against population profiles. Confirm sensory gradient is deliverable with proposed layout. Review H-01 and H-02 control positions against functional reach envelopes. Confirm accessible bathroom layout (I-04) against population-specific transfer requirements. | Review item specifications against individual assessment. Confirm all Tier 1 ranges are resolved to person-specific values. Co-design session with named occupant on layout, control positions, and assistive technology interface. Issue Tier 2 specification schedule to architect. |
| Construction Documents | Review specification for consistency with OT scope of services outputs. Flag any changes from Design Development that affect occupational performance. | Review drawings for consistency with Tier 2 specification schedule. Confirm grab bar positions on blocking drawing. Confirm hoist track layout. Issue construction-stage compliance check. |
| Ready for Occupancy | Attend ready for occupancy walkthrough. Assess whether completed environment meets the OT-specified outcomes. Issue ready for occupancy assessment report. | Ready for occupancy assessment with named occupant present where possible. Assess occupational performance in completed environment. Issue assessment report identifying any outstanding modifications required. |
| Post-Occupancy | Contribute to POE at 6 and 12 months. Assess occupational performance outcomes against design intent. Identify any residual barriers for gap register. | POE with named occupant. Assess change in occupational independence since occupation. Document outcomes. Recommend any further adaptations. |

### 9.2.4 OT Assessment Report — Architectural Brief Format

For Tier 2 projects, the OT assessment report is the primary brief document from which architectural specifications are derived. To be usable for this purpose, the report must be structured to include:

- Functional capacity profile by domain (mobility, reach, grip, visual, cognitive, sensory processing, communication)
- Functional variation across time (good-day/bad-day range for fluctuating conditions)
- Current assistive technology in use and anticipated future technology
- Priority occupations (the activities that matter most to the individual and that the environment must support)
- Specific environmental barriers in the current environment (for adaptation projects)
- Specification inputs in architectural terms: turning circle requirement; reach envelope; transfer approach (side, angled); grab bar positions; threshold and gradient requirements; lighting, acoustic, and thermal preferences
- DAR recommendations: provisions for anticipated functional trajectory over building lifespan

An OT report that does not include specification inputs in architectural terms cannot be used directly as a design brief. Where a report is received without this structure, the architect should request a supplementary architectural brief session with the OT before schematic design commences.

### 9.2.5 Co-Design and CRPD Article 4.3

OT assessment does not fulfil the co-design obligation in CRPD Article 4.3. The Convention requires States Parties to actively involve disabled people in the development of accessibility standards and measures. This obligation extends to individual building projects where a named person is the occupant: the person's own preferences, priorities, and choices are required inputs to the design alongside OT clinical findings.

Co-design is not user consultation at the end of a design process. It is structured involvement of the disabled person — and where relevant, their family or support network — in design decisions throughout the process. At minimum, co-design sessions are to be held at schematic design stage and at developed design stage. Where the individual's communication needs require adapted participation formats, the OT advises on appropriate process design.

Lived experience of the relevant condition(s) is also a form of evidence. Where the individual client is the primary co-designer, this is straightforward. Where the project serves a population rather than a named individual (Tier 1), the design team is to engage disabled people with lived experience of the relevant condition(s) in design review. The Kelsey IDS co-design methodology, the Gallaudet DeafSpace participatory design process, and Motionspot's neuroinclusive design protocols are established frameworks for population-level co-design; see Volume 1, §1.5 (Evidence Hierarchy — Co-primary Tier 1).

### 9.2.6 Relationship to this Guidebook

This guidebook is a distillation of OT evidence into architectural specification form. Its design library items (Part 7) are derived from Tier 1 sources — primarily OT clinical research and lived experience evidence — and expressed as population-informed ranges calibrated to functional capacity distributions.

An architect using this guidebook without OT involvement is working at Tier 1 using population medians. This is appropriate for straightforward Tier 1 projects — a single primary population, low co-occurrence complexity, and building types where the standard library medians are well-calibrated to the anticipated user profile. It is not appropriate where multiple primary populations are present, where co-occurrence patterns produce irresolvable conflicts under the standard library alone, or where the populations involved (NDV, DEM, NEU, OFS, DBL) require environmental calibration beyond what population-level medians can reliably deliver. In those circumstances, OT or specialist consultant involvement at Tier 1 is the appropriate standard, not an elective enhancement. It is never appropriate for Tier 2 projects, and it does not replace OT involvement where the appointment triggers in §10.2.2 are met.

Where OT assessment findings conflict with the specification ranges in Part 7 of this guidebook, the OT assessment governs for the individual concerned. The ranges in Part 7 define the population evidence envelope; the individual's functional capacity determines where within that envelope the design should land. Departures from the stated range require documented clinical justification from the OT.

## 9.3 Dementia Design Specialist

### 9.3.1 Role

A dementia design specialist applies the environmental research base specific to dementia — principally the DSDC (Dementia Services Development Centre, University of Stirling) evidence body — to building design and assessment. The DSDC EADDAT tool (Environments for Ageing and Dementia Design Assessment Tool, 2022) provides the primary audit and design framework; see Volume 1, §1.5 (Evidence Hierarchy) and the 17-Jurisdiction Research Compendium §B3 for evidence classification.

A dementia design specialist is not a substitute for an OT where individual Tier 2 assessment is required. The two roles are complementary: the dementia design specialist brings environmental design expertise calibrated to the dementia population; the OT brings functional capacity assessment of the individual.

### 9.3.2 Appointment Triggers

Appoint a dementia design specialist where:

- DEM is a primary population code for the building
- The project is a dementia care facility, memory care unit, specialist housing, or any environment where the primary users include people with dementia
- The project involves spatial decisions with the highest DEM-specific stakes: floor plan topology (D-01), toilet visibility from primary living space (D-03), wayfinding landmark strategy (D-04), and colour strategy (C-02, C-05, C-06)

### 9.3.3 Scope

The dementia design specialist reviews the design against the DSDC EADDAT framework and the DEM-specific items in Part 7 of this guidebook (Category C, D, and F items where DEM is a primary population). The specialist's primary contribution is at schematic design stage — confirming the spatial topology and adjacency decisions that are NOT-RETROFITTABLE. At developed design stage, the specialist reviews colour strategy, signage system, and sensory environment against the DEM evidence base.

**Relationship to this guidebook:** DEM item specifications in Part 7 (D-01, D-03, D-04, C-02, C-05) are derived from DSDC evidence. A dementia design specialist using the DSDC framework will be working from the same evidence base. Where findings diverge, the specific DSDC EADDAT assessment for the project environment governs over the guidebook's general population-level specifications.

## 9.4 DeafSpace / Deaf Design Consultant

### 9.4.1 Role

A DeafSpace or Deaf design consultant applies the Gallaudet University DeafSpace design principles — a catalogue of 100+ spatial and material patterns developed through participatory design with Deaf communities — to building projects where Deaf or hard of hearing users are the primary population. See Volume 1, §1.5 (Evidence Hierarchy) and the 17-Jurisdiction Research Compendium §B2 for evidence classification.

The DeafSpace framework addresses five design categories: space and proximity (sightlines for signed language); sensory reach (visual communication range); mobility and proximity (how spatial sequences affect Deaf social interaction); light and colour (conditions for visual communication); acoustics (for hearing device users and those with residual hearing). Several of these — particularly sightline and spatial proximity decisions — fall outside the standard accessible design library and require specialist input.

### 9.4.2 Appointment Triggers

Appoint a DeafSpace or Deaf design consultant where:

- DEAF is a primary population code and the project involves significant social, educational, or civic function — spaces where visual communication and signed language use are primary modes of interaction
- The project is a Deaf school, Deaf community facility, or any environment designed primarily for Deaf users
- Sightline strategy and spatial proximity design are primary design decisions (common in open-plan offices, classrooms, and communal residential spaces)

### 9.4.3 Scope

The DeafSpace consultant reviews sightline strategy, spatial sequence, lighting quality for visual communication, and the design library items most relevant to DEAF users (A-10, A-11, H-03, H-04, B-10). The consultant's primary contribution is at schematic design stage — confirming that the spatial topology supports visual communication — and at developed design stage, for lighting and surface finish decisions.

**Relationship to this guidebook:** The DEAF items in Part 7 address hearing loop infrastructure (A-10, A-11), visual fire alarm (B-10), and accessible intercom (H-04). These are the engineering-deliverable provisions. The spatial and proxemic provisions of DeafSpace design fall largely outside the Part 7 library and are the specialist's primary contribution.

## 9.5 Sensory Design Consultant

### 9.5.1 Role

A sensory design consultant applies population-specific sensory environment evidence — principally PAS 6463:2022 (BSI), the ASPECTSS Design Index (Mostafa), and the OT sensory processing literature — to buildings where NDV, NEU, or MH populations are primary users. The consultant's scope overlaps significantly with OT practice in sensory environments; in many projects, a suitably qualified OT with sensory processing expertise fulfils both roles.

### 9.5.2 Appointment Triggers

Appoint a sensory design consultant where:

- NDV (including NDV/AUT, NDV/ADHD, NDV/SENS) is a primary population code
- The project is a school, workplace, healthcare facility, or supported housing development with a significant neurodivergent population
- Sensory gradient strategy (F-01 through F-07) and the sensory room specification (A-16) are major design decisions requiring population-specific calibration beyond the standard library medians

### 9.5.3 Scope

The sensory design consultant reviews the sensory gradient strategy (F-01), sensory room specification (A-16), acoustic specification (Categories A), lighting specification (Category B), and material/finish choices (Category C) against the NDV/NEU population evidence base. The consultant's primary contribution is at schematic design stage — confirming zone placement and adjacency — and at developed design stage, for material and finish specification.

**Relationship to this guidebook:** The F-category items in Part 7 provide population-level specifications for sensory zoning. The sensory design consultant applies these to the specific building layout and user population, confirms calibration, and identifies where project-specific conditions require deviation from standard medians.

## 9.6 Accessibility Auditor

### 9.6.1 Role

An accessibility auditor provides an independent assessment of a completed or near-completed building's compliance with applicable accessibility standards and with the design intent of the accessibility specification. The auditor's role is verification, not design — the auditor operates after design decisions have been made and construction has occurred or is near completion.

An accessibility audit in the compliance sense (code verification against statutory minimum) is distinct from a functional accessibility assessment of the kind an OT provides. The former asks: does this building meet the applicable standard? The latter asks: can a person with a specific disability use this building independently? Both questions are relevant; they are not the same question.

### 9.6.2 Appointment Triggers

Commission an accessibility audit where:

- The project requires independent verification of code compliance (many healthcare, education, and publicly funded projects require this)
- The design team has specified above-code accessibility provisions and requires verification that those provisions have been delivered as specified
- Post-occupancy: a building is the subject of an access complaint, a change-of-use application, or a significant refurbishment that requires current baseline assessment

### 9.6.3 Relationship to this Guidebook

An accessibility audit verifying compliance with this guidebook's provisions should reference the item specification library (Part 7) as the specification baseline, not only the statutory code. The audit checklist maps directly to the Part 7 item library. Where items are found to be non-compliant, findings are to be recorded against the item code for gap register entry.

The Rick Hansen Foundation Accessibility Certification (RHFAC) programme and the WELL Accessibility Rating provide structured audit frameworks aligned with above-code accessible design practice; see the 17-Jurisdiction Research Compendium §A3 (Canada) and §A1 (USA) respectively.

## 9.7 Coordination Protocol Across All Specialist Consultants

### 9.7.1 Sequencing

Specialist consultant inputs must feed the design process in the right sequence. The following sequence governs where multiple specialist consultants are engaged:

1. **OT assessment** (Tier 2) or **population brief confirmation** (Tier 1) — completed before schematic design commences. This is the input that calibrates all subsequent design decisions.
2. **Dementia design specialist / DeafSpace consultant / sensory design consultant** — engaged at schematic design stage. Their review of the spatial topology must occur before the schematic floor plan is signed off, since their primary concerns (toilet visibility, sightline geometry, zone placement) are NOT-RETROFITTABLE.
3. **Engineering consultants** (Part 9) — brief issued after specialist consultant schematic-stage inputs have been incorporated. The engineering brief reflects the spatial and performance decisions confirmed by specialist consultant review.
4. **OT developed design review** (Tier 2) — after engineering brief is issued and item specifications are drafted. The OT confirms that the technical specifications match the individual's functional requirements.
5. **Accessibility auditor** — commissioned at ready for occupancy or post-occupancy.

### 9.7.2 The Architect's Coordination Responsibility

The architect, as prime consultant, is responsible for ensuring that specialist consultant inputs are:

- Received at the right stage (not after the relevant design decision has been made)
- Translated from clinical or specialist language into architectural specification
- Communicated to other consultants whose scope is affected
- Documented in the project specification with traceability to the source assessment or review

Specialist consultant outputs are not self-executing. An OT assessment report that is received and filed without architectural translation has not been incorporated into the design. A dementia design specialist's review that is received after the schematic floor plan is signed off has arrived too late. The coordination responsibility is the architect's.

### 9.7.3 Documentation

The following documents are to be produced and maintained as part of the project record where specialist consultants are engaged:

| Document | Produced by | Timing | Filing requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| OT functional capacity assessment report (Tier 2) | OT | Before SD | Project brief file |
| OT architectural brief (Tier 2) | OT (with architect) | Before SD | Project specification file |
| Tier 2 specification schedule | Architect (from OT brief) | DD | Specification file |
| Schematic-stage specialist review report(s) | Specialist consultant(s) | End of SD | Design review file |
| Co-design session records | Architect (facilitated) | SD and DD | Project record |
| OT developed design review (Tier 2) | OT | Mid-DD | Design review file |
| Ready for occupancy assessment | OT / auditor | RFO | Handover file; O&M manual |
| Post-occupancy evaluation | OT / specialist | 6 months and 12 months | POE register |

### 9.7.4 VE Protection — Specialist Consultant Scope

The following specialist consultant scopes are not to be reduced or removed from the project without written agreement of the lead architect:

- OT assessment (Tier 2 projects) — removing OT assessment from a Tier 2 project means the design reverts to Tier 1 medians; this must be documented and the client informed
- Dementia design specialist schematic review (DEM primary population) — the NOT-RETROFITTABLE schematic decisions (D-01, D-03) cannot be verified without this review; removing it creates unresolvable design risk at the stage where intervention costs are highest
- Accessibility audit at ready for occupancy — where required by the project brief or funding conditions, this is a contract deliverable and cannot be omitted

## 9.8 Disability Organisations and Lived Experience Input

Specialist consultant appointment and OT assessment are clinical and technical processes. They do not substitute for direct engagement with disabled people and disability organisations — a distinct form of knowledge with distinct validity.

Disability organisations — advocacy groups, community organisations, peer support networks, and user-led organisations — hold lived experience knowledge that clinical assessment cannot access: the knowledge of what it is like to navigate environments designed without adequate input from the people who use them. This knowledge is co-primary evidence under this guidebook's evidence hierarchy (Volume 1, §1.5 — Co-primary Tier 1).

For Tier 1 projects, engagement with the relevant disability community organisations is recommended at schematic design stage. For building types where disabled people are the primary user population (dementia care, Deaf schools, supported housing, neuroinclusive workplaces), this engagement should be structured and documented. The organisations listed in the 17-Jurisdiction Research Compendium §A1–A17 (jurisdiction-specific disability organisations) provide appropriate contact points by country.

For Tier 2 projects, the named individual and their chosen support network are the primary lived experience input. The co-design process described in §10.2.5 applies.
