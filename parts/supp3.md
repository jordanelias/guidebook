## Section III: Accessible Environments for Persons of Extreme Height Including Those with Gigantism or Acromegaly (EXH)

---

## III.0 Purpose, Scope, and Language

This section addresses the design of built environments for persons whose height significantly exceeds the population norm. This population is referred to throughout this section by the code **EXH** (Extreme Height).

**Scope:** This section addresses persons whose standing height is approximately 200 cm or greater, or whose build — including arm span, limb length, and body width — creates systematic difficulties in environments designed for the typical adult population range. It includes persons with clinical conditions associated with excess growth hormone (acromegaly in adults; gigantism in those where the condition onset occurred before growth plate closure) as well as persons of extreme height without pathology.

**Language note:** The terms "gigantism" and "acromegaly" are clinical diagnoses and are used in this section in the context of those specific conditions. For persons of extreme height without a diagnosed condition, the design code EXH is used. "Giant" as an unqualified descriptor is considered inappropriate and is not used.

**What this section does not address:** Management of the underlying endocrine conditions (acromegaly, gigantism) is a medical matter outside the scope of this section. This section addresses only the physical built environment.

---

## III.1 Population and Clinical Background

### III.1.1 Conditions and Prevalence

**Acromegaly** is caused by excess secretion of growth hormone (GH) in adults, typically from a pituitary adenoma, after growth plate closure. It causes progressive enlargement of extremities (hands, feet, facial bones) and soft tissue, and increases in height above the person's pre-condition baseline. Prevalence: approximately 40–60 cases per million population (Colao et al., 2019, *Endocrine Reviews*; Katznelson et al., 2014, Endocrine Society clinical practice guidelines). There are approximately 25,000 people with acromegaly in the USA, \~6,000 in the UK.

**Gigantism** (pituitary gigantism) is caused by excess GH secretion in children or adolescents, before growth plate closure. The result is extreme linear height growth during development. Gigantism is substantially rarer than acromegaly — historically documented at approximately 100 cases per century globally (Eugster \& Pescovitz, 1999), though more cases are now diagnosed with improved imaging. Typical adult heights in gigantism range from approximately 200 to 270 cm; cases exceeding 250 cm are exceptionally rare.

**Tall stature without pathology (EXH/TAL):** Persons at the extreme upper end of normal height distribution — above the 99th percentile, typically ≥195 cm for males, ≥185 cm for females in most European/North American populations — may encounter the same built environment difficulties as persons with clinical conditions. Their needs are addressed by the same design provisions.

### III.1.2 Population Sub-Codes

| Sub-code | Description | Key design characteristics |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| EXH/ACR | Acromegaly | Extreme height + disproportionate enlargement of extremities (hands, feet, facial features); joint complications; cardiovascular comorbidities; possible vision impairment (tumour compression of optic chiasm) |
| EXH/GIG | Gigantism | Extreme height (200–270 cm); proportionate or near-proportionate build at the extreme height; joint complications from skeletal loading |
| EXH/TAL | Tall stature without pathology | Extreme height (approximately ≥195 cm male, ≥185 cm female) without endocrine diagnosis; proportionate build |
| EXH/MOB | EXH + mobility impairment | Extreme height + mobility aid use; requires BOTH EXH and MOB specifications |

### III.1.3 Musculoskeletal and Mobility Complications

This is the critical OT dimension of the EXH population. Acromegaly and gigantism are associated with significant musculoskeletal complications:

- **Arthropathy:** 50–70% of persons with acromegaly develop arthropathy, typically affecting the knees, hips, and spine (Wassenaar et al., 2010, *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology \& Metabolism*; Colao et al., 2019). This is caused by both the mechanical loading of an extreme-height skeleton and direct GH-related changes to cartilage.
- **Spinal stenosis and kyphosis:** Vertebral changes are common in acromegaly and gigantism. This may reduce forward flexion and produce a stooped or kyphotic posture in some individuals — meaning that stated standing height may not equal functional clearance height.
- **Carpal tunnel syndrome:** Occurs in 20–50% of persons with acromegaly (Colao et al., 2019). Relevant to door hardware grip, operable part force requirements.
- **Peripheral neuropathy:** May affect hand and foot sensation.
- **Vision impairment:** Pituitary adenoma may compress the optic chiasm, causing bitemporal hemianopia or other visual field loss in some EXH/ACR individuals. Cross-reference VIS population code.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** The clinical complication rates cited above are from acromegaly clinical literature, which is the most evidence-rich source for this population. Gigantism literature is substantially sparser due to extreme rarity. OT literature specifically addressing built environment provision for EXH populations was not identified in searches; recommendations in this section are derived from clinical anthropometric data, ergonomics research on tall workers, and first-principles dimensional analysis. Post-occupancy evaluation with EXH users is strongly recommended.

## III.2 Anthropometric Data

### III.2.1 Standing Height and Body Dimensions

For design purposes, the relevant EXH population range is approximately 195–270 cm standing height. The following reference data applies:

**General population 99th percentile heights** (Fryar et al., 2016, CDC/NCHS NHANES data; McDowell et al., 2021):

- Adult males, 99th percentile: approximately 193–196 cm
- Adult females, 99th percentile: approximately 181–183 cm

**Acromegaly height data:** Persons with acromegaly typically exceed their pre-condition height by 2–10 cm, or have heights in the upper 2–3% of the general population if the condition was present for many years before diagnosis (Chanson \& Salenave, 2008, *Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases*). Heights of 195–215 cm are commonly documented.

**Gigantism height data:** Extremely variable. Heights of 220–250 cm have been documented in the historical and clinical literature. For design purposes, a working maximum of 250 cm is used; specifications should accommodate persons to at least 220 cm standing height as a minimum.

**Arm span:** For EXH/GIG and EXH/TAL, arm span is approximately equal to standing height (standard human proportion). For EXH/ACR, arm span may slightly exceed height due to hand and extremity enlargement.

**Reach envelope:** At a standing height of 210 cm, typical maximum upward reach (fingertip) is approximately 250–260 cm. Comfortable reach (without stretching) is approximately 210–225 cm. This means standard overhead storage at 200 cm, and standard room height at 240 cm, may be below or at the maximum reach of an EXH person — not above it as for the standard population.

**Table III.2.1 — Approximate Anthropometric Parameters for Design Reference**

| Parameter | EXH/TAL 195 cm | EXH 210 cm | EXH 230 cm |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Standing height | 195 cm | 210 cm | 230 cm |
| Eye level (standing) | \~183 cm | \~197 cm | \~216 cm |
| Maximum upward reach | \~235 cm | \~253 cm | \~277 cm |
| Shoulder height | \~160 cm | \~172 cm | \~189 cm |
| Seated eye height | \~115 cm | \~124 cm | \~136 cm |
| Seated elbow height (AFF) | \~69 cm | \~74 cm | \~82 cm |

Values are approximate, derived from proportional data (Pheasant \& Haslegrave, 2006, *Bodyspace*; DIN 33402-2, 2005 anthropometric data table).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Direct anthropometric datasets for the EXH clinical population are absent from the published literature reviewed. The values above are derived from standard ergonomic proportional formulae applied to height as a reference variable. Clinical researchers have not established a dedicated anthropometric database for persons with gigantism or acromegaly comparable to the CLARITY dataset for achondroplasia.

## III.3 Key Built Environment Barriers

The built environment creates the following systematic barriers for EXH persons, in descending order of frequency and impact:

**1. Doorway headroom:** The dominant UK/European standard door height is 2000–2100 mm (UK BS 8300; EN 14351-1; German DIN). The minimum clear height under a structural lintel is often 2000 mm or less in older buildings. A person of 210 cm standing height has a standing eye level of approximately 197 cm — meaning they are ducking at nearly every door in older buildings, and are at headroom limit at most modern standard doors. Persons above 220 cm tall are typically taller than most standard residential and commercial doorways.

**2. Bed length:** Standard single and double beds (UK, EU) are 190–200 cm internal sleeping length. A person of 210 cm cannot sleep flat in a standard bed. Hospital beds (NHS standard: 210 cm) are at the lower limit; most hotel, residential, and care home beds are shorter.

**3. Ceiling height and overhead hazards:** Standard residential ceiling heights (2400 mm in UK Building Regulations; 2600–2700 mm in Scandinavia) provide nominal clearance for persons to approximately 210–230 cm. However, suspended ceilings, light fittings, signage, and overhead piping frequently reduce effective clearance below nominal ceiling height.

**4. Standard seating and ergonomics:** Standard chair seat heights (430–450 mm AFF) and desk heights (720–740 mm AFF) are designed for a population median of approximately 170 cm male / 163 cm female. At 210 cm height, a person's knees will be above desk level when seated in a standard chair, and their thighs will not clear under standard desk heights.

**5. Vehicle access:** Standard vehicles (including most accessible taxis and minibuses) have cabin headroom of approximately 1200–1400 mm — adequate for the average population but severely restrictive for persons above \~190 cm who need to remain seated, and impossible for persons above \~200 cm when entering/exiting.

**6. Shower and bath:** Standard shower cubicle height (typically 1900–2000 mm spray head and threshold clearance) is below standing head height for persons above 190–200 cm.

**7. Standard accessible WC facilities:** The standard ambulant accessible WC, designed for the mid-height adult, presents unexpected hazards: the overhead projecting cistern in a close-coupled WC may be at face height for an EXH person; grab bars installed at standard height (700–800 mm AFF) may catch against EXH users' lower legs.

## III.4 Standards Gap

No reviewed international accessibility standard — including ISO 21542:2021, BS 8300:2018, ADA 2010, DIN 18040-1, BCA 2025, NS 11001-1, or CSA B651:2023 — contains any provision specifically addressing the needs of persons of extreme height.

The standards that do address building headroom (minimum ceiling heights, door heights) do so from a safety perspective (minimum necessary for the general population), not from an accessibility perspective for EXH users.

The gap is therefore total: EXH persons are entirely absent from the international accessibility framework.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE (GAP-029):** No OT studies specifically addressing built environment design for EXH populations (gigantism, acromegaly, extreme tall stature) were identified in searches of AJOT, OTJR, British Journal of OT, Scandinavian Journal of OT, or allied health databases. This is a complete evidence gap. The recommendations in this section are derived from: clinical literature on acromegaly/gigantism complications (endocrinology journals); ergonomics literature for tall workers (DIN 33402; Pheasant \& Haslegrave); lived experience accounts (advocacy organisations and media interviews); and first-principles dimensional analysis. Gap-register entry GAP-029 is raised for P2 primary research on EXH built environment access.

## III.5 EXH Design Item Specifications

---

### EXH-01 — Door and Opening Headroom

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook entry and circulation items

**Problem:** Standard door heights (2000–2100 mm clear opening in most European and UK standards) are insufficient for persons of 210 cm or above standing height.

**Specifications:**

- New construction: where EXH users are known or likely, clear door opening height minimum 2300 mm. 2400 mm preferred in healthcare, residential care, hotel, and hospitality contexts where EXH users may be guests or residents.
- Door head clearance (clear height from finished floor to underside of structural lintel): minimum 2300 mm in all primary circulation routes.
- Existing buildings: where structural increase of door height is not feasible, identify alternative routes with adequate clearance. Provide wayfinding to EXH-adequate routes from primary entrance.
- Automatic doors: ensure sensor field activates for standing persons to 250 cm height.
- Revolving doors: minimum compartment height 2400 mm to permit upright passage.

**Standards cited:** BS 8300:2018 §8.3 (door minimum 2000 mm clear opening — general standard, not EXH-specific); ISO 21542:2021 §8.2.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No standard mandates door heights above 2100 mm. The 2300–2400 mm specification is derived from the EXH height range and standard proportional clearance margins (minimum 50–100 mm above standing height of intended user population). No OT study confirms this specification; first-principles derivation.

---

### EXH-02 — Ceiling Height and Overhead Clearance

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook general provisions

**Specifications:**

- Primary rooms and circulation: minimum 2600 mm ceiling height from finished floor to lowest solid element (including suspended ceiling panels, light fittings, ducting).
- Corridors serving EXH users: minimum 2400 mm clear height throughout (all hanging elements, signs, light fittings above 2400 mm).
- Overhead hazards: all overhead projections below 2300 mm in circulation routes must be clearly signed (tactile and visual warning per main guidebook VIS items) or physically guarded. This is the general requirement for the whole population; the EXH population makes this requirement critical.
- Internal signage: suspend all hanging directional signs from a minimum 2300 mm to underside.
- Structural beams, pipes, and services: where these create obstructions below 2300 mm at head height for EXH users, either raise them or provide high-contrast visual warning at a normal sightline level (1500–1600 mm AFF).

**Standards cited:** UK Building Regulations Approved Document B (minimum habitable room height 2300 mm); BS 8300 general accessibility requirements.

---

### EXH-03 — Bed and Sleeping Provision

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook healthcare and residential typologies

**Problem:** Standard beds (190–200 cm internal length) are insufficient for sleeping comfort for persons of 210 cm or above.

**Specifications:**

- New construction in residential care, hospital, hotel, and short-stay accommodation: minimum bed provision of one bed per floor with internal sleeping length ≥220 cm.
- NHS standard hospital bed (210 cm): adequate for persons to \~200 cm standing height; specify ≥220 cm internal length for EXH-identified beds.
- Bed frames with adjustable foot end (extending footboard) are preferred in settings where a range of body lengths is expected.
- Bedroom door and en suite door: per EXH-01 (minimum 2300 mm clear opening height) where EXH accommodation is provided.
- Overhead space above bed: minimum 800 mm clearance from pillow level to ceiling or overhead fixture in new construction.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Standard bed specification data from British Standards (BS 3379) and EN 747-1. No OT study on EXH bed provision was identified.

---

### EXH-04 — Seating and Workstations

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook seating provisions

**Problem:** Standard seating (430–450 mm seat height; 720–740 mm desk height) creates significant discomfort and musculoskeletal strain for persons of extreme height because knees cannot be accommodated under standard desk heights and legs cannot be positioned flat.

**Specifications:**

- Height-adjustable seating: minimum seat height range 430–600 mm (extending the upper limit of standard adjustable office chairs to accommodate EXH users).
- Desk height: for an EXH/GIG person of 210 cm, comfortable seated desk height is approximately 790–830 mm AFF. All height-adjustable desks in settings where EXH users are employed or regularly use the space must have a maximum height adjustment of ≥850 mm.
- Seated knee clearance under desk: minimum 750 mm height (standard is 685 mm; EXH thigh length requires additional vertical clearance).
- Horizontal leg clearance under desk: minimum 750 mm depth (standard 600 mm) to accommodate proportionally longer lower limbs.
- Public seating: standard public seating (park benches, transport seats, theatre seating) is uniformly too low for comfort at extreme height. Where EXH users are known users of a facility, higher seating options (500–550 mm seat height) should be provided alongside standard seating.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Specifications derived from Pheasant \& Haslegrave (2006) *Bodyspace* ergonomic proportional formulae and DIN 33402-2 (2005) German anthropometric standard. No specific OT study on EXH workstation design identified.

---

### EXH-05 — Sanitary Provisions

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook items I-03, sanitary fittings

**Shower:**

- Shower head height: minimum 2100 mm AFF for a person of up to 210 cm standing height; 2400 mm preferred in settings where EXH users are expected. Adjustable-height rail or ceiling-mounted shower head preferred.
- Shower cubicle clear internal height: minimum 2300 mm to any overhead obstruction.
- Shower tray: no unnecessary raised lip — flat entry preferred for EXH/MOB users.

**WC:**

- Standard toilet seat height (400–430 mm AFF) is appropriate for EXH users. Raised toilet seats are contraindicated (already tall; raised seat creates uncomfortable posture, higher centre of gravity).
- Overhead cistern clearance: close-coupled cisterns project to approximately 900–1000 mm AFF from floor — at knee or lower-thigh level for an EXH person. Wall-hung or concealed cistern WCs are strongly preferred in EXH contexts.
- Grab bars at standard height (700–800 mm AFF) may obstruct leg clearance for EXH users; specify with lower edge at 650 mm minimum to allow leg passage.

**Washbasin:**

- Standard basin height (800–820 mm AFF) requires excessive forward stooping for a person of 210 cm — the comfortable basin rim height for an EXH user is approximately 900–950 mm. Where EXH-specific facilities are provided, specify basin rim at 900–950 mm. Where standard basin height is used, note that EXH users will require significant forward bending — grab bars or support rails adjacent to basin are recommended.

---

### EXH-06 — Vehicle Access and Transport Facilities

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook transport-related items

**Note:** This item addresses built environment provisions around transport (station facilities, vehicle boarding, accessible taxi bays) rather than vehicle specifications, which are outside this guidebook's scope.

**Specifications:**

- Accessible taxi bays: where vehicles have cabin heights <1800 mm, EXH users cannot enter upright. EXH-accessible vehicles (such as wheelchair-accessible minibuses with ≥1800 mm cabin height) should be identified and made available through booking systems where EXH users are a service population.
- Station and platform facilities: seating, wayfinding, and access routes per main EXH provisions. In bus and rail shelters, overhead clearance per EXH-02 (minimum 2300 mm).
- Aircraft boarding: bridge jetties and gate doorways are typically 2000–2200 mm internal height — at the limit or below for EXH/GIG persons. This is largely outside the scope of standard building design; note for facilities management.

---

### EXH-07 — Ergonomics of Controls and Reach

**Population:** All EXH sub-codes

**Counterintuitive challenge:** For EXH persons, the ergonomic problem is not insufficient reach (they can easily reach the standard maximum of 1220 mm) but awkward posture caused by controls, handles, and counters positioned too low. Bending and stooping repeatedly at standard counter heights (760–900 mm) creates significant spinal loading, particularly for EXH/ACR persons who already have arthropathy.

**Specifications:**

- Controls and door handles in EXH-specific spaces: 1000–1100 mm AFF is preferable to the standard 900–1100 mm — in the upper part of the standard accessible range.
- Reception and service counters: standard counter heights (1050–1100 mm) create a bending posture for EXH users. Where EXH-specific service points are provided, a counter section at 1100–1200 mm AFF is appropriate.
- Grab bars and support rails (if provided for EXH/ACR users with joint complications): position at 900–1000 mm AFF rather than the standard 700–800 mm.
- Storage: top shelves at 2000–2200 mm AFF are accessible without stretching for most EXH users; specify accordingly.

## III.7 EXH Population: Jurisdiction and Rights Notes

**Disability recognition:**

- **Acromegaly and gigantism:** Both conditions are recognised as disabilities in most jurisdictions where the condition causes substantial functional limitation (USA ADA, UK Equality Act, EU disability frameworks, Australian DDA). The musculoskeletal complications (arthropathy, neuropathy) are the primary basis for functional limitation assessment. Vision impairment (from pituitary tumour effects) may provide a separate basis for recognition.
- **Tall stature without pathology (EXH/TAL):** Not recognised as a disability in any jurisdiction. Design provisions for EXH/TAL are not legally mandated; they are best-practice provisions for an underserved user group.

**Advocacy and research bodies:**

- Pituitary Foundation (UK) — www.pituitary.org.uk — includes acromegaly patient support
- Pituitary Network Association (PNA) — USA — www.pituitary.org
- European Acromegaly Consortium (EAC) — clinical research network
- EURORDIS (Rare Diseases Europe) — includes gigantism and acromegaly

## III.8 OT Practice Notes: EXH Environments

### Key Assessment Priorities

OT practitioners working with EXH clients should assess:

1. **Headroom audit:** Does the person's primary living, working, and transit environment provide adequate headroom throughout? Identify all overhead hazards below 2300 mm in primary circulation routes.
2. **Sleeping adequacy:** Is the bed of adequate length? Poor sleep posture from insufficient bed length is a significant long-term musculoskeletal risk.
3. **Workstation ergonomics:** Is the work surface height adequate to prevent persistent spinal flexion? Footrest is not required for EXH users (feet are flat and well-positioned); the priority is elevated desk height.
4. **Joint protection (EXH/ACR):** For persons with acromegaly-related arthropathy, prioritise reductions in bending and stooping at standard-height counters, controls, and basins. This is a direct OT domain.
5. **Handgrip:** For persons with acromegaly-related carpal tunnel syndrome or peripheral neuropathy, assess door hardware and operable parts — lever handles with low operating force preferred.
6. **Visual field (EXH/ACR with visual field loss):** Cross-reference main guidebook VIS provisions as appropriate.
