## Part 3: Designing for Multiple Disability Categories

*Accessible Built Environments — Utopian

Best Practice Guidebook v8.0*

### §3.1 Introduction and Rationale

Disability is not experienced in isolation. Epidemiological data consistently show that more than 55% of disabled adults have two or more disability types simultaneously (WHO Global Disability Report, 2011; Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2022). The Code-as-Floor Principle (§1.2) applies to single-category provisions --- but for persons with multiple disability categories, even beyond-code single-category provisions may be systematically inadequate. Three failure modes are documented where single-category provisions are applied to multiple-category users: - Provisions for one category create barriers for another (e.g., tactile flooring for VIS creating vibration pain triggers for PAIN users; see Part IV for conflict resolution). - Single-category specifications are calibrated to that category's OT evidence without accounting for the compound functional burden of co-occurring conditions. - Design briefs treat disability categories as discrete user groups, failing to design for the majority who present with multiple categories.

### §3.2 Theoretical Framework

#### §3.2.1 ICF and Intersectional Disability

The ICF (WHO, 2001) framework treats disability as arising from the interaction between health conditions, body functions and structures, and environmental and personal factors. Multiple co-occurring health conditions produce compounded environmental interaction — the same environmental barrier affects multiple functional domains simultaneously. A staircase is a mobility barrier for MOB; the effort of navigating around it is a fatigue trigger for CFS/ME; the cognitive demand of the reroute is an additional load for DEM. Single-category design addresses one of these interactions; multiple-category design addresses all three.

#### §3.2.2 Competence-Press Model at Compound Strain

Lawton \& Nahemow's (1973) Competence-Press Model holds that adaptive behaviour occurs in a zone of moderate environmental press relative to individual competence. For multiple-category users, the competence level relevant to environmental press is the lowest (most constrained) functional domain across all co-occurring categories. A person with MOB + CFS/ME has the physical mobility competence of the MOB category and the energy expenditure ceiling of the CFS/ME category. Environmental press that is appropriate for MOB alone may exceed the compound competence ceiling of MOB + CFS/ME. Design for multiple disability categories requires assessment of compound press against compound competence.

### §3.4 Key Co-occurrence Design Guidance
#### MOB + OFS (including CFS/ME, POTS, MS)

Rest seating every ≤20 m (E-10); lift provision even under 3 storeys; automatic doors (E-11); level entry. Energy expenditure budget governs all circulation decisions. MS-specific: thermal management (≤18°C, no humidity accumulation); temporal accessibility provision on high-fatigue days.

#### MOB + DEM

Loop floor plan (D-01) is essential — dead-end corridors generate agitation and disorientation for DEM users navigating with a wheelchair. Toilet visibility from bed (D-03). All MOB turning radii and DEM wayfinding provisions apply simultaneously.

#### NEU + PAIN (MS + fibromyalgia)

Thermal conflict: Uhthoff's (MS, NEU) requires cool temperature ≤18°C; fibromyalgia (PAIN) requires warmth. Resolution: zoned thermal management — primary activity spaces at ≤18°C with individual supplemental heating available (warm throw, portable radiant panel). See Part IV, §IV.4.4.

#### NDV + MH (AUT + PTSD)

Sensory-retreat provision at ground-floor level (no lift required for crisis access); acoustic isolation STC ≥35; individual environmental control; defensible seating with back-to-wall; no exposed structural services overhead.

#### VIS + DBL

DeafBlind users with residual vision require high luminance contrast (LRV ≥50 at critical junctions, exceeding standard VIS ≥30 specification); tactile floor indicators; vibrotactile fire alerting; Braille at all key signs with tactile indicator approaching sign from walking surface.

#### DEM + OFS

Both categories require toilet proximity — DEM for cognitive navigation; OFS/POTS for orthostatic intolerance (sudden urgency, limited standing time). Toilet visible and accessible without more than 20 m travel from any primary occupied space.

#### MOB + NEU (Mobility and Neurological/ABI)

Common presentation: MS (MOB + NEU); stroke (MOB + NEU + DEM); TBI (MOB + NEU). Design must accommodate both the physical access layer and the neurological layer simultaneously. Physical provisions (turning circles, step-free, grab bars) are necessary but not sufficient --- without acoustic and lighting management, a physically accessible space may remain sensorially hostile for a NEU user with sensory hypersensitivity or post-concussion photophobia. Key items: D-01 (loop plan, reduces navigation cognitive load for ABI); B-09 (glare control — critical for PCS); A-13 (no sound masking); E-10 (rest seating, essential for MS fatigue). Temporal accessibility (good days/bad days) governs design: the space must remain accessible at low functional capacity.

#### MOB + PAIN (Mobility and Chronic Pain)

Common presentation: MS with fibromyalgia; arthritis (MOB + PAIN); rheumatoid arthritis (MOB + PAIN + UPL). Physical access provisions (grab bars, turning space) must be specified without creating vibration or cold-contact triggers. Key conflicts: grab bar surface temperature (cold metal triggers PAIN allodynia — specify insulated or coated grab bars per G-03); flooring vibration from textured tactile indicators (E-09) may trigger PAIN — use recessed indicators or acoustic vinyl alternative where PAIN is the primary population. Thermal management critical: PAIN users require warmth; MS users may require cool (see Part IV §IV.4.4 for resolution protocol).

#### NEU + MH (Neurological/ABI and Mental Health/PTSD)

Common presentation: TBI with PTSD (documented in veteran populations);

ABI with depression and anxiety. Both categories require: safe retreat space (A-16 sensory room); individual environmental control (H-02); acoustic isolation from high-stimulation environments (A-03, A-14); defensible seating (F-01). MH adds: no enclosed spaces without visible exits; no overhead exposed services (threat-activation trigger in PTSD); natural light access. NEU adds: no sound masking (A-13); glare suppression (B-09). The intersection is a low-stimulation, controllable environment with visible exits and natural light.

#### NEU + OFS (Neurological/ABI and Orthostatic/Fatigue Spectrum)

Common presentation: MS (NEU + OFS); post-COVID with both cognitive and fatigue symptoms. This pairing produces compound exertion budgeting: the OFS post-exertional malaise threshold constrains total activity volume; the NEU cognitive fatigue threshold constrains cognitive task duration. Design implication: rest seating with back support (not just perching options) at ≤15 m on primary routes (more frequent than the ≤20 m standard); seated workstation options for all primary tasks (F-05); access to all building functions without sustained standing or aerobic exertion (E-10 extended frequency). Thermal management for MS overlap: ≤18°C in primary activity spaces.

#### MH + PAIN (Mental Health/PTSD and Chronic Pain)

Common presentation: fibromyalgia with depression (PAIN + MH); chronic pain syndrome with PTSD. Both populations benefit from: individual thermal and acoustic control (H-02); access to natural light (biophilic provisions); fragrance-free environments (F-02) — pain amplification is worsened by chemical sensitivity. PAIN requires warmth; MH requires controllability. Resolution: individual radiant heating available at seated workstations, with cool ambient air as default. Design must not create environments that amplify pain perception (hard surfaces, cold materials, high acoustic reverberation all documented as pain-amplifying environmental factors).

#### MH + OFS (Mental Health/PTSD and Orthostatic/Fatigue Spectrum)

Common presentation: PTSD with ME/CFS; anxiety disorder with POTS (documented in post-COVID populations). Both categories require: low exertion access (E-10 rest seating, step-free throughout); individual environmental control (H-02); and access to retreat without navigating high-stimulation spaces. MH adds: visibility of exits; choice of open or enclosed seating; no overhead surveillance or enclosed approaches to spaces. OFS adds: no sustained standing requirements; no routes requiring aerobic exertion. The design intersection is a low-exertion, controllable environment with spatial choice.
