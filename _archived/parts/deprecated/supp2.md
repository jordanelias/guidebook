## Section II: Accessible Environments for People of Short Stature Including Those with Dwarfism (LPA)

---

## II.0 Purpose, Scope, and Language

This section addresses the design of accessible built environments for people of short stature, including those with dwarfism and related skeletal dysplasias. This population — referred to throughout this section by the code **LPA** (Little People and associated conditions) — is entirely absent from all reviewed international accessibility standards, including ADA 2010, BS 8300:2018, ISO 21542:2021, DIN 18040-1, NS 11001-1, BCA 2025, and CSA B651:2023.

**Language note:** This section uses person-first language — "person with dwarfism," "person of short stature" — consistent with this guidebook's language standards. "Little person" and "little people" are widely used and accepted terms within the community (Little People of America [LPA]; Restricted Growth Association UK [RGA UK]). "Restricted growth" is the preferred clinical and community term in the UK. The term "midget" is considered derogatory and is not used. See also AUCD Language Research Guide (2024).

**Scope:** This section addresses the design of buildings and built environments for adult and adolescent persons of short stature, regardless of the underlying medical condition.

---

## II.1 Why Existing Standards Are Insufficient — The LPA Design Gap

Accessibility standards have developed primarily around two reference populations: the ambulant adult (with and without mobility aids) and the manual wheelchair user. The anthropometric data underpinning most standards reflects a population range from the 5th percentile female (typically \~1520 mm standing height in European populations) to the 95th percentile male (\~1905 mm). People of short stature — adults whose standing height ranges from approximately 1100 to 1470 mm for the most common conditions — fall entirely below the 5th-percentile female design baseline.

The consequences are systematic:

- Controls, counters, ATMs, and service points are positioned above comfortable reach range.
- Reception counters create complete communication barriers — LPA users may not be visible above counter level.
- Standard accessible toilets are designed around wheelchair transfer, not the ambulant short-stature user.
- Stair handrails at standard heights may be unreachable during use.
- Accessible spaces create a second barrier: accessible WC cubicles, designed for wheelchair users, have grab bars at heights inaccessible to LPA users and frequently have raised toilet seats that further complicate use.
- Social and dignity barriers arise in everyday life: being unseen at service counters, needing assistance for routine actions that an average-height person performs independently.

**The "Statuarized Spaces" Problem:** Sociological research conducted with persons with dwarfism in the UK (*Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research*, 2015; *Disability \& Society*, 2020) documented a paradox: people with dwarfism are not only excluded by mainstream spaces but are often also excluded from — and challenged when using — accessible spaces designed for wheelchair users. A "hierarchy of impairments" operates in practice: the accessible WC designed for wheelchair use may have a raised toilet seat that creates new difficulties for an ambulant LPA user. This guidebook takes the position that accessible design must address the full population of disabled persons, including those whose needs differ from the wheelchair-user norm around which most standards have been constructed.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** Peer-reviewed OT literature specifically addressing LPA built environment design is sparse. The most relevant sources are: the *Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research* (2015) qualitative study (UK, n=22, semi-structured interviews); the *Disability \& Society* (2020) paper (UK, photo-elicitation + interviews); and the ANSI accessibility data cited by Kennedy (2003, *Little People*, Rodale Press) regarding reach heights. Design specifications in this section are derived from anthropometric data combined with clinical and community knowledge. Architects should commission post-occupancy evaluations with LPA users.

## II.2 Population Sub-Codes: LPA Framework

**Table II.2.1 — LPA Sub-Codes**

| Sub-code | Description | Key design characteristics |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| LPA/ACH | Achondroplasia and disproportionate short stature (most common) | Short limbs, normal-range trunk; arm span significantly shorter than height; macrocephaly possible; may have cervical instability requiring ergonomic head support |
| LPA/PSS | Proportionate short stature (all-over reduced height) | All dimensions proportionately reduced; arm span proportionate to height |
| LPA/MOB | LPA + mobility impairment (wheelchair user) | Requires BOTH LPA AND MOB specifications; wheelchair dimensions will differ from adult standard (shorter seat depth, lower push handle) |
| LPA/JT | LPA + joint complications (SED, pseudoachondroplasia, Morquio) | Pain, joint instability, and mobility limitations in addition to short stature; may require grab bars; lower force thresholds |

**Note on LPA/ACH vs LPA/PSS reach envelopes:** These differ significantly. A person with achondroplasia may have a standing height of \~130 cm but an arm span of only \~110 cm (female) to \~122 cm (male), making comfortable reach substantially shorter than height suggests. A person with proportionate short stature at the same standing height will have a proportionately longer arm span. Design specifications should accommodate the more restrictive envelope (LPA/ACH) wherever both sub-types may be present.

## II.3 Anthropometric Data

**Primary source:** Hoover-Fong et al. (2021), CLARITY study — 37,349+ data points from 1,374 subjects with achondroplasia across 4 US centres, birth–75 years. Published in *Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases*.

**Table II.3.1 — Adult Achondroplasia Anthropometric Summary**

| Parameter | Male (median, SD) | Female (median, SD) | Design use |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Standing height | 129.9 cm (±6.25) | 122.4 cm (±5.9) | Floor-level reach design: 5th percentile female \~110 cm |
| Arm span | \~122 cm | \~110 cm | Controls forward reach: max comfortable \~700–850 mm |
| Leg length | \~50% shorter than general population | \~50% shorter | Step/stair geometry |
| Sitting height | Within low-normal range (spine relatively unaffected) | Within low-normal range | Seating height, desk height |
| Foot length | \~20% shorter | \~20% shorter | Footrest dimensions |

Source: Hoover-Fong et al. (2021) *Orphanet J Rare Diseases*; Merker et al. (2018) *American Journal of Medical Genetics*.

**Australian population data** (Tofts et al., 2017, n=138): Female adult 125 cm (range 116–132 cm); Male 134 cm (range 125–141 cm). Consistent with CLARITY data.

**Key derivation — maximum reach:**

- ANSI survey of LPA members (cited Kennedy, 2003; Stepnwash, 2025): maximum upward reach of persons with dwarfism ≈ 1220 mm (48 inches).
- This finding drove the US reduction of ATM maximum height from 1370 mm to 1220 mm.
- 1220 mm is the maximum, not comfortable, reach. Comfortable forward reach (without extension) for an average LPA/ACH adult female is estimated at approximately 750–900 mm AFF, based on arm span data.
- **Design principle:** 1220 mm is the absolute maximum height for any operable element. Controls in frequently-used or time-pressured contexts (fire escapes, door hardware, payment terminals) should be at or below 1000 mm wherever possible.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE (GAP-024):** The ANSI reach figure of 1220 mm is cited via Kennedy (2003) as a secondary source. Primary ANSI survey data has not been independently verified for this guidebook. This figure is widely referenced in LPA advocacy and accommodation literature and is treated here as the best available evidence, but primary source verification is recommended before use in mandatory specifications.

## II.4 LPA Design Item Specifications

---

### LPA-01 — Reception and Service Counters

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook counters and service points items

**Problem:** Standard service counters (typically 1050–1100 mm AFF in UK/European practice; 864–915 mm for accessible counters per BS 8300) remain above the sight and reach line of LPA users. Even BS 8300 "accessible counters" at 760 mm AFF — designed for wheelchair users — may be above LPA forward comfortable reach and eye level.

**Specifications:**

- All new public-facing service counters: provide a low-counter section at 760 mm AFF (minimum 760 mm wide) per BS 8300 / ADA wheelchair provision.
- Preferred provision: additional counter section at 660–700 mm AFF (depth 300 mm) where LPA users are a known or likely user group.
- At any counter, a low-level transaction zone must be provided: written material, payment terminals, and communication at ≤760 mm AFF.
- Payment terminals and PINpads: maximum height 1000 mm AFF; screen at ≤760 mm AFF preferred. Screen readable from 300–600 mm distance.
- Glazed screens above counters (security, pharmacy): ensure a sightline opening at 760–900 mm AFF in addition to the standard eye-level opening.
- Intercom/call systems at counters: button at ≤760 mm AFF.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** The sightline barrier at service counters is documented in lived experience literature as creating not only functional barriers but social dignity barriers that constitute a form of discrimination under the UK Equality Act 2010 and the ADA (Van Etten, cited Kennedy, 2003).

**Standards cited:** BS 8300:2018 §9.5; ADA §904.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No standard specifies additional LPA-specific counter provisions beyond the wheelchair counter provision.

---

### LPA-02 — Door Hardware and Controls

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook item K-01

**Specifications:**

- Door handles: lever type at 900–1000 mm AFF (standard). Force required must not exceed 22 N for LPA/JT users.
- Internal door openers (button-operated): maximum 1000 mm AFF; minimum 700 mm AFF.
- Where door handles are at standard 1000–1100 mm AFF: provide a secondary pull handle or push plate at 700–800 mm AFF for LPA/ACH users, where reach at 1000 mm requires arm extension and raises safety concerns (cervical instability — see LPA-12).
- Turnstiles: avoided wherever LPA users are anticipated. Turnstile hip height (typically 900–1100 mm) is at or above LPA shoulder height, creating a physical barrier. Gate or pass door must be provided in all cases.
- Smart locks and access systems: control panel at 700–1000 mm AFF; touchscreen accessible at ≤800 mm preferred.

**Standards cited:** Main guidebook K-01; BS 8300:2018 §9.4; ISO 21542:2021 §8.3.

---

### LPA-03 — Lift Controls

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook vertical circulation items

**Problem:** Controls above 1000 mm require significant overhead reach for an LPA/ACH adult of average height (\~1230 mm standing). Standard lift control panels (900–1500 mm AFF) are theoretically within LPA maximum reach (1220 mm) but require arm extension throughout.

**Specifications:**

- Lift control panel: button range 700–1100 mm AFF.
- Current international standards (ISO 21542 §11.7; BS 8300 §8.6; ADA §407) require button centres at 900–1220 mm for wheelchair accessibility. An LPA-accessible lift must additionally provide controls at ≤1100 mm AFF.
- Best-practice provision: dual-panel lift with second set of controls at 700–1000 mm AFF on the side wall; or control panel spanning 700–1100 mm AFF vertically.
- Emergency controls (door-open button, emergency call, ground floor button): 700–1000 mm AFF.
- Tactile floor designators on buttons: required (ISO 7000; ADA).

**Standards cited:** ISO 21542:2021 §11.7; BS 8300:2018 §8.6; ADA §407.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No standard specifically addresses LPA lift control placement. The 700–1100 mm range is derived from anthropometric data and ANSI findings cited in Kennedy (2003).

---

### LPA-04 — Accessible Toilet Provisions

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook item I-03

This is the most complex LPA design challenge. Standard accessible toilet facilities are designed around the wheelchair-transfer model. Several features of wheelchair-accessible toilets create secondary barriers for ambulant LPA users:

| Feature | Impact for LPA users |
|---------|---------------------|
| Raised toilet seat (430–480 mm AFF) | Provides better height than standard (400 mm) — coincidentally helpful for some LPA users |
| Grab bars at 700–800 mm AFF (wheelchair transfer) | At or above comfortable reach for many LPA/ACH adults; unhelpful for balance |
| Large accessible cubicle | Full-length cubicle may feel insecure for LPA users |
| Paper dispensers at 850–1050 mm AFF | May be above comfortable reach |

**Specifications:**

- Toilet seat height: 400–430 mm AFF is the most functional height for ambulant LPA users.
- WC centreline: 460 mm from side wall (standard accessible) — appropriate.
- Grab bars for ambulant LPA users who need balance support: a grab bar at 650–700 mm AFF (wall-mounted, side of WC) provides reachable support for an LPA adult of \~1220–1300 mm standing height. Standard wheelchair grab bars at 700–800 mm AFF are acceptable.
- Flush control: maximum 900 mm AFF. Sensor flush preferred.
- Toilet paper dispenser: 600–750 mm AFF (lower than adult standard of 850–1000 mm).
- Best practice: a "universal" unisex accessible cubicle providing BOTH wheelchair-transfer provisions AND a grab bar at 650–700 mm for ambulant LPA users, plus TP dispenser at 600–750 mm.
- Mirror: lower edge at ≤900 mm AFF.
- Washbasin: 760 mm AFF rim height (wheelchair standard) — coincidentally appropriate for LPA users.
- Soap, paper towel, hand dryer: 600–900 mm AFF.

**Commentary:** People with dwarfism have documented experiences of being challenged or denied access to accessible toilet facilities in the UK because they do not appear to be disabled in the stereotypical (wheelchair-user) sense (*Disability \& Society*, 2020). Universal accessible design must include LPA users. Architects and facilities managers should ensure that accessible WC signage and policy does not exclude LPA users.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No controlled study confirms optimal WC seat height for LPA.

### LPA-05 — Stair and Ramp Handrails

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook vertical circulation items

**Problem:** Standard handrail height (865–1000 mm AFF per ISO, BS, ADA) is within LPA maximum reach but at or above comfortable grip height for many LPA/ACH adults. A person with achondroplasia ascending a stair may need to reach overhead to grasp a standard-height rail — an awkward and potentially unsafe posture.

**Specifications:**

- All public stairs and ramps: dual handrail recommended.
- Primary rail: 865–1000 mm AFF (adult standard).
- Secondary rail: 600–750 mm AFF, continuous, same diameter and return specification as primary.
- Stair geometry (LPA/ACH): standard tread depth 280 mm and riser height 150–170 mm are appropriate — achondroplasia does not affect leg stride in proportion to stair geometry.
- LPA/JT users (joint complications): lower risers (≤150 mm) and deeper treads (≥300 mm) are recommended. Consistent with the ambulant mobility aid user specification in the main guidebook.
- Note on stair nosings: LPA users with achondroplasia may have shorter foot length (\~20% below average) — standard nosing projections (max 25 mm) are appropriate.

**Standards cited:** ISO 21542:2021 §8.4; BS 8300:2018 §8.5; Sensory Trust UK (mid-rail 600–750 mm).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No confirmed mandatory dual-rail standard in reviewed national codes specifically addresses LPA users.

---

### LPA-06 — Wayfinding and Signage

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook wayfinding items

**Adult-appropriate provision:** LPA adults are literate and navigating environments independently. Wayfinding provisions must be adult-appropriate and must not patronise LPA users with pictographic-only signage.

**Specifications:**

- Signage mounting height: lower edge at ≤1000 mm AFF for wayfinding signs in environments where LPA users are a significant population.
- Tactile wayfinding (TWSI): ground-level tactile strips benefit LPA users as well as visually impaired users (ISO 23599:2019).
- Directory/information displays: interactive touchscreens in lobbies and public buildings at 760–1000 mm AFF. Screen tilt preferred over vertical-only display.
- Maps and floor plans: at accessible counter height (760 mm) or as touchscreen at ≤1000 mm AFF.

---

### LPA-07 — Seating and Workstations

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook seating provisions

**Rationale:** Persons with achondroplasia have sitting height within lower-normal range (trunk relatively unaffected) and may be able to use a standard desk surface if seating height is appropriately adjusted. Leg length is the critical variable — significantly shorter legs mean a standard chair seat height will leave feet entirely unsupported, creating musculoskeletal strain over prolonged use.

**Specifications:**

- Workplace seating: height-adjustable chair (350–550 mm seat height range — lower than standard office chair range of 430–500 mm). Adjustable footrest or integrated footrest required; footrest height 150–250 mm.
- Desk surface: 680–720 mm AFF — appropriate for the seated LPA/ACH adult.
- All height-adjustable standing desks must have a lower-limit seated height of ≤720 mm.
- Public seating (waiting areas, theatres): LPA adults will typically sit in standard seating. Footrests or a low footbar under seating rows is recommended in venues where LPA users are a population group.

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No specific study confirming optimal seating parameters for LPA users was identified for the footrest recommendation; derived from clinical principles.

---

### LPA-08 — Kitchen and Catering

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook kitchen provisions

**Problem:** Standard kitchen counter heights (860–900 mm European practice; 865 mm BS/ISO) are at or above LPA shoulder height. This makes cooking and food preparation significantly more effortful and potentially hazardous.

**Specifications:**

- Counter height: 750–800 mm AFF preferred (lower limit of wheelchair-accessible counter range).
- Hob/cooktop: maximum 750 mm AFF; touch controls preferred (not physical knobs requiring elbow-height reach).
- Overhead storage: maximum accessible shelf at 1100 mm AFF without step assistance; or pull-down shelf mechanisms.
- Step stool: integral fold-out step at 150–200 mm height in cabinet base units — as supplementary provision for shelf access above 1100 mm.
- Kitchen sinks: 760 mm AFF rim height; knee clearance 685 mm.

**Caution:** Open flames and hot surfaces at counter level create a burn risk for LPA/ACH users whose hands and arms approach at a lower angle than average. Induction hobs reduce this risk. Barrier or front-control design preferred.

---

### LPA-09 — Parking and Approach Routes

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook site access items

**Context:** LPA users typically drive using standard cars (often with pedal extensions or raised seating) and do not require the wide lateral transfer zone of a wheelchair-accessible bay. However, they do require proximity to the building entrance, as walking longer distances may be tiring for LPA/JT users.

**Accessible parking entitlement:**

- USA: ADA disabled parking — confirmed eligible.
- UK: Blue Badge scheme — confirmed eligible.
- EU, Australia, Canada: recognised under equivalent disability legislation.

**Specifications:**

- No LPA-specific parking bay dimensions required. Standard accessible bay (BS 8300:2018: 2400 mm + 1200 mm transfer zone) is appropriate.
- Parking payment machines and intercom systems: controls at ≤1000 mm AFF. Most existing pay-and-display machines (1200–1600 mm AFF) are inaccessible to LPA users.
- Route from parking to entrance: compact distance (≤50 m recommended for LPA/JT users).

---

### LPA-10 — Swimming Pools and Sports Facilities

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook recreational provisions

**Specifications:**

- Pool entry: steps or ramp entry (not ladder-only). Handrail at 600–750 mm AFF secondary rail on pool steps — consistent with dual-rail provision recommended at LPA-05.
- Changing rooms: height-adjustable benches preferred. If fixed, provide at 380–420 mm AFF.
- Locker controls: at ≤1000 mm AFF. Lower lockers must be assigned for LPA users; upper sections of standard full-height lockers are inaccessible.
- Turnstiles: not appropriate for LPA users — gate alternative required (see LPA-02).

---

### LPA-11 — Healthcare Settings

**Population:** LPA all sub-codes

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook healthcare building typology items

**Note:** People with dwarfism use specialist healthcare services at higher rates than the general population for orthopaedic, neurological, and other dwarfism-related care.

**Specifications:**

- Examination tables: height-adjustable to ≤430 mm for patient mounting. Step at 150–200 mm at table end.
- Consultation seating: 380–420 mm seat height; footrest provided.
- Reception counters: per LPA-01.
- Communications design: the physical environment should support direct face-to-face interaction between clinician and LPA patient. Never direct communication to a carer above the LPA patient.

---

### LPA-12 — Cervical and Spinal Considerations (LPA/ACH)

**Population:** LPA/ACH specifically

**Cross-reference:** Main guidebook NEU items

**Clinical note (for OT practitioners):** Achondroplasia involves a narrow foramen magnum at the base of the skull and may cause cervical spine instability or stenosis. Extreme head extension (looking straight up) should be avoided. This has environmental design implications:

- Overhead controls, displays, and wayfinding signs requiring upward gaze should be avoided. Preferred: horizontal sightline (760–1000 mm AFF) or slight upward gaze (≤30° above sightline).
- Overhead screens and departure boards in public transport, airports, and theatres: provide supplementary information at seated/lower eye-level displays.
- This is an OT advisory note, not a mandatory design specification. OT practitioners working with individual LPA/ACH clients should assess cervical status and advise on environmental modifications accordingly.

**Standards cited:** Based on clinical literature on achondroplasia neurosurgical complications (Trotter \& Hall, 2005, American Academy of Pediatrics guidance; Hecht et al. review).

> **⚠ EVIDENCE NOTE:** No specific built environment study identified. Recommendation derived from clinical principles and OT practice.

## II.7 OT Practice Notes: LPA Environments

### Assessment Framework

OT practitioners assessing environments for LPA clients should apply the Person-Environment-Occupation (PEO) model (Law et al., 1996), examining:

1. **Reach envelope:** Which elements in the environment exceed LPA comfortable forward reach (≤900 mm AFF for LPA/ACH) or maximum reach (1220 mm AFF)?
2. **Gaze angle:** Which elements require significant neck extension or upward gaze that may be contraindicated for LPA/ACH clients?
3. **Social visibility:** Where does the physical environment create social barriers (unseen at counters, unreachable signage) that diminish the client's independent occupational performance?
4. **Accessible space paradox:** Do the existing "accessible" provisions (accessible WC, accessible routes) actually accommodate the LPA client's needs, or do they create secondary barriers?
5. **Workplace:** Does the workstation set-up provide appropriate seating height, footrest, and tool reach?

### Priority Modifications in Existing Buildings

| Modification | Priority | Estimated cost tier |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| Lower TP dispenser in existing WC | P1 | Low (hardware change) |
| Lower kitchen shelves / add step | P1 | Low–medium |
| Add footrest to workstation | P1 | Very low |
| Add secondary stair handrail | P1 | Medium |
| Lower lift button panel | P2 | Medium–high |
| Modify service counter with low section | P2 | Medium |
| Lower reception counter | P3 | High (structural if counter is fixed) |

### Escalation Threshold

Where an LPA client is denied access to their place of work, their toilet facilities, or their primary means of vertical circulation in a building (lift with inaccessible buttons, no secondary handrail on only stair), these are P1-level items and potential breaches of the applicable equality legislation in all reviewed jurisdictions.
