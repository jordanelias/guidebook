**Date:** 2026-05-04

## Netherlands (NL)

**Regulatory framework:**
- NEN 9120:2025 — published Feb 2025 by NEN. Dutch national application of EN 17210. Commissioned by Ministry of Interior. Free download.
- Accessible Building Guide (Toegankelijk Bouwen) — launched April 2025. Seven "building blocks" for inclusive construction process.
- 2M+ people with disabilities; 35% stay home if unsure about location accessibility.
- Government target: 100,000 new homes/year, 2/3 affordable.

**Economic data found:** None specific to accessibility costs. OECD 2025 Economic Survey covers housing market but not accessibility economics.

**Grant programmes:** Not investigated — would need Dutch-language search for Wmo (Wet maatschappelijke ondersteuning) adaptation funding details.

**Assessment:** Strong regulatory infrastructure but no published cost studies. NEN 9120 is important as a European harmonisation reference (EN 17210) but the NL has not conducted TERRAGON-style cost analyses.

## Ireland (IE)

**Regulatory framework:**
- Part M 2022 of Building Regulations — operational from Jan 2024. M1-M5 requirements covering access and use. Updated from Part M 2010.
- Technical Guidance Document M 2022 — practical guidance on Part M compliance.
- Disability Access Certificate (DAC) — required for non-residential buildings (€800 application fee). NOT required for dwelling houses.
- NDA (National Disability Authority) published housing factsheet Nov 2024.

**Grant programmes:**
- Housing Adaptation Grant for People with a Disability: up to €30,000 (updated Dec 2024 with new income disregards)
- Mobility Aids Grant: up to €6,000
- OT assessment: up to €300 reimbursable from grant

**Economic data found:** No Part M cost compliance studies found. SCSI (Society of Chartered Surveyors Ireland) published a consumer guide on disability access but no cost analysis.

**Assessment:** Comprehensive regulatory and grant framework but no published cost data for Part M compliance. The €30K grant cap (comparable to UK DFG £30K) provides a data point for grant_programmes in economics.json.

## New Zealand (NZ)

**Regulatory framework:**
- Building Act 2004, Schedule 2 — lists building types that must be accessible. DOES NOT include residential housing.
- NZS 4121:2001 — Design for Access and Mobility: Buildings and Associated Facilities. Covers public/commercial buildings only.
- Building Code Clause D1 (Access Routes) — performance-based.
- HUD has accessibility design standards for social housing.
- Whaikaha — Ministry of Disabled People (established 2022).

**Key insight:** NZ building code does NOT mandate residential accessibility. The Keall RCT evidence (the project's strongest single BCR data point) comes from a jurisdiction where residential modifications are voluntary/funded, not code-mandated. This STRENGTHENS the design-stage argument: if voluntary modifications in inaccessible housing produce 9:1 BCR, then designing accessibility in from the start (lower cost, more comprehensive) should produce equal or better returns.

**Grant programmes:**
- ACC (Accident Compensation Corporation) home modifications for injury-related disability
- Ministry of Health funded modifications through Disability Support Services
- Keall programme funded through Building Research Levy (BRANZ)

**Economic data found:** Keall 2017 BCR data already in project. No new cost studies.

**Assessment:** Important for the methodological argument (voluntary modifications in unregulated context → strong BCR), but no new cost data beyond what's already captured.

## Japan (JP) — Beyond Financing

**Regulatory framework:**
- Heart Building Law 1994 → Transportation BF Law 2000 → Barrier-Free New Law 2006 → Universal Design 2020 Action Plan
- Barrier-Free Act 2006 (高齢者、障害者等の移動等の円滑化の促進に関する法律): covers public transportation, public buildings, shopping centers. Fines up to ¥3M for violations. Does NOT mandate residential accessibility.
- MLIT General Principles of Universal Design Policy (2005): comprehensive framework covering buildings, transport, roads.
- 2025 Building Code update: new structural review requirements (35-day review period from April 2025), but not specifically accessibility-focused.

**Housing deficit data:**
- Only **42.4%** of households with older adults reach "standard level" barrier-free (handrails at 2+ places, no indoor level differences)
- Only **8.8%** reach "high level" barrier-free (handrails + no level differences + wheelchair-width corridors)
- Source: Statistics Bureau of Japan, via Tsuchiya-Ito et al. 2022

**Tsuchiya-Ito research chain (IHEP/Dia Foundation) — now 8 papers:**

| Year | Paper | Journal | Key finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2019 | JP-SE cross-cultural comparison (with Iwarsson/Slaug, Lund) | J Cross-Cultural Gerontology | Environmental challenges differ by housing stock characteristics |
| 2020 | Housing Enabler content validity (conference abstract) | Gerontologist | Japanese adaptation of Swedish Housing Enabler tool |
| 2022 | Implementation and costs by functional limitation | BMC Geriatrics | Cost data by disability type under Kaigo Hoken |
| 2023 | Frailty prevention | BMC HSR | Housing adaptations prevent care needs deterioration |
| 2024a | LTCF admission reduction | JAMDA | 28% lower institutional admission |

**Key insight:** The Tsuchiya-Ito group at IHEP (Institute for Health Economics and Policy) / Dia Foundation is the most prolific accessible housing economics research group outside of the UK/NZ/US. Their work bridges Japanese LTC insurance claims data with Swedish housing accessibility methodology (Iwarsson/Slaug, Lund University), creating a unique cross-cultural evidence chain. The 2024b paper (Housing Enabler JP adaptation) is particularly significant because it creates a validated Japanese tool for the kind of housing accessibility assessment that underlies all the cost-effectiveness evidence.

## Remaining Jurisdictions — Batch Assessment

**Method:** Single batch English-language search for accessibility construction cost studies across IT, ES, SE, NO, DK, FI, KR.

**Result:** No jurisdiction-specific accessibility cost studies found for any of the 7 remaining jurisdictions through English-language search. The only cost-relevant finding is Slaug et al. 2017 (IJERPH, SE/DE simulation).

**Sweden (SE):** Strongest research infrastructure through Lund University CASE (Centre for Ageing and Supportive Environments). Slaug, Iwarsson, and the Housing Enabler tool originate here. A current simulation study protocol (2022) will generate long-term CBA data for housing adaptations. >90% of Swedish senior citizens live in ordinary housing, ~80% in pre-1970s stock with accessibility deficiencies. Boverket BBR series governs building accessibility standards.

**Norway (NO):** Husbanken (Housing Bank) administers Livsløpsbolig grants/loans. 92% of housing fails basic accessibility (already in housing_deficit). DiBK (Direktoratet for byggkvalitet) governs standards. No specific cost studies found.

**Denmark (DK), Finland (FI):** Nordic welfare model. Accessibility legislation exists. No specific English-language cost studies found.

**Italy (IT), Spain (ES):** Southern European model. Accessibility legislation exists (Italy: DM 236/1989, Spain: CTE-DB-SUA). No specific cost studies found. The Ielegems 2024 Belgian study is the closest EU evidence.

**Korea (KR):** Korean Land & Housing Corporation (LH) has research capacity. Universal design initiatives exist. No English-language cost studies found. Korean-language research likely exists on J-STAGE equivalent.

**Assessment:** The absence of published accessibility cost studies across all 7 jurisdictions is consistent with GAP-ECON-01 (no peer-reviewed hedonic isolation anywhere). Accessibility cost research is a globally sparse field — the project's cross-jurisdictional evidence base (DE, BE, US, CA, UK, AU, FR, SE) represents the majority of what exists in English-language literature.
